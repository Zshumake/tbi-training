# Chapter 12: Concussion, Mild Traumatic Brain Injury, and Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy

---

## SECTION 12.1 [BOARD] CONCUSSION DEFINITION

### 12.1.1 ACRM Definition of Mild TBI

The American Congress of Rehabilitation Medicine (ACRM) established the foundational definition of mild traumatic brain injury in 1993, which remains the most widely used diagnostic framework in rehabilitation medicine. According to the ACRM, mild TBI is defined as a traumatically induced physiologic disruption of brain function, manifested by at least ONE of the following:

1. Any period of loss of consciousness (LOC)
2. Any loss of memory for events immediately before or after the accident (post-traumatic amnesia, PTA)
3. Any alteration in mental state at the time of the accident (e.g., feeling dazed, disoriented, or confused)
4. Focal neurological deficit(s) that may or may not be transient

**Critical Severity Thresholds (injury does NOT exceed):**
- Loss of consciousness: <30 minutes
- Post-traumatic amnesia: <24 hours
- Initial Glasgow Coma Scale: 13-15 (when assessed at 30 minutes or later post-injury)

**Clinical Pearl:** Only ONE criterion is needed for diagnosis. A patient who felt "dazed" but never lost consciousness and has no amnesia still meets ACRM criteria for mild TBI. Conversely, loss of consciousness is NOT required for the diagnosis. This is a frequently tested board concept.

### 12.1.2 Amsterdam/Berlin 2022 Consensus Definition

The 6th International Conference on Concussion in Sport was held in Amsterdam in October 2022 (published in the British Journal of Sports Medicine in 2023). This consensus statement replaced the prior 2016 Berlin (5th) consensus and represents the current standard for sport-related concussion (SRC) definition and management (Patricios et al., 2023).

**Definition:**
Sport-related concussion is a traumatic brain injury caused by a direct blow to the head, neck, or body with an impulsive force transmitted to the brain that:
- Results in neurological impairment that evolves rapidly (onset within minutes to hours)
- Resolves spontaneously (typically within days to weeks in adults, up to 4 weeks in children)
- Has largely normal structural neuroimaging
- Results from FUNCTIONAL rather than STRUCTURAL injury

**Key Updates from the 2022 Consensus:**

1. **Symptom onset may be delayed:** Recognition that symptoms may take HOURS to fully develop, not always presenting immediately at the time of impact.
2. **Early exercise is recommended:** Sub-symptom threshold aerobic exercise starting 24-48 hours post-injury is now recommended, replacing the previous "complete rest until symptom-free" approach.
3. **Prolonged rest is harmful:** Strict rest beyond 48 hours may actually delay recovery.
4. **Children recover more slowly:** Recovery in children may take up to 4 weeks (vs. 10-14 days in adults).
5. **Same-day return to play:** NOT recommended for any athlete with suspected concussion.
6. **Preferred terminology:** "Sport-related concussion" (SRC) is the preferred term; "mild TBI" is considered synonymous for clinical purposes.

## SECTION 12.2 [BOARD] MILD TBI CRITERIA

### 12.2.1 Severity Classification Thresholds

The classification of TBI severity uses three primary parameters: GCS score, duration of loss of consciousness, and duration of post-traumatic amnesia.

| Parameter | Mild TBI | Moderate TBI | Severe TBI |
|-----------|----------|--------------|------------|
| GCS (at 30 min) | 13-15 | 9-12 | 3-8 |
| LOC duration | <30 minutes | 30 min - 24 hours | >24 hours |
| PTA duration | <24 hours | 1-7 days | >7 days |

**Clinical Pearl:** GCS should be assessed at 30 minutes or more after injury when possible. A patient with GCS 15 at 30 minutes who had a brief period of disorientation at the scene meets criteria for mild TBI, even though their current GCS is perfect.

### 12.2.2 Epidemiology

- Estimated 1.6-3.8 million sports-related concussions per year in the United States
- Approximately 300,000 sports concussions annually among high school athletes
- Highest incidence sports: football, ice hockey, lacrosse, soccer (heading), rugby
- Female athletes have higher concussion rates than males in comparable sports (soccer, basketball, softball vs. baseball)
- Most concussions occur WITHOUT loss of consciousness (>90%)
- Repeat concussion risk is increased 3-6 times after a first concussion
- Typical adult recovery: 10-14 days; children/adolescents may take up to 4 weeks
- Approximately 10-30% of patients develop persistent post-concussive symptoms beyond the expected recovery window

**Risk Factors for Prolonged Recovery:**
- Prior concussion history (strongest predictor)
- Female sex
- Younger age (children/adolescents recover more slowly)
- Pre-existing psychiatric conditions (depression, anxiety, PTSD)
- Premorbid migraine history
- Learning disabilities and ADHD
- Higher initial symptom burden
- LOC or amnesia at time of injury
- Litigation/secondary gain (controversial but documented association)

## SECTION 12.3 [BOARD] SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS

### 12.3.1 Symptom Domains

Concussion symptoms are organized into four primary domains:

**1. Physical/Somatic Symptoms:**
- Headache (most common symptom, reported in >90% of concussions)
- Dizziness and balance problems
- Nausea and vomiting
- Visual disturbances (blurred vision, double vision, light sensitivity)
- Noise sensitivity (phonophobia)
- Fatigue
- Neck pain

**2. Cognitive Symptoms:**
- Difficulty concentrating
- Difficulty remembering (short-term memory impairment)
- Feeling mentally "foggy" or "in a fog"
- Feeling slowed down
- Confusion
- Difficulty with processing speed

**3. Emotional/Behavioral Symptoms:**
- Irritability
- Sadness or depression
- Anxiety or nervousness
- Emotional lability (being "more emotional" than usual)
- Feeling "not right"

**4. Sleep Symptoms:**
- Difficulty falling asleep
- Difficulty staying asleep
- Sleeping more than usual
- Sleeping less than usual
- Daytime drowsiness

**Clinical Pearl:** Headache is the most common symptom of concussion. Loss of consciousness occurs in fewer than 10% of concussions and is NOT required for diagnosis. Sleep disturbances are present in a majority of concussion patients and may independently delay recovery if not addressed.

## SECTION 12.4 [BOARD] SCAT6: SPORT CONCUSSION ASSESSMENT TOOL

### 12.4.1 SCAT6 Components (Ages 13+)

The SCAT6 is the standardized sideline and office assessment tool for evaluating suspected concussion in athletes aged 13 and older. The Child-SCAT6 is used for ages 8-12 (updated from 5-12 in the SCAT5).

**IMMEDIATE/ON-FIELD ASSESSMENT:**

1. **Red flags screening:** Neck pain, deteriorating consciousness, seizure/convulsion, repeated vomiting, severe or worsening headache, double vision, increasing confusion or agitation
2. **Observable signs:** Lying motionless on the playing surface, motor incoordination/ataxia, blank or vacant stare, balance/gait difficulty, facial injury suggesting head trauma, slow to get up
3. **Glasgow Coma Scale:** Standard 15-point assessment
4. **Cervical spine assessment:** Enhanced in SCAT6 with more detailed cervical screening

**OFF-FIELD ASSESSMENT:**

1. **Athlete background:** Concussion history, medical history, current medications, mental health history
2. **Symptom evaluation:** 22-item graded symptom checklist, each rated 0-6 severity. Maximum symptom severity score = 132
3. **Cognitive screening (SAC-2):**
   - Orientation: month, date, day of week, year, time of day (5 points)
   - Immediate memory: 10-word list x 3 trials (previously 5 words in SCAT5)
   - Concentration: digits backward and months in reverse (timed component added)
   - Delayed recall: 10-word list (5 minutes later)
   - Cognitive composite score: combines orientation, immediate memory, concentration, and delayed recall
4. **Neurological screening:** Reading assessment, finger-to-nose test, single leg stance, tandem gait (timed)
5. **Balance examination (Modified BESS):** Three stances (double leg, single leg, tandem) on firm surface and foam pad, 20 seconds each, counting errors

**Clinical Pearl:** The SCAT6 explicitly states it cannot be completed in less than 10-15 minutes, preventing rushed sideline assessments. Baseline SCAT6 testing is RECOMMENDED but NOT required for concussion diagnosis. The SCAT6 is a diagnostic aid, not a standalone diagnostic tool.

### 12.4.2 [BOARD] Key Changes from SCAT5 to SCAT6

| Feature | SCAT5 | SCAT6 |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Word list for memory | 5 words | 10 words (reduces ceiling effect) |
| Cognitive assessment | SAC | SAC-2 (improved reliability) |
| Digits backward | Standard | Extended by 2 digits (reduces ceiling effect) |
| Months backward | Standard | Timed component added (processing speed measure) |
| Cognitive composite | Not calculated | Calculated (improves diagnostic discrimination) |
| Minimum administration time | ~10 minutes | Explicitly 10-15 minutes |
| Child version age range | 5-12 years | 8-12 years (children under 8 have insufficient reliability) |
| Serial evaluation | "Consider" repeat assessments | Emphasizes ongoing serial evaluations within hours and days |
| New companion tool | None | SCOAT6 (Sport Concussion Office Assessment Tool) for follow-up |
| Cervical screening | Basic | Enhanced cervical spine assessment |
| Terminology | "Medical professionals" | "Healthcare professionals" (broader inclusion) |

**Clinical Pearl:** The shift from 5-word to 10-word memory lists in the SCAT6 was driven by ceiling effects observed with the SCAT5 -- many athletes scored perfectly on the 5-word list even when concussed, reducing the test's sensitivity. The addition of a cognitive composite score improves test-retest reliability and reduces false-positive rates.

## SECTION 12.5 [BOARD] GRADUATED RETURN-TO-PLAY PROTOCOL

### 12.5.1 The 6-Step Protocol

The 2022 Amsterdam consensus maintained the 6-step graduated return-to-play (RTP) protocol. Each step requires a MINIMUM of 24 hours symptom-free at that level before advancing to the next step. The athlete must be symptom-free at rest before starting Step 1.

| Step | Activity | Objective | Duration |
|------|----------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited activity | Daily activities that do not provoke symptoms; gradual reintroduction of work/school | Minimum 24 hours |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise | Walking, swimming, stationary cycling; HR <70% max; no resistance training; <15-20 min | Minimum 24 hours |
| 3 | Sport-specific exercise | Running/skating drills; no head impact activities; progressive complex movement | Minimum 24 hours |
| 4 | Non-contact training drills | Complex drills; may start resistance training; increased cognitive load | Minimum 24 hours |
| 5 | Full-contact practice | Normal practice with contact; requires MEDICAL CLEARANCE before this step | Minimum 24 hours |
| 6 | Return to competition | Normal game play | -- |

**Key Rules:**
- If symptoms recur at any step, drop back to the PREVIOUS asymptomatic step and wait 24 hours
- Minimum 24 hours per step; total minimum protocol duration is 6 days from start (not from injury date)
- Medical clearance is required BEFORE Step 5 (full-contact practice), NOT before return to competition
- Children and adolescents should take a MORE CONSERVATIVE approach with longer time at each step

**Clinical Pearl:** Medical clearance is needed before STEP 5 (full-contact practice), NOT before Step 6 (return to competition). This is a commonly tested distinction. The purpose of Step 5 is to restore confidence and allow coaching staff to assess functional readiness in a controlled practice setting before the athlete returns to game play.

### 12.5.2 [BOARD] Return-to-Learn Protocol

The graduated return-to-learn (RTL) protocol guides students back to academic activities and should PRECEDE the return-to-play protocol.

| Step | Activity | Goal |
|------|----------|------|
| 1 | Daily activities at home | Activities that do not provoke symptoms; limit screen time initially |
| 2 | School activities at home | Homework, reading, cognitive activities outside classroom; gradually increase duration |
| 3 | Return to school part-time | Gradual introduction of schoolwork; modified schedule, extra time, reduced workload |
| 4 | Return to school full-time | Full academic schedule with accommodations as needed |

**Key Principles:**
- Return-to-learn PRECEDES return-to-play
- Students should NOT return to full sport participation until they have returned to full academic activities
- Brief initial rest (24-48 hours) followed by graduated cognitive activity
- Prolonged cognitive rest (>48 hours) is NOT recommended and may worsen symptoms
- Academic accommodations may include: reduced workload, extended test time, preferential seating, breaks during class, reduced screen time

**Clinical Pearl:** The old recommendation was "complete rest until symptom-free." The current 2022 consensus recommends only 24-48 hours of initial rest, followed by gradual reintroduction of cognitive and physical activity below the symptom threshold. Prolonged rest >48 hours may actually DELAY recovery. This paradigm shift is heavily tested.

## SECTION 12.6 [BOARD] SECOND IMPACT SYNDROME

### 12.6.1 Definition and Pathophysiology

Second impact syndrome (SIS) is a catastrophic and often fatal condition that occurs when a second concussion is sustained before the brain has fully recovered from a first concussion. The underlying mechanism involves loss of cerebrovascular autoregulation, leading to massive, rapid cerebral edema and transtentorial herniation.

**Key Facts:**
- Mortality: approximately 50%
- Morbidity: approaching 100% (virtually all survivors have severe permanent disability)
- Predominantly occurs in adolescents and young adults
- Onset: rapid deterioration within minutes of the second impact; progression from alert to coma can occur within 2-5 minutes
- Prevention: strict adherence to graduated RTP protocol; do NOT allow return to play until fully symptom-free

**Pathophysiology:** The first concussion disrupts cerebrovascular autoregulation and neurometabolic homeostasis. If a second impact occurs during this vulnerable period, the brain's already impaired ability to regulate blood flow and intracranial pressure fails catastrophically, leading to diffuse cerebral vascular engorgement, massive edema, and rapid herniation.

**Clinical Pearl:** Second impact syndrome has approximately 50% mortality and approximately 100% morbidity. These statistics are the primary justification for conservative return-to-play protocols and the principle "when in doubt, sit them out." Same-day return to play is NEVER recommended.

## SECTION 12.7 [BOARD] POST-CONCUSSION SYNDROME

### 12.7.1 Diagnostic Criteria

Post-concussion syndrome (PCS) refers to a constellation of symptoms persisting beyond the expected recovery window after mild TBI.

**ICD-10 Criteria:**
History of head trauma with loss of consciousness preceding symptom onset by up to 4 weeks, with symptoms in 3 or more of 6 categories:

1. Headache, dizziness, malaise, fatigue
2. Irritability, depression, anxiety, emotional lability
3. Subjective concentration/memory difficulty, intellectual difficulties (without neuropsychological confirmation required)
4. Insomnia
5. Reduced alcohol tolerance
6. Preoccupation with above symptoms and fear of brain damage

**Duration:** Symptoms persist beyond 3 months (some definitions use >1 month for adults, >4 weeks for children).

**Prevalence:** Most mTBI patients recover within 10-14 days (adults) or up to 4 weeks (children). Approximately 10-30% develop persistent post-concussive symptoms (PPCS). The "miserable minority" -- approximately 10-15% -- have symptoms persisting beyond 1 year.

### 12.7.2 Symptom Overlap and Differential Diagnosis

PCS symptoms overlap significantly with several other conditions, complicating diagnosis and management:

- **Depression:** Fatigue, concentration difficulty, sleep disturbance, irritability
- **PTSD:** Cognitive impairment, emotional disturbance, sleep disruption, hyperarousal
- **Chronic pain syndromes:** Headache, fatigue, cognitive difficulty, mood disturbance
- **Sleep disorders:** Fatigue, cognitive impairment, mood changes
- **Cervical spine dysfunction:** Headache, dizziness, cognitive difficulty

**Clinical Pearl:** In military populations, up to 33-44% of blast TBI patients have comorbid PTSD, making it challenging to attribute symptoms to TBI versus PTSD. Treatment should target SPECIFIC SYMPTOMS (headache, sleep, mood, vestibular, cognitive) rather than treating "the concussion" as a monolithic entity. Multidisciplinary evaluation is recommended when symptoms persist beyond 30-90 days.

## SECTION 12.8 [COMPREHENSIVE] CONCUSSION PATHOPHYSIOLOGY: THE NEUROMETABOLIC CASCADE

### 12.8.1 Ionic and Metabolic Disturbances

Concussion produces a complex sequence of neurometabolic events that disrupts normal cellular function without causing macroscopic structural damage. This "neurometabolic cascade" was first described comprehensively by Giza and Hovda (2001, updated 2014).

**Sequence of Events:**

1. **Mechanical deformation:** Biomechanical forces stretch neuronal membranes and axons, disrupting cell membrane integrity and opening ion channels nonselectively.

2. **Ionic flux:** Potassium (K+) effluxes from cells into the extracellular space. Sodium (Na+) and calcium (Ca2+) flow into cells. This ionic disequilibrium depolarizes neurons indiscriminately.

3. **Glutamate excitotoxicity:** Depolarized neurons release excessive glutamate (the primary excitatory neurotransmitter) into the synaptic cleft. Glutamate binds NMDA and AMPA receptors, further exacerbating ionic flux and cellular depolarization.

4. **Energy crisis:** The Na+/K+-ATPase pump works overtime to restore ionic equilibrium, dramatically increasing glucose consumption ("hypermetabolism"). Simultaneously, cerebral blood flow is paradoxically DECREASED. This mismatch between increased metabolic demand and decreased blood supply creates a cellular energy crisis.

5. **Calcium sequestration:** Excess intracellular calcium is sequestered by mitochondria, impairing mitochondrial oxidative metabolism and reducing ATP production. This further exacerbates the energy crisis.

6. **Inflammatory response:** Neuroinflammation develops, with microglial activation, cytokine release, and blood-brain barrier dysfunction.

7. **Axonal injury:** Cytoskeletal disruption and impaired axonal transport occur, particularly in areas of high mechanical strain. While this does not constitute the frank axonal transection seen in severe DAI, it produces functional impairment.

8. **Recovery phase:** Ionic homeostasis is gradually restored over days to weeks. Mitochondrial function recovers. Cerebral blood flow normalizes. This recovery timeline corresponds to the clinical resolution of symptoms.

**Clinical Significance:** The neurometabolic cascade explains the vulnerability period during which a second impact is dangerous (the brain is in a state of energy crisis and cannot tolerate additional metabolic demands). It also provides the rationale for early sub-symptom threshold exercise, which may help restore cerebrovascular autoregulation and accelerate metabolic recovery.

## SECTION 12.9 [COMPREHENSIVE] VOMS: VESTIBULAR/OCULOMOTOR SCREENING

### 12.9.1 Overview and Development

The Vestibular/Ocular Motor Screening (VOMS) was developed by Mucha and colleagues at the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center in 2014. It was designed to screen for vestibular and oculomotor dysfunction after concussion -- domains that standard neurocognitive testing (such as ImPACT) may miss entirely.

**Administration:** Takes 5-10 minutes. Approximately 90% sensitivity for identifying concussion when combined with standard assessment tools.

### 12.9.2 The 7 VOMS Assessments

| Assessment | Procedure | What It Tests |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Smooth pursuit | Track a finger moving horizontally and vertically | Extraocular muscle coordination; cerebellar and brainstem pathways |
| 2. Horizontal saccades | Rapidly shift gaze between two targets horizontally | Frontal eye field function; saccade generation pathways |
| 3. Vertical saccades | Rapidly shift gaze between two targets vertically | Brainstem vertical gaze centers; midbrain function |
| 4. Near point of convergence (NPC) | Bring target toward nose until double vision; measure distance | Convergence function; CN III (medial rectus); brainstem convergence center |
| 5. Horizontal VOR | Fixate on a stationary target while turning head horizontally | Vestibulo-ocular reflex; semicircular canal-brainstem-ocular motor pathway |
| 6. Vertical VOR | Fixate on a stationary target while nodding head vertically | Vertical VOR; semicircular canal-brainstem pathway |
| 7. Visual motion sensitivity (VMS) | Stand with eyes open while being rotated or viewing moving visual field | Visual-vestibular integration; central processing of motion |

### 12.9.3 Scoring and Interpretation

**Baseline Symptoms:** Before testing begins, the patient rates four symptoms on a 0-10 scale: headache, dizziness, nausea, and fogginess.

**Post-Assessment Ratings:** After EACH of the 7 assessments, the patient re-rates the same four symptoms on the 0-10 scale.

**Positive Finding:**
- Any symptom provocation score >=2 points ABOVE baseline on any item
- NPC distance >=5 cm (normal is <5 cm); associated with convergence insufficiency

**Clinical Interpretation:**
- NPC >=5 cm OR any VOMS symptom score >=2 above baseline significantly increases the probability of concussion diagnosis
- Persistent positive VOMS findings beyond 7-10 days warrant referral to vestibular rehabilitation therapy
- Specific VOMS abnormalities guide targeted rehabilitation: convergence insufficiency requires convergence exercises; VOR abnormalities require gaze stabilization exercises; visual motion sensitivity requires habituation therapy

**Clinical Pearl:** VOMS identifies vestibular and oculomotor problems that standard neurocognitive testing misses. The two key positive findings are: NPC >=5 cm and symptom provocation >=2 above baseline. Vestibular rehabilitation has strong evidence for treating post-concussive vestibular/oculomotor dysfunction and significantly shortens recovery time.

## SECTION 12.10 [COMPREHENSIVE] BUFFALO CONCUSSION TREADMILL TEST (BCTT)

### 12.10.1 Protocol and Purpose

The Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test (BCTT) was developed by Dr. John Leddy and colleagues at the University at Buffalo. It serves dual purposes: (1) determining exercise tolerance after concussion, and (2) identifying the heart rate threshold (HRt) at which symptoms are provoked, which then guides exercise prescription.

**Protocol:**
- Modified Balke treadmill test
- Starting parameters: 3.3 mph at 0% incline
- Incline increases by 1% every minute while speed remains constant
- Patient rates symptoms every minute on a 0-10 scale
- Test endpoints: symptom increase by >=3 points on the 0-10 scale, OR patient reaches 85% of age-predicted maximum heart rate, OR volitional exhaustion

**Heart Rate Threshold (HRt):** The heart rate at which symptoms are provoked. This becomes the upper limit for exercise prescription.

**Exercise Prescription:** Sub-symptom threshold aerobic exercise at 80% of HRt for 20 minutes daily.

**Timing:** The BCTT can be performed as early as 48 hours post-injury per the 2022 consensus.

### 12.10.2 Evidence for Sub-Symptom Threshold Exercise

The 2022 Amsterdam consensus represents a paradigm shift from prolonged rest to early controlled aerobic exercise:

- **Leddy et al. RCT evidence:** Sub-symptom threshold aerobic exercise starting within days of concussion reduces recovery time by approximately 4 days compared to stretching control.
- **PPCS risk reduction:** Approximately 48% reduction in risk of persistent post-concussion symptoms compared to rest/placebo.
- **Mechanisms of benefit:** Exercise improves cerebral blood flow autoregulation, reduces neuroinflammation, upregulates brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), and restores autonomic nervous system balance.
- **Exercise intolerance as prognostic indicator:** Symptom exacerbation before reaching 85% max HR on BCTT predicts prolonged recovery; approximately 55% of patients show exercise intolerance at 1 week post-concussion.
- **Harm of prolonged rest:** Rest exceeding 48 hours may worsen outcomes through deconditioning, mood deterioration, symptom amplification, and social isolation.

**Alternative:** The Buffalo Concussion Bike Test (cycle ergometer) is an alternative for patients who cannot use a treadmill, with comparable diagnostic value.

**Clinical Pearl:** Early sub-symptom threshold aerobic exercise (starting 24-48 hours post-injury) is now the standard of care. Prolonged complete rest >48 hours is considered HARMFUL. The BCTT identifies the heart rate threshold to guide safe exercise prescription.

## SECTION 12.11 [COMPREHENSIVE] CERVICOGENIC CONTRIBUTION TO CONCUSSION SYMPTOMS

### 12.11.1 The Cervical Spine-Concussion Connection

The cervical spine and brain are injured by the SAME biomechanical mechanism: acceleration-deceleration forces that produce concussion simultaneously injure cervical structures. Up to 50% of concussion patients have concurrent cervical spine dysfunction that contributes to symptom persistence.

**Cervicogenic Symptoms Overlapping with Concussion:**
- Headache (cervicogenic headache from C2-3 facet joint or upper cervical musculature)
- Dizziness (cervicogenic dizziness from disrupted cervical proprioceptors)
- Visual disturbances
- Nausea
- Cognitive difficulty (indirectly, through pain and proprioceptive disruption)
- Balance problems
- Neck pain

### 12.11.2 Key Cervical Structures

- **Upper cervical facet joints (C0-C3):** Major source of cervicogenic headache; dense innervation by C2-3 dorsal rami; pain referred to occipital, temporal, and frontal regions
- **Cervical proprioceptors (muscle spindles):** Upper cervical muscles have the highest density of muscle spindles in the body; disruption causes proprioceptive dysfunction and dizziness
- **Vertebral artery:** Runs through the transverse foramina of C1-C6; vulnerability to compression or irritation
- **Cervical nerve roots:** Contribute to referred pain patterns

### 12.11.3 Clinical Assessment

- Cervical range of motion (active and passive)
- Joint mobility testing (segmental assessment)
- Flexion rotation test (FRT): specific for C1-2 dysfunction; positive if rotation is <32 degrees or reproduces symptoms
- Cervical proprioception testing: joint position error (JPE) test; patient returns head to a neutral starting position with eyes closed; error >4.5 degrees is abnormal
- Palpation of upper cervical segments and suboccipital musculature

### 12.11.4 Treatment Implications

**Evidence:** Cervical spine manual therapy combined with vestibular rehabilitation reduces symptoms and speeds recovery more effectively than either intervention alone.

**Clinical Pearl:** Always evaluate the cervical spine in concussion patients, especially those with persistent headache and dizziness. Cervicogenic and vestibular symptoms overlap significantly and can be distinguished through targeted examination (flexion rotation test for cervical, VOMS for vestibular). Treating cervicogenic dysfunction may resolve symptoms previously attributed to the concussion itself.

## SECTION 12.12 [COMPREHENSIVE] BLOOD BIOMARKERS FOR CONCUSSION

### 12.12.1 FDA-Cleared Biomarkers: GFAP and UCH-L1

**GFAP (Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein):**
- Source: astrocyte intermediate filament protein
- Released when astrocytes are damaged or the blood-brain barrier is disrupted
- Marker of GLIAL injury
- Peaks within hours of injury

**UCH-L1 (Ubiquitin C-terminal Hydrolase L1):**
- Source: neuronal enzyme concentrated in cell bodies
- Released with neuronal injury/death
- Marker of NEURONAL injury
- Elevated within hours; peaks at 8-12 hours

**Banyan Brain Trauma Indicator (BTI):**
- FDA-cleared in February 2018 as the first blood test for TBI evaluation
- Combines GFAP + UCH-L1
- Must be drawn within 12 hours of injury
- Sensitivity ~97% for detecting intracranial lesions on CT
- Negative predictive value >99.6%
- Clinical utility: identifies patients who do NOT need CT scan (high NPV)
- Does NOT diagnose concussion -- identifies patients who can safely AVOID CT imaging

**Subsequent FDA Clearances:**
- Abbott i-STAT TBI Plasma test (2021): point-of-care GFAP + UCH-L1 on handheld platform
- Abbott Alinity i and ARCHITECT platforms (2023): high-throughput laboratory GFAP and UCH-L1
- bioMerieux VIDAS TBI test (2024): GFAP + UCH-L1 using enzyme-linked fluorescent detection

### 12.12.2 Emerging Biomarkers

**Neurofilament Light (NfL):**
- Source: axonal cytoskeletal protein (neurofilament)
- Marker of AXONAL injury
- Rises more slowly than GFAP/UCH-L1 (days to weeks); stays elevated longer
- Useful for subacute and chronic monitoring
- Elevated NfL correlates with prolonged recovery; strongest prognostic biomarker candidate
- Significant elevations at 7 days and 4 months post-concussion in pediatric studies

**Tau Protein:**
- Source: microtubule-associated protein from neurons/axons
- Marker of neuronal/axonal injury
- Increases within hours after concussion
- Plasma tau elevations correlate with concussion diagnosis and return-to-play timing
- Less specific than GFAP or NfL (also elevated in neurodegenerative conditions)

**S100B:**
- Source: astrocyte calcium-binding protein
- Widely used in Europe for TBI assessment
- Very sensitive but low specificity (false positives from extracranial injuries -- fractures, muscle injury)
- Less clinically useful than GFAP/UCH-L1 due to poor specificity

**Clinical Pearl:** GFAP/UCH-L1 (Banyan BTI and subsequent platforms) is the first FDA-cleared blood test for TBI. Its clinical role is to RULE OUT the need for CT scan (high negative predictive value), NOT to diagnose concussion. NfL is the most promising prognostic biomarker for predicting prolonged recovery.

## SECTION 12.13 [COMPREHENSIVE] CHRONIC TRAUMATIC ENCEPHALOPATHY (CTE)

### 12.13.1 Definition and Core Concepts

Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease associated with repetitive head impacts (RHI), including both symptomatic concussions and subconcussive impacts. As of 2025, CTE can ONLY be definitively diagnosed post-mortem via neuropathological examination of brain tissue.

**Key Distinctions:**
- CTE is associated with REPETITIVE head impacts, not a single TBI
- The total burden of subconcussive exposure may be more important than the number of diagnosed concussions
- Duration of contact sport exposure (12+ years) may be a stronger predictor of CTE than the number of diagnosed concussions
- No validated in vivo biomarkers or diagnostic imaging tests exist as of 2025
- No proven treatments to slow or reverse CTE; management is symptomatic
- Prevention through reducing head impact exposure is the primary strategy

### 12.13.2 Pathognomonic Finding

The pathognomonic neuropathological finding of CTE, established by the 2016 NINDS/NIBIB consensus criteria, is the perivascular accumulation of hyperphosphorylated tau (p-tau) in neurons and astroglia at the DEPTHS of cortical SULCI in an IRREGULAR pattern.

This lesion is REQUIRED for CTE diagnosis and distinguishes CTE from all other tauopathies. It is considered unique to CTE and is not seen in Alzheimer disease, progressive supranuclear palsy, corticobasal degeneration, or other tau-related neurodegenerative conditions.

**Why sulcal depths?** The biomechanical explanation is that acceleration-deceleration forces produce maximum shear stress at the depths of cortical sulci (where cortical gray matter bends), creating focal zones of axonal and vascular injury that initiate tau pathology.

### 12.13.3 [COMPREHENSIVE] McKee Staging System (Traditional 4-Stage)

The McKee staging system, developed by Dr. Ann McKee and colleagues at Boston University, classifies CTE neuropathological severity into four progressive stages:

| Stage | Pathology | Clinical Correlation |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| I (Mild) | Isolated perivascular p-tau foci in frontal cortex at sulcal depths | May be asymptomatic or have headache, attention/concentration difficulties |
| II (Mild-Moderate) | Multiple p-tau foci at sulcal depths; spread to superficial cortical layers; nucleus basalis of Meynert and locus coeruleus involved | Depression, mood swings, short-term memory loss, headaches, reduced impulse control |
| III (Moderate-Severe) | Widespread p-tau in frontal, temporal, parietal, insular cortex; hippocampus and amygdala involved; mild cerebral atrophy; septal abnormalities | Significant cognitive impairment, executive dysfunction, explosivity, visuospatial difficulties |
| IV (Severe) | Widespread p-tau throughout cortex, white matter, brainstem, cerebellum, spinal cord; prominent TDP-43; severe frontal/temporal atrophy; septal perforation | Profound dementia, aggressive behavior, parkinsonism, gait disturbance, dysarthria |

### 12.13.4 [COMPREHENSIVE] Simplified Low/High CTE Classification

The 2021 NINDS/NIBIB consensus refined the staging criteria and proposed a simplified two-tier classification system to improve inter-rater reliability among neuropathologists:

| Classification | Criteria |
|---------------|----------|
| **LOW CTE** | p-tau pathology RESTRICTED to focal cortical lesions only (sulcal depths); perivascular p-tau aggregates in neurons at sulcal depths without widespread medial temporal lobe involvement |
| **HIGH CTE** | WIDESPREAD p-tau pathology extending to medial temporal lobe structures (hippocampus, entorhinal cortex) and diencephalon, IN ADDITION to focal cortical lesions |

**Rationale:** The original 4-stage McKee system had limited inter-rater reliability, particularly for distinguishing adjacent stages (I vs. II, III vs. IV). The simplified Low/High system demonstrated improved consistency among neuropathologists and maintains prognostic validity.

**Supportive (but NOT required) features:** TDP-43 pathology, beta-amyloid deposits, astrocytic tangles, neuroinflammation.

## SECTION 12.14 [COMPREHENSIVE] CTE vs. ALZHEIMER'S DISEASE

Distinguishing CTE from Alzheimer disease (AD) is a critical neuropathological and clinical challenge:

| Feature | CTE | Alzheimer Disease |
|---------|-----|-------------------|
| Tau protein location | p-tau at DEPTHS of cortical SULCI (perivascular) | p-tau at CRESTS of GYRI (neurofibrillary tangles) |
| Amyloid-beta | Present in ~50% (NOT required for diagnosis) | ALWAYS present (amyloid plaques are a defining feature) |
| Earliest cortex affected | Frontal and temporal (sulcal depths) | Entorhinal cortex and hippocampus |
| Age of onset | Can be young (30s) | Typically >65 years |
| History of RHI | Required | Not related |
| TDP-43 pathology | Common (Stage III-IV / High CTE) | Less common |
| Clinical onset | Often behavioral/mood changes first | Usually memory loss first |
| Progression | Variable; can be stable for years | Relentlessly progressive |
| Amyloid PET scan | Often NEGATIVE | POSITIVE |

**Clinical Pearl:** The PATHOGNOMONIC difference between CTE and Alzheimer disease: CTE tau is at the DEPTHS of cortical SULCI in a perivascular distribution; Alzheimer tau is at the CRESTS of GYRI. Amyloid-beta plaques are NOT required for CTE diagnosis but ARE required for Alzheimer diagnosis. This is one of the most commonly tested neuropathology distinctions on board examinations.

## SECTION 12.15 [COMPREHENSIVE] TRAUMATIC ENCEPHALOPATHY SYNDROME (TES)

### 12.15.1 Clinical Criteria

Since CTE can only be definitively diagnosed post-mortem, the NINDS convened an expert panel that proposed clinical diagnostic criteria for Traumatic Encephalopathy Syndrome (TES) -- the clinical syndrome associated with CTE pathology (Katz et al., 2021).

**Diagnostic Requirements (ALL must be met):**

1. **Substantial exposure to repetitive head impacts:** From contact sports (e.g., football, hockey, boxing, soccer), military service, or other sources of repetitive head trauma
2. **Core clinical features:** Cognitive impairment (episodic memory and/or executive function) AND/OR neurobehavioral dysregulation (explosivity, impulsivity, rage, mood lability, apathy)
3. **Progressive course:** Documented progression over at least 1 year
4. **Not better explained:** Clinical features are not fully accounted for by another neurologic, psychiatric, or medical condition

**Diagnostic Certainty Levels:**
- Suggestive TES
- Possible TES
- Probable TES (based on number and specificity of features)

**Functional Dependence Grading:** 5 levels ranging from independent to severe dementia.

**Important Caveat:** TES is currently a RESEARCH framework. It is NOT yet a validated clinical diagnosis for individual patient management decisions. No FDA-approved in vivo biomarkers or imaging tests can confirm CTE pathology during life.

### 12.15.2 Clinical Variants of CTE

CTE has been described with two clinical presentation patterns that frequently overlap:

**Behavioral/Mood Variant (Younger Onset, 20s-30s):**
- Emotional instability and mood lability
- Impulsivity and poor judgment
- Aggression and explosivity
- Depression
- Paranoia
- Suicidality

**Cognitive Variant (Older Onset, 50s-60s):**
- Progressive memory impairment (episodic memory)
- Executive dysfunction
- Visuospatial difficulties
- Language decline
- Eventually progresses to dementia

**Motor Features (Later Stages):**
- Parkinsonism (bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor)
- Gait disturbance
- Dysarthria
- Features may overlap with ALS (CTE-ALS variant described in some cases)

**Clinical Pearl:** The two clinical variants are not mutually exclusive. Many patients initially present with one pattern and develop features of the other over time. Younger onset with behavioral/mood changes is more characteristic; older onset with cognitive decline may be difficult to distinguish from Alzheimer disease without neuropathological examination.

## SECTION 12.16 [COMPREHENSIVE] UNITE STUDY FINDINGS

### 12.16.1 Overview

The Understanding Neurologic Injury and Traumatic Encephalopathy (UNITE) study is the largest clinicopathological investigation of CTE, conducted through the VA-BU-CLF (Veterans Affairs-Boston University-Concussion Legacy Foundation) Brain Bank, directed by Dr. Ann McKee. The brain bank has collected over 1,700 brain donations, with more than 800 diagnosed with CTE using the NINDS consensus criteria.

### 12.16.2 Key Findings

**NFL Players:**
- CTE was identified in 110 of 111 (99%) of former NFL players examined from the brain bank
- This striking finding generated enormous public attention but must be interpreted with awareness of ascertainment bias

**All Football Players (High School through Professional):**
- CTE found in approximately 87% of all football players studied across all levels

**Young Athletes Study (2023, JAMA Neurology):**
- 152 deceased athletes under age 30 with history of contact sport exposure
- CTE pathology found in 63 (41%)
- Nearly all CTE cases in young athletes were Stage I or II (mild/low CTE)
- Included the first American woman athlete diagnosed with CTE: a 28-year-old collegiate soccer player

**Duration-CTE Association:**
- 223 of 266 participants met neuropathological criteria for CTE
- Each additional year of football play was associated with increased odds of CTE (OR = 1.30 per year played)
- Duration of play was associated with CTE severity
- Duration of contact sport exposure, not just number of diagnosed concussions, correlated with CTE pathology

### 12.16.3 Ascertainment Bias -- A Critical Caveat

**The true population prevalence of CTE in contact sport athletes is UNKNOWN.** Brain bank studies have significant ascertainment bias: families donate brains because the deceased had symptoms suggestive of CTE or because of concerns about the effects of head impacts. This selection bias dramatically inflates the apparent prevalence in brain bank cohorts. The 99% prevalence among NFL brain bank donors does NOT mean that 99% of all NFL players have CTE.

**Clinical Pearl:** UNITE data convincingly demonstrates that CTE is strongly associated with repetitive head impacts from contact sports, and that duration of exposure is a key risk factor. However, the true population prevalence cannot be determined from brain bank studies due to ascertainment bias. This nuance is important for clinical counseling and is tested on examinations.

## SECTION 12.17 [COMPREHENSIVE] SUBCONCUSSIVE IMPACTS AND CUMULATIVE EXPOSURE

### 12.17.1 The Hidden Risk

Subconcussive impacts are head impacts that do NOT cause clinically recognizable symptoms of concussion but still transmit force to the brain. These impacts are far more frequent than symptomatic concussions and may collectively pose greater long-term neurodegenerative risk.

**Key Data:**
- Football linemen may sustain 1,000-1,500 subconcussive impacts per season
- Cumulative subconcussive exposure may be MORE important than diagnosed concussions for long-term neurodegenerative risk
- Duration of contact sport exposure (>12 years) is a stronger predictor of CTE than total number of diagnosed concussions
- Imaging studies: repeated subconcussive impacts above 10g produce detectable white matter alterations on diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), even without clinical symptoms
- Biomarker changes: subconcussive exposure is associated with elevated serum NfL and S100B in the absence of diagnosed concussion

### 12.17.2 Heading in Soccer

Soccer heading is the most extensively studied subconcussive exposure in non-collision sports:

- **US Soccer policy:** Heading BANNED for children under 10; limited heading in practice for ages 11-13
- **England FA:** Heading banned in training for primary school children (under 11); graduated introduction ages 12-16
- **FIFA:** No universal heading ban; supports age-appropriate heading restrictions
- **Research status:** Lack of consensus on long-term neurologic effects; some studies show white matter changes with cumulative heading exposure; others find no significant association
- **Current stance:** Limiting heading exposure in youth sports is recommended as a precautionary measure while longitudinal research continues

**Clinical Pearl:** Subconcussive impacts (not just diagnosed concussions) contribute to long-term neurodegenerative risk. Duration of exposure matters more than number of concussions for CTE risk. Youth heading restrictions are based on the precautionary principle given insufficient longitudinal data.

## SECTION 12.18 [COMPREHENSIVE] SPORT-SPECIFIC RETURN-TO-PLAY CONSIDERATIONS

### 12.18.1 General Principles

While the 6-step graduated RTP protocol applies across all sports, several sport-specific considerations influence management:

**Collision Sports (Football, Ice Hockey, Rugby, Lacrosse):**
- Highest concussion incidence and recurrence risk
- Full-contact practice (Step 5) carries inherent risk; careful assessment of readiness essential
- Position-specific risks: football linemen (repetitive subconcussive impacts), skill positions (higher-velocity collisions)
- Equipment considerations: helmet fit and technology do not prevent concussion but may reduce risk of more severe injury

**Contact Sports (Soccer, Basketball, Wrestling):**
- Heading in soccer: age-appropriate restrictions
- Female athletes in soccer and basketball have higher concussion rates than males
- Wrestling: risk from takedowns and head contact with mat

**Limited-Contact Sports (Baseball/Softball, Cycling, Skiing):**
- Lower overall concussion rates but significant when they occur (e.g., collision with ball, falls)
- Sport-specific functional testing may be important (visual tracking for batting sports)

### 12.18.2 Special Populations

**Children and Adolescents:**
- More conservative management with longer recovery expectations (up to 4 weeks)
- Academic accommodations are critical and may take precedence over sport return
- Developing brains may be more vulnerable to repeated injury
- Parental education and school-based management plans are essential

**Military Personnel:**
- Blast TBI presents unique challenges; MACE-2 is the military assessment equivalent of SCAT6
- High rates of comorbid PTSD complicate diagnosis and management
- Progressive return to duty protocols parallel civilian RTP protocols

**Female Athletes:**
- Higher concussion rates in comparable sports
- Potentially longer recovery times
- Hormonal influences on concussion susceptibility and recovery under investigation
- May have different symptom profiles (more emotional and sleep-related symptoms)

### 12.18.3 Retirement Considerations

- No specific number of concussions mandates retirement from sport
- Decisions should be individualized based on: number and severity of concussions, progressively longer recovery with each concussion, persistent cognitive deficits, lower threshold for concussion (occurring with less force), personal and family considerations
- Retirement counseling should involve the athlete, family, team physician, and neuropsychologist
- Genetic factors (ApoE4 status) may influence risk but routine clinical genotyping is NOT currently recommended for management decisions

## SECTION 12.18A [COMPREHENSIVE] NEUROPSYCHOLOGICAL TESTING IN CONCUSSION

### 12.18A.1 Computerized Neurocognitive Testing

Computerized neurocognitive testing has become widely integrated into concussion evaluation, particularly in organized sports where baseline testing is feasible.

**ImPACT (Immediate Post-Concussion Assessment and Cognitive Testing):**
- Most widely used computerized neurocognitive test for concussion
- Measures: verbal memory, visual memory, visual motor processing speed, reaction time
- Baseline testing recommended (but not required) before sport participation
- Post-injury testing compared to individual baseline or normative data
- Sensitivity approximately 80-85% when comparing post-injury to baseline performance
- Limitations: practice effects with repeated testing, sensitivity to effort and motivation, cannot be used in isolation for return-to-play decisions
- Should be interpreted by a trained clinician (not used as a standalone diagnostic tool)

**Other Computerized Platforms:**
- CogSport/CogState: simple reaction time, choice reaction time, one-back working memory, continuous learning
- Axon Sports: similar domains to ImPACT; used in some collegiate programs
- CNS Vital Signs: attention, memory, processing speed, executive function

### 12.18A.2 Formal Neuropsychological Evaluation

Formal (paper-and-pencil) neuropsychological testing by a board-certified neuropsychologist is indicated for:
- Persistent symptoms beyond 30 days
- Complex cases with premorbid learning disabilities, ADHD, or psychiatric conditions
- When computerized testing results are equivocal or inconsistent with clinical presentation
- Medico-legal evaluations
- Return-to-play clearance in complicated cases
- Academic accommodation planning for students

**Domains Assessed:**
- Attention and concentration (Trail Making Test A, Digit Span)
- Processing speed (Symbol Digit Modalities Test, Trail Making Test A)
- Memory and learning (Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test, Brief Visuospatial Memory Test)
- Executive function (Trail Making Test B, Wisconsin Card Sorting Test, Stroop Test)
- Language (Controlled Oral Word Association Test)
- Visuospatial function (Rey Complex Figure)
- Effort and validity testing (Test of Memory Malingering, Word Memory Test)

**Clinical Pearl:** Neuropsychological testing provides objective cognitive data that complements symptom reporting and physical examination. However, symptom resolution does NOT always correlate with cognitive recovery -- an athlete may feel "fine" but still demonstrate measurable cognitive deficits on testing. Conversely, cognitive testing may normalize while symptoms persist, suggesting non-cognitive contributors (vestibular, cervicogenic, psychological).

### 12.18A.3 Concussion Imaging

**Routine Neuroimaging:**
- CT and MRI are typically NORMAL in concussion (by definition, concussion is a functional, not structural injury)
- CT is indicated when red flags are present: deteriorating consciousness, focal neurologic deficits, worsening headache, seizure, GCS <15 after 2 hours, suspected skull fracture, coagulopathy
- Canadian CT Head Rule and New Orleans Criteria guide CT utilization in emergency settings

**Advanced Imaging (Research Tools):**
- Diffusion Tensor Imaging (DTI): Measures white matter tract integrity; can detect subtle axonal injury not visible on conventional MRI; promising research tool but NOT validated for individual clinical decision-making
- Susceptibility-Weighted Imaging (SWI): Detects microhemorrhages (microbleeds) indicative of shearing injury; may identify structural injury in patients classified as mTBI
- fMRI (functional MRI): Demonstrates altered activation patterns during cognitive tasks; research tool only
- MR Spectroscopy: Can detect metabolic changes (decreased NAA, elevated choline/creatine ratio) suggesting neuronal injury or metabolic dysfunction

**Clinical Pearl:** Routine neuroimaging is NOT indicated for sport concussion unless red flags are present. A normal CT or MRI does not exclude concussion -- it excludes more serious structural injury. Advanced imaging modalities (DTI, SWI, fMRI) are research tools and should not be used for individual clinical decisions at this time. The GFAP/UCH-L1 blood test can help reduce unnecessary CT imaging by identifying low-risk patients.

## SECTION 12.19 APOLIPOPROTEIN E4 AND TBI OUTCOMES

ApoE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for Alzheimer disease and has important implications for TBI outcomes:

- ApoE4 carriers have WORSE outcomes after TBI: longer coma duration, prolonged PTA, slower cognitive recovery
- Associated with increased risk of developing CTE with repeated head impacts
- May impair neuronal repair and amyloid clearance after injury
- ApoE4 carriers have increased risk of post-traumatic Alzheimer disease
- Clinical implication: ApoE4 status is a prognostic factor but routine clinical genotyping is NOT currently recommended for management decisions
- Not currently used to make return-to-play or retirement decisions

**Clinical Pearl:** ApoE4 is associated with WORSE TBI outcomes and increased risk of CTE and Alzheimer disease. It is the most commonly tested genetic factor in TBI on board examinations.

## SECTION 12.20 POST-CONCUSSIVE HEADACHE MANAGEMENT

| Headache Type | Clinical Features | First-Line Treatment | Notes |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| Tension-type | Bilateral, pressing/tightening quality, mild-moderate intensity | NSAIDs, acetaminophen | Most common post-concussive headache type; avoid opioids |
| Migraine-type | Unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, nausea, photophobia, phonophobia | Triptans, NSAIDs | Consider prophylaxis (amitriptyline, topiramate, propranolol) if frequent |
| Cervicogenic | Unilateral, starts in suboccipital region, radiates anteriorly, associated neck pain/stiffness | Physical therapy, manual therapy, occipital nerve blocks | Often co-occurs with whiplash; C2-3 facet joint common source |
| Medication overuse | Daily or near-daily headache, worsening despite analgesic use | Withdraw offending medication | Suspect if using acute medications >2-3 days/week for >3 months |
| Occipital neuralgia | Sharp, shooting, electric-like pain in occipital distribution | Occipital nerve block, gabapentin | May result from direct nerve injury or cervical dysfunction |
