## Introduction

Neuroimaging constitutes the backbone of traumatic brain injury (TBI) evaluation, guiding acute clinical decision-making, surgical planning, prognostication, and longitudinal monitoring. The selection of imaging modality, timing of acquisition, and interpretation of findings require a nuanced understanding of the pathophysiology of traumatic intracranial lesions and the biophysical principles underlying each imaging technique. Computed tomography (CT) remains the first-line modality in the acute setting because of its speed, widespread availability, and exceptional sensitivity for surgically actionable lesions such as epidural hematomas, large subdural collections, and herniation. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) provides superior tissue contrast resolution and detects lesions frequently invisible on CT, particularly diffuse axonal injury (DAI), non-hemorrhagic contusions, and brainstem pathology. Advanced imaging modalities --- diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS), and functional MRI (fMRI) --- extend the clinician's view from macroscopic structure to microstructural integrity, metabolic function, and neural network connectivity. This chapter systematically examines each imaging modality, the validated classification and scoring systems used for prognostication, and the clinical decision rules that govern when imaging should be obtained.

## CT Indications and Clinical Decision Rules `[BOARD]`

### Rationale for Selective CT Imaging

Not every patient who sustains head trauma requires neuroimaging. The vast majority of patients with mild TBI (GCS 13--15) will have a normal CT scan, with only approximately 5--8% demonstrating intracranial pathology and fewer than 1% requiring neurosurgical intervention. Consequently, validated clinical decision rules have been developed to identify patients who warrant CT scanning while avoiding unnecessary radiation exposure and resource utilization. Two decision rules dominate clinical practice: the Canadian CT Head Rule (CCHR) and the New Orleans Criteria (NOC).

### Canadian CT Head Rule

The Canadian CT Head Rule (CCHR) was derived by Stiell and colleagues in a prospective multicenter study of 3,121 patients and subsequently validated in 2,707 additional patients (Stiell et al., *Lancet*, 2001). The rule applies to adults (age greater than or equal to 16 years) with minor head injury defined as witnessed loss of consciousness, definite amnesia, or witnessed disorientation, with GCS 13--15 at initial evaluation.

The CCHR identifies two tiers of risk:

**High-Risk Criteria (100% sensitive for neurosurgical intervention):**

1. GCS score less than 15 at 2 hours after injury
2. Suspected open or depressed skull fracture
3. Any sign of basilar skull fracture (hemotympanum, raccoon eyes, CSF otorrhea or rhinorrhea, Battle sign)
4. Two or more episodes of vomiting
5. Age 65 years or older

**Medium-Risk Criteria (sensitive for clinically important brain injury):**

6. Retrograde amnesia of 30 minutes or more before impact
7. Dangerous mechanism of injury (pedestrian struck by motor vehicle, occupant ejected from motor vehicle, fall from height greater than 3 feet or 5 stairs)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The CCHR has 100% sensitivity for detecting lesions requiring neurosurgical intervention when the high-risk criteria are applied. Specificity is approximately 50%, which is significantly higher than the New Orleans Criteria (specificity approximately 13%). On board examinations, both rules apply only to patients with GCS 13--15 and witnessed LOC, amnesia, or disorientation. Neither rule applies to penetrating injuries, patients on anticoagulation, or those with obvious depressed skull fractures.

### New Orleans Criteria

The New Orleans Criteria (NOC) were developed by Haydel and colleagues at Charity Hospital in New Orleans and published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in 2000. The NOC apply to patients with minor head injury who have a GCS of 15, a normal neurological examination, and loss of consciousness after blunt head trauma.

**A CT scan is indicated if any one of the following is present:**

1. Headache
2. Vomiting (any episode)
3. Age greater than 60 years
4. Drug or alcohol intoxication
5. Short-term memory deficit (persistent anterograde amnesia)
6. Physical evidence of trauma above the clavicles
7. Seizure

| Feature | Canadian CT Head Rule | New Orleans Criteria |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Population | GCS 13--15, witnessed LOC/amnesia/disorientation | GCS 15, any LOC |
| Sensitivity for neurosurgical intervention | 100% | 100% |
| Specificity | ~50% | ~13% |
| Number of criteria | 7 (in 2 tiers) | 7 |
| Clinical utility | More specific; reduces CT rate more | More sensitive but lower specificity |
| Anticoagulant patients | Not specifically addressed (original) | Not specifically addressed |
| Validation | Large multicenter (Canada, international) | Single-center derivation and validation |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Canadian CT Head Rule is more specific than the New Orleans Criteria. In practice, this means the CCHR identifies fewer patients who need CT scanning while maintaining the same sensitivity. This is a commonly tested distinction on board examinations. Both rules have 100% sensitivity for neurosurgical intervention, but the CCHR would result in approximately 50% fewer CT scans.

### Additional Indications for Acute CT

Beyond formal decision rules, CT scanning is universally indicated in several clinical scenarios that fall outside the scope of these mild TBI decision rules:

- All patients with moderate TBI (GCS 9--12)
- All patients with severe TBI (GCS 3--8)
- Any focal neurological deficit
- New-onset seizure after head trauma
- Patients on anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy with any head trauma
- Worsening headache despite analgesics
- Coagulopathy from any cause
- Clinical signs of elevated intracranial pressure (altered consciousness, Cushing triad)

### Repeat CT Imaging

The role of routine repeat CT in the first 24 hours after initial imaging remains debated. Current consensus supports repeat imaging if there is any neurological deterioration, failure to improve as expected, or the need for surgical decision-making. In patients with an initial CT showing intracranial hemorrhage, a follow-up scan at 6--8 hours is reasonable to assess for hemorrhage expansion, particularly in those on anticoagulation.

## Hemorrhage Types on CT `[BOARD]`

Understanding the imaging appearance, anatomic source, and clinical significance of each hemorrhage type is fundamental to acute TBI management and is heavily tested on board examinations.

### Epidural Hematoma (EDH)

The epidural hematoma is a collection of blood between the inner table of the skull and the dura mater. The most common etiology is laceration of the middle meningeal artery, typically in the temporal region following a temporal bone fracture. Venous epidural hematomas occur less commonly (10--40% of cases) and tend to develop more slowly.

**CT Appearance:**
- **Shape:** Biconvex (lens-shaped or lenticular)
- **Location:** Typically temporal or temporoparietal
- **Boundaries:** Does NOT cross suture lines (because the dura is tightly adherent to the inner table at suture lines)
- **Density:** Uniformly hyperdense (50--70 HU) in the acute phase
- **Swirl sign:** Areas of hypodensity within an otherwise hyperdense collection, indicating active bleeding or unclotted blood; this is an ominous sign suggesting rapid expansion

**Clinical Correlates:**
- Classic lucid interval occurs in approximately 50% of patients (brief loss of consciousness, followed by transient improvement, then rapid deterioration)
- Represents a true neurosurgical emergency: rapid evacuation can be lifesaving
- Overall mortality is lower than SDH when promptly treated because the underlying brain injury is often less severe
- Temporal epidural hematomas are most common because the middle meningeal artery runs in a groove on the inner table of the temporal bone

> **Clinical Pearl:** The EDH does not cross suture lines because the dura is tightly adherent to the periosteum of the inner table at suture lines. In contrast, the EDH can cross the midline (unlike subdural hematomas, which cannot cross the falx cerebri). The biconvex shape results from the dura being stripped away from the skull by the expanding hematoma, but the adherence at the sutures limits its extent.

### Subdural Hematoma (SDH)

The subdural hematoma is a collection of blood between the dura mater and the arachnoid membrane. It results from tearing of bridging veins that traverse the subdural space from the cortical surface to the dural venous sinuses.

**CT Appearance:**
- **Shape:** Crescent-shaped (concavo-convex), conforming to the brain surface
- **Location:** Overlying the cerebral convexity; can extend along the falx or tentorium
- **Boundaries:** Crosses suture lines freely but does NOT cross the midline (the falx cerebri prevents this)
- **Density:** Varies with age of the hematoma (see Blood Density Evolution section)

**Temporal Classification:**

| Stage | Time Frame | CT Density | Appearance |
|-------|-----------|------------|------------|
| Acute | 0--3 days | Hyperdense (50--70 HU) | Bright white crescent |
| Subacute | 3 days -- 3 weeks | Isodense (20--40 HU) | Same density as brain; easy to miss |
| Chronic | >3 weeks | Hypodense (5--25 HU) | Dark crescent (similar to CSF) |

**Clinical Correlates:**
- More common in elderly patients (brain atrophy stretches bridging veins, making them more susceptible to shearing)
- Acute SDH carries approximately 50--90% mortality in large series
- Chronic SDH may present insidiously with confusion, gait disturbance, or personality changes, particularly in elderly patients

> **Clinical Pearl:** The subacute subdural hematoma (3 days to 3 weeks) becomes isodense to brain parenchyma on CT, making it extremely difficult to detect. Clues to an isodense SDH include: asymmetric sulcal effacement, midline shift without an obvious mass lesion, compression of the ipsilateral lateral ventricle, and white matter buckling. MRI with FLAIR is superior for detecting isodense subdurals. This is a classic board question.

### Traumatic Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (tSAH)

Traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage results from disruption of small cortical vessels and manifests as blood within the subarachnoid space (sulci, fissures, and cisterns).

**CT Appearance:**
- Hyperdense blood tracking along the cerebral sulci, sylvian fissures, and basal cisterns
- Often linear or curvilinear following the sulcal pattern
- CT sensitivity is approximately 95% at 12 hours but drops to approximately 30% at 2 weeks as blood is reabsorbed

**Clinical Correlates:**
- Present in approximately 40% of moderate-to-severe TBI
- Associated with worse outcomes when present in combination with other hemorrhagic lesions
- May cause delayed vasospasm, though less commonly than aneurysmal SAH
- Blood in the basal cisterns (interpeduncular cistern, ambient cistern) is an important prognostic indicator and a component of the Rotterdam CT Score

### Intraparenchymal Hemorrhage (ICH) and Contusions

Traumatic intracerebral hemorrhage or hemorrhagic contusion represents bleeding within the brain parenchyma itself. Contusions characteristically occur in locations where the brain contacts rough bony surfaces: the inferior frontal lobes (orbital surfaces), the anterior temporal lobes, and the occipital poles.

**CT Appearance:**
- Mixed-density lesions (hemorrhage intermixed with edema)
- May initially appear as small petechial hemorrhages that coalesce over 24--48 hours ("hemorrhagic blossoming" or contusion evolution)
- Surrounding hypodense edema is common
- Coup contusions occur at the site of impact; contrecoup contusions occur at the opposite pole

**Clinical Correlates:**
- Contusion evolution (expansion) occurs in up to 50% of traumatic contusions within the first 24 hours
- May require surgical evacuation if producing significant mass effect (generally >25 cm3)
- Frontal and temporal contusions may produce behavioral and cognitive deficits disproportionate to the patient's level of consciousness

| Hemorrhage Type | CT Shape | Source Vessel | Crosses Sutures? | Crosses Midline? | Surgical Urgency |
|----------------|----------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Epidural (EDH) | Biconvex (lens) | Middle meningeal artery | No | Yes | Emergency |
| Subdural (SDH) | Crescent | Bridging veins | Yes | No (falx blocks) | Urgent-emergent |
| Subarachnoid (tSAH) | Fills sulci/cisterns | Cortical vessels | N/A | Can be bilateral | Medical (usually) |
| Intraparenchymal (ICH) | Irregular, mixed density | Parenchymal vessels | N/A | N/A | If mass effect |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Classic board imaging associations: Biconvex hyperdensity = EDH (middle meningeal artery). Crescent-shaped hyperdensity = acute SDH (bridging veins). Blood in sulci and basal cisterns = traumatic SAH. Petechial hemorrhages at the gray-white junction = DAI Grade 1. Hemorrhage in the corpus callosum = DAI Grade 2. Hemorrhage in the dorsolateral midbrain = DAI Grade 3 (worst prognosis). Effaced basal cisterns with midline shift = elevated ICP requiring urgent intervention.

## Marshall CT Classification `[BOARD]`

### Overview

The Marshall CT Classification is the most widely used CT-based grading system for categorizing diffuse traumatic brain injury. It was developed by Lawrence Marshall and colleagues at the Traumatic Coma Data Bank and published in 1991. The system categorizes injury into six grades based on the presence of mass lesions, the status of the basal cisterns, and the degree of midline shift. Its primary value lies in prognostication: higher grades predict progressively worse outcomes.

### Classification System

| Grade | Name | CT Findings | Mortality |
|-------|------|------------|-----------|
| I | Diffuse Injury I | No visible intracranial pathology on CT (normal scan) | ~10% |
| II | Diffuse Injury II | Cisterns PRESENT; midline shift 0--5 mm; no high- or mixed-density lesion >25 cm3. May have small petechial hemorrhages, bone fragments, or small contusions | ~14% |
| III | Diffuse Injury III (Swelling) | Cisterns COMPRESSED or ABSENT; midline shift 0--5 mm; no high- or mixed-density lesion >25 cm3 | ~34% |
| IV | Diffuse Injury IV (Shift) | Midline shift >5 mm; no high- or mixed-density lesion >25 cm3 | ~56% |
| V | Evacuated Mass Lesion | Any lesion that has been surgically evacuated | ~39% (variable) |
| VI | Non-Evacuated Mass Lesion | High- or mixed-density lesion >25 cm3 that is NOT surgically evacuated | ~53% |

### Key Concepts for Board Examinations

The Marshall Classification has several features that make it a frequent subject of board questions:

1. **Basal cistern status** is one of the most powerful prognostic indicators on CT. Compressed or absent cisterns (Grade III) indicate severe diffuse cerebral swelling and elevated intracranial pressure. The transition from Grade II (cisterns present) to Grade III (cisterns compressed/absent) is associated with a dramatic increase in mortality (from approximately 14% to 34%).

2. **The 25 cm3 threshold** is a critical volume cutoff. Mass lesions exceeding 25 cm3 that have not been surgically evacuated (Grade VI) carry approximately 53% mortality, underscoring the importance of timely surgical decision-making.

3. **Midline shift greater than 5 mm** (Grade IV) without a surgical mass lesion indicates severe diffuse swelling and carries approximately 56% mortality.

4. **Grade V (Evacuated Mass Lesion)** has variable mortality depending on the type of lesion evacuated and the timeliness of surgery. Rapid evacuation of an epidural hematoma, for example, carries better prognosis than delayed evacuation of a large subdural hematoma.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Marshall Classification is limited by its categorical nature --- it does not account for the specific type of hemorrhage present (EDH vs. SDH vs. ICH) or the combination of multiple lesion types. The Rotterdam CT Score was developed to address these limitations by treating individual CT features as independent continuous predictors. However, the Marshall system remains the most widely recognized and cited CT classification for TBI in clinical and research settings.

## Rotterdam CT Score `[BOARD]`

### Development and Rationale

The Rotterdam CT Score was developed by Maas and colleagues (2005) to improve upon the Marshall Classification by incorporating individual CT features as independent prognostic variables using logistic regression analysis. Data from the Traumatic Coma Data Bank and the European Brain Injury Consortium were used for derivation and validation. The score provides a more granular mortality prediction than the Marshall system.

### Scoring Components

| Parameter | Finding | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Basal cisterns | Normal | 0 |
| | Compressed | 1 |
| | Absent | 2 |
| Midline shift | No shift or shift <5 mm | 0 |
| | Shift >5 mm | 1 |
| Epidural mass lesion | Present | 0 |
| | Absent | 1 |
| Intraventricular hemorrhage or traumatic SAH | Absent | 0 |
| | Present | 1 |
| **Constant** | **Add +1 to the sum** | |

**Total score range: 1--6**

### Mortality Prediction

| Rotterdam Score | 6-Month Mortality |
|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | ~0% |
| 2 | ~7% |
| 3 | ~16% |
| 4 | ~26% |
| 5 | ~53% |
| 6 | ~61% |

### The EDH Paradox

One of the most commonly tested aspects of the Rotterdam score is the paradoxical scoring of epidural hematomas. The **presence** of an EDH scores 0 points (favorable), while its **absence** scores 1 point (unfavorable). This reflects the statistical finding that patients with an EDH as their primary lesion tend to have better outcomes when surgically evacuated compared to patients with diffuse injury, subdural hematoma, or intraparenchymal hemorrhage as their predominant pathology. The EDH is typically caused by an arterial source (middle meningeal artery), and the underlying brain parenchyma is often relatively uninjured. Rapid surgical evacuation can therefore be curative.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Rotterdam CT Score is additive and includes a +1 constant, so the minimum possible score is 1 (not 0). The higher the score, the worse the predicted 6-month mortality. The EDH paradox --- in which presence of an EDH is scored as favorable --- is a classic board question. Remember: EDH present = 0 points (good); EDH absent = 1 point (bad, because it means the injury is diffuse or involves SDH/ICH with worse parenchymal damage).

## Helsinki and Stockholm CT Scores `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Helsinki CT Score

The Helsinki CT Score was developed by Raj and colleagues at Helsinki University Hospital (2014) using data from 869 patients with TBI. Unlike the Marshall and Rotterdam systems, the Helsinki score was specifically designed to predict long-term outcomes including both mortality and unfavorable neurological outcome at 6 and 12 months.

**Components of the Helsinki CT Score:**

| Parameter | Finding | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Mass lesion type | No mass lesion | 0 |
| | Epidural hematoma | -3 |
| | Subdural hematoma: acute, thin (thickness <10 mm) | +2 |
| | Subdural hematoma: acute, thick (thickness >=10 mm) | +4 |
| | Intracerebral hemorrhage | +2 |
| Intraventricular hemorrhage | Absent | 0 |
| | Present | +3 |
| Suprasellar cisterns | Normal | 0 |
| | Compressed or obliterated | +1 |

**Key Features:**
- EDH scores negatively (-3), reflecting the favorable prognosis associated with EDH when surgically treated, similar to the Rotterdam paradox
- Large subdural hematomas (>=10 mm thickness) receive the highest positive score (+4), reflecting their association with worse outcomes
- The scoring system incorporates hemorrhage type and size, providing more granular information than the Rotterdam score

### Stockholm CT Score

The Stockholm CT Score was developed by Nelson and Nystrom at the Karolinska Institute (2010) using data from severe TBI patients. It was designed to provide continuous rather than categorical CT-based prognostic information.

**Components of the Stockholm CT Score:**

| Parameter | Finding | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Midline shift | Amount in mm | Continuous variable (0 to actual shift in mm) |
| Degree of compression of basal cisterns or ventricles | Normal | 0 |
| | Partially compressed | 1 |
| | Fully compressed/absent | 2 |
| Epidural hematoma | Present | -2 |
| | Absent | 0 |
| Traumatic SAH or IVH | Absent | 0 |
| | Present | 1 |
| Diffuse axonal injury (imaging evidence) | Absent | 0 |
| | Present | 2 |

**Key Features:**
- Treats midline shift as a continuous variable rather than a binary threshold (unlike Marshall and Rotterdam)
- Includes DAI as a scored component, making it unique among major CT scoring systems
- The continuous nature of the midline shift variable provides greater discriminative ability for intermediate degrees of shift

### Comparative Performance

A 2017 multicenter observational study (Thelin et al., *PLoS Medicine*) evaluated all three newer CT scoring systems (Marshall, Rotterdam, Helsinki, and Stockholm) in a combined cohort and found that the Stockholm and Helsinki CT scores added the most independent prognostic value beyond known clinical predictors (age, GCS motor score, pupillary reactivity). The Stockholm CT score demonstrated the highest overall prognostic performance (Nagelkerke's pseudo-R2 range 0.24--0.28), followed by the Helsinki CT score (0.18--0.22), and then the Rotterdam CT score (0.13--0.15).

> **Clinical Pearl:** While the Marshall Classification remains the most widely recognized and cited system for clinical communication and research enrollment, the Helsinki and Stockholm scores provide more granular prognostic information. The Stockholm score is unique in treating midline shift as a continuous variable and incorporating DAI as a scored component. For board purposes, the Marshall Classification and Rotterdam CT Score are most commonly tested; the Helsinki and Stockholm scores represent advanced knowledge that may appear on subspecialty examinations.

## MRI Sequences and Indications `[BOARD]`

### When to Obtain MRI in TBI

MRI is not indicated as a first-line imaging study in acute TBI because it takes longer to acquire (30--60 minutes vs. minutes for CT), has limited availability in the acute setting, is less sensitive for acute osseous injury, and poses logistical challenges for critically ill patients requiring ferromagnetic monitoring equipment. However, MRI is superior to CT in several clinically important scenarios:

1. **CT-negative suspected significant injury:** When the CT is normal but the clinical presentation suggests substantial brain injury (e.g., coma disproportionate to CT findings), MRI should be obtained to evaluate for DAI.
2. **Subacute and chronic evaluation:** MRI is the modality of choice for characterizing the extent and pattern of injury during the subacute phase (days to weeks) for prognostication and rehabilitation planning.
3. **Persistent symptoms after mild TBI:** When symptoms persist beyond expected recovery timelines, MRI may reveal contusions, DAI, or other structural abnormalities not detected on CT.
4. **Posterior fossa evaluation:** CT is limited by beam-hardening artifact from the petrous bones; MRI provides superior visualization of the brainstem and cerebellum.
5. **Prognostication in disorders of consciousness:** The pattern and extent of injury on MRI (particularly DAI grading and brainstem involvement) provides essential prognostic information.

### Key MRI Sequences

| Sequence | Full Name | What It Detects | Clinical Application | Board Yield |
|----------|-----------|----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| FLAIR | Fluid-Attenuated Inversion Recovery | Edema, non-hemorrhagic DAI, periventricular changes, contusions | Best for non-hemorrhagic contusions and DAI; hyperintense signal in injured white matter; suppresses CSF signal to improve lesion detection | High |
| GRE | Gradient Recalled Echo | Microhemorrhages (hemosiderin deposits) as dark "blooming" artifacts | Detects hemorrhagic DAI; hemosiderin persists indefinitely, allowing detection of old injuries | High |
| SWI | Susceptibility-Weighted Imaging | Microhemorrhages with 3--6x greater sensitivity than GRE | Most sensitive conventional MRI sequence for hemorrhagic DAI; number and location of lesions correlate with severity and outcomes | Very High |
| DWI | Diffusion-Weighted Imaging | Acute cytotoxic edema (restricted diffusion) | Detects acute ischemia and early cytotoxic injury from DAI; bright signal on DWI with corresponding dark ADC map | High |
| DTI | Diffusion Tensor Imaging | White matter tract integrity (water diffusion directionality) | Research/advanced: detects subclinical axonal injury invisible on conventional MRI; see Advanced Imaging section | Moderate |
| T1-weighted | Standard anatomic sequence | Structural anatomy, subacute hemorrhage (methemoglobin is bright on T1) | Baseline structural imaging; subacute blood products are hyperintense | Low-Moderate |
| T2-weighted | Standard tissue contrast | Edema, fluid collections, chronic hemorrhage | General tissue characterization; complements FLAIR for non-hemorrhagic lesions | Low-Moderate |

### FLAIR in TBI

FLAIR imaging suppresses the signal from cerebrospinal fluid, making it uniquely sensitive for detecting lesions adjacent to CSF-containing spaces (ventricles, sulci, cisterns). In TBI, FLAIR detects:
- Non-hemorrhagic DAI lesions appearing as hyperintense foci in white matter
- Cortical and subcortical contusions
- Periventricular edema
- Subarachnoid hemorrhage (appears hyperintense rather than the isointense signal seen on T2)
- Isodense subdural hematomas that are invisible on CT

### GRE and SWI: Microhemorrhage Detection

Gradient echo (GRE) sequences exploit the magnetic susceptibility differences between tissues. Hemosiderin and deoxyhemoglobin are paramagnetic, creating local magnetic field inhomogeneities that cause signal loss ("blooming artifact") on susceptibility-sensitive sequences. This property makes GRE and SWI exquisitely sensitive for detecting microhemorrhages associated with DAI.

**SWI vs. GRE:**

| Feature | GRE | SWI |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Sensitivity for microhemorrhages | Good | 3--6x more sensitive than GRE |
| Technical basis | Uses gradient echoes susceptible to field inhomogeneities | Uses both magnitude and phase data; high spatial resolution; thin slices |
| Blooming artifact | Present | More pronounced due to higher sensitivity |
| Clinical availability | Widely available | Available on most modern 1.5T and 3T scanners |
| Board relevance | Know that GRE detects hemorrhagic DAI | Know that SWI is 3--6x more sensitive and is preferred |

> **Clinical Pearl:** SWI is 3--6 times more sensitive than conventional GRE for detecting hemorrhagic DAI lesions. The number and location of SWI lesions correlate with injury severity, duration of coma, and long-term cognitive and functional outcomes. SWI may detect hemorrhagic DAI even when CT and all other conventional MRI sequences appear normal. Hemosiderin deposits persist indefinitely, so SWI can detect injuries from months or years earlier.

## Diffuse Axonal Injury Imaging `[BOARD]`

### Pathophysiology

Diffuse axonal injury results from rotational acceleration-deceleration forces that cause shearing of axons at interfaces between tissues of different densities. The severity of DAI depends on the magnitude and direction of the rotational force. DAI is the leading cause of morbidity in TBI and the most common cause of prolonged unconsciousness following head trauma.

### Adams Grading System

The Adams grading system (Adams et al., 1989) classifies DAI into three grades based on the anatomic distribution of axonal injury, progressing from the cerebral hemispheres to the corpus callosum to the brainstem. Higher grades indicate progressively more severe rotational forces and carry worse prognosis.

| Grade | Location | Imaging Findings | Prognosis |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Grade 1 | Gray-white matter junction (parasagittal lobar white matter) | Petechial hemorrhages or FLAIR hyperintensities at the gray-white junction; most common in frontal and temporal lobes | Best prognosis among DAI grades; potential for good functional recovery with rehabilitation |
| Grade 2 | Corpus callosum (in addition to Grade 1 findings) | Hemorrhage or signal abnormality in the corpus callosum, especially the posterior body and splenium; the falx constrains hemispheric rotation, creating focal shearing forces at the callosum | Moderate prognosis; often prolonged coma and significant cognitive deficits; callosal disconnection syndromes |
| Grade 3 | Dorsolateral midbrain/upper brainstem (in addition to Grade 1 and 2 findings) | Hemorrhage or signal abnormality in the dorsolateral rostral brainstem; associated with prolonged coma or death | Worst prognosis; highest mortality; survivors often remain in vegetative state or severe disability |

### CT Sensitivity for DAI

CT is **negative** in up to 50--80% of DAI cases. When CT does detect DAI, it typically shows only the hemorrhagic component: small, punctate hyperdensities at the gray-white junction, within the corpus callosum, or in the brainstem. The non-hemorrhagic axonal injury --- which constitutes the majority of the pathology --- is invisible on CT.

### Recommended Imaging Protocol for Suspected DAI

1. **CT first** to rule out surgical lesions (epidural hematoma, large subdural hematoma, herniation)
2. **MRI with SWI/GRE** for hemorrhagic DAI (most sensitive conventional sequences)
3. **FLAIR** for non-hemorrhagic edema and white matter injury
4. **DWI** for acute cytotoxic edema indicating acute axonal injury
5. **DTI** for subclinical white matter tract disruption (research/advanced, gaining clinical acceptance)

> **Clinical Pearl:** A comatose patient with a normal CT scan should raise high suspicion for DAI. The absence of CT findings does NOT exclude significant brain injury. MRI with SWI is the most sensitive conventional imaging modality for detecting DAI. On boards, remember: Grade 1 = gray-white junction (most common). Grade 2 = add corpus callosum. Grade 3 = add brainstem (worst). The hierarchy reflects increasing rotational force severity.

### Clinical Significance of DAI Location

The location of DAI lesions on MRI has specific clinical correlates:

- **Frontal white matter DAI:** Executive dysfunction, behavioral disinhibition, impaired planning
- **Temporal white matter DAI:** Memory impairment
- **Corpus callosum DAI:** Interhemispheric disconnection syndromes, impaired bimanual coordination, slowed processing
- **Brainstem DAI:** Impaired arousal and consciousness, autonomic instability, cranial nerve dysfunction
- **Internal capsule DAI:** Motor deficits (corticospinal tract involvement)

## CT vs MRI Comparison `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Comprehensive Comparison

| Feature | CT | MRI |
|---------|----|----|
| **Role in TBI** | First-line acute imaging; surgical decision-making | Subacute/chronic evaluation; prognostication; DAI detection |
| **Speed** | Fast (minutes) | Slow (30--60 minutes) |
| **Availability** | Widely available, 24/7 in most emergency departments | Limited availability; may require scheduling |
| **Acute hemorrhage** | Excellent (hyperdense blood at 50--70 HU) | Good; T1 hyperintensity for subacute blood; SWI for all stages |
| **Skull fractures** | Excellent (bone windows) | Poor for cortical bone |
| **Midline shift** | Excellent | Good |
| **DAI detection** | Poor (normal in 50--80% of DAI) | Superior (SWI, GRE, FLAIR, DWI, DTI) |
| **Posterior fossa** | Poor (beam-hardening artifact from petrous bones) | Superior |
| **Non-hemorrhagic contusions** | Poor | Superior (FLAIR) |
| **Brainstem lesions** | Poor | Superior |
| **Isodense SDH** | May be missed entirely | Easily detected (FLAIR) |
| **Radiation** | Yes (ionizing radiation; ~2 mSv per head CT) | No radiation |
| **Cost** | Lower | Higher |
| **Patient compatibility** | Few contraindications (pregnancy is relative) | Contraindicated with ferromagnetic implants, certain pacemakers, some aneurysm clips |
| **Monitoring equipment** | Standard monitoring compatible | Requires MRI-compatible equipment |
| **Sedation requirement** | Rarely needed | Often needed for agitated or uncooperative patients |

### Timing of Each Modality

**Acute phase (0--24 hours):**
- CT is the primary modality
- Indications: rule out surgical emergencies, detect hemorrhage, assess for herniation
- Repeat CT if clinical deterioration or persistent concern for hemorrhage expansion

**Subacute phase (1--14 days):**
- MRI becomes the modality of choice
- Indications: characterize the full extent of injury, detect DAI, evaluate brainstem, establish baseline for prognostication
- CT may still be needed for interval follow-up of known hemorrhages

**Chronic phase (>2 weeks):**
- MRI is the preferred modality
- Indications: prognostication, rehabilitation planning, evaluation of persistent symptoms, monitoring for post-traumatic hydrocephalus
- Advanced MRI sequences (DTI, MRS) may be obtained for research or additional clinical characterization

> **Clinical Pearl:** The choice between CT and MRI is not either/or --- they are complementary modalities used at different phases of the injury trajectory. CT answers the urgent question: "Is there a surgical emergency?" MRI answers the subacute question: "What is the full extent of brain injury, and what is the prognosis?"

## Advanced Imaging Modalities `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Diffusion Tensor Imaging (DTI)

DTI is an extension of diffusion-weighted MRI that measures the directionality (anisotropy) of water molecule diffusion along white matter tracts. In intact axons, water molecules diffuse preferentially along the axon's long axis (high anisotropy). When axonal integrity is disrupted by injury, water diffuses more randomly (low anisotropy).

**Key DTI Metrics:**

| Metric | Full Name | Scale | Interpretation in TBI |
|--------|-----------|-------|----------------------|
| FA | Fractional Anisotropy | 0--1 | Decreased FA = axonal injury (disrupted myelin/axonal membranes). Higher values indicate more intact white matter tracts |
| MD | Mean Diffusivity | mm2/sec | Increased MD = tissue damage, edema, or axonal loss |
| AD | Axial Diffusivity | mm2/sec | Measures diffusion parallel to axons; may decrease in acute axonal injury |
| RD | Radial Diffusivity | mm2/sec | Measures diffusion perpendicular to axons; increases with demyelination |

**Tractography:** DTI data can be reconstructed into three-dimensional representations of white matter tracts (tractography), allowing visualization of disrupted connections. This technique is particularly useful for demonstrating damage to specific functionally important tracts such as the corticospinal tract, arcuate fasciculus, or cingulum bundle.

**Temporal Considerations:**
- In acute TBI, FA may paradoxically **increase** due to cytotoxic edema restricting extracellular water diffusion. This can confound interpretation in the first days after injury.
- Decreased FA becomes a more reliable marker of axonal injury in the subacute-to-chronic phase (weeks to months post-injury).
- FA in the corpus callosum and internal capsule has the strongest clinical correlates: decreased callosal FA correlates with cognitive impairment, while decreased internal capsule FA correlates with motor deficits.

> **Clinical Pearl:** DTI is the most sensitive imaging modality for detecting mild TBI-related white matter injury and can reveal axonal damage invisible on all conventional MRI sequences. While primarily used in research settings, DTI is gaining clinical acceptance for prognostication and medicolegal documentation of injury. The paradoxical acute increase in FA is an important caveat for interpretation: subacute and chronic DTI measurements are more reliable than acute measurements.

### Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS)

MRS measures the concentration of specific metabolites within a defined region of interest in the brain, providing information about the metabolic and biochemical status of tissue that may appear normal on structural imaging.

**Key MRS Metabolites:**

| Metabolite | Abbreviation | Marker Of | Change in TBI | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| N-Acetylaspartate | NAA | Neuronal integrity and viability | **Decreased** | Most clinically significant finding; NAA is found exclusively in neurons; decreased NAA = neuronal loss or dysfunction |
| Choline | Cho | Cell membrane turnover | **Increased** | Indicates membrane damage, gliosis, or demyelination; reflects cellular proliferation or destruction |
| Creatine | Cr | Energy metabolism | Relatively **stable** | Used as internal reference denominator for ratios (NAA/Cr, Cho/Cr) |
| Lactate | Lac | Anaerobic metabolism | **Increased** | Indicates ischemia or metabolic stress; not normally present in significant quantities in healthy brain |
| Myoinositol | mI | Glial cells (astrocyte marker) | **Increased** | Indicates gliosis and astrocytic proliferation |
| Glutamate/Glutamine | Glx | Excitatory neurotransmission | **Increased** (early) | Reflects excitotoxicity in the acute phase |

**Clinical Applications:**
- The **decreased NAA/Cr ratio** is the most clinically significant MRS finding in TBI and correlates with neuronal loss severity
- An **increased Cho/Cr ratio** suggests ongoing membrane destruction and demyelination
- The combination of decreased NAA, increased Cho, and increased Lactate indicates neuronal death, membrane damage, and metabolic failure --- a particularly ominous metabolic profile
- MRS can detect metabolic injury in tissue that appears normal on conventional structural MRI, providing prognostic information beyond structural imaging alone

> **Clinical Pearl:** NAA is found exclusively in neurons and is the most important MRS metabolite in TBI. Decreased NAA indicates neuronal loss or dysfunction. Creatine (Cr) serves as the stable internal reference for metabolite ratios. Remember: "NAA = Neurons Are Absent (when decreased)." MRS is particularly valuable in disorders of consciousness for prognostication: severely decreased NAA/Cr ratios in apparently normal-appearing white matter predict poor recovery.

### Functional MRI (fMRI)

Functional MRI measures the Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent (BOLD) signal, which reflects changes in the ratio of oxygenated to deoxygenated hemoglobin associated with neural metabolic activity. Two major paradigms are used in TBI:

**Task-based fMRI:**
- The patient performs cognitive or motor tasks during scanning (or is asked to imagine performing them)
- Maps the brain regions that activate during specific tasks
- Critical application in disorders of consciousness: can detect covert awareness in patients who appear unresponsive
- The seminal study by Owen et al. (*Science*, 2006) demonstrated that a patient clinically diagnosed as vegetative state could modulate brain activity in response to mental imagery commands (imagining playing tennis vs. navigating through a house), producing activation patterns indistinguishable from healthy controls

**Resting-state fMRI (rs-fMRI):**
- Measures intrinsic brain network connectivity at rest without requiring patient cooperation
- Identifies disruptions in functional connectivity networks (default mode network, salience network, executive control network)
- Particularly useful when patients cannot follow commands because it requires no active participation
- Disrupted default mode network connectivity after TBI correlates with cognitive impairment and level of consciousness

> **Clinical Pearl:** fMRI studies have demonstrated that approximately 15--20% of patients clinically diagnosed as vegetative state show brain activation patterns consistent with awareness when given mental imagery tasks. This phenomenon --- termed "cognitive motor dissociation" (CMD) or "covert awareness" --- has profound ethical and clinical implications for decisions about withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment. The CRS-R combined with advanced neuroimaging provides a more accurate assessment of awareness than bedside behavioral examination alone.

## Blood Density Evolution on CT `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Temporal Changes in Hemorrhage Appearance

The CT appearance of intracranial blood changes predictably over time as hemoglobin is degraded through a series of biochemical reactions. Understanding this evolution is essential for dating hemorrhages, recognizing mixed-age collections, and avoiding the diagnostic pitfall of isodense subdural hematomas.

| Time Period | CT Density (HU) | Appearance | Biochemical Basis |
|-------------|-----------------|------------|-------------------|
| Hyperacute (<6 hours) | Hyperdense (50--70 HU) | Bright white | Fresh unclotted blood; high protein content of hemoglobin |
| Acute (1--3 days) | Hyperdense (50--70 HU) | Bright white | Clotted blood; intact hemoglobin; deoxyhemoglobin formation |
| Subacute (3 days -- 3 weeks) | Isodense (20--40 HU) | Same density as brain parenchyma | Hemoglobin degradation products (methemoglobin); protein breakdown reduces density to match brain |
| Chronic (>3 weeks) | Hypodense (5--25 HU) | Dark (similar to CSF) | Liquefied hematoma; protein content further degraded; approaches CSF density |

### MRI Signal Characteristics of Blood Products

MRI signal characteristics follow a complementary but distinct pattern, governed by the paramagnetic properties of different hemoglobin degradation products:

| Stage | Blood Product | T1 Signal | T2 Signal |
|-------|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| Hyperacute (<24 hours) | Oxyhemoglobin | Isointense | Bright (hyperintense) |
| Acute (1--3 days) | Deoxyhemoglobin | Isointense | Dark (hypointense) |
| Early subacute (3--7 days) | Intracellular methemoglobin | Bright (hyperintense) | Dark (hypointense) |
| Late subacute (1--4 weeks) | Extracellular methemoglobin | Bright (hyperintense) | Bright (hyperintense) |
| Chronic (>4 weeks) | Hemosiderin | Dark (hypointense) | Dark (hypointense) |

### Clinical Significance of the Isodense Phase

The transition to isodensity during the subacute phase (approximately 3 days to 3 weeks) creates a significant diagnostic challenge, particularly for subdural hematomas. An isodense SDH may be entirely invisible on CT unless the clinician recognizes indirect signs:

1. **Asymmetric sulcal effacement** --- the sulci on one side are less prominent than on the other
2. **Midline shift without an obvious cause** --- the septum pellucidum and third ventricle are displaced without a visible mass
3. **Compression of the ipsilateral lateral ventricle** --- the ventricle on the side of the hematoma is smaller than the contralateral ventricle
4. **White matter buckling** --- the gray-white matter interface is compressed inward
5. **Enhancement of the displaced cortical veins** on contrast-enhanced CT

When an isodense SDH is suspected, MRI with FLAIR is the diagnostic study of choice, as the subdural collection will appear hyperintense relative to both brain parenchyma and CSF.

### Mixed-Density Collections

Subdural collections may demonstrate mixed density on CT, reflecting either:
- **Acute-on-chronic SDH:** A new hemorrhage (hyperdense) within a chronic collection (hypodense), creating a layered or swirled appearance
- **Hyperacute hematoma with the "swirl sign":** Unclotted blood (relatively hypodense) intermixed with clotted blood (hyperdense), indicating active hemorrhage

> **Clinical Pearl:** A mixed-density subdural collection in an elderly patient is the hallmark of an acute-on-chronic subdural hematoma, representing acute rebleeding into a pre-existing chronic collection. In patients on anticoagulation, even minor trauma can precipitate this event. The "swirl sign" within an epidural hematoma indicates active bleeding and is an indication for emergent surgical evacuation.

## Skull Fractures `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Overview

Skull fractures result from direct impact to the calvarium and are identified on CT using bone window settings (window width approximately 2000--4000 HU, center approximately 400--700 HU). While skull fractures themselves are not the primary source of morbidity in TBI, they serve as important indicators of the force of impact and are associated with specific patterns of intracranial injury.

### Classification of Skull Fractures

#### Linear Fractures

Linear fractures are the most common type of skull fracture, representing approximately 70--80% of all cranial fractures. They appear as sharp, non-branching lucent lines through the full thickness of the calvarium and must be distinguished from cranial sutures and vascular grooves.

**Key characteristics:**
- Usually result from a broad area of impact with moderate force
- Most common in the temporal and parietal bones
- Clinically significant when they cross the path of the middle meningeal artery (temporal fractures) --- associated with epidural hematoma
- Fractures crossing a venous sinus (particularly the transverse sinus in occipital fractures) may cause venous epidural hematoma or sinus thrombosis

#### Depressed Fractures

Depressed skull fractures occur when a bone fragment is displaced inward below the plane of the surrounding intact calvarium. They are typically caused by focal high-energy impact from a small object.

**CT characteristics:**
- The inner table of the skull is displaced inward
- "Significant" depression is generally defined as displacement greater than the full thickness of the adjacent skull (approximately 8--10 mm)
- May be open (overlying scalp laceration) or closed

**Clinical significance:**
- Open depressed fractures carry a significant risk of infection (meningitis, brain abscess) and generally require surgical elevation and debridement
- Depressed fractures increase the risk of post-traumatic seizures (3--70% depending on associated injuries and whether the fracture is open or closed)
- Underlying dural tears may be present, creating a route for CSF leakage or meningitis

**Surgical indications for elevation:**
- Depression greater than the thickness of the skull
- Open fracture with wound contamination
- Significant cosmetic deformity
- Underlying dural tear with CSF leak
- Associated intracranial hematoma requiring evacuation

#### Basilar Skull Fractures

Basilar skull fractures involve the base of the skull (anterior fossa, middle fossa, or posterior fossa) and are frequently associated with specific clinical signs that are heavily tested on board examinations.

**Clinical Signs of Basilar Skull Fracture:**

| Sign | Description | Location Involved | Timing |
|------|-------------|-------------------|--------|
| Raccoon eyes (periorbital ecchymosis) | Bilateral periorbital ecchymosis | Anterior cranial fossa fracture | Delayed (12--24 hours after injury) |
| Battle sign (retroauricular ecchymosis) | Ecchymosis over the mastoid process | Middle or posterior fossa (petrous temporal bone) fracture | Delayed (24--48 hours after injury) |
| CSF otorrhea | CSF leaking from the ear | Middle fossa (tegmen tympani) fracture with dural and tympanic membrane tear | Acute to subacute |
| CSF rhinorrhea | CSF leaking from the nose | Anterior fossa (cribriform plate or frontal sinus) fracture | Acute to subacute |
| Hemotympanum | Blood behind the tympanic membrane | Petrous temporal bone fracture | Acute |
| Cranial nerve deficits | CN I (anosmia), CN VII, CN VIII | Corresponding fossa depending on nerve course | Acute |

**CSF Leak Identification:**
- CSF can be identified by testing for glucose (CSF glucose is approximately 60% of serum glucose)
- The "halo sign" or "ring sign": when a mixture of CSF and blood is dropped on filter paper, the blood settles centrally and the CSF diffuses outward, creating a clear ring around a blood-stained center
- Beta-2 transferrin assay is the most specific test for CSF (present in CSF but absent from other body fluids)
- Most CSF leaks (approximately 85%) resolve spontaneously within 7--10 days with conservative management (head elevation, avoidance of straining)
- Persistent CSF leak beyond 7--10 days may require surgical repair

**Complications of basilar skull fractures:**
- Meningitis (risk increased with CSF leak; typically caused by *Streptococcus pneumoniae*)
- Cranial nerve injury (CN I is the most commonly injured cranial nerve in TBI overall; CN VII and CN VIII are vulnerable in temporal bone fractures)
- Carotid-cavernous fistula (if the fracture disrupts the cavernous segment of the internal carotid artery)
- Pneumocephalus (air entering the cranial cavity through the fracture site)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Raccoon eyes and Battle sign are DELAYED signs that develop 12--48 hours after injury, not immediate findings. Their presence on initial evaluation suggests a pre-existing or earlier injury. CSF otorrhea and rhinorrhea are indications for hospitalization and observation. Prophylactic antibiotics for basilar skull fractures with CSF leak remain controversial; current evidence does not support routine antibiotic prophylaxis (Cochrane review 2015), though many centers still use it. The beta-2 transferrin assay is the definitive test for CSF, but the halo sign is the bedside screening test.
