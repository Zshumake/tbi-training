## Introduction

The acute management of severe traumatic brain injury (TBI) is governed by the dual imperatives of preventing secondary brain injury and optimizing conditions for neural recovery. The primary injury --- the immediate mechanical damage to neurons, axons, and vasculature at the moment of impact --- is irreversible. All acute management efforts therefore target the secondary injury cascade: a complex sequence of ischemia, excitotoxicity, cerebral edema, and metabolic failure that unfolds over hours to days after the initial trauma. The Brain Trauma Foundation (BTF) Guidelines, now in their 4th Edition (2016), provide the evidence-based framework for this management. These guidelines, supplemented by the Seattle International Severe TBI Consensus Conference (SIBICC) algorithm and evolving trial data, define the standard of care for neurocritical management of severe TBI. This chapter systematically examines each major domain of acute management, from intracranial pressure (ICP) monitoring and cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP) optimization to pharmacologic interventions, failed neuroprotection trials, and emerging biomarker-guided approaches.

## BTF 4th Edition Guidelines: Overview and Evidence Hierarchy `[BOARD]`

### Background

The Brain Trauma Foundation Guidelines for the Management of Severe Traumatic Brain Injury, 4th Edition, were published by Carney and colleagues in *Neurosurgery* (2017). This edition represented a significant departure from prior editions in that the evidence threshold for recommendations was raised substantially. As a result, many recommendations from the 3rd Edition were downgraded or eliminated entirely due to insufficient quality evidence.

### Evidence Classification

| Level | Definition | Strength |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Level I | Based on high-quality evidence from randomized controlled trials (RCTs) | Strongest; should be followed |
| Level IIA | Based on moderate-quality evidence from RCTs or well-designed cohort studies | Strong recommendation |
| Level IIB | Based on lower-quality RCT evidence or well-designed cohort studies | Moderate recommendation |
| Level III | Based on case series, expert opinion, or case-control studies | Weak recommendation; insufficient evidence for definitive guidance |

### Key Structural Points

- The 4th Edition contains only **ONE Level I recommendation** (steroids contraindicated) compared to multiple Level I recommendations in prior editions
- Many recommendations from the 3rd Edition were downgraded due to recognition of methodological limitations in the supporting evidence
- The guidelines apply primarily to adults with severe TBI (GCS 3--8)
- Pediatric guidelines are published separately

> **Clinical Pearl:** The BTF 4th Edition key numbers to memorize: ICP threshold = 22 mmHg. CPP target = 60--70 mmHg. Mannitol serum osmolality limit = 320 mOsm/L. Seizure prophylaxis duration = 7 days. These four numbers cover the most commonly tested BTF parameters on board examinations.

## The Only Level I Recommendation: Steroids Are Contraindicated `[BOARD]`

### The CRASH Trial

The Corticosteroid Randomisation After Significant Head Injury (CRASH) trial, published in *The Lancet* (2004), is one of the largest RCTs ever conducted in TBI. This international, multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial enrolled more than 10,000 patients with moderate-to-severe TBI (GCS <=14 within 8 hours of injury) and randomized them to receive methylprednisolone (2 g loading dose followed by 0.4 g/hour for 48 hours) or placebo.

### Results

| Outcome | Methylprednisolone | Placebo | Risk Ratio (95% CI) |
|---------|-------------------|---------|---------------------|
| 2-week mortality | 21.1% | 17.9% | 1.18 (1.09--1.27) |
| 6-month mortality | 25.7% | 22.3% | 1.15 (1.07--1.24) |

Methylprednisolone significantly **increased** mortality at both 2 weeks and 6 months. The risk was consistent across all severity subgroups and geographic regions.

### Mechanism of Harm

The mechanism by which corticosteroids increase mortality in TBI is not fully established but is hypothesized to involve:
- Hyperglycemia (corticosteroids elevate blood glucose, which worsens ischemic brain injury)
- Immunosuppression (increasing infection risk in critically ill patients)
- Potential exacerbation of excitotoxic injury

### Clinical Significance

The CRASH trial result is the foundation of the **sole Level I recommendation** in the entire BTF 4th Edition: the use of steroids is NOT recommended for improving outcome or reducing ICP in patients with severe TBI. This stands in stark contrast to spinal cord injury, where high-dose methylprednisolone was historically used based on the NASCIS trials (though this practice is also now controversial).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Steroids are CONTRAINDICATED in TBI --- the CRASH trial showed INCREASED mortality. This is the single most important BTF fact for boards. Mnemonic: "CRASH landed steroids" --- steroids CRASH and burn in TBI. Do not confuse TBI management with spinal cord injury protocols: the pathophysiology and treatment paradigms are distinct.

## ICP Monitoring Indications and Thresholds `[BOARD]`

### Treatment Threshold

**Level IIB Recommendation:** Treating intracranial hypertension at an ICP threshold of greater than 22 mmHg is recommended. This threshold was increased from the previous 20 mmHg threshold in the 3rd Edition based on the analysis by Sorrentino and colleagues demonstrating that ICP values above 22 mmHg were associated with increased mortality in a large observational dataset.

### Normal ICP Values

| ICP Range | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|----------------------|
| 2--5 mmHg | Normal range |
| Up to 15 mmHg | Generally considered harmless |
| >20 mmHg sustained | Traditional definition of elevated ICP |
| >22 mmHg | BTF 4th Edition treatment threshold |
| >40 mmHg | Neurological dysfunction and impaired electrical activity |
| >60 mmHg | Invariably fatal |

### Indications for ICP Monitoring

**Level IIB Recommendation:** ICP monitoring is indicated in all salvageable patients with severe TBI (GCS 3--8 after resuscitation) who have an abnormal CT scan (hematomas, contusions, swelling, herniation, or compressed basal cisterns).

ICP monitoring is also indicated in patients with severe TBI and a **normal CT scan** if **two or more** of the following are present at admission:
- Age >40 years
- Unilateral or bilateral motor posturing (decerebrate or decorticate)
- Systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg

### Methods of ICP Monitoring

| Method | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| External Ventricular Drain (EVD) | Gold standard; allows both monitoring AND therapeutic CSF drainage; can be recalibrated | Infection risk (~5--10%); hemorrhage risk during placement; requires neurosurgical placement |
| Intraparenchymal monitor (Codman, Camino) | Easy placement; lower infection risk than EVD | Cannot be recalibrated once placed; measurement drift over time; no therapeutic CSF drainage |
| Epidural monitor | Least invasive | Least accurate; rarely used in modern practice |

**EVD Calibration:** The EVD is calibrated at the level of the foramen of Monro, which is approximated externally by the tragus of the ear (alternatively, at the level of the outer canthus of the eye). Accurate calibration is essential --- an incorrectly leveled EVD will produce systematically inaccurate ICP readings.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The EVD is the gold standard for ICP monitoring because it serves a dual purpose: diagnostic (ICP measurement) and therapeutic (CSF drainage to reduce ICP). Intraparenchymal monitors cannot be recalibrated once placed, introducing the risk of measurement drift. The change from 20 to 22 mmHg as the treatment threshold is a commonly tested update. Mnemonic: "22 is the new 20."

### BEST-TRIP Trial

The Benchmark Evidence from South American Trials: Treatment of Intracranial Pressure (BEST-TRIP) trial (Chesnut et al., *NEJM*, 2012) was a landmark study that compared ICP-monitor-guided management versus imaging and clinical examination-guided management in 324 severe TBI patients in Bolivia and Ecuador. The results showed no difference in 6-month mortality (39% vs. 41%, P = 0.60).

Critically, this trial did **not** demonstrate that ICP monitoring is useless. Rather, it showed that two different treatment protocols yielded equivalent outcomes. The imaging-clinical group received significantly more empiric brain-targeted treatments (4.8 vs. 3.4 days of osmotherapy, P = 0.002). The trial's greatest relevance is for resource-limited settings where ICP monitors may not be available.

## Cerebral Perfusion Pressure Targets `[BOARD]`

### Definition and Target Range

Cerebral perfusion pressure is defined as:

**CPP = MAP - ICP**

where MAP is mean arterial pressure and ICP is intracranial pressure.

**Level IIB Recommendation:** The target CPP range is **60--70 mmHg**.

### Critical Thresholds

| CPP Range | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|----------------------|
| <50 mmHg | Associated with decreased brain oxygenation and increased morbidity/mortality; ischemic threshold |
| 50--59 mmHg | Marginal perfusion; should be avoided when possible |
| 60--70 mmHg | Optimal target range per BTF 4th Edition |
| >70 mmHg | Do NOT aggressively push CPP above 70 with vasopressors --- risk of ARDS (Level III) |

### The CPP-ARDS Connection

The BTF specifically warns against using aggressive fluid resuscitation and vasopressors to maintain CPP above 70 mmHg because this strategy increases the risk of acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). The Lund protocol and other physiologically-based approaches have emphasized this concern. The mechanism involves the combined effects of excessive intravenous fluid administration, positive fluid balance, and sympathomimetic-induced pulmonary vasoconstriction.

### Autoregulation Considerations

Cerebral autoregulation --- the ability of the cerebral vasculature to maintain constant cerebral blood flow across a range of perfusion pressures --- is frequently impaired after severe TBI. When autoregulation is intact, increases in MAP are accompanied by cerebrovascular vasoconstriction, maintaining constant cerebral blood flow. When autoregulation is impaired, increases in MAP lead to passive increases in cerebral blood volume and potentially increased ICP.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The optimal CPP target of 60--70 mmHg is a frequently tested board question. The classic board distractor is an answer choice suggesting "maintain CPP >80 mmHg" --- this is WRONG. Too low (CPP <50) = ischemia. Too high (CPP >70 with vasopressors) = ARDS. The correct answer is always 60--70 mmHg.

## Hyperosmolar Therapy `[BOARD]`

### Mannitol

**Level II Recommendation:** Mannitol (0.25--1 g/kg body weight) is effective for reducing intracranial pressure.

**Mechanism:** Mannitol is an osmotic diuretic that creates an osmotic gradient across the blood-brain barrier, drawing water from the brain parenchyma into the intravascular space. It also has rheological effects, reducing blood viscosity and improving microvascular flow (the immediate mechanism of ICP reduction, occurring within minutes before the osmotic effect takes full effect).

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Dose | 0.25--1 g/kg IV bolus |
| Onset | 15--30 minutes |
| Duration | 2--6 hours |
| Monitoring | Serum osmolality: keep <320 mOsm/L |
| Contraindications | Hypotension (causes osmotic diuresis and volume depletion); serum osmolality >320 mOsm/L; renal failure |
| Administration | Bolus dosing (not continuous infusion); via peripheral or central IV |

### Hypertonic Saline (HTS)

**Level III Recommendation:** Hypertonic saline may be used for ICP reduction.

**Mechanism:** HTS creates an osmotic gradient similar to mannitol but does not cause diuresis. Instead, it produces intravascular volume **expansion**, making it preferable in hypotensive patients.

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Concentrations | 3%, 7.5%, or 23.4% NaCl |
| Dose | Varies by concentration; 3% 150--250 mL bolus; 23.4% 30 mL via central line for acute herniation |
| Onset | 15--30 minutes |
| Duration | Variable; may be longer than mannitol |
| Monitoring | Serum sodium: keep <160 mEq/L |
| Key risk | Central pontine myelinolysis if sodium corrected too rapidly |
| Administration | 23.4% requires central venous access; lower concentrations may be given peripherally |

### Mannitol vs. HTS: Key Distinctions

| Feature | Mannitol | Hypertonic Saline |
|---------|----------|-------------------|
| Volume effect | Diuresis (volume DEPLETION) | Volume EXPANSION |
| Use in hypotension | CONTRAINDICATED | Preferred |
| BTF evidence level | Level II | Level III |
| Monitoring parameter | Serum osmolality <320 | Serum sodium <160 |
| Rebound ICP risk | Yes (with prolonged use) | Lower rebound risk |
| Route | Peripheral or central IV | High concentrations require central access |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Mannitol is CONTRAINDICATED in hypotensive TBI patients because it causes osmotic diuresis and volume depletion, worsening hypotension. Use hypertonic saline in hypotensive patients with elevated ICP --- it provides ICP reduction AND volume expansion. For acute herniation, 23.4% NaCl (30 mL via central line) provides the most rapid osmotic shift. Mnemonic: "Mannitol Makes Blood Pressure Drop."

## Seizure Prophylaxis `[BOARD]`

### BTF Recommendation

**Level IIA Recommendation:** Phenytoin is recommended to decrease the incidence of early post-traumatic seizures (PTS) (within 7 days of injury) in patients with severe TBI, when the overall benefit is felt to outweigh the complications associated with such treatment. Prophylaxis beyond 7 days is NOT recommended. Note: The BTF 4th Edition does NOT recommend valproate for seizure prophylaxis; a separate Level IIB recommendation states that valproate is NOT recommended for prophylaxis of late PTS due to a trend toward increased mortality in the Temkin et al. (1999) valproate trial.

### Temkin Trial (1990)

The landmark trial by Temkin and colleagues (published in *NEJM*, 1990) provided the foundational evidence for seizure prophylaxis in TBI. This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial enrolled 404 patients with serious head trauma.

**Key Results:**

| Time Period | Phenytoin | Placebo | P Value |
|-------------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Early PTS (Day 0--7) | 3.6% | 14.2% | <0.001 |
| Late PTS (Day 8 -- Year 1) | 21.5% | 15.7% | >0.2 (NS) |
| Late PTS (Through Year 2) | 27.5% | 21.1% | >0.2 (NS) |

Phenytoin significantly reduced early PTS (73% relative risk reduction) but had **no effect** on late PTS. This finding is the basis for the 7-day prophylaxis recommendation.

### Seizure Classification

| Category | Timing | Clinical Significance |
|----------|--------|----------------------|
| Immediate PTS | Within 24 hours | Acute response to injury; may be provoked |
| Early PTS | 24 hours to 7 days | Preventable with phenytoin/levetiracetam; occurs during period of greatest metabolic vulnerability |
| Late PTS | >7 days | NOT preventable with prophylactic AEDs; constitutes post-traumatic epilepsy if recurrent |

### Risk Factors for Late PTS

| Risk Factor | Incidence of Late PTS |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Penetrating head injury | 33--50% |
| Intracranial hematoma | 25--30% |
| Early PTS (24h--7d) | 25% |
| Prolonged coma or PTA >24 hours | 35% |
| Depressed skull fracture | 3--70% (varies by associated factors) |
| Cortical contusion | 20--25% |

### Levetiracetam vs. Phenytoin

Levetiracetam (Keppra) has become increasingly preferred in clinical practice due to several advantages:
- Fewer drug-drug interactions
- No serum level monitoring required
- Less cognitive impairment
- No hepatic enzyme induction
- Comparable efficacy for early PTS prevention (though large RCTs directly comparing the two are limited)

The BTF 4th Edition states there is insufficient evidence to recommend levetiracetam over phenytoin, but evolving clinical practice has shifted toward levetiracetam as the preferred agent.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phenytoin prevents EARLY but NOT late seizures. AED prophylaxis should be used for 7 days only. Long-term phenytoin has adverse cognitive effects and may impede neural recovery. Levetiracetam is increasingly preferred in practice but is not formally recommended over phenytoin in BTF guidelines. Chronic AED use for seizure prophylaxis (as opposed to treatment of established PTE) is contraindicated --- it does not prevent epileptogenesis and carries significant cognitive side effects.

## Blood Pressure Targets by Age `[BOARD]`

### BTF Recommendation (Level III)

The 4th Edition introduced age-stratified blood pressure targets:

| Age Group | Minimum SBP Target |
|-----------|-------------------|
| 15--49 years | >=110 mmHg |
| 50--69 years | >=100 mmHg |
| >70 years | >=110 mmHg |

### Significance of Hypotension

A single episode of hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg) **doubles** mortality in severe TBI. Hypotension is the single most detrimental secondary insult in TBI, and its avoidance is a cornerstone of acute management. When hypotension occurs in combination with hypoxemia (PaO2 <60 mmHg or SpO2 <90%), the effects are synergistically harmful, with mortality rates approaching 75% in some series.

The pathophysiology is straightforward: the injured brain is exquisitely vulnerable to ischemia. Cerebral autoregulation is frequently impaired after TBI, meaning that cerebral blood flow becomes passively dependent on systemic blood pressure. Any decrease in MAP directly reduces cerebral perfusion and oxygen delivery to already-compromised tissue.

### Fluid Resuscitation

- Isotonic crystalloids (normal saline or lactated Ringer's) are the preferred resuscitation fluids
- Hypotonic solutions (D5W, half-normal saline) are CONTRAINDICATED as they worsen cerebral edema
- Albumin is NOT recommended based on the SAFE-TBI substudy, which showed increased mortality with albumin resuscitation in TBI patients

> **Clinical Pearl:** The age-stratified blood pressure targets are a commonly tested component of the BTF 4th Edition. The key teaching point is that ANY episode of hypotension (SBP <90) doubles mortality and must be aggressively prevented and treated. Both hypotension and hypoxemia independently worsen outcomes, and together they are synergistically harmful.

## SIBICC Algorithm `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Overview

The Seattle International Severe Traumatic Brain Injury Consensus Conference (SIBICC) was convened in 2019 and published two consensus algorithms for ICP management in patients with severe TBI: one for patients with ICP monitoring alone and one for patients with both ICP and brain tissue oxygen (PbtO2) monitoring. The algorithm was developed using a Delphi-method consensus approach with 42 experienced, clinically active severe TBI specialists from six continents, requiring 80% agreement for consensus.

### Three-Tier Structure

The SIBICC algorithm organizes interventions into three tiers based on their risk-benefit profiles:

**Tier 1** --- Favorable side-effect profile; employ first:
- Head of bed elevation to 30 degrees, head in neutral midline position
- Sedation and analgesia (propofol 20--75 mcg/kg/min OR midazolam 0.03--0.2 mg/kg/hr; fentanyl 1--3 mcg/kg/hr OR remifentanil 0.05--0.5 mcg/kg/min)
- Controlled normothermia (target 36--37.5 degrees C; fever prevention)
- CSF drainage via EVD
- Osmotic therapy (mannitol 0.25--1 g/kg bolus OR HTS 3% 150--250 mL bolus)

**Tier 2** --- Moderate risk; escalate when Tier 1 fails:
- Mild hyperventilation (PaCO2 30--35 mmHg) as a bridge therapy only
- Neuromuscular blockade (cisatracurium 1--3 mcg/kg/min preferred; Hofmann elimination, no histamine release)
- MAP challenge / CPP optimization (autoregulation-based ICP treatment)

**Tier 3** --- Highest risk; last resort:
- Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital 10 mg/kg loading dose, then 1--3 mg/kg/hr; target EEG burst suppression)
- Therapeutic hypothermia (32--35 degrees C for refractory ICP ONLY, not prophylactic)
- Decompressive craniectomy (flap >=12 x 15 cm)

### MAP Challenge (Novel SIBICC Element)

The MAP challenge is an innovative element of the SIBICC algorithm designed to test cerebral autoregulation status:

1. Increase MAP by 10 mmHg using vasopressors
2. Observe the ICP response:
   - **If ICP decreases** with higher MAP: Autoregulation is INTACT, and CPP optimization is the correct strategy
   - **If ICP increases** with higher MAP: Autoregulation is IMPAIRED, and volume-reducing strategies (osmotherapy, CSF drainage) should be prioritized

This approach reflects the paradigm shift toward individualized, autoregulation-guided management rather than a one-size-fits-all protocol.

### SIBICC Pharmacologic Details

| Tier | Intervention | Drug/Dose | Target/Threshold | Key Consideration |
|------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Sedation | Propofol 20--75 mcg/kg/min OR midazolam 0.03--0.2 mg/kg/hr | RASS -4 to -5 | Propofol: monitor triglycerides; risk of PRIS at >5 mg/kg/hr for >48h |
| 1 | Analgesia | Fentanyl 1--3 mcg/kg/hr OR remifentanil 0.05--0.5 mcg/kg/min | Pain control; reduce sympathetic tone | Avoid morphine (histamine release, active metabolites in renal failure) |
| 1 | Osmotic therapy | Mannitol 0.25--1 g/kg OR HTS 3% 150--250 mL | ICP <22 mmHg; Osm <320; Na <160 | HTS preferred if hypotensive; 23.4% 30 mL for acute herniation |
| 2 | NMB | Cisatracurium 1--3 mcg/kg/min | Refractory ICP after Tier 1 | Hofmann elimination; no histamine release |
| 2 | MAP challenge | Norepinephrine or phenylephrine | Raise MAP by 10 mmHg; observe ICP | Tests autoregulation status |
| 3 | Barbiturate coma | Pentobarbital 10 mg/kg load, then 1--3 mg/kg/hr | EEG burst suppression | Causes severe hypotension requiring vasopressors |
| 3 | Hypothermia | Target 32--35 degrees C | Refractory ICP only | NOT prophylactic; Eurotherm showed harm with early use |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The SIBICC algorithm represents consensus-based (Class III) evidence from 42 international experts using a modified Delphi process. Key novel elements include the MAP challenge for testing autoregulation and the emphasis on controlled normothermia (36--37.5 degrees C) rather than targeted hypothermia. The algorithm provides a structured framework that complements the BTF guidelines.

## ICP Management Ladder `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Stepwise Approach

Management of elevated ICP follows a stepwise escalation from least to most invasive interventions. Each step is escalated only when the previous step fails to control ICP below 22 mmHg. This "ladder" or "staircase" approach is among the most commonly tested acute TBI management concepts.

| SIBICC Tier | Step | Intervention | Key Considerations |
|-------------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Tier 1 | 1 | HOB elevation 30 degrees, head midline | Facilitates jugular venous drainage; simple first step |
| Tier 1 | 2 | Sedation/analgesia (propofol, fentanyl) | Reduces cerebral metabolic demand and sympathetic tone |
| Tier 1 | 3 | Controlled normothermia (36--37.5 degrees C) | Fever prevention; each degree C above 37 increases cerebral metabolic rate by 10--13% |
| Tier 1 | 4 | CSF drainage via EVD | Gold standard monitor + therapeutic drainage |
| Tier 1 | 5 | Osmotic therapy (mannitol or HTS) | Bolus dosing; monitor osmolality/sodium |
| Tier 2 | 6 | Mild hyperventilation (PaCO2 30--35) | BRIDGE therapy only; transient effect (hours); risk of ischemia if PaCO2 <25 |
| Tier 2 | 7 | Neuromuscular blockade (cisatracurium) | Reduces thoracoabdominal pressure transmission; avoid prolonged use (ICU myopathy) |
| Tier 2 | 8 | MAP challenge / CPP optimization | Autoregulation-based approach per SIBICC |
| Tier 3 | 9 | Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital) | Last-resort medical therapy; causes hypotension; loss of neurological exam |
| Tier 3 | 10 | Therapeutic hypothermia (32--35 degrees C) | Only for refractory ICP; NOT prophylactic |
| Tier 3 | 11 | Decompressive craniectomy | Flap >=12 x 15 cm; reduces mortality but increases vegetative state rate |

### Hyperventilation: Bridge Therapy Only

Hyperventilation reduces ICP by causing cerebral vasoconstriction, which decreases cerebral blood volume. However, this effect is temporary (hours) as CSF bicarbonate equilibrates, and excessive vasoconstriction can cause cerebral ischemia. Guidelines are clear:

- **PaCO2 30--35 mmHg:** Acceptable as a brief bridge therapy
- **PaCO2 <30 mmHg:** Should be AVOIDED in the first 24 hours when cerebral blood flow is already reduced
- **PaCO2 <=25 mmHg:** Prolonged prophylactic hyperventilation is NOT recommended (Level IIB)
- Brief hyperventilation is acceptable as a temporizing measure during acute herniation while definitive treatment is prepared

## Herniation Syndromes `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Overview

Herniation occurs when elevated ICP causes brain tissue to shift from one intracranial compartment to another through rigid dural folds or bony openings. Recognizing herniation patterns is critical for emergent management and is heavily tested on board examinations.

### Classification

| Type | Mechanism | Clinical Signs | Key Features |
|------|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Uncal (Transtentorial)** | Medial temporal lobe (uncus) herniates over the tentorium cerebelli | Ipsilateral CN III palsy (dilated pupil, "down and out" eye), contralateral hemiparesis, then bilateral posturing and coma | MOST COMMON herniation type; first sign is ipsilateral pupil dilation; Kernohan notch = FALSE localizing sign (ipsilateral hemiparesis from contralateral peduncle compression) |
| **Central (Transtentorial)** | Bilateral downward displacement of hemispheres through tentorium | Bilateral small reactive pupils (early), then fixed midposition pupils; progressive rostral-to-caudal deterioration; decorticate then decerebrate | Orderly progression; Cushing triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations) is a LATE sign |
| **Tonsillar (Cerebellar)** | Cerebellar tonsils herniate through foramen magnum | Neck stiffness, lower cranial nerve palsies, respiratory arrest, sudden cardiopulmonary collapse | Most rapidly FATAL; compresses medulla; CONTRAINDICATION to lumbar puncture |
| **Upward (Ascending)** | Posterior fossa mass pushes cerebellum upward through tentorium | Conjugate downward gaze (Parinaud syndrome), miosis, altered consciousness | Rare; can be caused by excessive CSF drainage from EVD |
| **Subfalcine (Cingulate)** | Cingulate gyrus herniates under falx cerebri | Contralateral leg weakness (ACA territory compression) | Often seen as midline shift on CT; may be clinically silent early |

### Uncal Herniation: Detailed Progression

The uncal herniation syndrome has a characteristic clinical progression that is the most commonly tested herniation pattern:

1. **Stage 1 (Early):** Ipsilateral pupil dilation (parasympathetic fibers on the surface of CN III are compressed first)
2. **Stage 2 (Progressive):** Ipsilateral complete CN III palsy ("down and out" eye); contralateral hemiparesis (compression of ipsilateral cerebral peduncle against the tentorium)
3. **Stage 3 (Late):** Bilateral posturing (decerebrate); bilateral fixed dilated pupils; loss of brainstem reflexes
4. **Terminal:** Respiratory failure; cardiovascular collapse

**Kernohan Notch Phenomenon:** This is a false localizing sign in which the contralateral cerebral peduncle is compressed against the opposite tentorial edge, causing **ipsilateral** hemiparesis (same side as the lesion, opposite to what is expected). This occurs in approximately 20--30% of uncal herniations and can lead to incorrect lateralization of the lesion.

**Cushing Triad:** Hypertension, bradycardia, and irregular respirations. This is a LATE sign of critically elevated ICP and impending herniation, not an early warning sign. The mechanism involves brainstem compression triggering a sympathetic surge (hypertension) with reflex vagal bradycardia.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Uncal herniation is the most commonly tested herniation syndrome. The earliest sign is ipsilateral pupil dilation. The Kernohan notch phenomenon is a false localizing sign that produces ipsilateral (rather than contralateral) hemiparesis. Cushing triad is a LATE sign --- by the time it appears, herniation is already occurring. Emergency management includes mannitol or 23.4% HTS, brief hyperventilation, and emergent surgical decompression.

## CRASH-3: Tranexamic Acid in TBI `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Trial Design

The CRASH-3 trial (Effects of tranexamic acid on death, disability, vascular occlusive events and other morbidities in patients with acute traumatic brain injury) was published in *The Lancet* in 2019. This international, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial enrolled 12,737 patients with TBI across 175 hospitals in 29 countries.

**Intervention:** TXA 1 g IV bolus followed by 1 g infused over 8 hours, administered within 3 hours of injury.

### Key Results

| Subgroup | TXA | Placebo | Risk Ratio (95% CI) |
|----------|-----|---------|---------------------|
| Overall (all patients, head injury death) | 18.5% | 19.8% | 0.94 (0.86--1.02) --- not statistically significant |
| Mild-moderate TBI (GCS 9--15) | --- | --- | 0.78 (0.64--0.95) --- **significant benefit** |
| Severe TBI (GCS 3--8) | --- | --- | 0.99 (0.91--1.07) --- NO benefit |

### Critical Subgroup Finding

The most important finding for clinical practice is the differential effect by GCS severity:
- **GCS 9--15 (mild-moderate):** Significant reduction in head injury-related death (RR 0.78)
- **GCS 3--8 (severe):** No benefit whatsoever (RR 0.99)

Early treatment was more effective than later treatment in patients with mild-moderate head injury (P = 0.005), but time to treatment had no effect in severe TBI (P = 0.73).

### Safety

TXA was safe across all severity groups. There was no increase in thromboembolic events (DVT, PE, stroke), seizures, or other adverse events in the TXA group compared to placebo.

### Mechanism

TXA is an antifibrinolytic agent that inhibits plasminogen activation, reducing fibrinolysis and stabilizing clot formation. In TBI, progressive hemorrhage expansion occurs in a substantial proportion of patients within the first hours, driven by traumatic coagulopathy and local fibrinolysis. TXA is hypothesized to limit this hemorrhage expansion.

> **Clinical Pearl:** TXA benefits patients with GCS 9--15 (mild-moderate TBI) when given within 3 hours. There is NO benefit in GCS 3--8 (severe TBI). This GCS subgroup distinction is critical for boards. Dose: 1 g bolus + 1 g over 8 hours. TXA is SAFE across all severities --- the question is efficacy, not safety. European guidelines (2023) strongly recommend TXA at the earliest opportunity, ideally within 3 hours.

## Failed Neuroprotection Trials `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Overview

Multiple promising neuroprotective agents have failed in Phase III clinical trials for TBI. Understanding these failures is important for board examinations (common question format: "Which of the following is NOT recommended for TBI?") and for understanding the challenges of translational neuroscience.

### Major Negative Trials

| Trial | Year | Agent | N | Mechanism/Rationale | Result |
|-------|------|-------|---|---------------------|--------|
| **ProTECT III** (Wright, *NEJM*) | 2014 | Progesterone IV | 882 | Anti-inflammatory, anti-edema, anti-apoptotic; promising Phase II (ProTECT II: 50% mortality reduction) | Stopped for FUTILITY. GOS-E 50.4% vs 50.5%. NO difference. |
| **SyNAPSe** (Skolnick, *NEJM*) | 2014 | Progesterone IV (BHR-100) | 1,195 | Same rationale; international trial with modified formulation | NEGATIVE. No benefit on GOS at 6 months. |
| **POLAR** (Cooper, *JAMA*) | 2018 | Prophylactic hypothermia 33 degrees C for >=72h | 511 | Reduce metabolic rate; decrease excitotoxicity | NEGATIVE. 48.8% vs 49.0% favorable outcomes. More pneumonia. |
| **Eurotherm3235** (Andrews, *NEJM*) | 2015 | Hypothermia 32--35 degrees C for ICP control | 387 | Hypothermia as FIRST-LINE ICP therapy | **HARMFUL.** Stopped early. Greater mortality and worse functional outcomes. |
| **CRASH** (Roberts, *Lancet*) | 2004 | Methylprednisolone | 10,008 | Reduce inflammation and edema | **HARMFUL.** Increased 2-week mortality (RR 1.18). Only Level I BTF recommendation. |
| **EPO-TBI** (Nichol, *Lancet*) | 2015 | Erythropoietin 40,000 IU SC weekly | 606 | Neuroprotective, anti-apoptotic | NEGATIVE. GOS-E 44% vs 45%. Increased DVT risk. |

### The Progesterone Story: A Cautionary Tale

Progesterone was the most promising TBI neuroprotective candidate based on Phase II data. ProTECT II showed a 50% reduction in mortality, generating enormous enthusiasm. However, two simultaneous Phase III trials (ProTECT III and SyNAPSe) published in the same issue of the *NEJM* in 2014 were both completely negative. This double failure definitively ended progesterone development for TBI and underscored the challenge of translating promising Phase II results to Phase III confirmation.

### The Hypothermia Distinction

Three different hypothermia strategies have been tested, with distinct results:

1. **Prophylactic hypothermia** (POLAR, NABIS:H II): Applied before ICP elevation to prevent secondary injury. **Result: NO BENEFIT.**
2. **Early hypothermia as first-line ICP therapy** (Eurotherm3235): Applied as the initial treatment for elevated ICP. **Result: HARMFUL.** Stopped early by the Data Safety Monitoring Board.
3. **Therapeutic hypothermia as last-resort for refractory ICP** (SIBICC Tier 3): Applied only after all other medical and surgical options have failed. **Result: Remains acceptable per consensus**, though without strong RCT evidence of benefit.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Know the distinction between three hypothermia approaches: prophylactic (NOT recommended, no benefit), first-line for ICP (HARMFUL per Eurotherm), and last-resort for refractory ICP (still permissible per SIBICC Tier 3). The double failure of progesterone (ProTECT III + SyNAPSe, same NEJM issue, 2014) is a classic board-testable fact. Common board question: "Which of the following is NOT recommended?" Answer: steroids, progesterone, prophylactic hypothermia, EPO.

## Multimodal Neuromonitoring `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Overview

Advanced multimodal monitoring goes beyond ICP and CPP to assess cerebral oxygenation, metabolism, and electrical activity in real time. Integration of these modalities enables precision-guided, patient-specific neurocritical care and is increasingly tested on board examinations.

### Monitoring Modalities

| Modality | Parameter | Critical Threshold | What It Measures | Clinical Pearl |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **PbtO2** (Brain Tissue Oxygen) | Partial pressure of O2 in brain parenchyma | <15 mmHg (BTF); target >20 mmHg (SIBICC/BOOST-3) | Local brain tissue oxygenation via Licox catheter in at-risk white matter | PbtO2 may be low despite normal ICP/CPP, occurring in 30--40% of monitoring periods |
| **SjvO2** (Jugular Venous Oximetry) | Oxygen saturation of jugular bulb venous blood | <50% = ischemia; >75% = hyperemia | GLOBAL cerebral oxygen extraction; catheter in internal jugular vein bulb (dominant side) | SjvO2 <50% indicates demand exceeds supply; >75% suggests hyperemia or brain death |
| **Cerebral Microdialysis** | Lactate/Pyruvate Ratio (LPR) | LPR >40 = metabolic crisis; LPR >25 = concerning | Cerebral metabolic state via interstitial fluid sampling | LPR >40 with low pyruvate = ischemia; LPR >40 with normal pyruvate = mitochondrial dysfunction |
| **Continuous EEG** | Cortical electrical activity | Nonconvulsive seizures in 20--30% of severe TBI | Detects NCSE, spreading depolarizations, burst suppression | NCSE is clinically silent and undetectable without cEEG |
| **PRx** (Pressure Reactivity Index) | Correlation of ICP and MAP slow waves | PRx >0.3 = impaired autoregulation | Continuous autoregulation assessment | Optimal CPP (CPPopt) identified where PRx is most negative |

### PbtO2 vs. SjvO2

These two modalities are complementary, not redundant:
- **PbtO2** measures **local** brain tissue oxygenation (focal ischemia detection)
- **SjvO2** measures **global** cerebral oxygen extraction (hemispheric assessment)
- PbtO2 may detect focal ischemia missed by SjvO2 and vice versa
- PbtO2 <15 mmHg occurs in 30--40% of monitoring periods despite normal ICP and CPP, demonstrating that ICP monitoring alone misses significant episodes of brain tissue hypoxia

### Microdialysis Interpretation

The lactate-to-pyruvate ratio (LPR) provides critical information about the type of metabolic failure:
- **LPR >40 with LOW pyruvate:** Ischemic injury (insufficient substrate delivery) --- responds to CPP optimization
- **LPR >40 with NORMAL pyruvate:** Mitochondrial dysfunction (substrate present but cannot be utilized) --- does NOT respond to CPP optimization
- **Glucose <0.2 mmol/L:** Cerebral energy failure regardless of LPR

### The BOOST Trials

The Brain Oxygen Optimization in Severe TBI (BOOST) trials represent the most important clinical trials evaluating PbtO2-guided therapy:
- **BOOST-2** (2017): Phase II trial demonstrating that PbtO2-guided therapy reduced brain tissue hypoxia compared to ICP/CPP-guided therapy alone
- **BOOST-3** (ongoing as of 2025): Phase III definitive RCT evaluating whether PbtO2-guided therapy improves functional outcomes

> **Clinical Pearl:** PbtO2 measures LOCAL brain tissue oxygenation; SjvO2 measures GLOBAL cerebral oxygen extraction. PbtO2 <15 mmHg occurs in 30--40% of monitoring periods despite normal ICP, highlighting the limitation of ICP monitoring alone. The microdialysis LPR distinction between ischemic (low pyruvate) and mitochondrial (normal pyruvate) metabolic crisis has direct therapeutic implications.

## Blood-Based Biomarkers `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### FDA-Cleared Biomarkers

**GFAP (Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein)** and **UCH-L1 (Ubiquitin C-terminal Hydrolase L1)** are the first FDA-cleared blood-based diagnostic tests for TBI.

| Biomarker | Cell Source | What It Measures | Peak Timing | FDA Status | Normal Values |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| GFAP | Astrocytes | Astrocytic injury/gliosis | 20--24 hours (detectable within 1 hour) | FDA-cleared 2018 (Banyan BTI); Abbott i-STAT 2024 | <30 pg/mL |
| UCH-L1 | Neurons | Neuronal cell body injury | Within 8 hours (shorter half-life) | FDA-cleared 2018 (Banyan BTI); Abbott i-STAT 2024 | <360 pg/mL |

### ALERT-TBI Pivotal Study

The ALERT-TBI study (Bazarian et al., *Lancet Neurology*, 2018) validated the combined GFAP + UCH-L1 test in 1,959 patients with GCS 9--15:
- **Sensitivity:** 97.6% for CT-positive intracranial lesions
- **Negative Predictive Value (NPV):** 99.6%
- **Specificity:** ~37% (many false positives)

The primary clinical utility is **ruling OUT** the need for CT in mild TBI: a negative test (GFAP <30 pg/mL + UCH-L1 <360 pg/mL) indicates low risk for CT-positive findings. A positive test still requires CT confirmation.

### Other Research Biomarkers

| Biomarker | Source | What It Measures | Status |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|--------|
| S100B | Astrocytes, Schwann cells | Astrocytic injury (less specific than GFAP) | European CT triage (Scandinavian Guidelines); NOT FDA-cleared; elevated by extracranial injuries |
| NfL (Neurofilament Light) | Axons | Axonal injury/degeneration | Research only; rises days-weeks post-injury; correlates with DAI severity |
| Tau | Axons (microtubule-associated) | Axonal injury; neurodegeneration | Research only; implicated in CTE; plasma p-tau217 under investigation |

> **Clinical Pearl:** GFAP + UCH-L1 are the FIRST FDA-cleared blood tests for TBI. Primary use is ruling out need for CT in mild TBI patients. The Abbott i-STAT TBI cartridge (2024) provides bedside results in 15 minutes using whole blood. GFAP <30 pg/mL + UCH-L1 <360 pg/mL = low risk. This is an emerging board topic.

## Coagulopathy of TBI `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Pathophysiology

TBI produces a unique brain-derived coagulopathy that differs from systemic trauma-induced coagulopathy. The brain has the **highest tissue factor concentration of any organ**. When brain tissue is injured, massive tissue factor release activates the extrinsic coagulation cascade, leading to a consumptive coagulopathy.

### Cascade of Events

1. **Brain injury** releases massive quantities of tissue factor into the systemic circulation
2. **Extrinsic pathway activation** leads to thrombin generation and widespread microthrombosis
3. **Consumptive coagulopathy** depletes clotting factors and fibrinogen
4. **Fibrinogen nadir** occurs at 3--6 hours post-injury
5. **Hyperfibrinolysis** from vascular endothelial damage
6. **Platelet dysfunction** with impaired aggregation despite normal platelet counts
7. **Progressive hemorrhage expansion** in up to 50% of contusions

### Incidence and Detection

- Coagulopathy occurs in up to 60% of severe TBI patients
- Standard coagulation tests (PT, PTT, INR) may **underestimate** the degree of coagulopathy
- **TEG (thromboelastography)** and **ROTEM (rotational thromboelastometry)** provide viscoelastic assessment of the entire clotting process and are superior for detecting TBI-associated coagulopathy
- TEG/ROTEM can identify specific clotting defects (factor deficiency, fibrinolysis, platelet dysfunction) to guide targeted therapy

### Management Principles

- Fresh frozen plasma (FFP) for factor replacement when INR is elevated
- Cryoprecipitate for fibrinogen replacement (target fibrinogen >150--200 mg/dL)
- Platelet transfusion for thrombocytopenia (<100,000 in TBI) or documented platelet dysfunction
- TXA 1 g IV for documented fibrinolysis on TEG/ROTEM
- Prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) for reversal of warfarin-associated coagulopathy
- Specific reversal agents for DOACs (idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The brain has the highest tissue factor concentration of any organ, explaining the unique coagulopathy of TBI. Standard labs (PT, PTT) may underestimate the coagulopathy --- TEG/ROTEM is superior. The fibrinogen nadir at 3--6 hours post-injury represents a window of vulnerability for hemorrhage expansion. Platelet dysfunction may occur despite normal platelet counts, a phenomenon detectable only with platelet function assays or viscoelastic testing.

## Nutrition `[BOARD]`

### BTF Recommendations

**Level IIA:** Feeding should be initiated by at least day 5 and at most day 7 post-injury to decrease mortality.

**Level IIB:** Transgastric jejunal feeding is recommended to reduce the incidence of ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP).

### Metabolic Response to TBI

TBI induces a profound hypermetabolic and hypercatabolic state:
- Caloric expenditure increases to **140%** of resting metabolic expenditure (some studies report up to 200% in severe TBI)
- The hypermetabolic response peaks at approximately 2 weeks post-injury and may persist for months
- Protein catabolism leads to rapid muscle wasting and negative nitrogen balance
- The sympathetic surge associated with TBI drives this hypermetabolic state

### Nutritional Targets

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Caloric goal | 140% of resting metabolic expenditure (or measured by indirect calorimetry) |
| Protein | 1.5--2 g/kg/day (significantly higher than standard ICU at 1.2--1.5 g/kg/day) |
| Protein calories | At least 15% of total calories as protein by day 7 |
| Timing | Initiate enteral nutrition by day 5--7 |
| Method | Indirect calorimetry is the gold standard for determining individual caloric needs |

### Feeding Route Considerations

| Route | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| Gastric (NG/OG) | Easy placement; accommodates bolus feeds | Aspiration risk; gastroparesis common in TBI (up to 50%) |
| Post-pyloric (NJ) | Reduced aspiration risk; feeds despite gastroparesis | Requires fluoroscopic/endoscopic placement |
| Transgastric jejunal (TGJ) | Simultaneous gastric decompression + jejunal feeding; reduces VAP (BTF Level IIB) | More complex tube management |
| PEG | Long-term access; easier medication administration | Requires procedure; wait 2--3 weeks post-injury typically |

> **Clinical Pearl:** TBI patients are hypermetabolic with caloric expenditure reaching 140% of baseline. Protein requirements are 1.5--2 g/kg/day. Feed by day 5--7 (BTF 4th Edition). Transgastric jejunal feeding reduces VAP. Underfeeding increases mortality and infection; overfeeding (especially excessive glucose) increases CO2 production and worsens ventilator dependence.

## DVT Prophylaxis `[BOARD]`

### Incidence and Risk

DVT is a major complication of severe TBI, with incidence rates of up to **54%** without prophylaxis in some series. TBI patients are at particularly high risk due to immobility, altered consciousness, venous stasis from paralysis, and the procoagulant state induced by tissue factor release.

### BTF 4th Edition Recommendation

**Level III:** Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) or low-dose unfractionated heparin (LDUH) may be used in combination with mechanical prophylaxis. However, there is an increased risk for expansion of intracranial hemorrhage, and there is **insufficient evidence to recommend specific timing, dose, or preferred agent**.

### Practical Approach

| Timing | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Immediate | Mechanical prophylaxis (graduated compression stockings AND/OR intermittent pneumatic compression devices) for ALL patients |
| 24--72 hours | Consider pharmacologic prophylaxis if brain injury is stable on repeat CT imaging and benefit outweighs risk of intracranial hemorrhage expansion |
| Stable CT at 24h | Many centers initiate LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily or 30 mg SC BID) after documented stable CT |
| Active intracranial hemorrhage | Delay pharmacologic prophylaxis; rely on mechanical prophylaxis only |
| Post-craniotomy | Pharmacologic prophylaxis generally initiated 24--48 hours post-operatively if hemostasis is assured |

### Agent Selection

| Agent | Dose | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Enoxaparin (LMWH) | 40 mg SC daily or 30 mg SC BID | More predictable pharmacokinetics; lower HIT risk; once-daily dosing | Cannot be fully reversed; renal dosing required for CrCl <30 |
| Unfractionated heparin (UFH) | 5,000 units SC q8--12h | Fully reversible with protamine; no renal adjustment | Requires more frequent dosing; higher HIT risk; less predictable |

> **Clinical Pearl:** DVT incidence in severe TBI without prophylaxis is approximately 54%. Mechanical prophylaxis should be started immediately in all patients. Pharmacologic prophylaxis is typically initiated after 24--72 hours if the brain injury is stable on repeat imaging. The BTF provides only Level III guidance on timing, reflecting the limited evidence and the tension between DVT prevention and intracranial hemorrhage expansion risk.

## Decompressive Craniectomy: DECRA and RESCUEicp `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### DECRA Trial (Cooper et al., *NEJM*, 2011)

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Population** | Severe TBI with DIFFUSE injury and ICP >20 mmHg refractory to first-tier therapies for >15 minutes within 1 hour |
| **Design** | Randomized: early bifrontal decompressive craniectomy (DC) vs. continued medical management |
| **ICP Result** | DC effectively DECREASED ICP |
| **Functional Result** | DC was associated with MORE UNFAVORABLE outcomes (worse GOS-E at 6 months) |
| **Conclusion** | Early bifrontal DC is NOT recommended for improving outcomes in diffuse severe TBI |
| **BTF Level** | Level IIA recommendation against routine bifrontal DC for diffuse injury |
| **Key Distinction** | This trial studied EARLY, PROPHYLACTIC DC, not DC as a last-resort rescue |

### RESCUEicp Trial (Hutchinson et al., *NEJM*, 2016)

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Population** | TBI with ICP >25 mmHg refractory to ALL medical management (last-resort scenario) |
| **Design** | Randomized: decompressive craniectomy vs. continued medical management |
| **6-Month Result** | DC REDUCED mortality (26.9% vs 48.9%) but INCREASED vegetative state (8.5% vs 2.1%) and severe disability |
| **12-Month Result** | More DC patients had FAVORABLE outcomes (upper severe disability or better) |
| **Conclusion** | DC reduces mortality but increases proportion surviving in vegetative or severely disabled state |
| **Clinical Implication** | DC as a life-saving last resort requires discussion with family regarding quality-of-life trade-offs |

### Surgical Technical Considerations

- The craniectomy flap must be at least **12 x 15 cm** (or 15 cm diameter)
- Small flaps can cause **external brain herniation** through the defect, paradoxically worsening outcomes
- The bone flap is removed and stored (either in the abdominal wall subcutaneous tissue or a bone bank) for later cranioplasty
- Cranioplasty is typically performed 6--12 weeks after craniectomy

### "Syndrome of the Trephined"

After craniectomy, some patients develop cognitive and neurological decline attributed to atmospheric pressure directly acting on the brain through the craniectomy defect. This "syndrome of the trephined" may manifest as:
- Paradoxical deterioration in neurological function after initial improvement
- Sinking of the skin flap (especially when upright)
- Cognitive and motor deficits that improve after cranioplasty
- The syndrome underscores the importance of timely cranioplasty

> **Clinical Pearl:** DECRA = early/prophylactic bifrontal DC for diffuse injury = NEGATIVE (worse outcomes). RESCUEicp = last-resort DC for refractory ICP = MIXED (reduced mortality but increased vegetative state at 6 months; more favorable outcomes at 12 months). The craniectomy flap must be at least 12 x 15 cm. Small flaps cause external brain herniation through the defect, worsening outcomes. DECRA tested the wrong question (early prophylactic DC) while RESCUEicp tested the clinically relevant question (last-resort DC).
