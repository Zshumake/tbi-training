# Chapter 13: Pediatric and Geriatric Traumatic Brain Injury

---

## Introduction

Traumatic brain injury at the extremes of age presents diagnostic, therapeutic, and prognostic challenges that are distinct from those encountered in the general adult population. The developing brain of a child and the aging brain of an elderly patient each respond to mechanical insult through fundamentally different pathophysiological pathways, and each demands age-appropriate assessment tools, treatment algorithms, and longitudinal follow-up strategies. This chapter provides a comprehensive treatment of pediatric and geriatric TBI, organized to serve both board preparation and clinical practice.

Pediatric TBI remains the leading cause of death and acquired disability in children in the United States. In the elderly, TBI hospitalization and mortality rates exceed those of every other age group, driven overwhelmingly by ground-level falls and complicated by anticoagulant use, medical comorbidities, and diminished physiologic reserve. A thorough understanding of the unique features of TBI at these two ends of the age spectrum is essential for every physiatrist.

### 13.1 Epidemiology of Pediatric TBI [BOARD]

Pediatric TBI is a major public health problem, accounting for approximately 475,000 emergency department visits, 35,000 hospitalizations, and 2,000 deaths annually in children aged 0 to 14 years in the United States (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022). Children aged 0 to 4 years have the highest rate of TBI-related emergency department visits of any age group, a statistic that is frequently tested on board examinations.

#### 13.1.1 Age-Specific Etiology

The mechanism of injury varies substantially across pediatric age groups, and understanding these transitions is essential for both clinical assessment and board preparation.

| Age Group | Leading Cause | Secondary Causes | Key Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 0-4 years | Falls | Abusive head trauma, struck by/against objects | Highest ED visit rate of any age group; must consider non-accidental trauma in all cases |
| 5-9 years | Falls | Pedestrian/bicycle injuries, sports | Helmet use becomes critical; organized sports participation begins |
| 10-14 years | Falls | Sports-related concussion, bicycle injuries | Sports concussion increasingly recognized; football, soccer, hockey, lacrosse |
| 15-19 years | Motor vehicle crashes | Sports, assaults | Risk-taking behavior; alcohol involvement; MVC overtakes falls as leading mechanism |

**Clinical Pearl:** The transition from falls to motor vehicle crashes as the leading mechanism occurs at approximately age 15. Falls dominate from birth through age 14, while motor vehicle crashes become the primary mechanism in adolescents aged 15-19. This epidemiologic transition point is a commonly tested board fact.

#### 13.1.2 Sex Differences and Trends

Boys sustain TBI at approximately twice the rate of girls across all age groups, a disparity that widens during adolescence as risk-taking behaviors increase. Sports-related concussion has received increasing attention, with an estimated 1.6 to 3.8 million sports-related concussions occurring annually in the United States. While football accounts for the highest absolute number of concussions, girls' soccer, basketball, and lacrosse demonstrate higher concussion rates per athletic exposure than their male counterparts in the same sports.

#### 13.1.3 Mortality and Morbidity

TBI is the leading cause of death and acquired disability in the pediatric population. Mortality rates are highest in infants (particularly those with abusive head trauma, with mortality rates of 15-25%) and in adolescents involved in motor vehicle crashes. Among survivors of moderate-to-severe pediatric TBI, approximately 30-40% experience significant long-term cognitive, behavioral, or motor disability. The societal cost is magnified by decades of lost productivity and the need for prolonged educational, rehabilitative, and psychosocial support.

### 13.2 Abusive Head Trauma [BOARD]

Abusive head trauma (AHT), formerly known as "shaken baby syndrome," is the leading cause of death from child abuse in the United States and a critical topic for every clinician who evaluates pediatric TBI. The current preferred terminology, "abusive head trauma," was adopted by the American Academy of Pediatrics in 2009 to encompass all mechanisms of inflicted cranial injury in children, including shaking, impact, and combined mechanisms (Christian CW, Block R; Committee on Child Abuse and Neglect. *Pediatrics*. 2009;123(5):1409-1411).

#### 13.2.1 Epidemiology and Risk Factors

AHT has a peak incidence in infants aged 2 to 4 months, with the vast majority of cases occurring in children under 2 years of age. Risk factors include male sex of the infant, prematurity, colic, parental substance abuse, domestic violence, young parental age, and social isolation. The perpetrator is most commonly a male caregiver (biological father, mother's partner, or male babysitter).

#### 13.2.2 Pathomechanics

Infants are uniquely susceptible to acceleration-deceleration injury due to several anatomic features:

1. **Large head-to-body ratio:** The infant head constitutes approximately 25% of total body length (versus 12% in adults), creating a large lever arm.
2. **Weak cervical musculature:** Underdeveloped neck muscles provide insufficient head stabilization during violent shaking.
3. **Unmyelinated brain:** Incomplete myelination renders axons more susceptible to shearing forces and diffuse axonal injury.
4. **Greater subarachnoid space:** The developing brain has a relatively larger subarachnoid space, permitting greater brain excursion and bridging vein stretching.

#### 13.2.3 The Classic Triad

The classic diagnostic triad of AHT consists of:

1. **Subdural hematomas** — often bilateral, frequently of different ages (acute, subacute, and chronic) indicating repeated episodes of trauma
2. **Retinal hemorrhages** — especially multilayered (pre-retinal, intra-retinal, sub-retinal) and extending to the periphery (ora serrata)
3. **Encephalopathy** — ranging from irritability and lethargy to seizures and coma

**Clinical Pearl — Retinal Hemorrhages:** Retinal hemorrhages are the most specific physical finding for abusive head trauma, present in approximately 85% of AHT cases. While mild, flame-shaped retinal hemorrhages confined to the posterior pole can occur from birth trauma (resolving within 2-4 weeks), Valsalva maneuvers, or severe accidental injury, multilayered retinal hemorrhages extending to the ora serrata are highly specific for AHT. An ophthalmology consultation with indirect ophthalmoscopy using scleral depression is mandatory when AHT is suspected.

#### 13.2.4 Skeletal Survey Findings

A full skeletal survey is mandatory when AHT is suspected (AAP recommendation). The following fracture patterns are highly specific for non-accidental trauma:

| Fracture Type | Mechanism | Specificity for Abuse |
|--------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Metaphyseal fractures ("bucket handle" or "corner" fractures) | Shearing forces at the growth plate when limbs are grabbed during shaking | Highly specific |
| Posterior rib fractures | Anteroposterior chest compression during shaking (squeezing) | Highly specific |
| Multiple fractures in different stages of healing | Repeated episodes of trauma over time | Highly specific |
| Complex, bilateral, or depressed skull fractures | Direct impact | More suspicious than simple linear fractures |
| Scapular fractures | Direct blow or violent grabbing | Uncommon in accidental trauma |

**Clinical Pearl:** A follow-up skeletal survey at 2 weeks is recommended by the AAP, as healing fractures that were not visible on the initial radiographic survey may become apparent as callus formation develops.

#### 13.2.5 Neuroimaging Findings

- **CT head (acute):** Demonstrates acute blood products. Bilateral subdural hematomas, interhemispheric subdural collections along the falx, and subarachnoid hemorrhage are characteristic.
- **MRI brain (subacute):** Superior to CT for detecting diffuse axonal injury (DAI), small subdural collections, and dating hemorrhages based on signal characteristics. MRI should be performed once the patient is clinically stable.
- **Subdural hematomas of different ages:** The presence of acute hemorrhage alongside subacute or chronic collections on imaging indicates repeated episodes of trauma over time. This is a hallmark finding in AHT and a classic board scenario.

#### 13.2.6 Comprehensive Evaluation Protocol

When AHT is suspected, the following evaluation is indicated:

1. Non-contrast CT head (emergent)
2. MRI brain (when stable)
3. Skeletal survey with follow-up at 2 weeks
4. Dilated fundoscopic examination by ophthalmology
5. Coagulation studies (PT, PTT, fibrinogen, von Willebrand panel — to exclude bleeding disorders)
6. Hepatic transaminases (AST, ALT) and lipase (screen for occult abdominal trauma)
7. Urinalysis (hematuria from renal injury)
8. Documentation of developmental stage of child and discrepancies between history and injury

#### 13.2.7 Legal Reporting Obligations

All 50 United States mandate that healthcare providers report suspected child abuse to child protective services (CPS). The legal standard is **reasonable suspicion**, not certainty. Failure to report is a criminal offense in most jurisdictions, and physicians have legal immunity for good-faith reports. The following red flags should trigger mandatory reporting:

- History inconsistent with the injury pattern
- History inconsistent with the child's developmental stage (e.g., a non-ambulatory infant reportedly "fell off the couch")
- Delay in seeking medical care
- Multiple injuries at different stages of healing
- Bruising in non-mobile infants ("those who don't cruise rarely bruise")

**Clinical Pearl:** Mandatory reporting requires only reasonable suspicion, not certainty or even a preponderance of evidence. The clinician is not expected to prove abuse but rather to initiate protective services investigation when clinical findings raise concern.

### 13.3 PECARN Decision Rules [BOARD]

The Pediatric Emergency Care Applied Research Network (PECARN) head injury clinical prediction rules represent the most rigorously validated decision instrument for identifying children at very low risk of clinically important traumatic brain injury (ciTBI) who do not require CT imaging. The original derivation and validation study enrolled 42,412 children and was published by Kuppermann et al. in *Lancet* in 2009.

#### 13.3.1 Definitions

- **Clinically important TBI (ciTBI):** Death from TBI, neurosurgical intervention, intubation for more than 24 hours for TBI, or hospitalization for 2 or more nights for TBI.
- **Goal:** Identify children at very low risk of ciTBI (<0.05%) who can safely forgo CT, thereby reducing unnecessary radiation exposure in the pediatric population.

#### 13.3.2 Algorithm for Children Under 2 Years

**Step 1 — High Risk (CT recommended):** Any of the following:
- GCS < 15
- Palpable skull fracture
- Altered mental status (agitation, somnolence, repetitive questioning, slow response)

*Risk of ciTBI: 4.4%*

**Step 2 — Intermediate Risk (Observation vs. CT):** If none of Step 1 criteria, assess for:
- Non-frontal scalp hematoma (occipital, parietal, or temporal locations)
- Loss of consciousness ≥ 5 seconds
- Not acting normally per parent assessment
- Severe mechanism of injury (MVC with ejection/rollover/fatality, fall >3 feet, head struck by high-impact object, bicycle without helmet)

*Risk of ciTBI: 0.9% — observation for 4-6 hours is a reasonable alternative to CT*

**Step 3 — Low Risk (CT NOT recommended):** None of the above criteria present.

*Risk of ciTBI: <0.02% — Negative predictive value >99.9%*

**Special consideration:** Infants under 3 months of age warrant a lower threshold for CT imaging given the difficulty of clinical assessment.

#### 13.3.3 Algorithm for Children 2 Years and Older

**Step 1 — High Risk (CT recommended):** Any of the following:
- GCS < 15
- Altered mental status
- Signs of basilar skull fracture (hemotympanum, periorbital ecchymosis ["raccoon eyes"], postauricular ecchymosis [Battle sign], CSF otorrhea or rhinorrhea)

*Risk of ciTBI: 4.3%*

**Step 2 — Intermediate Risk (Observation vs. CT):** If none of Step 1 criteria, assess for:
- Any loss of consciousness
- History of vomiting
- Severe headache
- Severe mechanism of injury (same criteria as <2 years)

*Risk of ciTBI: 0.8% — observation for 4-6 hours is reasonable*

**Step 3 — Low Risk (CT NOT recommended):** None of the above criteria present.

*Risk of ciTBI: <0.05% — Negative predictive value >99.9%*

#### 13.3.4 Key Differences Between Age Groups

| Feature | Children < 2 Years | Children ≥ 2 Years |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| High-risk skull finding | Palpable skull fracture | Signs of basilar skull fracture |
| Intermediate scalp finding | Non-frontal scalp hematoma | Not included |
| Parent assessment | "Not acting normally" per parent | Not included as separate criterion |
| LOC criterion | ≥ 5 seconds | Any LOC |
| Headache | Not reliably reported | Severe headache |
| Vomiting | Not included | History of vomiting |

**Clinical Pearl:** The PECARN rules have been validated with sensitivity >99% for ciTBI and have been shown to reduce unnecessary CT scans by 25-30% without missing clinically important injuries. For intermediate-risk patients, the presence of two or more risk factors increases ciTBI risk to 2.1%, while an isolated single risk factor carries only a 0.6% risk, favoring observation.

**References:**
1. Kuppermann N, Holmes JF, Dayan PS, et al. Identification of children at very low risk of clinically-important brain injuries after head trauma: a prospective cohort study. *Lancet*. 2009;374(9696):1160-1170.
2. Schonfeld D, Bressan S, Da Dalt L, et al. Pediatric Emergency Care Applied Research Network head injury clinical prediction rules are reliable in practice. *Arch Dis Child*. 2014;99(5):427-431.
3. Nigrovic LE, Schunk JE, Foerster A, et al. The effect of observation on cranial computed tomography utilization for children after blunt head trauma. *Pediatrics*. 2011;127(6):1067-1073.

### 13.4 Pediatric Glasgow Coma Scale Modifications [BOARD]

The standard Glasgow Coma Scale was designed for adult assessment and requires modification in preverbal children (typically those under 2 years of age). The pediatric modifications apply primarily to the verbal component, while the eye opening and motor components remain largely unchanged.

| Component | Score | Adult Response | Pediatric Adaptation (Preverbal) |
|-----------|-------|---------------|--------------------------------|
| Verbal | 5 | Oriented | Coos, babbles appropriately; smiles, tracks objects |
| Verbal | 4 | Confused | Irritable, cries but is consolable |
| Verbal | 3 | Inappropriate words | Cries persistently to pain stimuli |
| Verbal | 2 | Incomprehensible sounds | Moans, grunts to pain |
| Verbal | 1 | None | No vocalization |
| Eye Opening | 4 | Spontaneous | Spontaneous (same as adult) |
| Eye Opening | 3 | To voice | To voice (same as adult) |
| Eye Opening | 2 | To pain | To pain (same as adult) |
| Eye Opening | 1 | None | None (same as adult) |
| Motor | 6 | Obeys commands | Spontaneous purposeful movements |
| Motor | 5 | Localizes pain | Localizes pain/withdraws to touch |
| Motor | 4 | Withdraws | Withdraws to pain |
| Motor | 3 | Abnormal flexion | Abnormal flexion (decorticate) |
| Motor | 2 | Extension | Extension (decerebrate) |
| Motor | 1 | None | None |

**Clinical Pearl:** The maximum score remains 15, and the classification thresholds for mild (13-15), moderate (9-12), and severe (3-8) TBI are identical to adult criteria. The verbal component is the primary modification. In infants, "spontaneous purposeful movements" replaces "obeys commands" for the motor score of 6. The inability to assess verbal responses in intubated children limits the GCS; in such cases, the GCS motor score alone may be the most reliable predictor of outcome, a principle also used in the IMPACT prognostic calculator.

### 13.5 Children's Orientation and Amnesia Test (COAT) [COMPREHENSIVE]

The Children's Orientation and Amnesia Test (COAT) is the pediatric equivalent of the Galveston Orientation and Amnesia Test (GOAT) and was developed to serially assess post-traumatic amnesia (PTA) in children and adolescents aged 3 to 15 years (Ewing-Cobbs L, Levin HS, Fletcher JM, et al. *Neurosurgery*. 1990;27(5):720-724).

#### 13.5.1 Test Structure

The COAT comprises 16 items distributed across three domains:

1. **General Orientation (7 items, maximum 40 points):** Assesses orientation to person, place, and general temporal information appropriate for the child's developmental level.
2. **Temporal Orientation (5 items):** Evaluates knowledge of time, day, date, month, and year with age-appropriate allowances for error.
3. **Memory (4 items):** Tests recall of events surrounding the injury, including circumstances before and after the traumatic event.

#### 13.5.2 Scoring and Interpretation

- Total scores are calculated for each domain, with higher scores indicating better performance.
- Age-specific norms have been developed from studies of 146 typically developing children aged 3-15 years, with regression-predicted norms available (Iverson GL, et al. *Child Neuropsychol*. 2001;7(3):190-194).
- Serial administration (daily or every-other-day) tracks emergence from PTA.
- Resolution of PTA is indicated by consistent performance at or above age-appropriate normative thresholds.

#### 13.5.3 Clinical Utility

The COAT has been shown to be a better predictor of verbal and nonverbal memory outcomes at 6 and 12 months post-injury than the Glasgow Coma Scale score alone. Duration of PTA as measured by the COAT correlates significantly with long-term cognitive outcomes, educational achievement, and adaptive functioning. The test is the recommended instrument for pediatric PTA assessment in clinical practice guidelines.

**Clinical Pearl:** The COAT fills a critical gap in pediatric TBI assessment. Standard adult PTA measures (GOAT, O-Log) are inappropriate for children under 16 because they require temporal orientation knowledge and contextual understanding that young children have not yet developed. The COAT uses age-adjusted items and norms to account for developmental differences.

**References:**
1. Ewing-Cobbs L, Levin HS, Fletcher JM, Miner ME, Eisenberg HM. The Children's Orientation and Amnesia Test: relationship to severity of acute head injury and to recovery of memory. *Neurosurgery*. 1990;27(5):720-724.
2. Iverson GL, Iverson AM, Barton E. Regression-predicted age norms for the Children's Orientation and Amnesia Test. *Child Neuropsychol*. 2001;7(3):190-194.

### 13.6 "Growing Into Deficits": Developmental Impact of Pediatric TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

One of the most important concepts in pediatric neurorehabilitation is the phenomenon of "growing into deficits." This principle challenges the historical "Kennard principle" — the erroneous belief that a younger brain is inherently more resilient to injury and recovers better than an older brain. Contemporary evidence demonstrates that the developing brain is, in many respects, more vulnerable to injury, and that deficits may not become apparent until years after the initial insult.

#### 13.6.1 Theoretical Framework

The developing brain differs from the mature brain in a critical way: while adult TBI disrupts pre-existing skills and knowledge, pediatric TBI disrupts the acquisition of skills that have not yet been developed. The injured child loses not only current function but also the neural substrate required to build future cognitive, social, and academic abilities. This means that deficits may be "silent" at the time of injury and emerge only when age-appropriate developmental milestones are expected to be reached.

#### 13.6.2 Executive Function Emergence Timeline

Executive functions are among the most commonly affected cognitive domains in pediatric TBI, and their protracted developmental timeline explains why frontal lobe injuries sustained in early childhood may not manifest clinically for years.

| Age Range | Executive Function Milestones |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| 3-5 years | Basic inhibitory control, simple rule following, elementary working memory |
| 6-8 years | Working memory maturation, sustained attention, self-regulation of behavior |
| 9-12 years | Cognitive flexibility, planning and organization, logical reasoning, metacognitive awareness |
| 13-18 years | Complex reasoning, abstract thinking, social decision-making, advanced metacognition, risk assessment |
| Early 20s | Full prefrontal cortex maturation, complete integration of executive systems |

**Example scenario:** A 3-year-old sustains a moderate TBI with frontal lobe involvement. At ages 4-6, the child appears to recover well — walking, talking, and playing age-appropriately. However, at age 10-12, as academic demands increase and executive function is expected to mature, the child fails to develop age-appropriate planning, organization, and abstract reasoning. By adolescence, significant executive dysfunction, social difficulties, and academic failure become apparent. This delayed emergence of deficits is the hallmark of "growing into deficits."

#### 13.6.3 Academic Impact at Different Ages

TBI sustained during critical developmental windows can disproportionately affect specific academic skills:

- **Injury at ages 0-3:** May disrupt language acquisition, social-emotional development, and foundational learning
- **Injury at ages 4-6:** May impair reading readiness and basic numeracy
- **Injury at ages 7-8:** May specifically affect reading fluency and comprehension
- **Injury at ages 9-10:** May impair written expression and complex mathematical reasoning
- **Injury at ages 11-13:** May disrupt abstract academic reasoning, organizational skills, and study habits

#### 13.6.4 Social Development

TBI in early childhood can impair the development of social cognition, theory of mind (the ability to understand others' mental states), and perspective-taking. These impairments may not become clinically apparent until the child enters complex social environments (middle school, high school) where sophisticated social navigation is required. The result is often progressive social isolation, peer rejection, and loneliness during adolescence.

#### 13.6.5 Educational Accommodations

Children with TBI are eligible for educational accommodations under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) through an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act through a 504 Plan. TBI is specifically listed as a qualifying disability category under IDEA.

Key educational considerations include:

1. **Longitudinal monitoring:** Neuropsychological re-evaluation at key developmental transitions (school entry, middle school transition, high school transition)
2. **Annual IEP/504 review:** Accommodations must be reassessed annually as cognitive demands increase
3. **Extended time on examinations**
4. **Reduced course load and assignment modifications**
5. **Preferential seating (reduced distractions)**
6. **Note-taking assistance and use of assistive technology**
7. **Behavioral support plans for emotional dysregulation and impulsivity**

**Clinical Pearl:** Physicians must be aware that TBI is a qualifying category under IDEA and should advocate for appropriate educational evaluations and accommodations for their pediatric patients. Unlike learning disabilities, TBI may cause progressive functional decline relative to peers even without additional injury, requiring ongoing reassessment of educational support needs.

**References:**
1. Anderson V, Catroppa C, Morse S, Haritou F, Rosenfeld J. Functional plasticity or vulnerability after early brain injury? *Pediatrics*. 2005;116(6):1374-1382.
2. Chapman SB, McKinnon L. Discussion of developmental plasticity: factors affecting cognitive outcome after pediatric traumatic brain injury. *J Commun Disord*. 2000;33(4):333-344.
3. Yeates KO, Swift E, Taylor HG, et al. Short- and long-term social outcomes following pediatric traumatic brain injury. *J Int Neuropsychol Soc*. 2004;10(3):412-426.

### 13.7 Return-to-Learn Protocols for Students [COMPREHENSIVE]

Return-to-learn (RTL) protocols provide a structured, graduated approach for students to resume academic activities following concussion. These protocols are analogous to return-to-sport protocols but focus specifically on cognitive demands.

#### 13.7.1 Graduated Return-to-Learn Steps

| Stage | Activity Level | Goal | Duration |
|-------|---------------|------|----------|
| 1 | Complete cognitive rest (24-48 hours maximum) | Symptom recovery from acute phase | 1-2 days |
| 2 | Light cognitive activity at home | Gradual introduction of cognitive demands (reading for 15-30 minutes, light screen use) | Variable |
| 3 | Return to school with significant accommodations | Attend school part-time or full-time with reduced workload, extra time, rest breaks | Variable |
| 4 | Increased school participation | Gradual reduction of accommodations; increasing assignment completion | Variable |
| 5 | Full academic load with accommodations as needed | Near-normal participation with residual supports | Variable |
| 6 | Full return to normal academics | Complete removal of accommodations | Ongoing monitoring |

#### 13.7.2 Evidence-Based Principles

Contemporary evidence, including the Canadian Living Guideline for Pediatric Concussion Care, supports the following principles:

1. **Brief initial rest, not prolonged isolation:** A period of relative cognitive rest during the first 24-48 hours is recommended, followed by gradual reintroduction of activities. Prolonged complete rest (beyond 48 hours) may be counterproductive and has been associated with longer symptom duration.
2. **Early return to school with accommodations:** Students should return to the school environment as soon as they can tolerate light cognitive activities (30-45 minutes of reading or conversation) without more than mild symptom exacerbation.
3. **Symptom-guided progression:** Advancement through stages should be guided by symptom tolerance, with students remaining at a given stage until activities can be performed without significant symptom worsening.
4. **Over 90% of students return fully by 10 days post-concussion** without need for additional support.

#### 13.7.3 Academic Accommodations During Recovery

- Extended time on tests and assignments
- Reduced homework and assignment volume
- Breaks from cognitively demanding tasks
- Preferential seating away from distractions
- Postponement of standardized testing
- Permission to leave class if symptoms worsen
- Written notes or note-taking assistance
- Reduced screen time if symptomatic
- Alternative testing formats (oral instead of written)

**Clinical Pearl:** Return-to-learn should precede return-to-sport. Academic recovery is the priority. The Amsterdam Consensus Statement (2022) and CDC HEADS UP guidelines emphasize that students should be making meaningful progress in academic recovery before beginning the return-to-sport protocol.

**References:**
1. DeMatteo C, Stazyk K, Singh SK, et al. Development of a conservative protocol to return children and youth to activity following concussive injury. *Clin Pediatr (Phila)*. 2015;54(2):152-163.
2. Zemek R, Barrowman N, Freedman SB, et al. Clinical risk score for persistent postconcussion symptoms among children with acute concussion in the ED. *JAMA*. 2016;315(10):1014-1025.
3. Leddy JJ, Haider MN, Ellis MJ, et al. Early subthreshold aerobic exercise for sport-related concussion. *JAMA Pediatr*. 2019;173(4):319-325.

### 13.8 Pediatric Concussion Management: Differences from Adults [COMPREHENSIVE]

While the general principles of concussion management apply across age groups, several important differences exist in the pediatric population that warrant specific attention.

#### 13.8.1 Recovery Duration

Children and adolescents typically require longer recovery periods than adults. While most adults recover from sport-related concussion within 10-14 days, pediatric patients may take up to 4 weeks for symptom resolution. The Amsterdam Consensus Statement on Concussion in Sport (2022) recommends a more conservative approach in children, with longer duration at each step of the graduated return-to-play protocol.

#### 13.8.2 Key Differences in Pediatric Concussion Management

| Feature | Adults | Pediatric/Adolescent |
|---------|--------|---------------------|
| Typical recovery time | 10-14 days | Up to 4 weeks |
| Return-to-play progression | 24 hours per step | Longer at each step (2-3 days minimum per stage recommended) |
| Academic considerations | Return to work is secondary | Return-to-learn is the primary priority; precedes return-to-sport |
| Same-day return to play | Considered in professional athletes (controversial) | Never permitted in pediatric/adolescent athletes |
| Baseline testing | Recommended but not universal | Increasingly standard in organized youth sports |
| Persistent symptoms | ~10-15% at 3 months | ~15-30% at 3 months; higher persistence rate |
| Exertional testing | Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test | Modified protocols; parental supervision required |

#### 13.8.3 State Concussion Laws

All 50 states and the District of Columbia have enacted youth concussion laws following the model established by Washington State's "Zackery Lystedt Law" (2009). While specifics vary by state, these laws typically mandate three components:

1. **Education:** Coaches, parents, and athletes must receive concussion education
2. **Removal from play:** Any athlete suspected of having a concussion must be immediately removed from play
3. **Medical clearance:** A licensed healthcare provider must provide written clearance before the athlete can return to play

**Clinical Pearl:** No same-day return to play is permitted for any child or adolescent with a suspected concussion, regardless of how quickly symptoms resolve. This represents a more conservative approach than is sometimes applied in adult professional sports.

### 13.9 TBI-Related Psychiatric Disorders in Children [COMPREHENSIVE]

Pediatric TBI is associated with a substantially elevated risk of new-onset psychiatric disorders. Prospective studies using structured psychiatric interviews have demonstrated that between one-third and one-half of children who sustain TBI develop at least one new psychiatric disorder within the first two years following injury.

#### 13.9.1 Prevalence and Types

| Disorder | Estimated Prevalence Post-TBI | Key Features |
|----------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| Personality change due to TBI | 10-30% | Affective lability, apathy, aggression, disinhibition; attributed to frontal/temporal injury |
| Secondary ADHD | 15-35% (new-onset) | Inattention, impulsivity, hyperactivity developing after TBI; distinct from pre-existing ADHD |
| Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD) | 10-15% (new-onset) | Argumentativeness, defiance, anger; often co-occurs with personality change |
| PTSD | 4-13% at 6-12 months | Re-experiencing, avoidance, hyperarousal; more common in milder injuries where memory of event is preserved |
| Depressive disorders | 10-25% | Sadness, anhedonia, sleep disturbance, suicidal ideation; may emerge months to years post-injury |
| Anxiety disorders (non-PTSD) | 10-20% | Separation anxiety, generalized anxiety, social anxiety; may worsen over time |

#### 13.9.2 Secondary ADHD

TBI-related ADHD deserves special emphasis because of its frequency and clinical significance. A meta-analysis published in *JAMA Pediatrics* (2021) found that 35.5% of children with severe TBI had ADHD more than 1 year post-injury, compared to the baseline population rate of approximately 10%. Importantly, the pre-injury rate of ADHD in children who sustain TBI is already elevated (approximately 16%), suggesting both that ADHD predisposes to TBI and that TBI independently causes new-onset ADHD.

Secondary ADHD following TBI is characterized by predominantly inattentive symptoms, often without the hyperactivity seen in developmental ADHD. It may respond to stimulant medications (methylphenidate), though evidence is more limited than for developmental ADHD. Methylphenidate has been studied in pediatric TBI and shown to improve attention and processing speed, though response may be more variable than in primary ADHD.

#### 13.9.3 Personality Change Due to TBI

This DSM-5 diagnosis encompasses persistent personality alterations that develop as a direct consequence of brain injury. Subtypes include:

- **Labile type:** Emotional instability with rapid mood shifts
- **Disinhibited type:** Impulsive, socially inappropriate behavior (frontal lobe syndrome)
- **Aggressive type:** Verbal and/or physical aggression disproportionate to provocation
- **Apathetic type:** Loss of motivation, initiative, and emotional reactivity
- **Paranoid type:** Suspiciousness, distrust
- **Combined type:** Features of two or more subtypes

#### 13.9.4 PTSD in Pediatric TBI

PTSD after TBI presents a conceptual paradox: the amnesia associated with TBI theoretically should protect against the re-experiencing symptoms that define PTSD. In practice, however, PTSD symptoms are relatively common (68%) in the first weeks after pediatric TBI and persist (12%) at 2 years, particularly in milder injuries where event memory is preserved or reconstructed from external sources. Children with milder TBI and intact memory of the traumatic event are at higher risk for PTSD than those with severe TBI and complete amnesia.

#### 13.9.5 Management Principles

1. **Pharmacotherapy:** Guided by the specific psychiatric disorder; SSRIs for depression and anxiety, stimulants for secondary ADHD, mood stabilizers for affective lability
2. **Psychotherapy:** Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) adapted for cognitive impairment; family therapy to address behavioral management
3. **Behavioral interventions:** Applied behavior analysis principles, structured environments, consistent routines
4. **School-based interventions:** Behavioral support plans, social skills groups, counseling services
5. **Longitudinal monitoring:** New psychiatric disorders can emerge months to years after injury; ongoing screening is essential

**Clinical Pearl:** Pre-injury psychiatric disorders are present in one-third to one-half of children who sustain TBI. This means that the clinician must carefully distinguish pre-existing from new-onset disorders. Obtaining a thorough pre-injury psychiatric history from parents is essential for accurate diagnosis and treatment planning.

2. Li L, Liu J. The effect of pediatric traumatic brain injury on behavioral outcomes: a systematic review. *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 2013;55(1):37-45.

## Part II: Geriatric Traumatic Brain Injury

---

### 13.10 Geriatric TBI Epidemiology [BOARD]

Adults aged 75 years and older have the highest rates of TBI-related hospitalization and mortality of any age group. The incidence of geriatric TBI is rising steadily due to increased life expectancy, more active lifestyles among older adults, and the growing proportion of the population over age 65.

#### 13.10.1 Falls as the Leading Mechanism

Falls are the overwhelmingly dominant mechanism of TBI in the elderly, accounting for more than 80% of TBI in adults aged 75 and older. Unlike the high-velocity mechanisms (motor vehicle crashes, assaults) that characterize TBI in younger adults, geriatric TBI frequently results from ground-level falls — seemingly minor mechanisms that can produce significant intracranial pathology due to age-related changes in brain anatomy.

Motor vehicle crashes are the second leading cause of geriatric TBI, often involving low-speed collisions. Pedestrian injuries also contribute, as age-related declines in mobility, vision, hearing, and reaction time increase vulnerability.

#### 13.10.2 Why Even "Minor" Falls Are Dangerous

Several age-related anatomic and physiologic changes explain why elderly patients are uniquely susceptible to significant TBI from minimal trauma:

1. **Brain atrophy:** Age-related cerebral volume loss creates a larger potential subdural space and stretches bridging veins across the increased distance between the brain surface and the dura mater. These stretched veins are more vulnerable to tearing even from minor acceleration-deceleration forces.

2. **Subdural hematoma predominance:** As a result of bridging vein vulnerability, subdural hematoma (SDH) is the most common traumatic intracranial lesion in the elderly. Chronic SDH can develop insidiously over weeks, presenting with confusion, gait instability, and personality changes that may be mistaken for dementia or normal pressure hydrocephalus.

3. **Anticoagulant and antiplatelet use:** A substantial proportion of elderly patients take warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), aspirin, or clopidogrel, all of which increase the risk and severity of intracranial hemorrhage following even minor trauma.

4. **Decreased physiologic reserve:** Reduced cardiovascular, pulmonary, and immune reserve limits the elderly patient's capacity to compensate for the secondary physiologic insults (hypotension, hypoxia, infection) that compound primary brain injury.

5. **Comorbidities:** Hypertension, diabetes mellitus, coronary artery disease, cerebrovascular disease, and chronic kidney disease are common comorbidities that worsen TBI outcomes.

**Clinical Pearl:** Any elderly patient on anticoagulation who sustains head trauma — even without loss of consciousness — should undergo CT imaging. Delayed hemorrhage can develop up to 24-72 hours after injury. Many institutions recommend repeat CT at 24 hours for anticoagulated patients with initially negative scans.

### 13.11 Anticoagulation Management in Geriatric TBI [BOARD]

The management of anticoagulation in the setting of acute geriatric TBI is a time-critical clinical challenge with direct implications for morbidity and mortality. Rapid identification of the anticoagulant agent and administration of the appropriate reversal agent can be lifesaving.

#### 13.11.1 Reversal Agents

| Anticoagulant | Class | Reversal Agent | Dose | Onset | Key Considerations |
|--------------|-------|---------------|------|-------|-------------------|
| Warfarin | Vitamin K antagonist | 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) + IV Vitamin K | PCC: 25-50 units/kg (INR-based); Vitamin K: 10 mg IV over 10-20 min | PCC: minutes; Vit K: 6-24 hours | PCC is preferred over FFP (faster onset, no thaw time, smaller volume). Vitamin K provides sustained reversal (PCC effect lasts 12-24 hours). Check INR at 30 minutes and 6 hours. |
| Dabigatran | Direct thrombin inhibitor | Idarucizumab (Praxbind) | 5 g total: two 2.5 g IV infusions | Minutes (immediate) | Specific monoclonal antibody fragment. Complete reversal in >98% of patients. FDA-approved 2015. Single-use; can re-dose if needed. |
| Rivaroxaban, Apixaban | Factor Xa inhibitors | Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) or 4-factor PCC | Andexanet: Low dose (400 mg bolus + 4 mg/min x 120 min) or High dose (800 mg bolus + 8 mg/min x 120 min) based on last dose timing and DOAC dose. PCC: 50 units/kg IV. | Minutes | Andexanet is FDA-approved specific reversal for rivaroxaban/apixaban. Very expensive (~$25,000-50,000). Thrombotic event rate ~10%. 4-factor PCC is a practical, more widely available alternative. |
| Edoxaban | Factor Xa inhibitor | 4-factor PCC (preferred) | PCC: 50 units/kg IV | Minutes | Andexanet is NOT FDA-approved for edoxaban (off-label). PCC is the practical choice. |
| Aspirin | COX inhibitor | Platelet transfusion (controversial); DDAVP | Platelet unit; DDAVP 0.3 mcg/kg IV | 30-60 min | PATCH trial (2016) showed platelet transfusion may worsen outcomes in aspirin-associated ICH. Decision should be individualized. |
| Clopidogrel | P2Y12 inhibitor | Platelet transfusion (controversial); DDAVP | Same as aspirin | 30-60 min | Same controversy as aspirin. Irreversible P2Y12 inhibition; new platelets needed. Platelet function testing (VerifyNow) can assess residual effect. |

#### 13.11.2 Timing Considerations

- **"Time is brain"** applies to anticoagulation reversal as it does to stroke. Every minute of delay in reversal increases the risk of hematoma expansion.
- **4-factor PCC is preferred over FFP** for warfarin reversal because PCC provides immediate reversal (minutes vs. hours for FFP), requires no thaw time, uses smaller volume (avoiding fluid overload), and achieves more complete INR correction.
- **Vitamin K must always be co-administered** with PCC for warfarin reversal, because PCC provides only temporary reversal (12-24 hours) while Vitamin K provides sustained reversal by enabling hepatic synthesis of clotting factors.
- **Activated charcoal** may be considered if DOAC ingestion occurred within 2-4 hours.

**Clinical Pearl:** Know the specific reversal agent for each anticoagulant. Idarucizumab reverses dabigatran (a direct thrombin inhibitor). Andexanet alfa or 4-factor PCC reverses the factor Xa inhibitors (rivaroxaban, apixaban). Warfarin requires both PCC and Vitamin K. The PATCH trial (Baharoglu et al., *Lancet*, 2016) has made platelet transfusion for aspirin/clopidogrel-associated bleeding controversial.

### 13.12 Life Expectancy Reduction After TBI [BOARD]

TBI in the elderly is associated with a significant reduction in life expectancy, estimated at approximately 6-9 years compared to age-matched controls without TBI. This statistic is a commonly tested board fact.

#### 13.12.1 Mortality Data

- **In-hospital mortality** is 2-3 times higher in elderly patients compared to younger adults with comparable injury severity.
- **Age is an independent predictor** of mortality and poor outcome after TBI, along with GCS score and pupillary reactivity (the three core IMPACT variables).
- **30-day mortality** for moderate-severe TBI in patients over 75 exceeds 50% in some series.
- **Long-term mortality** remains elevated even years after injury: the TBIMS database shows 1.5-2.9 times higher mortality than the general population across all follow-up periods.

#### 13.12.2 Mechanisms of Reduced Life Expectancy

The mechanisms underlying reduced life expectancy after geriatric TBI are multifactorial:

1. **Increased susceptibility to complications:** Pneumonia, urinary tract infections, sepsis, venous thromboembolism, and pressure injuries
2. **Accelerated cognitive decline:** TBI increases the risk of subsequent dementia by 1.5-4 times, and cognitive impairment accelerates functional decline
3. **Seizure risk:** Post-traumatic epilepsy increases mortality risk
4. **Falls and recurrent injury:** Prior TBI increases subsequent fall risk by 2-3 times
5. **Depression and social isolation:** Untreated depression (25-50% prevalence) contributes to functional decline, poor nutrition, and reduced physical activity
6. **Loss of independence:** Increased rates of institutionalization and decreased community participation

**Clinical Pearl:** When counseling families about prognosis after geriatric TBI, the 6-9 year life expectancy reduction statistic provides important contextual information for goals-of-care discussions and advance directive planning. This reduction applies even to patients who make a reasonable functional recovery.

**References:**
1. Harrison-Felix C, Pretz C, Hammond FM, et al. Life expectancy after inpatient rehabilitation for traumatic brain injury in the United States. *J Neurotrauma*. 2015;32(23):1893-1901.
2. Brooks JC, Strauss DJ, Shavelle RM, Paculdo DR, Hammond FM, Harrison-Felix CL. Long-term disability and survival in traumatic brain injury: results from the National Institute on Disability and Rehabilitation Research Model Systems. *Arch Phys Med Rehabil*. 2013;94(11):2203-2209.

### 13.13 Polypharmacy and Beers Criteria Medications [COMPREHENSIVE]

Polypharmacy (defined as the concurrent use of five or more medications) is present in more than 50% of elderly TBI patients and represents a significant risk factor for adverse drug events, falls, cognitive impairment, and drug-drug interactions. The American Geriatrics Society (AGS) Beers Criteria provides an evidence-based list of potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) in adults aged 65 and older, with particular relevance to the post-TBI population.

#### 13.13.1 Anticholinergic Burden

Anticholinergic medications are the highest priority to avoid in elderly TBI patients because they directly impair cognition, promote delirium, and increase fall risk — all of which compound the existing deficits from TBI.

| Medication Class | Examples | Anticholinergic Burden Score (ACB) | Specific Concerns in TBI |
|-----------------|---------|-----------------------------------|------------------------|
| First-generation antihistamines | Diphenhydramine (Benadryl), hydroxyzine, chlorpheniramine | ACB 3 (definite) | Sedation, confusion, delirium; worsens cognitive impairment |
| Bladder antispasmodics | Oxybutynin, tolterodine | ACB 3 (definite) | Cognitive worsening; prefer mirabegron (beta-3 agonist, no anticholinergic effect) |
| Tricyclic antidepressants | Amitriptyline, nortriptyline | ACB 3 (definite) | Strong anticholinergic effects; sedation; cardiac conduction effects; avoid for depression (use SSRIs) |
| First-generation antipsychotics | Chlorpromazine, thioridazine | ACB 3 (definite) | Anticholinergic + extrapyramidal effects; worsen confusion |
| Skeletal muscle relaxants | Cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol | ACB 2-3 | Sedation, falls, minimal evidence for efficacy in elderly |
| Antiparkinsonians | Benztropine, trihexyphenidyl | ACB 3 (definite) | Strong anticholinergic; sedation; confusion |
| Certain SSRIs | Paroxetine | ACB 3 (definite) | Most anticholinergic of the SSRIs; prefer sertraline, citalopram, or escitalopram |

**Anticholinergic Burden Score (ACB):** The cumulative ACB is calculated by summing the anticholinergic potency scores (1-3) for each medication. A cumulative ACB score of 3 or greater is associated with significantly increased risk of delirium, cognitive decline, falls, hospitalization, and mortality.

#### 13.13.2 Other Medications to Avoid or Use with Caution

- **Benzodiazepines:** Increased falls (OR 1.4), paradoxical agitation, respiratory depression, prolonged half-life in elderly, cognitive worsening. Avoid in elderly TBI patients.
- **Opioids:** Respiratory depression, constipation, delirium, falls. If analgesics are needed, start with low doses of short-acting agents and use multimodal pain management.
- **NSAIDs:** GI bleeding risk (especially with concurrent anticoagulants), renal impairment, cardiovascular risk. Prefer acetaminophen for pain management.
- **Sliding-scale insulin:** Hypoglycemia risk is particularly dangerous in TBI patients with impaired awareness.
- **Alpha-blockers (for BPH):** Orthostatic hypotension increases fall risk.

**Clinical Pearl:** Every medication in an elderly TBI patient should be evaluated through the lens of fall risk, anticholinergic burden, and cognitive side effects. A thorough medication reconciliation with active deprescribing should be performed at every clinical encounter, with the goal of minimizing polypharmacy and eliminating medications on the Beers Criteria list.

**References:**
1. American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria Update Expert Panel. American Geriatrics Society 2023 updated AGS Beers Criteria for potentially inappropriate medication use in older adults. *J Am Geriatr Soc*. 2023;71(7):2052-2081.
2. Campbell N, Boustani M, Limbil T, et al. The cognitive impact of anticholinergics: a clinical review. *Clin Interv Aging*. 2009;4:225-233.

### 13.14 Falls Prevention [COMPREHENSIVE]

Elderly TBI survivors are at high risk for recurrent falls, with prior TBI increasing subsequent fall risk by 2-3 times due to residual balance deficits, cognitive impairment, gait disorders, visual impairment, and polypharmacy. Falls prevention is therefore a critical component of geriatric TBI management.

#### 13.14.1 Otago Exercise Program

The Otago Exercise Program is an evidence-based, individually prescribed home exercise program delivered by a physical therapist, designed to prevent falls in community-dwelling older adults.

**Components:**
- 17 strength and balance exercises (progressive difficulty)
- Walking program (graduated duration and frequency)
- Delivered by PT in the home setting
- 4 PT visits over the initial 8 weeks, then independent self-management
- Performed 3 times per week independently

**Evidence:** The Otago program has been shown to reduce falls by 35-40% in community-dwelling older adults in randomized controlled trials. It has been validated in frail elderly populations and stroke survivors and is applicable to TBI survivors with balance deficits.

#### 13.14.2 CDC STEADI (Stopping Elderly Accidents, Deaths, and Injuries)

STEADI is a comprehensive falls prevention framework developed by the CDC that provides a systematic clinical approach:

1. **Screen:** Ask about falls in the past year, fear of falling, unsteadiness
2. **Assess:** Multifactorial falls risk assessment including:
   - Vision screening
   - Orthostatic vital signs
   - Feet and footwear evaluation
   - Medication review (Beers Criteria screening)
   - Cognitive screening
   - Gait and balance testing (Timed Up and Go, 30-second chair stand, 4-stage balance test)
3. **Intervene:** Address all modifiable risk factors simultaneously

#### 13.14.3 Home Modification

An occupational therapy home safety assessment is the gold standard for individualized home modification recommendations. Key modifications include:

- Remove throw rugs, clutter, and cords from walkways
- Install grab bars next to toilet and in shower/tub
- Improve lighting throughout the home, especially nightlights along the path to the bathroom
- Non-slip mats in bathroom and kitchen
- Handrails on all stairways (both sides)
- Raised toilet seat if needed
- Shower chair or transfer bench
- Bed rail or assistive device for safe bed mobility

#### 13.14.4 Additional Interventions

- **Vitamin D supplementation:** 800-1000 IU/day if deficient; reduces fall risk and fracture risk
- **Assistive devices:** Cane, walker, or rollator as appropriate for balance and gait deficits
- **Vision correction:** Updated eyeglasses; cataract surgery if indicated; avoid bifocals or progressive lenses on stairs
- **Medication review and deprescribing:** Eliminate or reduce anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids, and other medications that increase fall risk
- **Tai chi:** Evidence-based balance training; reduces fall risk by approximately 20-40%

**Clinical Pearl:** Falls prevention after geriatric TBI requires a multifactorial approach. No single intervention is sufficient. The combination of exercise (Otago or similar), home modification, medication review with deprescribing, vision correction, vitamin D supplementation, and assistive devices provides the best reduction in fall risk.

**References:**
1. Campbell AJ, Robertson MC. Otago Exercise Programme to prevent falls in older adults. *NZ Ministry of Health*. 2003.
2. Stevens JA, Burns ER. A CDC compendium of effective fall interventions: what works for community-dwelling older adults. 3rd ed. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention; 2015.

### 13.15 "Start Low, Go Slow" Medication Management [COMPREHENSIVE]

The principle of "start low, go slow" is the guiding framework for all pharmacotherapy in geriatric TBI patients. This conservative prescribing approach reflects the unique pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic vulnerabilities of the elderly population.

#### 13.15.1 Pharmacokinetic Changes with Aging

| Parameter | Age-Related Change | Clinical Implication |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Absorption | Generally preserved | Minimal impact on most medications |
| Distribution | Increased body fat, decreased lean mass, decreased total body water | Increased Vd for lipophilic drugs (benzodiazepines); decreased Vd for hydrophilic drugs |
| Hepatic metabolism | Reduced CYP450 activity (Phase I), preserved Phase II | Slower metabolism of many drugs; increased half-life and accumulation |
| Renal clearance | Decreased GFR (approximately 1 mL/min/year after age 40) | Slower elimination of renally cleared drugs; dose adjustment required |
| Protein binding | Decreased albumin | Increased free fraction of highly protein-bound drugs |
| Blood-brain barrier | Increased permeability | Greater CNS sensitivity to medications |

#### 13.15.2 Pharmacodynamic Changes

Elderly patients demonstrate increased sensitivity to CNS-active medications, including enhanced sedative effects, greater susceptibility to delirium, increased fall risk from psychotropic medications, and exaggerated responses to anticholinergic agents.

#### 13.15.3 Practical Prescribing Principles

1. **Start at 25-50% of the usual adult dose** for most CNS-active medications
2. **Titrate slowly** with extended intervals between dose increases
3. **Monitor closely** for adverse effects at each dose change
4. **Use the simplest regimen possible** to minimize polypharmacy
5. **Regularly reassess** the need for each medication ("deprescribing")
6. **Choose medications with fewer drug interactions** and shorter half-lives when possible

#### 13.15.4 Specific Medication Considerations in Geriatric TBI

| Medication | Starting Dose Adjustment | Key Precautions |
|-----------|-------------------------|-----------------|
| Sertraline (SSRI for depression) | Start 25 mg daily (adult: 50 mg) | Monitor for hyponatremia (SIADH risk); QTc monitoring |
| Methylphenidate (for attention) | Start 2.5-5 mg AM (adult: 5-10 mg) | Monitor blood pressure, heart rate; insomnia risk |
| Baclofen (spasticity) | Start 5 mg daily or BID (adult: 5 mg TID) | Higher risk of sedation and cognitive impairment |
| Levetiracetam (seizure prophylaxis) | Dose adjust for renal function | Behavioral side effects (irritability, depression) |
| Trazodone (sleep) | Start 12.5-25 mg (adult: 50-100 mg) | Orthostatic hypotension; priapism (rare) |
| Amantadine (arousal) | Start 50 mg daily (adult: 100 mg BID) | Renal dose adjustment critical; confusion, hallucinations |

**Clinical Pearl:** The combination of age-related pharmacokinetic changes and TBI-related blood-brain barrier disruption creates a "double vulnerability" in elderly TBI patients. Medications that would be tolerated in younger TBI patients or in non-injured elderly patients may produce disproportionate adverse effects in the geriatric TBI population.

### 13.16 Age as Independent Risk Factor for Mortality [COMPREHENSIVE]

Age is one of the three strongest independent predictors of outcome after TBI, alongside GCS motor score and pupillary reactivity. These three variables form the "core model" of the IMPACT (International Mission for Prognosis and Analysis of Clinical Trials) prognostic calculator.

#### 13.16.1 Evidence for Age-Related Outcomes

- Each decade of life beyond age 40 is associated with progressively worse outcomes after TBI.
- In-hospital mortality for moderate-severe TBI approaches 50-70% in patients over 75 years.
- The relationship between age and outcome is continuous, not a step function: there is no single "threshold" age, but risk increases steadily with advancing age.
- Even controlling for injury severity, comorbidities, and treatment variables, age remains an independently significant predictor of mortality and functional outcome.

#### 13.16.2 Mechanisms

Age-related vulnerability to TBI is explained by a convergence of factors:

1. **Reduced neural plasticity:** Diminished capacity for synaptic reorganization and neurogenesis
2. **Chronic neuroinflammation:** Aging is associated with a baseline pro-inflammatory state ("inflammaging") that primes the brain for an exaggerated inflammatory response to injury
3. **Reduced cerebrovascular reserve:** Impaired autoregulation and reduced capacity to compensate for hemodynamic perturbations
4. **Preexisting neurodegeneration:** Subclinical Alzheimer's pathology, cerebral small vessel disease, and white matter disease reduce the brain's functional reserve
5. **Systemic comorbidities:** Cardiovascular, pulmonary, renal, and immunologic comorbidities limit the body's capacity to support brain recovery

**Clinical Pearl:** Age-related mortality risk in TBI must be contextualized carefully in goals-of-care discussions. While age is a strong negative prognostic factor, it should not be used as the sole determinant of treatment decisions. Individual factors including premorbid functional status, advance directives, and family preferences should guide care planning.

### 13.17 Elder Abuse Screening in the TBI Context [COMPREHENSIVE]

Elder abuse is an underrecognized cause of TBI in the geriatric population, with an estimated 1 in 24 cases of elder abuse identified and reported. Clinicians evaluating elderly patients with TBI should maintain a high index of suspicion for abuse, particularly when the mechanism of injury is unclear or the history is inconsistent.

#### 13.17.1 Types of Elder Abuse

1. **Physical abuse:** Intentional infliction of pain or injury, including hitting, pushing, or shaking
2. **Neglect:** Failure to provide necessary care, including medication, nutrition, hygiene, and safety
3. **Psychological/emotional abuse:** Verbal threats, intimidation, humiliation, or social isolation
4. **Financial exploitation:** Unauthorized use of an elder's funds, property, or assets
5. **Sexual abuse:** Non-consensual sexual contact
6. **Self-neglect:** Failure to provide for one's own basic needs (not technically "abuse" but a related concern)

#### 13.17.2 Screening Tools

- **Elder Assessment Instrument (EAI):** 41-item instrument that reviews signs, symptoms, and complaints of elder abuse, neglect, exploitation, and abandonment. Can be completed in approximately 12-15 minutes.
- **Elder Abuse Suspicion Index (EASI):** Brief 6-question screening tool designed for use in clinical settings.
- **ED Senior AID (Abuse Identification):** Developed specifically for emergency department use; includes mental status assessment, targeted questions, and physical examination. Demonstrated sensitivity of 94.1% and specificity of 84.3% in validation studies.

#### 13.17.3 Red Flags for Elder Abuse in TBI Presentation

- Injury pattern inconsistent with stated mechanism
- Delay in seeking medical care
- Caregiver providing conflicting history or preventing the patient from speaking privately
- Patient expresses fear of caregiver
- Evidence of poor hygiene, malnutrition, or untreated medical conditions
- Multiple ED visits for falls or injuries
- Bruising in unusual locations (face, neck, inner arms, inner thighs)
- Subtherapeutic medication levels despite reported compliance

#### 13.17.4 Reporting Requirements

Like child abuse, elder abuse is subject to mandatory reporting requirements in most US jurisdictions, though the specific reporting obligations vary by state. Healthcare providers should be familiar with their state's reporting requirements and the process for contacting Adult Protective Services (APS).

**Clinical Pearl:** The cognitive impairments associated with TBI and dementia make elderly patients particularly vulnerable to abuse and exploitation. Conversely, cognitive impairment may prevent the patient from reporting or even recognizing abuse. Private patient interviews (without the caregiver present) are essential whenever abuse is suspected.

**References:**

2. Dong XQ. Elder abuse: systematic review and implications for practice. *J Am Geriatr Soc*. 2015;63(6):1214-1238.

### 13.18 ApoE4 Allele and TBI Outcomes [COMPREHENSIVE]

Apolipoprotein E4 (ApoE4) is the most well-established genetic risk factor for poor outcomes after TBI and the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease. Understanding the role of ApoE4 in TBI outcomes is relevant for both board preparation and prognostic counseling.

#### 13.18.1 Genetics and Prevalence

- The APOE gene exists in three common alleles: E2 (protective), E3 (most common, neutral), and E4 (deleterious).
- Approximately 15-25% of the general population carries at least one copy of the E4 allele.
- Heterozygous carriers (one E4 allele) have a 3-fold increased risk of Alzheimer's disease.
- Homozygous carriers (two E4 alleles) have a 12-15-fold increased risk.

#### 13.18.2 ApoE4 and TBI Outcomes

Patients who carry the ApoE4 allele demonstrate worse outcomes after TBI across multiple domains:

- Longer duration of coma
- Prolonged post-traumatic amnesia
- Slower cognitive recovery trajectories
- Higher mortality rates
- Increased risk of post-traumatic Alzheimer's disease
- Increased risk of chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) with repetitive head impacts

#### 13.18.3 Mechanisms

ApoE4 adversely affects TBI outcomes through several mechanisms:

1. **Impaired amyloid-beta clearance:** ApoE4 is less effective than E2 or E3 at clearing amyloid-beta aggregates, which accumulate rapidly after TBI
2. **Reduced neuronal repair capacity:** ApoE4 is associated with less effective synapse repair and axonal regeneration
3. **Exaggerated neuroinflammation:** ApoE4 promotes a more robust and sustained inflammatory response to brain injury
4. **Vascular dysfunction:** ApoE4 impairs blood-brain barrier integrity and cerebrovascular function

#### 13.18.4 Clinical Implications

Currently, genetic testing for ApoE4 is not recommended for routine clinical management of TBI patients. There are no established ApoE4-specific treatment modifications, and the test result may have unintended psychosocial consequences (insurance implications, anxiety about dementia risk). However, awareness of ApoE4 as a prognostic modifier is important for understanding outcome variability in TBI patients.

**Clinical Pearl:** ApoE4 is the most commonly tested genetic factor in TBI on PM&R board examinations. The key facts to remember are: (1) ApoE4 carriers have worse TBI outcomes, (2) ApoE4 increases Alzheimer's disease risk (3x heterozygous, 12-15x homozygous), and (3) clinical genotyping is not currently recommended for routine TBI management.

**References:**
1. Zhou W, Xu D, Peng X, Zhang Q, Jia J, Crutcher KA. Meta-analysis of APOE4 allele and outcome after traumatic brain injury. *J Neurotrauma*. 2008;25(4):279-290.
2. Lawrence DW, Comper P, Bhalerao S, Bhalerao S. The role of apolipoprotein E esilon 4 (ApoE) allele on outcome following traumatic brain injury: a systematic review. *Brain Inj*. 2015;29(9):1018-1031.
