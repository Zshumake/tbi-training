## SECTION 11.1 [BOARD] POSTTRAUMATIC HYPOPITUITARISM: OVERVIEW

### 11.1.1 Prevalence and Clinical Significance

Neuroendocrine dysfunction is one of the most commonly underdiagnosed complications of traumatic brain injury. Over the past two decades, a substantial body of evidence has emerged demonstrating that posttraumatic hypopituitarism (PTHP) is far more prevalent than historically appreciated. Meta-analytic data indicate that approximately 27.5% of TBI patients develop some degree of hypopituitarism, encompassing the full spectrum from isolated single-axis deficiency to panhypopituitarism (Schneider et al., 2007).

Key epidemiologic points include:

- PTHP can occur after mild, moderate, or severe TBI; injury severity increases risk but does not exclude mild TBI patients from developing hormonal deficiencies.
- Growth hormone (GH) deficiency is the most common permanent anterior pituitary deficit, affecting 10-20% of moderate-to-severe TBI patients.
- Gonadotropin deficiency (hypogonadism) is the second most common persistent deficit, affecting 10-15%.
- ACTH deficiency (secondary adrenal insufficiency) occurs in 5-10% and represents the most dangerous deficit to miss.
- TSH deficiency (central hypothyroidism) occurs in 5-10%.
- Posterior pituitary dysfunction (diabetes insipidus) occurs acutely in up to 25% of severe TBI but persists in only 2-5%.
- Early hormonal abnormalities (within the first 3 months) may be transient and resolve spontaneously; deficiencies persisting beyond 12 months are more likely permanent.

**Clinical Pearl:** The clinical significance of PTHP extends beyond classic endocrine symptoms. Fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, mood disturbance, reduced quality of life, decreased lean body mass, and increased visceral adiposity -- symptoms commonly attributed to the TBI itself -- may actually be manifestations of unrecognized hormonal deficiencies. Adequate screening and replacement can substantially improve recovery trajectories.

### 11.1.2 Anatomic Vulnerability of the Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

The hypothalamic-pituitary axis is uniquely vulnerable to traumatic injury for several anatomic and vascular reasons:

1. **Sella turcica encasement:** The pituitary gland sits in the sella turcica, a bony cavity at the skull base enclosed by rigid bone on three sides. While this provides physical protection, it also creates a confined space where edema and hemorrhage can produce compressive damage.

2. **Pituitary stalk vulnerability:** The pituitary stalk (infundibulum) traverses the suprasellar cistern, connecting the hypothalamus to the pituitary gland. This delicate structure is susceptible to shearing forces during acceleration-deceleration injury, the same biomechanical forces that produce diffuse axonal injury.

3. **Hypophyseal portal system:** The anterior pituitary receives its blood supply indirectly through the hypophyseal portal vessels -- long portal veins that descend from the hypothalamus along the pituitary stalk. This indirect vascular supply is vulnerable to disruption during trauma, leading to anterior pituitary ischemia and infarction.

4. **Lateral wing vulnerability:** The GH-secreting somatotroph cells are concentrated in the lateral wings of the anterior pituitary, which have the most tenuous blood supply via the long portal vessels. This anatomic distribution explains why GH deficiency is the most common persistent deficit.

5. **Basilar skull fracture association:** Fractures through the skull base are particularly associated with pituitary injury and diabetes insipidus, as the fracture line may directly damage the pituitary stalk, posterior pituitary, or hypothalamus.

6. **Posterior vs. anterior pituitary blood supply:** The posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis) receives a direct arterial supply from the inferior hypophyseal artery, making it somewhat more resistant to ischemic injury than the anterior pituitary, though direct traumatic damage can still occur.

**Clinical Pearl:** The anterior pituitary's dependence on portal venous blood supply (an indirect supply) makes it vulnerable to ischemia during episodes of hypotension, elevated intracranial pressure, or direct vascular disruption during trauma. The posterior pituitary's direct arterial supply provides somewhat greater protection, though it remains vulnerable to direct mechanical damage.

## SECTION 11.2 [BOARD] SODIUM DISORDERS AFTER TBI

Sodium disturbances are among the most common and dangerous neuroendocrine complications after TBI. The three major disorders -- SIADH, cerebral salt wasting (CSW), and diabetes insipidus (DI) -- require rapid recognition and precise treatment. The distinction between SIADH and CSW is one of the highest-yield board topics in PM&R because the treatments are opposite, and misdiagnosis can lead to devastating neurologic consequences.

### 11.2.1 SIADH: Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone Secretion

**Pathophysiology:** Inappropriate secretion of ADH (vasopressin) from the posterior pituitary or ectopic sources causes excessive free water reabsorption in the renal collecting ducts. This leads to water retention, expansion of extracellular fluid volume (but not to the point of clinically apparent edema due to compensatory natriuresis), and dilutional hyponatremia. SIADH is the most common cause of hyponatremia after TBI.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Serum sodium | LOW (<135 mEq/L) |
| Serum osmolality | LOW (<280 mOsm/kg) |
| Urine osmolality | HIGH (>100 mOsm/kg) -- inappropriately concentrated |
| Urine sodium | HIGH (>40 mEq/L) |
| Volume status | EUVOLEMIC |
| Urine output | Decreased (oliguric) |
| Additional criteria | Normal thyroid and adrenal function; no diuretic use; no renal, cardiac, or hepatic disease |

**Treatment:**
- **First-line:** Fluid restriction (1.0-1.2 L/day)
- **Second-line:** Demeclocycline (inhibits ADH action at the collecting duct by decreasing aquaporin-2 expression)
- **Vasopressin receptor antagonists (vaptans):** Conivaptan (IV), tolvaptan (oral) -- block V2 receptors in the collecting duct
- **Salt tablets:** Oral sodium supplementation
- **3% hypertonic saline:** Reserved for severe symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures, obtundation) -- bolus 100-150 mL over 10-20 minutes to raise Na by 4-6 mEq/L acutely

**Sodium Correction Rate:** NEVER exceed 10 mEq/L per 24 hours (some guidelines recommend a maximum of 8 mEq/L per 24 hours for chronic hyponatremia). Overcorrection risks osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS).

### 11.2.2 Cerebral Salt Wasting (CSW)

**Pathophysiology:** Excessive renal sodium excretion, possibly mediated by brain natriuretic peptide (BNP) and atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) released from the injured brain, causes obligate water loss along with the excreted sodium. This results in hypovolemic hyponatremia. CSW is unique to central nervous system injury and does not occur without brain pathology. It typically occurs within the first 1-2 weeks after TBI or subarachnoid hemorrhage.

**Diagnostic Features:**

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Serum sodium | LOW (<135 mEq/L) -- SAME as SIADH |
| Serum osmolality | LOW (<280 mOsm/kg) -- SAME as SIADH |
| Urine osmolality | HIGH (concentrated) -- SAME as SIADH |
| Urine sodium | HIGH (>40 mEq/L) -- SAME as SIADH |
| Volume status | HYPOVOLEMIC -- KEY DIFFERENCE from SIADH |
| Fluid balance | Negative |
| Body weight | Decreasing |

**Clinical Clues for Hypovolemia:**
- Dry mucous membranes
- Decreased skin turgor
- Tachycardia
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Low central venous pressure (CVP)
- Negative fluid balance
- Elevated BUN/creatinine ratio
- Elevated hematocrit (hemoconcentration)
- Weight loss

**Treatment:**
- **GIVE FLUIDS** -- normal saline or hypertonic saline to replace BOTH volume AND sodium. This is the OPPOSITE of SIADH treatment.
- **Fludrocortisone** (0.1-0.2 mg daily): mineralocorticoid that promotes renal sodium retention
- **Salt tablets:** Oral sodium supplementation

### 11.2.3 [BOARD] SIADH vs. CSW: The Critical Distinction

This is the single most important neuroendocrine board topic for TBI. The laboratory findings in SIADH and CSW are virtually identical. The ONLY reliable way to differentiate them is through careful volume status assessment.

| Feature | SIADH | CSW |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Serum Na | LOW | LOW |
| Serum Osm | LOW | LOW |
| Urine Osm | HIGH (concentrated) | HIGH (concentrated) |
| Urine Na | HIGH (>40 mEq/L) | HIGH (>40 mEq/L) |
| Urine Output | Decreased | Variable/Increased |
| **Volume Status** | **EUVOLEMIC** | **HYPOVOLEMIC** |
| Serum ADH | Elevated | Low/Normal |
| Weight | Stable/Increased | Decreased |
| Hematocrit | Low (hemodiluted) | High (hemoconcentrated) |
| CVP | Normal/High | LOW |
| BUN/Cr Ratio | Normal | Elevated |
| Fluid Balance | Neutral/Positive | NEGATIVE |
| **Treatment** | **RESTRICT fluids** | **GIVE fluids** |
| Unique to CNS injury? | No (many causes) | YES (only occurs with brain pathology) |

**Clinical Pearl:** Getting the diagnosis wrong is dangerous in both directions. Fluid restricting a CSW patient worsens hypovolemia and can cause cerebral ischemia. Giving fluids to a SIADH patient worsens dilutional hyponatremia. The mnemonic "SIADH Sits, CSW Shrinks" captures the volume status difference: SIADH patients are euvolemic (they "sit" at normal volume), while CSW patients are hypovolemic (they "shrink" from volume loss).

### 11.2.4 Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS)

Overcorrection of hyponatremia carries the risk of osmotic demyelination syndrome (formerly called central pontine myelinolysis, CPM). This devastating complication results from rapid osmotic shifts that damage myelin in the pons and extrapontine structures.

**Key Facts:**
- Caused by Na correction exceeding 10 mEq/L in 24 hours (higher risk with chronic hyponatremia)
- Symptoms appear 2-6 days after overcorrection: dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis, locked-in syndrome, coma
- Higher risk patients: chronic hyponatremia (>48 hours duration), alcoholism, malnutrition, hypokalemia, liver disease
- If overcorrecting: administer D5W (free water) and/or DDAVP to "re-lower" sodium back to a safe correction rate
- **Board Pearl:** The number to memorize is 10 mEq/L per 24 hours as the maximum safe correction rate. Slower correction is always safer.

## SECTION 11.2A [BOARD] ADDITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS IN SODIUM DISORDER MANAGEMENT

### 11.2A.1 Acute Symptomatic Hyponatremia

Severe symptomatic hyponatremia (serum Na typically <120 mEq/L) can present with life-threatening neurologic complications including seizures, obtundation, coma, and brain herniation due to cerebral edema. In TBI patients, severe hyponatremia is particularly dangerous because it can exacerbate existing cerebral edema, increase intracranial pressure, and provoke secondary brain injury.

**Emergency Management Protocol:**
1. Administer 3% hypertonic saline 100-150 mL IV bolus over 10-20 minutes
2. May repeat bolus once or twice at 10-minute intervals if symptoms persist
3. Goal: raise serum Na by 4-6 mEq/L in the first 1-2 hours to reverse acute neurologic deterioration
4. After acute stabilization, slow the correction to <=10 mEq/L total in the first 24 hours
5. Frequent serum Na monitoring (every 2-4 hours during active treatment)

**Practical Formula for 3% Saline:**
- 3% NaCl contains 513 mEq/L sodium
- Expected Na rise from 100 mL bolus approximately 1-2 mEq/L (varies by patient weight and total body water)
- This is an estimate; actual response must be monitored with serial labs

### 11.2A.2 Chronic vs. Acute Hyponatremia: Risk Stratification

The risk of ODS from correction is significantly higher with chronic hyponatremia (>48 hours) than acute hyponatremia (<48 hours):

| Feature | Acute Hyponatremia (<48 hours) | Chronic Hyponatremia (>48 hours) |
|---------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Brain adaptation | Incomplete; brain cells have not equilibrated | Complete; brain cells have adapted by expelling osmolytes |
| Primary risk | Brain edema and herniation from the hyponatremia itself | ODS from overcorrection of the hyponatremia |
| Correction approach | Can correct more rapidly if needed (still aim for <=10/24h but may tolerate slightly faster correction) | Must correct slowly (<=8-10 mEq/L per 24 hours, some experts recommend <=6 mEq/L in highest-risk patients) |
| ODS risk with rapid correction | Lower | Higher |

**Clinical Pearl:** In TBI patients, the timing of hyponatremia onset is often difficult to determine precisely because sodium may not have been checked recently. When uncertain, treat as chronic hyponatremia (>48 hours) and correct slowly. Overcorrection can be partially reversed by administering D5W and/or DDAVP.

### 11.2A.3 The Role of Brain Natriuretic Peptide (BNP) in CSW

The pathogenesis of cerebral salt wasting involves the release of natriuretic peptides from the injured brain, which promote renal sodium excretion:

- **BNP (Brain Natriuretic Peptide):** Released from injured brain tissue (and from cardiac tissue in response to volume expansion); promotes renal sodium excretion by inhibiting sodium reabsorption in the collecting duct and suppressing the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system
- **ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide):** May also be elevated in CSW, contributing to natriuresis
- **CNP (C-type Natriuretic Peptide):** Less studied but may play a role
- Elevated BNP levels in TBI patients with hyponatremia suggest CSW rather than SIADH, though BNP alone is not diagnostic (it can also be elevated in cardiac disease and SIADH)

Some investigators have proposed that BNP levels may help differentiate SIADH from CSW when volume status assessment is equivocal, but this remains investigational and is not yet standard clinical practice.

## SECTION 11.3 [BOARD] DIABETES INSIPIDUS

### 11.3.1 Central Diabetes Insipidus After TBI

Central diabetes insipidus (DI) results from damage to the neurohypophysis (posterior pituitary) or the hypothalamic nuclei (supraoptic and paraventricular) that produce ADH (vasopressin). Without adequate ADH, the renal collecting ducts cannot concentrate urine, resulting in massive output of dilute urine and progressive hypernatremia.

**Association:** Most commonly associated with basilar skull fractures, which may directly damage the pituitary stalk or posterior pituitary.

**Diagnostic Features:**

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Serum sodium | HIGH (>145 mEq/L) |
| Serum osmolality | HIGH (>295 mOsm/kg) |
| Urine osmolality | LOW (<300 mOsm/kg) -- dilute urine despite high serum osmolality |
| Urine specific gravity | LOW (<1.005) |
| Urine output | MARKEDLY INCREASED (>3 L/day, can exceed 10-15 L/day) |

**Treatment:**
- **DDAVP (desmopressin):** Synthetic ADH analog; given intranasally (10-40 mcg/day in divided doses), subcutaneously, intravenously (1-4 mcg every 8-12 hours), or orally (0.1-0.4 mg 2-3 times daily)
- Free water replacement: match fluid intake to urine output
- Close monitoring of serum sodium, urine output, and fluid balance
- For partial DI: chlorpropamide (enhances renal response to remaining endogenous ADH) or carbamazepine (increases ADH release and renal sensitivity)

### 11.3.2 [BOARD] The Triphasic Response

The triphasic response is a classic pattern of sequential sodium disturbances that may occur following severe damage to the hypothalamic-neurohypophyseal tract. It is one of the most heavily tested neuroendocrine concepts on board examinations.

**Phase 1: Initial Polyuria / DI (Days 1-5)**
- **Mechanism:** Axonal shock from injury to the hypothalamic-neurohypophyseal tract prevents action potentials from reaching axon terminals in the posterior pituitary. ADH release ceases abruptly.
- **Clinical presentation:** Urine output >3 L/day (often 5-15 L/day), urine osmolality <300 mOsm/kg, urine specific gravity <1.005, rising serum sodium (>145 mEq/L).
- **Management:** DDAVP to replace ADH action; free water replacement matched to urine output; serum sodium monitoring every 4-6 hours.

**Phase 2: Transient Antidiuresis / SIADH-like (Days 5-10)**
- **Mechanism:** Dying posterior pituitary neurons and severed axons undergo lysis, releasing their entire preformed stores of arginine vasopressin (AVP) into the circulation. This unregulated release of stored ADH produces an SIADH-like picture.
- **Clinical presentation:** Abrupt decrease in urine output (oliguria), falling serum sodium, concentrated urine despite low serum osmolality.
- **CRITICAL MANAGEMENT:** STOP DDAVP immediately when Phase 2 begins. Continuing DDAVP during this phase causes severe water intoxication and potentially fatal hyponatremia. Implement fluid restriction (1.0-1.2 L/day). Monitor serum sodium every 4-6 hours. Resume DDAVP only when polyuria recurs (Phase 3).

**Phase 3: Permanent DI (After Day 10)**
- **Mechanism:** All stored AVP has been released and depleted. If greater than 80% of AVP-producing neurons in the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei are destroyed, permanent DI results. If less than 80% are destroyed, partial recovery may occur.
- **Clinical presentation:** Recurrence of polyuria and hypernatremia (same pattern as Phase 1, but now permanent).
- **Management:** Lifelong DDAVP replacement; adequate free water access; regular monitoring of serum sodium.

**Important Caveats:**
- Not all patients progress through all three phases. Some experience only Phase 1 (transient DI that resolves). Others skip Phase 2.
- The triphasic response occurs in approximately 3.4% of post-TBI DI cases; isolated transient DI is more common.
- The key danger point is Phase 2: if DDAVP is continued during the antidiuretic phase, severe hyponatremia and water intoxication can result.

**Monitoring Protocol During Triphasic Response:**

| Parameter | Frequency | Target/Action |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Serum sodium | Every 4-6 hours (ICU) | Maintain 135-145 mEq/L |
| Serum osmolality | Every 6-8 hours | >295 suggests DI; <280 suggests SIADH phase |
| Urine output | Hourly | >250 mL/hr or >3 L/day suggests DI phase; oliguria suggests SIADH phase |
| Urine osmolality | Every 6-8 hours | <300 = dilute (DI); >300 = concentrated (SIADH) |
| Urine specific gravity | With each void | <1.005 = DI; >1.020 = SIADH phase |
| Daily weight | Every morning | Weight gain = fluid retention (SIADH); weight loss = fluid loss (DI) |
| Fluid balance | Every shift | Match intake to output during DI; restrict during SIADH |
| DDAVP dose | Per phase | Give in Phases 1 and 3; STOP in Phase 2 |

## SECTION 11.4 [BOARD] SCREENING TIMELINE: THE GHIGO ALGORITHM

### 11.4.1 Historical Context

Ghigo and colleagues published the first proposed screening protocol for posttraumatic hypopituitarism in 2005, establishing the foundation for systematic hormonal evaluation after TBI. Since then, at least seven published screening algorithms have been proposed, with most recommending routine screening for patients with moderate to severe TBI at defined time points post-injury (Ghigo et al., 2005).

### 11.4.2 Recommended Screening Protocol

The Endocrine Society and most expert consensus panels recommend the following screening timeline:

**Screening at 3 Months Post-Injury:**
- Morning cortisol (8 AM fasting draw)
- Free T4 and TSH
- Morning total testosterone (males) or estradiol + LH/FSH (females)
- Prolactin
- Serum sodium, osmolality
- Basic metabolic panel

**Screening at 12 Months Post-Injury:**
- Repeat all 3-month screening tests
- Add GH axis evaluation: IGF-1 level (screening); if low or clinical suspicion is high, proceed to GH stimulation testing
- GH testing is typically deferred to 12 months because early GH deficiency is often transient

**Rationale for Timing:**
- Early deficiencies (within days to weeks) are frequently transient and may resolve spontaneously as post-injury edema and inflammation subside.
- Deficiencies persisting at 3 months require ongoing monitoring and possible treatment.
- Deficiencies persisting at 12 months are more likely permanent and generally warrant hormone replacement therapy.
- GH deficiency evaluation is deferred to 12 months because (a) transient GHD is common in the early post-injury period, and (b) GH stimulation testing is burdensome and should be reserved for cases where the deficiency is likely permanent.

**Clinical Pearl:** Adequate screening is essential because the symptoms of PTHP overlap extensively with typical post-TBI sequelae (fatigue, cognitive impairment, mood disturbance, reduced energy). Without screening, these deficiencies will be attributed to the TBI itself and will go untreated. Not every TBI patient needs screening; the 2024 review literature suggests focusing screening efforts on moderate-to-severe TBI patients, those with basilar skull fractures, and those with symptoms suggestive of hormonal deficiency (Caputo et al., 2024).

## SECTION 11.5 [COMPREHENSIVE] GROWTH HORMONE DEFICIENCY

### 11.5.1 Prevalence and Mechanism

GH deficiency is the most common permanent anterior pituitary deficit after TBI, affecting 10-20% of moderate-to-severe TBI patients. It has also been reported in 6-10% of mild TBI patients, though the clinical significance in this population remains debated.

**Why GH is most vulnerable:** The GH-secreting somatotroph cells are concentrated in the lateral wings of the anterior pituitary, which have the most tenuous blood supply via the long portal vessels. These lateral regions are the first to become ischemic when portal blood flow is disrupted by trauma (Agha et al., 2004).

**Clinical Features:**
- Fatigue and reduced energy
- Decreased lean body mass with increased visceral adiposity (truncal obesity)
- Impaired cognition: attention, memory, processing speed, executive function
- Reduced exercise capacity and stamina
- Depression and reduced quality of life
- Dyslipidemia (elevated LDL, decreased HDL)
- Insulin resistance and increased cardiovascular risk
- Decreased bone mineral density (long-term)

### 11.5.2 Diagnosis: IGF-1 Screening and Stimulation Testing

**IGF-1 as a Screening Test:**
- Insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) is used as an initial screening test because it is a stable, easy-to-measure surrogate marker of GH secretion.
- **Critical limitation:** IGF-1 can be falsely normal in up to 30% of GH-deficient adults. A normal IGF-1 does NOT exclude GHD. This is a key board concept.
- Very low IGF-1 (below age/sex-specific lower limit) is suggestive but not diagnostic without confirmatory testing.

**GH Stimulation Testing:**
Per Endocrine Society guidelines, GH stimulation testing is required to confirm the diagnosis of GHD.

| Test | Protocol | Diagnostic Cutoff | Key Considerations |
|------|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Insulin Tolerance Test (ITT) | IV insulin (0.1-0.15 U/kg) to induce hypoglycemia (glucose <40 mg/dL); measure GH at 0, 30, 60, 90, 120 min | Peak GH <5 mcg/L = GHD (some use <3 for severe GHD) | GOLD STANDARD per Endocrine Society; CONTRAINDICATED in seizure disorders (common in TBI), coronary artery disease, elderly; requires physician supervision |
| Glucagon Stimulation Test (GST) | IM glucagon (1 mg if <90 kg; 1.5 mg if >90 kg); measure GH at 0, 30, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180 min | Peak GH <3 mcg/L = GHD | PREFERRED ALTERNATIVE to ITT in TBI patients; comparable diagnostic accuracy; safer; well-tolerated; can cause nausea/vomiting |
| GHRH-Arginine Test | IV GHRH (1 mcg/kg) + IV arginine (0.5 g/kg, max 30 g); measure GH at intervals | BMI-dependent: <11.5 (lean), <8.0 (overweight), <4.2 (obese) mcg/L | May miss hypothalamic GHD; GHRH no longer available in the US |
| Macimorelin Oral Test | Oral macimorelin (0.5 mg/kg); measure GH at 45, 60, 90 min | Peak GH <2.8 mcg/L = GHD | FDA-approved oral GH secretagogue; convenient; validated in adults; gaining clinical use |

**Clinical Pearl:** The ITT is the gold standard but is CONTRAINDICATED in patients with seizure disorders, which are common after TBI. The glucagon stimulation test (GST) is therefore the preferred alternative in TBI patients. It has comparable diagnostic accuracy and a superior safety profile. Always evaluate and replace other pituitary axes (cortisol, thyroid, gonadal) BEFORE GH testing, as untreated deficiencies in these axes can affect GH testing results.

### 11.5.3 GH Replacement Therapy

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Starting dose | 0.2-0.4 mg/day subcutaneous injection (lower in elderly, higher in younger patients) |
| Titration | Every 4-8 weeks based on IGF-1 levels (target mid-normal range for age/sex) and clinical response |
| Monitoring | IGF-1 levels, fasting glucose, lipid panel, body composition; screen for side effects |
| Common side effects | Edema, arthralgias, myalgias, carpal tunnel syndrome, hyperglycemia |
| Contraindications | Active malignancy, uncontrolled diabetes, active proliferative retinopathy |

**Benefits of GH Replacement After TBI:**
- Improved cognition: attention, memory, processing speed, executive function
- Improved body composition: decreased visceral fat, increased lean body mass
- Improved bone mineral density
- Improved quality of life (measured by QoL-AGHDA and SF-36)
- Improved lipid profile: decreased LDL, increased HDL
- Improved insulin sensitivity
- Improved energy and exercise capacity

**Important Clarification:** GH replacement does NOT directly improve neurological recovery from TBI. The benefits are related to correcting the hormonal deficiency and its metabolic, cognitive, and quality-of-life consequences.

## SECTION 11.6 [COMPREHENSIVE] ADRENAL INSUFFICIENCY

### 11.6.1 Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency: The Most Dangerous Deficit

ACTH deficiency causing secondary adrenal insufficiency (AI) affects 5-10% of TBI patients and is the most dangerous hormonal deficiency to miss. Unrecognized AI can progress to adrenal crisis and death during physiologic stress (illness, surgery, trauma).

**Key Differences from Primary AI (Addison Disease):**

| Feature | Secondary AI (Post-TBI) | Primary AI (Addison Disease) |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| Mechanism | Pituitary ACTH deficiency | Adrenal gland destruction |
| Aldosterone | INTACT (RAAS is preserved) | Deficient |
| Hyperkalemia | Typically ABSENT | Present |
| Hyperpigmentation | ABSENT | Present (elevated ACTH/MSH) |
| Sodium abnormality | Hyponatremia (impaired free water clearance) | Hyponatremia AND hyperkalemia |

**Clinical Pearl:** Secondary AI (post-TBI) characteristically presents with hyponatremia but typically WITHOUT hyperkalemia, because the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system remains intact (aldosterone production is preserved). Primary AI (Addison disease) presents with BOTH hyponatremia and hyperkalemia because both cortisol and aldosterone are deficient.

### 11.6.2 Cortisol Screening Algorithm

**Morning Cortisol (8 AM fasting draw):**
- **<3 mcg/dL:** DIAGNOSTIC of adrenal insufficiency. No further testing required. Initiate replacement immediately.
- **>18 mcg/dL:** Normal adrenal function. AI excluded.
- **3-18 mcg/dL:** INDETERMINATE. Requires cosyntropin stimulation test for definitive diagnosis.

**Cosyntropin (ACTH) Stimulation Test:**
- Administer IV or IM cosyntropin 250 mcg
- Measure cortisol at baseline, 30 minutes, and 60 minutes
- **Normal response:** Peak cortisol >=18 mcg/dL at 30 or 60 minutes (some newer assays use 14-16 mcg/dL cutoff)
- **Subnormal response:** Peak cortisol <18 mcg/dL confirms adrenal insufficiency

**Important Limitation:** The high-dose (250 mcg) cosyntropin test may be FALSELY NORMAL in RECENT secondary AI. In early secondary AI (within weeks of TBI), the adrenal glands have not yet undergone sufficient atrophy from ACTH deprivation and may still respond to the supraphysiologic dose of exogenous ACTH. The low-dose cosyntropin test (1 mcg) may be more sensitive for early secondary AI but is not standardized and requires careful technique (adsorption to IV tubing can reduce the delivered dose).

**Clinical Pearl:** Check cortisol BEFORE initiating any glucocorticoid therapy. Exogenous steroids suppress the HPA axis and invalidate testing results. If glucocorticoids have already been started (e.g., for cerebral edema), testing must be deferred until after an adequate washout period.

### 11.6.2A Clinical Presentation of Adrenal Crisis

Adrenal crisis is a life-threatening emergency that may occur in TBI patients with unrecognized secondary adrenal insufficiency during physiologic stress such as infection, surgery, or additional trauma. Recognition requires a high index of suspicion because the presentation overlaps with many other acute conditions in TBI patients.

**Classic Presentation:**
- Refractory hypotension (unresponsive to fluid resuscitation and vasopressors -- a critical clue)
- Hyponatremia (from impaired free water excretion due to cortisol deficiency)
- Hypoglycemia (cortisol is necessary for gluconeogenesis)
- Altered mental status, lethargy progressing to obtundation
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
- Fever (may be present from the precipitating illness or the crisis itself)

**When to Suspect Adrenal Crisis in TBI:**
- Unexplained hemodynamic instability during intercurrent illness
- Hypotension refractory to standard vasopressor support
- Persistent hyponatremia despite appropriate sodium correction
- Hypoglycemia without other explanation
- Any acutely deteriorating TBI patient who has not been screened for adrenal insufficiency

**Emergency Management:**
- Do NOT wait for cortisol results to treat if clinical suspicion is high
- Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus immediately
- Aggressive IV normal saline resuscitation
- Dextrose-containing fluids if hypoglycemic
- Identify and treat the precipitating cause (infection, surgical stress)
- Once stable, transition to stress-dose then maintenance cortisol replacement

### 11.6.3 Hydrocortisone Replacement and Stress Dosing

| Scenario | Dose | Key Points |
|----------|------|------------|
| Daily replacement | 15-25 mg/day in 2-3 divided doses (e.g., 10 mg AM, 5 mg afternoon, 5 mg evening) | Mimic diurnal cortisol rhythm: largest dose in the morning; use the lowest effective dose |
| Minor illness (cold, UTI) | Double or triple the daily dose for 2-3 days | Patient self-management; must be educated on "sick day rules" |
| Moderate stress (fever >38.5C, minor surgery) | 50 mg hydrocortisone IV/IM, then 25 mg q8h for 1-2 days | Taper back to maintenance as stress resolves |
| Major stress/surgery/trauma | 100 mg hydrocortisone IV bolus, then 50 mg IV q8h for 24-72 hours | Taper by 50% per day as clinical condition improves |
| Adrenal crisis (acute) | 100 mg hydrocortisone IV bolus IMMEDIATELY, then 50 mg IV q6-8h + aggressive IV NS | LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY; treat empirically; do not wait for lab confirmation |

**Patient Education:** All patients with secondary AI must carry a medical alert identification and be educated on:
- Sick day rules (dose doubling/tripling during minor illness)
- When to seek emergency medical attention (inability to take oral medications, persistent vomiting, signs of adrenal crisis)
- The need for stress-dose steroids before surgical procedures
- The requirement for injectable hydrocortisone at home for emergency self-administration

**Clinical Pearl:** ALWAYS evaluate and replace cortisol BEFORE initiating thyroid hormone replacement. Starting levothyroxine in a patient with unrecognized adrenal insufficiency increases metabolic rate and cortisol clearance, which can precipitate a life-threatening adrenal crisis. The rule is: cortisol first, thyroid second. This is a classic board trap.

## SECTION 11.7 [COMPREHENSIVE] CENTRAL HYPOTHYROIDISM

### 11.7.1 Pathophysiology and Diagnosis

Central (secondary) hypothyroidism occurs in 5-10% of TBI patients due to damage to TSH-secreting thyrotroph cells in the anterior pituitary or to hypothalamic TRH-producing neurons.

**The Diagnostic Trap -- TSH Interpretation:**

In primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto thyroiditis, iodine deficiency), TSH is always elevated as a compensatory response to low thyroid hormone levels. Clinicians are trained to rely on TSH as the primary thyroid screening test.

However, in central hypothyroidism from pituitary injury, TSH may be:
- LOW (insufficient TSH production)
- NORMAL (inappropriately normal for the low T4)
- Even SLIGHTLY ELEVATED (due to secretion of biologically inactive TSH isoforms with altered glycosylation)

**The Critical Rule:** In TBI patients, ALWAYS check free T4 in addition to TSH. A "normal" TSH does NOT rule out hypothyroidism in a TBI patient.

**Diagnostic Interpretation:**

| Free T4 | TSH | Interpretation |
|---------|-----|----------------|
| Low | Low or normal or slightly elevated | CENTRAL hypothyroidism (inappropriate TSH for low T4) |
| Low | Elevated (>10 mIU/L) | PRIMARY hypothyroidism |
| Normal | Normal | Euthyroid |

**Treatment:**
- Levothyroxine replacement, starting at 25-50 mcg/day
- Titrate based on FREE T4 level (target mid-normal range), NOT TSH
- TSH is unreliable for dose adjustment in central hypothyroidism because the pituitary cannot produce a normal TSH response
- Ensure cortisol sufficiency before starting levothyroxine (cortisol first, thyroid second)

**Clinical Pearl:** A TBI patient presenting with fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, and cognitive slowing who has a "normal" TSH but a low free T4 has central hypothyroidism, NOT euthyroid status. This is one of the most commonly tested neuroendocrine board traps. The key teaching point is that TSH alone is insufficient for thyroid screening in TBI patients.

## SECTION 11.8 [COMPREHENSIVE] HYPOGONADISM

### 11.8.1 Prevalence and Clinical Features

Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (central/secondary) is the second most common persistent anterior pituitary deficit after GHD, affecting 10-15% of TBI patients. It results from damage to LH/FSH-secreting gonadotroph cells.

**Males:**
- Low testosterone: decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, fatigue, depression, mood lability
- Body composition: decreased muscle mass, increased visceral fat
- Bone health: accelerated bone loss, osteoporosis, increased fracture risk
- Cognitive effects: impaired attention, verbal memory, processing speed

**Females:**
- Low estradiol: amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea, decreased libido, mood changes, vaginal dryness, hot flashes
- Bone health: accelerated bone loss, osteoporosis
- Infertility

### 11.8.2 Screening and Diagnosis

**Males:**
- Morning total testosterone (8-10 AM draw -- testosterone has diurnal variation; afternoon values may be physiologically lower)
- Low testosterone (<300 ng/dL) + low or normal LH/FSH = central (secondary) hypogonadism (consistent with pituitary cause from TBI)
- Low testosterone + elevated LH/FSH = primary hypogonadism (testicular failure -- NOT TBI-related)
- Also check: free testosterone, SHBG, prolactin

**Females:**
- Estradiol + LH/FSH (premenopausal women)
- Menstrual history
- Low estradiol + low or normal LH/FSH = central hypogonadism

### 11.8.3 Treatment

**Males:**
- Testosterone replacement: topical gel (1-2% testosterone gel, applied daily), intramuscular injection (testosterone cypionate or enanthate, 100-200 mg every 1-2 weeks), or transdermal patch
- Monitoring: hematocrit (testosterone stimulates erythropoiesis; risk of polycythemia), PSA, lipid panel, liver function
- **Contraindications:** prostate cancer, breast cancer, hematocrit >54%, severe untreated sleep apnea, desire for fertility (testosterone suppresses spermatogenesis via negative feedback on LH/FSH)

**Females:**
- Estrogen/progesterone replacement in premenopausal women
- If uterus intact: combined estrogen-progesterone (progesterone for endometrial protection)
- If hysterectomized: estrogen alone

**Clinical Pearl:** Always draw morning testosterone in males (8-10 AM) because of diurnal variation. The pattern of low testosterone with low/normal LH is diagnostic of central hypogonadism (pituitary origin, consistent with TBI). Low testosterone with HIGH LH indicates primary hypogonadism (testicular origin, not from TBI).

## SECTION 11.9 [COMPREHENSIVE] HYPERPROLACTINEMIA: THE STALK EFFECT

### 11.9.1 Mechanism

Unlike all other anterior pituitary hormones, prolactin secretion is under tonic inhibitory control by dopamine released from the hypothalamus, which travels down the pituitary stalk to the anterior pituitary via the portal vasculature. Damage to the pituitary stalk disrupts this dopaminergic inhibition, resulting in disinhibited prolactin release -- the "stalk effect."

**Clinical Significance:**
- Mildly elevated prolactin (<100 ng/mL) in a TBI patient suggests stalk damage (stalk effect), NOT a prolactinoma
- Very high prolactin (>200 ng/mL) should raise concern for a prolactinoma (though this would be unrelated to TBI)
- Prolactin elevation from the stalk effect is typically mild to moderate (25-100 ng/mL)

**Clinical Consequences:**
- Males: decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, gynecomastia (rare)
- Females: galactorrhea, amenorrhea, infertility
- Both sexes: decreased bone mineral density (chronic hyperprolactinemia inhibits GnRH)

**Clinical Pearl:** The stalk effect is the OPPOSITE pattern from other anterior pituitary hormones: damage to the stalk causes prolactin to INCREASE (loss of inhibition), while all other anterior pituitary hormones DECREASE (loss of stimulation). This distinction is a commonly tested board concept.

### 11.9.2 Management of Hyperprolactinemia After TBI

Management of post-TBI hyperprolactinemia depends on the clinical context:

- **Mild elevation (<100 ng/mL) without symptoms:** Observation and serial monitoring. May resolve as post-injury edema and inflammation subside. Not all patients with mild stalk-effect hyperprolactinemia require treatment.
- **Mild elevation with symptoms (galactorrhea, menstrual irregularity, decreased libido):** Consider dopamine agonist therapy (cabergoline 0.25-1 mg twice weekly, or bromocriptine 1.25-10 mg daily). Cabergoline is generally preferred due to better tolerability and once- or twice-weekly dosing.
- **Elevation >150-200 ng/mL:** Investigate for prolactinoma (MRI sella with gadolinium). While the timing concurrent with TBI might suggest stalk effect, very high levels are more consistent with a prolactin-secreting adenoma, which may have been incidentally present before injury or, rarely, develop independently.

**Monitoring:**
- Serial prolactin levels every 3-6 months initially
- If treated with dopamine agonist: prolactin level 1 month after initiation and at dose changes; annual prolactin once stable
- MRI sella if prolactin rises above expected stalk-effect range or fails to respond to dopamine agonist therapy
- Bone density monitoring in patients with prolonged hyperprolactinemia (particularly premenopausal women), as chronic prolactin elevation suppresses GnRH and leads to hypogonadism with associated bone loss

**Relationship to Other Pituitary Deficits:**
Hyperprolactinemia from the stalk effect often coexists with deficiencies of other anterior pituitary hormones, since the same mechanism (stalk damage) that releases prolactin from inhibition also disrupts the delivery of hypothalamic releasing hormones (GHRH, CRH, GnRH, TRH) to the anterior pituitary. When hyperprolactinemia is identified, a comprehensive anterior pituitary evaluation should be performed to screen for concurrent GH, ACTH, TSH, and gonadotropin deficiencies.

## SECTION 11.10 [COMPREHENSIVE] PSYCHOGENIC POLYDIPSIA

### 11.10.1 Definition and Relevance to TBI

Psychogenic (primary) polydipsia is a condition of excessive water intake driven by behavioral or psychiatric factors rather than true thirst or ADH deficiency. After TBI, it may arise from frontal lobe dysfunction affecting impulse control, disrupted hypothalamic thirst regulation, or comorbid psychiatric conditions.

**Clinical Significance:** Psychogenic polydipsia can mimic diabetes insipidus (both present with polyuria and polydipsia), making differential diagnosis essential.

### 11.10.2 Differential Diagnosis: Psychogenic Polydipsia vs. Central DI

| Feature | Psychogenic Polydipsia | Central DI |
|---------|----------------------|------------|
| Serum sodium | LOW or low-normal (<135 mEq/L) | HIGH (>145 mEq/L) |
| Serum osmolality | LOW (<280 mOsm/kg) | HIGH (>295 mOsm/kg) |
| Urine osmolality | LOW (dilute) | LOW (dilute) |
| Urine specific gravity | LOW (<1.005) | LOW (<1.005) |
| Polyuria | Yes | Yes |
| Polydipsia | Yes (primary; drives the polyuria) | Yes (secondary to polyuria) |
| Nocturnal polyuria | Typically absent (drinking stops during sleep) | Present (polyuria continues during sleep) |
| Water deprivation test | Urine concentrates normally (osm >800 mOsm/kg) | Urine remains dilute; concentrates after DDAVP (central DI) |
| Response to DDAVP | No change (kidneys already concentrating appropriately) | Urine concentrates (central DI responds to exogenous ADH) |
| Copeptin levels | Normal or appropriately suppressed | Abnormally low (central DI) |

**Key Diagnostic Features:**
- In psychogenic polydipsia, serum sodium is typically LOW or low-normal (dilutional effect of excessive water intake), whereas in central DI, serum sodium is HIGH (>145 mEq/L due to water loss exceeding intake).
- Nocturnal polyuria is typically absent in psychogenic polydipsia (patients stop drinking during sleep), but persists in DI (ADH deficiency is continuous).
- Water deprivation test (WDT): In psychogenic polydipsia, urine osmolality rises above 800 mOsm/kg with water restriction, demonstrating intact ADH secretion and renal concentrating ability. In central DI, urine remains dilute during water deprivation but concentrates after exogenous DDAVP administration.
- Copeptin (a stable byproduct of ADH precursor processing) is emerging as a diagnostic tool: it is appropriately low/suppressed in psychogenic polydipsia and abnormally low in central DI despite elevated serum osmolality.

**Clinical Pearl:** The simplest initial clue is serum sodium: psychogenic polydipsia tends toward hyponatremia (too much water dilutes sodium), while central DI tends toward hypernatremia (too much water is lost in dilute urine). Nocturnal polyuria favors DI. A water deprivation test confirms the diagnosis.

## SECTION 11.10A [COMPREHENSIVE] MANAGEMENT OF SODIUM DISORDERS: CLINICAL DECISION-MAKING

### 11.10A.1 Approach to Hyponatremia After TBI

The systematic approach to hyponatremia in TBI patients follows a structured algorithm:

**Step 1: Confirm True Hyponatremia**
- Measure serum osmolality to exclude pseudohyponatremia (e.g., hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia)
- Hypo-osmolar hyponatremia (serum osm <280 mOsm/kg) = true hyponatremia
- If serum osmolality is normal or elevated with low serum Na, evaluate for pseudohyponatremia or hyperglycemia (corrected Na = measured Na + 1.6 mEq/L for every 100 mg/dL glucose above 100)

**Step 2: Assess Symptom Severity**
- Severe symptoms (seizures, obtundation, coma): treat emergently with 3% hypertonic saline bolus (100-150 mL over 10-20 minutes, may repeat once or twice) to raise Na by 4-6 mEq/L acutely
- Moderate symptoms (headache, nausea, confusion): more aggressive evaluation and treatment warranted but not emergent bolus
- Mild or asymptomatic: standard evaluation and treatment

**Step 3: Assess Volume Status**
This is the critical step that distinguishes SIADH from CSW:
- Physical examination: vital signs (HR, BP, orthostatics), mucous membranes, skin turgor, JVP/CVP, peripheral edema
- Laboratory surrogates: BUN/Cr ratio (>20:1 suggests hypovolemia), hematocrit (elevated with hemoconcentration), serum uric acid (low in SIADH, high in hypovolemia)
- Fluid balance: daily weights, intake/output records, net fluid balance
- Central hemodynamic monitoring (CVP) when physical examination is equivocal

**Step 4: Check Urine Indices**
- Urine osmolality, urine sodium, urine specific gravity
- In both SIADH and CSW: urine Na >40 mEq/L, concentrated urine
- The urine cannot distinguish between these two conditions; volume status is the differentiator

**Step 5: Evaluate Other Causes**
- Rule out thyroid dysfunction (check free T4) and adrenal insufficiency (check AM cortisol) -- both can cause hyponatremia and are common after TBI
- Review medication list for drugs that can cause SIADH (carbamazepine, SSRIs, opioids, desmopressin, oxcarbazepine)
- Consider cerebral edema as contributing factor

**Step 6: Initiate Treatment**
- SIADH (euvolemic): fluid restriction, salt tablets, demeclocycline, or vaptans
- CSW (hypovolemic): volume replacement with NS or HTS, fludrocortisone
- Monitor Na every 4-6 hours initially; adjust treatment to maintain correction rate <=10 mEq/L per 24 hours

### 11.10A.2 Drug-Induced SIADH in TBI Patients

Several medications commonly used in TBI management can cause or exacerbate SIADH, making it essential to review the medication list in any TBI patient with hyponatremia:

| Drug | Mechanism of SIADH Induction | Clinical Relevance in TBI |
|------|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Carbamazepine | Enhances ADH effect at renal collecting duct; may also increase ADH release | Commonly used for seizure prophylaxis and neuropathic pain |
| Oxcarbazepine | Similar to carbamazepine; may have higher SIADH risk | Alternative antiepileptic |
| SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline) | Increase ADH release; direct effect on renal water handling | Commonly prescribed for post-TBI depression |
| Opioids | Increase ADH release | Used for pain management |
| Desmopressin (DDAVP) | Exogenous ADH analog; iatrogenic SIADH if dose is excessive | Used for DI treatment; risk during Phase 2 of triphasic response |
| Phenothiazines | Increase ADH release | Used for nausea/agitation |
| NSAIDs | Enhance renal response to ADH; reduce prostaglandin-mediated free water excretion | Commonly used for headache/pain |

**Clinical Pearl:** When a TBI patient develops hyponatremia, always review the medication list. Drug-induced SIADH can occur independently of or in addition to injury-related SIADH. Carbamazepine (a commonly used antiepileptic in TBI) is one of the most frequent pharmacologic causes of SIADH and can dramatically exacerbate hyponatremia.

### 11.10A.3 Long-Term Endocrine Follow-Up

Patients with documented posttraumatic hypopituitarism require ongoing endocrine surveillance:

- **Annual reassessment** of all replaced hormones with dose adjustment as needed
- **Bone density screening** (DEXA scan) in patients with GH deficiency, hypogonadism, or glucocorticoid replacement -- at baseline and every 1-2 years
- **Cardiovascular risk monitoring:** Lipid panel, fasting glucose, HbA1c, blood pressure -- GHD and hypogonadism increase cardiovascular risk
- **Quality of life assessment:** Standardized questionnaires (QoL-AGHDA, SF-36) to monitor the subjective impact of hormone replacement
- **Medication interactions:** Estrogen replacement increases cortisol-binding globulin, potentially requiring cortisol dose adjustment; thyroid hormone replacement increases cortisol clearance, necessitating cortisol evaluation before thyroid treatment
- **Pregnancy planning:** Female patients with central hypogonadism require specialized reproductive endocrinology consultation for fertility management
- **Transition of care:** Ensure that endocrine follow-up is maintained during transitions between rehabilitation settings and community providers, as PTHP is frequently "lost" during care transitions

## SECTION 11.13 BOARD-STYLE CLINICAL SCENARIO ANALYSIS

### Scenario 1: Hyponatremia Day 5, Euvolemic Patient

A 32-year-old male with severe TBI (GCS 6) is now day 5 in the ICU. Labs: Na 125 mEq/L, serum osmolality 260, urine osmolality 450, urine Na 65 mEq/L. Vitals: HR 72, BP 128/78, weight stable, moist mucous membranes, CVP 8.

**Analysis:** Hyponatremia with concentrated urine and high urine sodium. The patient is EUVOLEMIC (normal vital signs, stable weight, normal CVP). Diagnosis: SIADH. Treatment: Fluid restriction to 1.0-1.2 L/day; monitor Na every 6 hours; correct no faster than 10 mEq/L per 24 hours.

### Scenario 2: Hyponatremia Day 7, Hypovolemic Patient

A 28-year-old female with SAH and TBI, day 7 in the ICU. Labs: Na 127 mEq/L, serum osmolality 264, urine osmolality 500, urine Na 80 mEq/L. Vitals: HR 110, BP 95/60, weight down 3 kg, dry mucous membranes, CVP 2, negative fluid balance -2.5 L.

**Analysis:** Same laboratory picture as Scenario 1 (hyponatremia, concentrated urine, high urine Na). However, the patient is HYPOVOLEMIC (tachycardia, hypotension, weight loss, low CVP, negative fluid balance). Diagnosis: Cerebral Salt Wasting. Treatment: Volume replacement with normal saline or hypertonic saline. Fludrocortisone may be added. Fluid restriction would be DANGEROUS.

### Scenario 3: Triphasic Response

A 45-year-old male with basilar skull fracture, day 2 post-TBI. Urine output abruptly increases to 800 mL/hr. Serum Na 152, urine specific gravity 1.002, urine osmolality 120.

**Analysis:** Massive polyuria with hypernatremia and dilute urine = Phase 1 DI. Treatment: DDAVP and free water replacement. On day 7, urine output abruptly decreases to 20 mL/hr, Na drops to 128. Analysis: Phase 2 (SIADH-like) -- STOP DDAVP, fluid restrict. On day 12, polyuria recurs with rising Na. Analysis: Phase 3 (permanent DI) -- resume DDAVP for likely lifelong therapy.

### Scenario 4: Fatigue with "Normal" TSH

A 35-year-old male, 6 months post-severe TBI, presents with fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, and cognitive slowing. TSH 2.1 mIU/L (normal range 0.4-4.0), free T4 0.5 ng/dL (normal range 0.8-1.8).

**Analysis:** Low free T4 with "normal" TSH = central hypothyroidism. The TSH is inappropriately normal for the low T4 level. Before starting levothyroxine, check morning cortisol to evaluate for concurrent adrenal insufficiency (cortisol first, thyroid second).
