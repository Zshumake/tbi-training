## SECTION 10.1 [BOARD] DEFINITION AND PATHOPHYSIOLOGY OF SPASTICITY

### 10.1.1 Definition

Spasticity is defined as a velocity-dependent increase in tonic stretch reflexes (muscle tone) resulting from hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex, as described in the seminal definition by Lance in 1980. The cardinal distinguishing feature of spasticity is its velocity dependence: the faster a muscle is passively stretched, the greater the resistance encountered. This property separates spasticity from other forms of increased muscle tone and is a cornerstone of clinical assessment and board examination questions.

Spasticity is a component of the upper motor neuron (UMN) syndrome. The UMN syndrome encompasses both "positive" features (phenomena that are exaggerated or released) and "negative" features (phenomena that are diminished or lost). Positive features include spasticity, clonus, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign, and spastic co-contraction. Negative features include weakness (paresis), loss of dexterity, and fatigability. In clinical practice, the negative features of UMN syndrome are often more disabling than the positive features, yet spasticity receives disproportionate attention because it is more readily measurable and treatable (Sheean & McGuire, 2009; Lance, 1980).

**Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between velocity-dependent and velocity-independent tone is among the most commonly tested concepts on PM&R board examinations. Spasticity is velocity-dependent (faster passive stretch produces more resistance). Rigidity is velocity-independent (constant resistance at any speed, described as "lead-pipe" or "cogwheel" in parkinsonism). Contracture is a fixed structural shortening that does not change with velocity.

### 10.1.2 Epidemiology and Incidence After TBI

Spasticity is one of the most common and functionally significant complications of severe traumatic brain injury. Epidemiologic data consistently demonstrate the following:

- Approximately 70% of patients with severe TBI develop clinically significant spasticity (Wedekind & Lippert-Gruner, 2005).
- The incidence of contracture, a consequence of untreated or undertreated spasticity, is projected to be as high as 85% among severe TBI cases (Singer et al., 2004).
- Onset typically occurs within weeks of injury and may worsen progressively over months.
- Spasticity may be apparent as early as one week following injury, with the onset of associated orthopedic sequelae being rapid (Thibaut et al., 2013).

The pattern and severity of spasticity after TBI differ from spasticity following spinal cord injury or stroke. TBI-related spasticity is frequently bilateral, affects both upper and lower extremities, and may be accompanied by concurrent cognitive and behavioral deficits that complicate assessment and treatment. Furthermore, paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (storming), which is unique to severe brain injury, can produce episodic increases in tone that confound spasticity assessment (Baguley et al., 2014).

### 10.1.3 Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of spasticity after TBI involves disruption of descending inhibitory pathways from the cortex, basal ganglia, and brainstem to spinal motor neurons. Under normal conditions, the corticospinal, reticulospinal, and vestibulospinal tracts modulate spinal reflex arcs. When these descending influences are interrupted by TBI, the balance between excitatory and inhibitory input to alpha motor neurons shifts toward excitation, resulting in hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex arc.

Key mechanisms include:

1. **Loss of descending inhibition:** Damage to the corticospinal and corticoreticular tracts removes tonic inhibitory input to spinal interneurons, leading to increased excitability of the monosynaptic stretch reflex arc.
2. **Increased excitatory drive:** Unopposed reticulospinal and vestibulospinal facilitatory input amplifies muscle tone.
3. **Spinal cord reorganization:** Over time, denervation hypersensitivity, collateral sprouting of afferent fibers, and changes in spinal interneuron circuitry contribute to chronicity.
4. **Peripheral changes:** Prolonged spasticity leads to structural changes in muscle and connective tissue, including fibrosis, loss of sarcomeres, and increased collagen deposition, which produce contracture independent of neural hyperexcitability (Gracies, 2005).

Understanding the interplay between neural (dynamic) and structural (fixed) components of increased tone is critical for treatment selection: neural spasticity responds to pharmacologic and chemodenervation interventions, while structural contracture may require mechanical (casting) or surgical approaches.

## SECTION 10.2 [BOARD] ASSESSMENT OF SPASTICITY

### 10.2.1 Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS)

The Modified Ashworth Scale is the most widely used clinical tool for grading spasticity. It is a 6-point ordinal scale that measures resistance to passive stretch at a single velocity (approximately one second through full range of motion).

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No increase in muscle tone |
| 1 | Slight increase in tone; catch-and-release or minimal resistance at end of range of motion |
| 1+ | Slight increase in tone; catch followed by minimal resistance throughout less than half of ROM |
| 2 | More marked increase in tone through most of ROM; affected part easily moved |
| 3 | Considerable increase in tone; passive movement difficult |
| 4 | Affected part(s) rigid in flexion or extension |

**Clinical Pearl:** The unique 1+ grade (between grade 1 and grade 2) is a frequently tested board concept. It represents a catch followed by sustained resistance through less than half of range, distinguishing it from the catch-and-release pattern of grade 1.

**Strengths of the MAS:**
- Quick to administer (seconds per muscle group)
- Widely recognized and understood
- Extensive use in published research, allowing comparison across studies
- Requires no specialized equipment

**Limitations of the MAS:**
- Tests at a single velocity; cannot distinguish spasticity (dynamic) from contracture (fixed)
- Ordinal scale with poor interval properties; a change from 1 to 2 is not equivalent to a change from 2 to 3
- Moderate inter-rater reliability, particularly at grades 1 and 1+ (Pandyan et al., 1999)
- Ceiling effect at grade 4
- Does not capture spasm frequency or functional impact

### 10.2.2 Tardieu Scale (Modified)

The Modified Tardieu Scale is considered a more specific instrument for assessing spasticity because it evaluates muscle response at multiple velocities, allowing clinicians to distinguish between the neural (spastic) and structural (contracture) components of increased tone.

**Components of the Tardieu Scale:**

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| V1 | As slow as possible; measures passive ROM (R2 angle) |
| V2 | Speed of the limb falling under gravity |
| V3 | As fast as possible; measures the catch angle (R1 angle) |
| X (Quality of muscle reaction) | 0 = no resistance; 1 = slight resistance, no catch; 2 = clear catch at precise angle followed by release; 3 = fatigable clonus (<10 sec); 4 = infatigable clonus (>10 sec); 5 = joint immobile |
| Y (Angle) | The angle at which the catch or clonus occurs |
| R2 - R1 (Dynamic component) | Large difference = mostly spasticity; Small difference = mostly contracture |

**The R2 - R1 Difference: The Key Clinical Measurement**

The most clinically important output of the Tardieu Scale is the difference between R2 (the passive ROM measured at slow stretch, V1) and R1 (the catch angle measured at fast stretch, V3). This difference estimates the dynamic, velocity-dependent component of increased tone:

- **Large R2 - R1 difference:** The majority of the tone increase is due to spasticity (neural, dynamic). This component is amenable to pharmacologic treatment (oral medications, botulinum toxin, intrathecal baclofen).
- **Small R2 - R1 difference:** The majority of the resistance is due to contracture (structural, fixed). This component may require mechanical intervention (serial casting) or surgical correction (tendon lengthening, release).

**Clinical Pearl:** The Tardieu Scale is superior to the Modified Ashworth Scale for guiding treatment decisions. If the clinical question is whether increased tone is primarily spasticity or contracture, the Tardieu provides the answer; the MAS cannot make this distinction because it tests at only one velocity.

### 10.2.3 When to Use MAS vs. Tardieu

| Feature | Modified Ashworth Scale | Modified Tardieu Scale |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------|
| What it measures | Resistance at one velocity | Muscle reaction at multiple velocities |
| Key output | Ordinal grade 0-4 (with 1+) | R1, R2 angles and R2-R1 difference |
| Distinguishes spasticity from contracture | No | Yes |
| Clinical utility | Quick screening; serial monitoring | Treatment planning; pre-surgical evaluation |
| Reliability | Moderate inter-rater; 1+ causes confusion | Better inter-rater for angle measurements |
| Best use | Initial screening, research comparisons | Deciding between botox vs. casting vs. surgery |
| Limitation | Cannot guide treatment selection | More time-consuming; requires goniometer |

**Research Supporting Tardieu Superiority:**

Multiple studies have demonstrated the limitations of the MAS and the superiority of the Tardieu for clinical decision-making:

- The MAS measures resistance at a single speed and cannot distinguish neural from non-neural components of increased tone (Patrick & Ada, 2006).
- The Tardieu provides angle measurements (degrees) rather than ordinal grades, making it more sensitive to change and better suited for tracking treatment response.
- Studies demonstrate poor inter-rater reliability for the MAS, particularly at grades 1 and 1+, whereas Tardieu angle measurements show improved consistency (Haugh et al., 2006).
- The 2024 AAPM&R consensus guidance on spasticity assessment endorses a comprehensive approach that includes velocity-dependent assessment when making treatment decisions (Verduzco-Gutierrez et al., 2024).

**Practical Recommendation:** Use the MAS for quick screening and serial monitoring in routine clinical practice. Use the Tardieu when the clinical question directly affects treatment selection (botulinum toxin vs. serial casting vs. surgical intervention).

### 10.2.4 Penn Spasm Frequency Scale (PSFS)

The Penn Spasm Frequency Scale is a patient-reported outcome measure originally developed to evaluate the effectiveness of intrathecal baclofen therapy. It quantifies spasm frequency and severity as a complement to clinician-assessed scales.

**Part 1: Spasm Frequency (0-4)**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No spasms |
| 1 | Mild spasms induced by stimulation |
| 2 | Infrequent full spasms occurring less than once per hour |
| 3 | Spasms occurring more than once per hour |
| 4 | More than 10 spontaneous spasms per hour |

**Part 2: Spasm Severity (1-3)**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Mild |
| 2 | Moderate |
| 3 | Severe |

Part 2 is scored only if spasms are present (Part 1 score greater than 0).

**Clinical Pearl:** The PSFS was specifically created to measure ITB therapy effectiveness. Unlike the MAS (clinician-assessed), the PSFS is a patient self-report measure that captures spasm frequency that may be missed during a brief clinical examination. It is commonly used as an outcome measure in ITB pump trials.

### 10.2.5 Additional Assessment Considerations

Before escalating spasticity treatment, clinicians must systematically identify and treat noxious stimuli that may be exacerbating tone. Common culprits include:

- Urinary tract infection
- Pressure injuries
- Ingrown toenails
- Constipation or fecal impaction
- Deep vein thrombosis
- Heterotopic ossification
- Occult fractures
- Tight clothing, braces, or orthotics
- Urinary retention or bladder distension
- Pain from any source

**Clinical Pearl:** Treating the underlying noxious stimulus may reduce spasticity without adding medications. This concept is heavily tested on board examinations. Always perform a systematic noxious stimulus search before escalating pharmacologic therapy.

## SECTION 10.3 [BOARD] BENEFITS OF SPASTICITY

Spasticity is not always pathologic or requiring treatment. In certain clinical situations, maintained muscle tone provides functional benefits. Reducing spasticity without considering these potential benefits may paradoxically worsen a patient's functional status.

**Recognized Benefits of Spasticity:**

1. **Facilitation of standing and transfers:** Lower extremity extensor tone can support weight-bearing during transfers and standing pivot activities, particularly in patients with severe hemiparesis or quadriparesis.
2. **Venous return enhancement:** Maintained lower extremity muscle tone improves venous return through the muscle pump mechanism, potentially reducing the risk of deep vein thrombosis.
3. **Preservation of muscle bulk:** Tonic muscle contraction helps maintain muscle mass and prevents disuse atrophy.
4. **Bone mineral density maintenance:** Loading forces generated by spastic muscles help preserve bone mineral density, reducing fracture risk.
5. **Bladder function:** Detrusor tone may assist with bladder emptying in some patients.

**Clinical Pearl:** Before treating spasticity, always ask: "Will reducing this spasticity improve or worsen function?" Reducing lower extremity extensor tone may eliminate a patient's ability to perform standing pivot transfers. This functional assessment should be documented before initiating treatment.

## SECTION 10.4 [BOARD] ORAL MEDICATIONS FOR SPASTICITY

Oral medications are indicated for generalized spasticity affecting multiple muscle groups. They represent the second tier of the spasticity management ladder (after conservative measures such as stretching, positioning, and splinting). All oral anti-spasticity agents have central nervous system side effects that may be particularly problematic in TBI patients with existing cognitive impairment.

### 10.4.1 Baclofen (Lioresal)

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Drug Class | GABA-B receptor agonist |
| Mechanism | Presynaptic GABA-B receptor agonist in the spinal cord; inhibits release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, aspartate); reduces monosynaptic and polysynaptic reflexes |
| Site of Action | Central (spinal cord) |
| Indication | Generalized spasticity; most effective for spinal-origin spasticity but also used in cerebral-origin |
| Starting Dose | 5 mg TID |
| Titration | Increase by 5 mg every 3-5 days |
| Maximum Dose | 80 mg/day (divided TID-QID) |
| Typical Effective Dose | 40-80 mg/day |
| Side Effects | Sedation (most common), weakness, dizziness, cognitive impairment, nausea |
| Critical Warning | NEVER stop abruptly; taper gradually |
| Withdrawal Syndrome | Life-threatening: seizures, hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, multi-organ failure (resembles malignant hyperthermia/NMS) |

**Clinical Pearl:** Baclofen acts at the GABA-B receptor (not GABA-A, which is the target of benzodiazepines). The withdrawal syndrome is a medical emergency that can be fatal. This distinction and the withdrawal risk are heavily tested.

### 10.4.2 Tizanidine (Zanaflex)

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Drug Class | Central alpha-2 adrenergic agonist |
| Mechanism | Acts in the spinal cord to increase presynaptic inhibition of motor neurons; reduces release of excitatory amino acids from spinal interneurons |
| Site of Action | Central (spinal cord) |
| Indication | Generalized spasticity; may cause less weakness than baclofen |
| Starting Dose | 2-4 mg at bedtime |
| Titration | Increase by 2-4 mg every 3-7 days |
| Maximum Dose | 36 mg/day (divided TID) |
| Side Effects | Sedation, dry mouth, dizziness, HEPATOTOXICITY |
| Monitoring | Liver function tests at baseline, 1, 3, and 6 months, then periodically |
| Drug Interactions | Avoid CYP1A2 inhibitors (fluvoxamine, ciprofloxacin) |
| Discontinuation Warning | Rebound hypertension with abrupt discontinuation |

**Clinical Pearl:** Tizanidine is in the same receptor class as clonidine (alpha-2 agonist). The key distinguishing feature from baclofen is the hepatotoxicity risk requiring LFT monitoring. Tizanidine may cause less generalized weakness than baclofen, making it potentially preferable when strength preservation is important.

### 10.4.3 Dantrolene (Dantrium)

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Drug Class | Direct-acting muscle relaxant |
| Mechanism | Acts PERIPHERALLY at skeletal muscle; blocks calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum via the ryanodine receptor; reduces excitation-contraction coupling |
| Site of Action | Peripheral (muscle) -- the ONLY oral anti-spasticity agent that acts peripherally |
| Indication | Generalized spasticity; also treatment for malignant hyperthermia and neuroleptic malignant syndrome |
| Starting Dose | 25 mg daily |
| Titration | Increase by 25 mg every 4-7 days |
| Maximum Dose | 300 mg/day (divided QID) |
| Typical Dose | 100-300 mg/day |
| Side Effects | HEPATOTOXICITY (most serious; can be fatal), weakness, drowsiness, diarrhea |
| Monitoring | Liver function tests; discontinue if ALT rises significantly |

**Clinical Pearl:** Dantrolene is the ONLY oral anti-spasticity medication that acts peripherally (at the muscle, not the CNS). Because of its peripheral mechanism, it causes less sedation and cognitive impairment than baclofen or tizanidine, but it produces more generalized weakness. Dantrolene is also the treatment for malignant hyperthermia and NMS. These distinctions are among the most commonly tested spasticity pharmacology questions.

### 10.4.4 Gabapentin (Neurontin)

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Drug Class | GABA analog / calcium channel modulator |
| Mechanism | Binds alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels; reduces excitatory neurotransmitter release. Does NOT bind GABA receptors despite its name. |
| Site of Action | Central |
| Indication | Adjunctive for spasticity; particularly useful when neuropathic pain coexists with spasticity |
| Starting Dose | 100-300 mg TID |
| Effective Range | 900-3600 mg/day (divided TID) |
| Side Effects | Sedation, dizziness, ataxia, peripheral edema, weight gain |

**Clinical Pearl:** Gabapentin is a useful adjunct when spasticity and neuropathic pain coexist, as it addresses both conditions. Despite its name, gabapentin does NOT act at GABA receptors; it acts at alpha-2-delta calcium channel subunits.

### 10.4.5 Comparative Summary of Oral Anti-Spasticity Agents

| Feature | Baclofen | Tizanidine | Dantrolene | Gabapentin |
|---------|----------|------------|------------|------------|
| Receptor/Target | GABA-B | Alpha-2 adrenergic | Ryanodine receptor | Alpha-2-delta Ca2+ channel |
| Site of Action | Central | Central | Peripheral | Central |
| Sedation | +++ | +++ | + | ++ |
| Weakness | ++ | + | +++ | + |
| Hepatotoxicity | - | ++ | +++ | - |
| Withdrawal Risk | Severe (fatal) | Hypertension | Low | Low |
| Unique Feature | Most studied | Less weakness | Only peripheral agent; treats MH | Treats neuropathic pain |

## SECTION 10.5 [BOARD] FOCAL TREATMENT: BOTULINUM TOXIN

### 10.5.1 Mechanism of Action

Botulinum toxin is a chemodenervation agent that blocks acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), producing focal reduction in muscle tone. It is the treatment of choice for focal spasticity affecting one or a limited number of muscle groups.

**Mechanism:** Botulinum toxin enters the presynaptic nerve terminal and cleaves SNARE proteins (soluble N-ethylmaleimide-sensitive factor attachment protein receptors), which are essential for vesicle fusion and neurotransmitter release:
- **Type A toxins** cleave SNAP-25 (synaptosomal-associated protein of 25 kDa)
- **Type B toxin** (rimabotulinumtoxinB / Myobloc) cleaves VAMP/synaptobrevin

Without functional SNARE proteins, acetylcholine-containing vesicles cannot fuse with the presynaptic membrane, and neuromuscular transmission is blocked.

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Onset: 3-5 days post-injection
- Peak effect: 2-6 weeks
- Duration: approximately 3 months (recovery occurs as new nerve terminal sprouts regenerate functional NMJs)

### 10.5.2 Formulations and Conversion Ratios

Three botulinum toxin type A formulations are commercially available for spasticity treatment. Their doses are NOT interchangeable.

| Feature | OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) | AbobotulinumtoxinA (Dysport) | IncobotulinumtoxinA (Xeomin) |
|---------|---------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| FDA approval for spasticity | Upper AND lower limb | Upper AND lower limb | Upper limb |
| Complexing proteins | Yes (900 kDa complex) | Yes (500-900 kDa complex) | NO (150 kDa pure neurotoxin) |
| Approximate conversion to Botox | 1:1 (reference) | 2.5-3:1 (2.5-3 units Dysport per 1 unit Botox) | 1:1 |
| Storage | Refrigerated before reconstitution | Refrigerated before reconstitution | Room temperature |
| Immunogenicity | Low | Low | Theoretically lowest (no complexing proteins) |
| Onset | 3-5 days | 2-5 days (may be slightly faster) | 3-5 days |
| Duration | ~3 months | ~3 months | ~3 months |
| Max per session (spasticity) | 400 units (some experts use up to 600) | 1000-1500 units | 400 units |

**Clinical Pearl:** Although approximate conversion ratios are commonly cited (Botox:Dysport:Xeomin = 1:2.5-3:1), official labeling states that units should NOT be converted directly between formulations. Published systematic reviews suggest that a conversion ratio of abobotulinumtoxinA to onabotulinumtoxinA of 3:1, or even lower, may be appropriate for spasticity, but clinical judgment and individualized dosing remain essential (Dashtipour et al., 2016).

### 10.5.3 Injection Guidance Techniques

Accurate muscle targeting improves botulinum toxin efficacy. Four guidance techniques are available:

1. **Anatomic landmarks alone:** Least accurate; acceptable only for large, superficial muscles (e.g., biceps, gastrocnemius). Not recommended for deep or small muscles.
2. **Electromyography (EMG) guidance:** Motor unit activity confirms needle placement in the target muscle. Requires patient cooperation (voluntary muscle activation), which limits utility in TBI patients with impaired cognition or disorders of consciousness.
3. **Electrical stimulation (E-stim) guidance:** Low-amplitude current through the injection needle causes visible muscle contraction, confirming needle tip location. Does NOT require patient cooperation, making it particularly useful in TBI patients.
4. **Ultrasound guidance:** Real-time visualization of the needle tip, muscle, and surrounding structures. Allows avoidance of nerves and vessels. Does not require patient cooperation. Increasingly the preferred method.

**Evidence:** Guided injections (any modality) are more effective than landmark-only techniques for reducing spasticity. Ultrasound and electrical stimulation guidance particularly improve outcomes for deep muscle targets. In TBI populations, E-stim and ultrasound are preferred because they do not require patient cooperation (Grigoriu et al., 2015).

### 10.5.4 Antibody Formation and Treatment Failure

Repeated botulinum toxin injections can induce formation of neutralizing antibodies against the toxin protein, resulting in secondary non-response (treatment failure after initial success). Risk factors include higher doses, shorter inter-injection intervals, and booster doses.

IncobotulinumtoxinA (Xeomin) contains no complexing proteins (150 kDa pure neurotoxin), theoretically reducing immunogenicity. Clinicians may consider switching to Xeomin when secondary non-response suggests antibody development. The minimum recommended interval between botulinum toxin injection sessions is 12 weeks (3 months).

## SECTION 10.6 [BOARD] INTRATHECAL BACLOFEN THERAPY

### 10.6.1 Indications and Mechanism

Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) therapy delivers baclofen directly into the intrathecal (subarachnoid) space via a surgically implanted programmable pump. By bypassing the blood-brain barrier and delivering medication directly to the spinal cord, ITB therapy uses approximately 100 times less drug than oral administration (typical intrathecal dose: 50-800 mcg/day vs. oral: 40-80 mg/day = 40,000-80,000 mcg/day), dramatically reducing systemic side effects while achieving superior spasticity control.

**Indications:**
- Diffuse, severe spasticity refractory to oral medications
- Spasticity interfering with function, hygiene, positioning, or comfort
- Paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (storming) -- ITB can effectively reduce sympathetic storming episodes in severe TBI
- Intolerance of oral anti-spasticity medications (excessive sedation, cognitive impairment)

### 10.6.2 Trial Dose and Pump Implantation

Before permanent pump implantation, a trial dose is administered to confirm therapeutic response:

1. **Trial procedure:** 50-100 mcg of baclofen is administered as an intrathecal bolus via lumbar puncture.
2. **Response assessment:** Spasticity is evaluated at baseline and serially over 4-8 hours post-injection.
3. **Positive response:** Significant reduction in Modified Ashworth Scale scores (typically 1-2 grade improvement) or Penn Spasm Frequency Scale scores.
4. **If no response at 50 mcg:** The trial may be repeated at 75 mcg or 100 mcg on separate occasions.
5. **Pump implantation:** If the trial is positive, a programmable pump (typically SynchroMed II, Medtronic) is surgically implanted in a subcutaneous pocket in the abdominal wall, with a catheter tunneled subcutaneously and threaded intrathecally, typically to the T10-L1 spinal level for lower extremity spasticity or the C5-T2 level for combined upper and lower extremity spasticity.

### 10.6.3 Pump Mechanics and Maintenance

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Pump location | Subcutaneous pocket, typically lower abdominal wall |
| Catheter tip placement | T10-L1 (lower extremity spasticity); C5-T2 (four-extremity spasticity) |
| Delivery modes | Continuous infusion, flex-dose (variable rate by time of day), bolus |
| Refill schedule | Every 1-6 months depending on concentration and daily dose |
| Battery life | 5-7 years; requires surgical pump replacement |
| Reservoir volume | 20 mL or 40 mL |
| Concentration | 500-2000 mcg/mL |
| Typical starting dose | 50-100 mcg/day (approximately double the effective trial dose) |
| Dose titration | 10-25% increases at intervals of no less than 24 hours in acute setting; monthly adjustments for chronic management |

### 10.6.4 ITB Complications

ITB pump complications are among the most tested topics in PM&R board examinations. They are categorized as follows:

| Category | Specific Complications | Presentation | Management |
|----------|----------------------|--------------|------------|
| Catheter-related | Kink, fracture, migration, disconnection, occlusion | Gradual or acute loss of efficacy; withdrawal symptoms; return of spasticity | Imaging (CT myelogram, catheter access study with contrast); surgical catheter revision |
| Pump pocket | Infection, seroma, CSF leak, skin erosion, pump flip/migration | Erythema, swelling, warmth, drainage, palpable CSF collection, pump rotated | Antibiotics; wound care; surgical revision; pump replacement if infected |
| ITB OVERDOSE | Programming error, refill error, catheter malposition | Flaccidity, drowsiness, respiratory depression, coma, bradycardia, hypotension | Stop pump delivery; supportive care (airway protection, mechanical ventilation); CSF withdrawal via LP (30-40 mL) to reduce baclofen load; physostigmine reported but not standard |
| ITB WITHDRAWAL | Catheter failure, missed refill, pump malfunction, battery depletion | Severe rebound spasticity, hyperthermia >40C, altered mental status, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, DIC, multi-organ failure | EMERGENCY: see below |

### 10.6.5 [BOARD] ITB Withdrawal: A Medical Emergency

ITB withdrawal is one of the most dangerous complications in rehabilitation medicine. If unrecognized or improperly managed, it carries significant mortality, with case reports and case series documenting death from multi-organ failure.

**Pathophysiology:** Abrupt cessation of intrathecal baclofen delivery removes GABA-B-mediated inhibition of spinal motor neurons. The resulting unopposed excitatory activity produces a syndrome that closely resembles malignant hyperthermia and neuroleptic malignant syndrome.

**Clinical Features:**
- Severe rebound spasticity and rigidity
- Hyperthermia (temperature >40C / 104F)
- Altered mental status progressing to coma
- Rhabdomyolysis (markedly elevated CK, myoglobinuria, risk of acute renal failure)
- Seizures
- Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
- Autonomic instability (tachycardia, labile blood pressure)
- Multi-organ failure
- Death

**Reported mortality:** Mortality from ITB withdrawal is significant but variable across studies. Larger retrospective cohorts report mortality rates of approximately 0--4% with prompt recognition and management, though individual small case series have reported rates as high as 22% (Coffey et al., 2002; Ross et al., 2011). Full clinical recovery occurs in approximately 97--98% of cases when treated promptly. The prognosis is significantly worse when recognition and treatment are delayed.

**Emergency Management Protocol:**

1. **Restore intrathecal baclofen delivery** as rapidly as possible (troubleshoot pump/catheter system, contact pump management team).
2. **High-dose oral/enteral baclofen:** 40-120 mg/day in divided doses (though oral baclofen crosses the blood-brain barrier poorly, high doses can partially compensate).
3. **Intravenous benzodiazepines:** Diazepam or lorazepam for seizure control and muscle relaxation.
4. **Intravenous dantrolene:** For hyperthermia (same dosing protocol as malignant hyperthermia: 1-2.5 mg/kg IV, may repeat to 10 mg/kg).
5. **Cyproheptadine:** Serotonin antagonist that may help reduce sympathetic hyperactivity.
6. **ICU admission:** Continuous monitoring, aggressive cooling measures, IV hydration, monitoring for rhabdomyolysis (CK, renal function), DIC screening.
7. **Supportive care:** Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation, urine alkalinization for myoglobinuria, electrolyte monitoring and correction.

**Clinical Pearl:** The triad of ITB withdrawal, malignant hyperthermia, and NMS share overlapping clinical features (hyperthermia, rigidity, altered mental status, rhabdomyolysis) and are treated similarly (dantrolene, benzodiazepines, cooling, supportive care). The history and context distinguish them: ITB withdrawal follows pump/catheter malfunction; MH follows exposure to inhalational anesthetics or succinylcholine; NMS follows exposure to dopamine-blocking agents.

### 10.6.6 [BOARD] ITB Overdose

ITB overdose is the opposite clinical scenario, occurring when excessive baclofen is delivered intrathecally.

**Causes:** Programming errors, refill errors (wrong concentration), catheter migration to a position delivering drug more rostrally than intended.

**Clinical Features:**
- Profound hypotonia / flaccidity
- Drowsiness progressing to obtundation and coma
- Respiratory depression (the most immediately life-threatening feature)
- Bradycardia
- Hypotension

**Management:**
- Stop pump delivery immediately (pump can be programmed to stop; maximum pause = 48 hours for SynchroMed pumps)
- Ensure airway protection; intubate and mechanically ventilate if necessary
- CSF withdrawal via lumbar puncture (30-40 mL) to physically remove baclofen from the intrathecal space
- Supportive care in ICU
- There is no specific antidote; physostigmine has been reported in case reports but is not established therapy
- Atropine for symptomatic bradycardia

## SECTION 10.7 [COMPREHENSIVE] SERIAL CASTING

### 10.7.1 Principles and Indications

Serial casting is a well-established technique for treating dynamic contractures that have a significant spastic component. It applies prolonged, low-load stretch to shortened muscles and soft tissues, gradually increasing range of motion over successive cast applications.

**Indications:**
- Dynamic contractures with a significant spastic component (R2 - R1 difference on Tardieu suggesting treatable dynamic tone)
- Most commonly applied to ankle equinus, elbow flexion, and knee flexion contractures
- Combined with botulinum toxin for enhanced effect ("inject and cast" technique)

**Contraindications:**
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Severe edema
- Open wounds in the casting area
- Heterotopic ossification with joint ankylosis
- DVT in the affected limb
- Unstable fractures

### 10.7.2 Protocol

1. **Pre-casting assessment:** Tardieu Scale assessment to confirm significant dynamic component; neurovascular examination; skin inspection.
2. **Cast application:** Well-padded fiberglass cast applied with the joint positioned at the maximum tolerable stretch angle. Generous padding over bony prominences. Bivalved cast may be used if edema fluctuation is anticipated.
3. **Cast change interval:** Every 5-7 days (some protocols use 7-10 days). At each change, the cast is removed, the skin is inspected, range of motion is reassessed, and a new cast is applied at a progressively increased stretch angle.
4. **Typical course:** 3-6 cast changes (3-6 weeks total), depending on contracture severity and rate of response.
5. **Endpoint:** Casting is discontinued when desired ROM is achieved or when no further gains are made between cast changes (plateau).
6. **Post-casting maintenance:** Immediately transition to a resting splint or orthosis to maintain gains. Without maintenance orthotic use, contracture will rapidly recur.

### 10.7.3 "Inject and Cast" Technique

The combination of botulinum toxin injection followed by serial casting is more effective than either intervention alone, particularly for ankle equinus contracture.

**Protocol:**
1. Inject botulinum toxin into the target muscles (e.g., gastrocnemius, soleus for ankle equinus).
2. Wait 3-7 days for the toxin to begin taking effect (reducing the active, neural component of tone).
3. Apply the first serial cast while the toxin is producing chemodenervation.
4. Continue serial cast changes every 5-7 days as described above.

**Rationale:** Botulinum toxin reduces the active spastic component (neural), allowing the cast to apply sustained stretch primarily against the passive structural component (muscle shortening, fibrosis). This synergistic approach produces greater ROM gains than either modality alone (Booth et al., 2003).

**Monitoring:**
- Skin integrity at each cast change
- Neurovascular status (pulses, capillary refill, sensation distal to cast)
- Edema
- Compartment syndrome (rare but potentially devastating)

## SECTION 10.8 [COMPREHENSIVE] CONTRACTURE MANAGEMENT ALGORITHM

Contracture management follows a stepwise algorithm from least to most invasive interventions:

### Prevention (Tier 1)
- Daily stretching and range of motion exercises (minimum 20-30 minutes per session per affected joint)
- Positioning programs (splints, orthoses, proper bed and wheelchair positioning)
- Weight-bearing and standing programs (tilt table, standing frame)
- Early spasticity management with oral medications

### Conservative Treatment (Tier 2)
- Prolonged stretching with sustained positioning devices
- Dynamic splinting
- Cryotherapy (temporary reduction through decreased nerve conduction velocity)
- Electrical stimulation of antagonist muscles (reciprocal inhibition)

### Serial Casting (Tier 3)
- For dynamic contractures with significant spastic component
- Typically combined with botulinum toxin ("inject and cast")
- 3-6 week protocol with weekly cast changes

### Chemodenervation (Tier 4)
- Botulinum toxin for focal spasticity contributing to contracture
- Phenol/alcohol neurolysis for select motor nerve targets
- Often combined with casting and aggressive stretching

### Surgical Intervention (Tier 5)
- Reserved for fixed contractures that have failed all conservative measures
- Tendon lengthening, release, or transfer
- Typically delayed until 12-18 months post-injury to allow neurologic plateau

**Clinical Pearl:** The algorithm is driven by the Tardieu Scale findings. A large R2-R1 difference (predominantly spasticity) directs treatment toward pharmacologic and chemodenervation approaches (Tiers 2-4). A small R2-R1 difference (predominantly contracture) directs treatment toward mechanical (casting) and surgical approaches (Tiers 3, 5).

## SECTION 10.9 [COMPREHENSIVE] PHENOL AND ALCOHOL NEUROLYSIS

### 10.9.1 Mechanism and Pharmacology

Phenol (5-7% aqueous solution) and ethyl alcohol (45-100%) produce chemical neurolysis through protein denaturation and destruction of nerve fibers at the injection site. Unlike botulinum toxin, which produces a reversible biochemical blockade, phenol/alcohol cause structural nerve damage with subsequent Wallerian degeneration.

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Onset: Immediate to within 24 hours (a major advantage over botulinum toxin)
- Duration: 3-12 months (generally longer than botulinum toxin)
- Nerve regeneration occurs over months, leading to return of tone

### 10.9.2 Technique

Phenol neurolysis is typically performed with electrical stimulation and/or ultrasound guidance. A stimulating needle is advanced toward the target nerve. When electrical stimulation at low current (0.2-0.5 mA) produces a brisk motor response in the target muscle, the needle tip is confirmed to be in proximity to the motor nerve, and phenol is injected.

**Commonly targeted nerves:**

| Target Nerve | Muscles Affected | Clinical Indication |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Obturator nerve | Hip adductors | Scissoring gait, hygiene difficulty, perineal care |
| Musculocutaneous nerve | Biceps, brachialis | Elbow flexor spasticity |
| Tibial nerve (motor branches) | Gastrocnemius, soleus, tibialis posterior | Equinovarus deformity |
| Pectoral nerves | Pectoralis major | Shoulder adduction/internal rotation |

### 10.9.3 Advantages vs. Botulinum Toxin

| Feature | Phenol/Alcohol Neurolysis | Botulinum Toxin |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Onset | Immediate | 3-5 days |
| Duration | 3-12 months | ~3 months |
| Cost | Significantly lower | Higher |
| Antibody formation | No | Possible with repeated injections |
| Dose ceiling | No systemic limit | Maximum doses per session apply |
| Repeat treatments | Can inject same nerve repeatedly | May develop tolerance |
| Major risk | Painful dysesthesias (mixed nerves) | Local weakness, rare systemic effects |
| Best targets | Pure motor nerves | Any muscle (including those with mixed innervation) |

### 10.9.4 Complications

The principal complication of phenol/alcohol neurolysis is **painful dysesthesias** when mixed (sensory and motor) nerves are injected. Destruction of sensory fibers produces burning pain, paresthesias, and hypersensitivity in the nerve's sensory distribution. This risk limits the use of phenol to nerves that are predominantly motor.

However, recent studies using combined ultrasound and electrical stimulation guidance have reported dysesthesia rates as low as 0.7%, suggesting that improved targeting of motor branches significantly mitigates this risk (Karri et al., 2017).

**Clinical Pearl:** Phenol is preferred for pure motor nerves (obturator, musculocutaneous) where the dysesthesia risk is minimal. Use botulinum toxin for muscles innervated by mixed sensory-motor nerves. Phenol injection of the obturator nerve for hip adductor spasticity is a classic board scenario. The obturator nerve is the most commonly targeted nerve for phenol neurolysis, accounting for approximately 36% of all phenol procedures in large clinical series (Karri et al., 2017).

## SECTION 10.10 [COMPREHENSIVE] COMMON CONTRACTURE PATTERNS AFTER TBI

The distribution of spasticity and resulting contracture patterns after TBI follows predictable UMN patterns:

| Joint | Typical Pattern | Key Muscles Involved | Functional Impact |
|-------|----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Shoulder | Adduction, internal rotation | Pectoralis major, subscapularis, latissimus dorsi | Hygiene, dressing, skin breakdown in axilla |
| Elbow | Flexion | Biceps brachii, brachialis, brachioradialis | Reaching, hygiene of antecubital fossa |
| Forearm | Pronation | Pronator teres, pronator quadratus | Hand function, grasping |
| Wrist | Flexion | Flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris | Hand function, hygiene |
| Hand | Clenched fist (finger flexion, thumb-in-palm) | Flexor digitorum superficialis/profundus, adductor pollicis | Hygiene, skin maceration, pressure injury in palm |
| Hip | Flexion, adduction, internal rotation | Iliopsoas, adductors, medial hamstrings | Gait, transfers, perineal hygiene |
| Knee | Flexion (less commonly extension) | Hamstrings (quadriceps for extension pattern) | Gait, wheelchair positioning |
| Ankle | Equinovarus (plantarflexion + inversion) | Gastrocnemius, soleus, tibialis posterior | Gait, balance, footwear, falls |

**Clinical Pearl:** Equinovarus is the most common lower extremity spasticity pattern after TBI and stroke. The gastrocnemius-soleus complex produces equinus (plantarflexion) while tibialis posterior produces varus (inversion). This combined deformity significantly impairs gait, prevents heel strike, and increases fall risk. Treatment typically includes an AFO, botulinum toxin to the gastrocnemius/soleus and tibialis posterior, and serial casting. The SPLATT (split anterior tibialis tendon transfer) procedure is a surgical option for dynamic varus deformity when the tibialis anterior is a significant contributor.

## SECTION 10.11 [COMPREHENSIVE] SURGICAL OPTIONS

### 10.11.1 Indications and Timing

Surgical intervention for spasticity-related contractures is generally reserved for:
- Fixed contractures (small R2-R1 on Tardieu) that impair function or hygiene
- Failure of all conservative and minimally invasive interventions
- Patients who have reached neurologic plateau (typically 12-18 months post-injury)

Premature surgical intervention is avoided because spontaneous neurologic recovery during the first 12-18 months may render planned procedures unnecessary or may change the clinical picture sufficiently to alter the surgical plan.

### 10.11.2 Surgical Procedures

**Tendon Lengthening:**
- Z-lengthening or fractional lengthening of contracted tendons
- Most commonly performed on the Achilles tendon (for equinus), hamstrings (for knee flexion contracture), and hip flexors (for hip flexion contracture)
- Fractional lengthening preserves more muscle function than Z-lengthening

**Tendon Transfers:**
- Redirect muscle force to improve function
- SPLATT (Split Anterior Tibialis Tendon Transfer): The lateral half of the anterior tibialis tendon is transferred to the cuboid or lateral cuneiform, converting a dynamic varus deformity to a balanced foot during swing phase
- Indicated for dynamic varus deformity due to the anterior tibialis muscle

**Tendon Release:**
- Complete release of contracted tendons when lengthening is insufficient
- Typically performed for severely contracted finger flexors or hip adductors

**Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (SDR):**
- Selective sectioning of dorsal (sensory) nerve rootlets at the spinal cord level
- Reduces afferent input to the stretch reflex arc, thereby reducing spasticity
- More commonly used in cerebral palsy than in TBI
- Requires careful patient selection and intact voluntary motor function

**Joint Arthrodesis:**
- Surgical fusion for severely deformed, painful joints with fixed contracture
- Used as a salvage procedure when joint function is not recoverable

**Clinical Pearl:** Surgical interventions are generally reserved for FIXED contractures (small R2-R1 on Tardieu) that impair function or hygiene. Dynamic spasticity (large R2-R1) should be managed with medications, chemodenervation, and casting before considering surgery. Timing is critical: surgery is typically delayed 12-18 months post-injury to allow neurologic plateau and maximum spontaneous recovery.

## SECTION 10.12 [COMPREHENSIVE] MOTOR RECOVERY AFTER TBI

### 10.12.1 UMN vs. LMN Patterns

Motor deficits after TBI are predominantly upper motor neuron in nature, resulting from damage to the motor cortex, subcortical white matter, internal capsule, brainstem, or descending corticospinal/corticoreticular tracts. However, concurrent peripheral nerve injuries and cranial nerve palsies can produce superimposed lower motor neuron findings.

**UMN Pattern (Most Common in TBI):**
- Weakness (paresis) with preserved or increased deep tendon reflexes
- Spasticity (velocity-dependent increase in tone)
- Hyperreflexia and clonus
- Babinski sign (extensor plantar response)
- Spastic co-contraction (simultaneous agonist/antagonist activation)
- No significant muscle atrophy in early stages (disuse atrophy may develop later)

**LMN Pattern (Less Common; Indicates Peripheral Injury):**
- Weakness (paresis/paralysis) with decreased or absent deep tendon reflexes
- Flaccidity (reduced muscle tone)
- Hyporeflexia or areflexia
- Fasciculations and fibrillations (on EMG)
- Early and significant muscle atrophy
- No Babinski sign

**Combined UMN/LMN Patterns:**
TBI patients may exhibit mixed findings when central and peripheral injuries coexist. Examples include:
- TBI with concurrent brachial plexus injury (e.g., from the mechanism of injury)
- TBI with peripheral nerve injuries from associated extremity fractures
- TBI with cranial nerve palsies (facial nerve, hypoglossal nerve)
- Critical illness polyneuropathy developing during prolonged ICU course

### 10.12.2 Prognostic Factors for Motor Recovery

Motor recovery after TBI is influenced by multiple patient-specific and injury-specific factors:

**Positive Prognostic Factors:**
- Younger age (greater neuroplasticity)
- Milder initial injury severity (higher GCS)
- Shorter duration of post-traumatic amnesia
- Early motor recovery (motor function returning within the first 3 months is favorable)
- Absence of brainstem injury
- Diffuse injury pattern (may recover more than focal lesions in critical motor pathways)

**Negative Prognostic Factors:**
- Older age
- Severe initial injury (GCS less than or equal to 5)
- Prolonged post-traumatic amnesia (>4 weeks)
- Bilateral motor cortex or brainstem involvement
- Persistent spasticity despite treatment
- Associated peripheral nerve injury
- Medical complications (prolonged mechanical ventilation, ICU stay, critical illness polyneuropathy)

### 10.12.3 Neuroplasticity and Motor Recovery

Recovery after TBI occurs through neuroplastic mechanisms that include both neural regeneration and functional reorganization:

1. **Diaschisis resolution:** Recovery of neural function in brain regions that were functionally suppressed but not structurally damaged by the initial injury (von Monakow's diaschisis).
2. **Synaptic reorganization:** Strengthening of existing synaptic connections and formation of new synaptic contacts in peri-lesional and remote cortical areas.
3. **Cortical remapping:** Motor representations in the intact cortex can reorganize to assume functions previously subserved by damaged regions.
4. **Axonal sprouting:** New axonal connections can form in the perilesional area and across hemispheres, contributing to functional compensation.
5. **Neurogenesis:** Limited new neuron generation occurs in the adult brain (primarily in the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus and the subventricular zone), though its contribution to motor recovery is uncertain.

**Critical Window for Recovery:**

After TBI, the brain undergoes a period of heightened plasticity, often referred to as the "critical window" of recovery. During this period, which is most robust in the first 3-6 months post-injury but may extend to 12-18 months or beyond, intensive rehabilitation can maximize functional gains by leveraging the brain's increased capacity for reorganization (Nudo, 2013).

**Clinical Pearl:** Motor recovery after TBI is more heterogeneous and often more favorable than motor recovery after stroke, because TBI typically causes diffuse rather than focal injury. Diffuse axonal injury may spare some motor pathways even when consciousness is severely impaired, allowing for motor recovery as consciousness improves.

## SECTION 10.13 [COMPREHENSIVE] EMERGING TREATMENTS

### 10.13.1 Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation (NMES)

NMES involves application of electrical current to motor nerves or motor points to produce muscle contraction. In spasticity management, NMES is applied to antagonist muscles to achieve reciprocal inhibition of spastic agonists. The evidence base is growing but not yet definitive.

**Applications:**
- Stimulation of wrist and finger extensors to reduce flexor spasticity
- Stimulation of ankle dorsiflexors to reduce plantarflexor spasticity
- Functional electrical stimulation (FES) for task-specific training during gait

### 10.13.2 Robotic-Assisted Rehabilitation

Robotic devices provide high-intensity, repetitive, task-specific motor training with consistent kinematic parameters. Examples include the Armeo (upper extremity), Lokomat (gait training), and Amadeo (hand/finger training).

**Potential benefits:**
- High repetition of task-specific movements
- Consistent, measurable training parameters
- Reduced therapist burden for intensive training protocols
- Real-time feedback and adaptive difficulty adjustment

**Current evidence:** Robotic therapy combined with conventional rehabilitation may improve motor outcomes compared to conventional rehabilitation alone, though the effect sizes are modest and the optimal patient selection criteria remain under investigation (Mehrholz et al., 2018).

### 10.13.3 Vibration Therapy

Whole-body vibration (WBV) and focal muscle vibration are emerging modalities for spasticity reduction:

- **Whole-body vibration:** Patient stands on a vibrating platform (20-50 Hz). Acute reduction in spasticity has been demonstrated in multiple studies, though the duration of effect is limited (minutes to hours).
- **Focal vibration:** Applied directly to the tendon or muscle belly of spastic muscles. May reduce H-reflex amplitude and spastic tone through modulation of Ia afferent input.
- **Limitations:** Effects are typically transient; long-term benefit with repeated sessions requires further investigation.

### 10.13.4 Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS)

Non-invasive brain stimulation using weak electrical currents (1-2 mA) applied to the scalp via surface electrodes. Preliminary studies suggest that anodal tDCS over the motor cortex, combined with rehabilitation, may enhance motor recovery and reduce spasticity after TBI, though large randomized controlled trials are needed.

### 10.13.5 Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (ESWT)

Focused or radial shock waves applied to spastic muscles have shown promise for reducing spasticity in pilot studies. The mechanism may involve modulation of neuromuscular junction function, alteration of muscle viscoelastic properties, and reduction of fibrosis. This modality remains investigational for TBI-related spasticity.

## SECTION 10.14 FOCAL VS. GENERALIZED SPASTICITY: TREATMENT SELECTION ALGORITHM

| Pattern | Best Approach | Examples |
|---------|--------------|----------|
| Focal (1-2 muscles) | Botulinum toxin or phenol neurolysis | Isolated ankle equinus, clenched fist, elbow flexion |
| Regional (one limb) | Botulinum toxin + oral medications | Hemiplegic upper limb spasticity pattern |
| Generalized (multiple limbs) | Oral medications; consider ITB pump | Bilateral severe spasticity affecting four extremities |
| Generalized + refractory | ITB pump | Failed oral medications; significant functional or hygiene impairment |
| Fixed contracture | Serial casting, surgical options | No dynamic component on Tardieu (small R2-R1) |
