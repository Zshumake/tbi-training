# Chapter 14: The TBI Rehabilitation Continuum

---

## Introduction

Rehabilitation after traumatic brain injury follows a structured continuum that begins in the intensive care unit and extends through community reintegration, a process that may span months to years and engage virtually every domain of medical, cognitive, psychosocial, and vocational functioning. The physiatrist serves as the central coordinator of this continuum, guiding patients through successive levels of care, directing interdisciplinary teams, managing complex medical comorbidities, and advocating for the resources needed to maximize functional independence and quality of life.

This chapter provides a comprehensive treatment of the TBI rehabilitation continuum, from acute care management through long-term community integration. It addresses the evidence base for cognitive rehabilitation, the management of neurogenic bowel and bladder, dysphagia and nutrition, vocational and driving rehabilitation, sexuality, caregiver burden, prognostic tools, and the ethical considerations that pervade TBI care. The content is organized to serve both clinical practice and board preparation, with critical distinctions between board-essential facts and comprehensive clinical knowledge.

## Part I: The Continuum of Care

---

### 14.1 Overview of the Rehabilitation Continuum [BOARD]

The TBI rehabilitation continuum comprises a series of interconnected care settings, each defined by the intensity of services, level of medical supervision, and rehabilitative goals. The appropriate placement of patients within this continuum is determined by injury severity, functional status, medical stability, and capacity to participate in and benefit from therapy.

#### 14.1.1 Levels of Care

| Level of Care | Setting | Therapy Intensity | Medical Supervision | Typical Patient Profile |
|--------------|---------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Acute care | ICU/hospital | Minimal; prevention-focused | 24-hour physician, nursing | Unstable, medically complex; ICP management; ventilator |
| Acute inpatient rehabilitation (IRF) | Rehabilitation hospital or unit | ≥3 hours/day, ≥2 disciplines | Physiatrist face-to-face ≥3x/week | Medically stable; can tolerate intensive therapy; requires interdisciplinary approach |
| Subacute rehabilitation (SNF) | Skilled nursing facility | 1-2 hours/day | Physician oversight (less frequent) | Cannot tolerate 3 hours/day; needs nursing care; slower recovery |
| Long-term acute care (LTAC) | Specialized hospital | Variable | 24-hour physician | Ventilator weaning; complex wounds; medically complex but not IRF-ready |
| Day treatment/Day program | Outpatient center | 4-6 hours/day, 3-5 days/week | Physician oversight | Lives at home or supported housing; needs intensive structured program |
| Residential rehabilitation | Group home or supervised apartment | Structured programming with 24-hour staff | Physician oversight | Needs supervision and structure; behavioral or cognitive support; not independent |
| Outpatient rehabilitation | Clinic or private practice | Individual sessions 1-5x/week | Referral-based | Independent transportation; adequate home support; can function between sessions |
| Home health | Patient's residence | Individual sessions in home | Referral-based | Homebound; transportation barriers; less intensive needs |
| Community integration | Community settings | Variable | Variable | Social, vocational, and recreational participation goals |

**Clinical Pearl:** The continuum is not strictly linear. Patients may move between levels in either direction as their medical and functional status changes. A patient who is progressing in outpatient rehabilitation may require readmission to an IRF after a seizure or fall, while a patient in an LTAC may transition to an IRF when ventilator weaning is achieved.

---

### 14.2 Inpatient Rehabilitation Criteria [BOARD]

Admission to an acute inpatient rehabilitation facility (IRF) is governed by specific criteria established by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS). Understanding these criteria is essential for board preparation and clinical practice.

#### 14.2.1 CMS Admission Criteria for IRF

The following criteria must be met for IRF admission:

1. **Medical stability:** The patient must be medically stable enough to participate in rehabilitation without requiring acute care level monitoring (although IRFs can manage many medical issues including tracheostomy, PEG tubes, and intravenous medications).

2. **Therapy tolerance:** The patient must be able to tolerate at least **3 hours of therapy per day** (or demonstrate the ability to benefit from intensive rehabilitation even if initially unable to tolerate the full 3 hours — a "modified intensity" admission may be justified).

3. **Two or more therapy disciplines:** The patient must require services from **two or more therapy disciplines** (physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language pathology, neuropsychology, prosthetics/orthotics, therapeutic recreation).

4. **Physician supervision:** The patient must require physician supervision, including face-to-face visits at least **3 days per week** by a rehabilitation physician.

5. **Interdisciplinary team approach:** The patient must require a coordinated interdisciplinary team approach with documented team conferences.

6. **Expectation of functional improvement:** The patient must demonstrate potential for meaningful functional gains within a reasonable time frame.

7. **Cannot be adequately served at a lower level of care:** The patient's rehabilitation needs cannot be met by a skilled nursing facility, home health, or outpatient services.

#### 14.2.2 The 60% Rule

CMS requires that at least 60% of an IRF's patient population have one of 13 qualifying diagnostic conditions. TBI is one of these qualifying conditions. Compliance with the 60% rule is assessed on a hospital-wide basis, not on a per-patient basis.

**Clinical Pearl — The Board Answer:** The simplified board answer for IRF admission criteria is: medically stable, tolerates 3 hours of therapy per day, requires 2 or more therapy disciplines, requires physician supervision (face-to-face 3x/week), and interdisciplinary team approach. "3 hours, 2 disciplines" is the most commonly tested element.

### 14.3 Interdisciplinary Team Composition and Roles [BOARD]

The interdisciplinary rehabilitation team is the cornerstone of TBI rehabilitation. Unlike a multidisciplinary team (where disciplines work in parallel with separate goals), an interdisciplinary team collaborates with shared goals, joint treatment planning, and integrated care delivery.

| Team Member | Primary Focus | Key Contributions in TBI |
|------------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Physiatrist (PM&R physician)** | Team leader; medical management | Medication management, spasticity treatment, seizure management, complication prevention, team coordination, family conferences, functional prognosis, discharge planning |
| **Physical Therapist (PT)** | Mobility and motor function | Gait training, balance assessment and training, transfers, wheelchair assessment, exercise prescription, vestibular rehabilitation, assistive device training |
| **Occupational Therapist (OT)** | Activities of daily living and upper extremity function | Self-care training (feeding, dressing, bathing, grooming), cognitive rehabilitation in functional context, visual-perceptual training, home modification assessment, adaptive equipment prescription |
| **Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP)** | Communication and swallowing | Language therapy, cognitive-communication rehabilitation, dysphagia evaluation and management (VFSS, FEES), voice therapy, augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices |
| **Neuropsychologist** | Cognitive assessment and behavioral management | Neuropsychological testing, cognitive rehabilitation program design, behavioral management plans, capacity evaluations, family education about cognitive deficits |
| **Rehabilitation Nurse** | 24-hour care coordination | Medication administration, bowel and bladder programs, skin integrity monitoring, fall prevention, patient and family education, behavioral management |
| **Social Worker / Case Manager** | Discharge planning and psychosocial support | Insurance authorization, community resource identification, family counseling, placement coordination, financial assistance, advance directive facilitation |
| **Therapeutic Recreation Specialist** | Leisure and community reintegration | Adapted recreational activities, community outing skills, social participation, leisure interest development, coping skills |
| **Rehabilitation Psychologist** | Emotional adjustment and coping | Individual psychotherapy, group therapy, adjustment to disability counseling, family therapy, pain management |
| **Certified Rehabilitation Counselor** | Vocational planning | Vocational assessment, job placement, workplace accommodation planning, benefits counseling |
| **Registered Dietitian** | Nutritional management | Caloric and protein optimization for hypermetabolic TBI patients, enteral formula selection, tube feeding management, weight management |
| **Orthotist** | Orthotic devices | Ankle-foot orthoses, resting hand splints, serial casting for contractures |

**Clinical Pearl:** The interdisciplinary team conference is a hallmark of inpatient rehabilitation. Conferences are held at least weekly (typically weekly or biweekly) and involve all disciplines discussing a shared set of goals, patient progress, barriers, and the plan for the coming week. This is distinct from multidisciplinary care, where disciplines may work with separate goals and communicate primarily through the chart.

### 14.4 FIM Efficiency in TBI Rehabilitation [BOARD]

The Functional Independence Measure (FIM) is the most widely used standardized functional assessment instrument in rehabilitation medicine. It serves as the primary outcome metric for CMS quality reporting and is central to understanding rehabilitation efficiency and outcomes.

#### 14.4.1 FIM Structure

The FIM comprises 18 items across two domains:

**Motor Domain (13 items):**
- Self-care: eating, grooming, bathing, upper body dressing, lower body dressing, toileting
- Sphincter control: bladder management, bowel management
- Transfers: bed/chair/wheelchair, toilet, tub/shower
- Locomotion: walk/wheelchair, stairs

**Cognitive Domain (5 items):**
- Communication: comprehension, expression
- Social cognition: social interaction, problem solving, memory

Each item is scored on a 7-point ordinal scale:

| Score | Level of Independence | Description |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|
| 7 | Complete independence | Performs safely without modification, assistive devices, or supervision; performs in reasonable time |
| 6 | Modified independence | Requires assistive device, extra time, or safety concerns |
| 5 | Supervision/Setup | Requires standby assistance, cueing, coaxing, or setup |
| 4 | Minimal assistance | Patient performs ≥75% of the task |
| 3 | Moderate assistance | Patient performs 50-74% of the task |
| 2 | Maximal assistance | Patient performs 25-49% of the task |
| 1 | Total assistance | Patient performs <25% of the task or is not testable |

- **Total score range:** 18 (total dependence) to 126 (complete independence)
- **Motor subscale:** 13-91
- **Cognitive subscale:** 5-35

#### 14.4.2 FIM Efficiency

FIM efficiency (also called rehabilitation efficiency) is calculated as:

> **FIM Efficiency = (Discharge FIM - Admission FIM) / Length of Stay in days**

This metric quantifies the rate of functional recovery per day of rehabilitation. For TBI patients, typical FIM efficiency ranges from 0.5-2.0 FIM points per day, varying substantially with injury severity, age, and comorbidities. TBI patients demonstrate the greatest percentage FIM improvement during inpatient rehabilitation of any diagnostic group (approximately 53% improvement).

#### 14.4.3 FIM Limitations in TBI

While the FIM is the standard functional outcome measure in rehabilitation, it has recognized limitations in the TBI population:

1. **Ceiling effects:** Many TBI patients, particularly those with mild-moderate injuries, may achieve maximum FIM scores while still having significant cognitive and behavioral impairments that limit community function
2. **Insensitivity to cognitive deficits:** The 5-item cognitive subscale does not adequately capture the range and severity of executive dysfunction, processing speed deficits, and social cognition impairments common in TBI
3. **Floor effects:** Patients in minimally conscious or vegetative states score at the floor of the FIM, limiting its utility for tracking small but meaningful changes in this population

**Clinical Pearl:** The FIM has been largely superseded by the Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility-Patient Assessment Instrument (IRF-PAI) for CMS quality reporting, which includes quality indicators derived from Section GG (self-care and mobility items). However, the FIM remains the most referenced functional outcome measure in TBI rehabilitation literature and is the primary metric used by the TBIMS National Database.

**References:**
1. Hall KM, Hamilton BB, Gordon WA, Zasler ND. Characteristics and comparisons of functional assessment indices: Disability Rating Scale, Functional Independence Measure, and Functional Assessment Measure. *J Head Trauma Rehabil*. 1993;8(2):60-74.
2. Granger CV, Divan N, Fiedler RC. Functional assessment scales: a study of persons after traumatic brain injury. *Am J Phys Med Rehabil*. 1995;74(2):107-113.

### 14.5 Acute Care Management Priorities [COMPREHENSIVE]

The acute care phase of TBI management focuses on preventing secondary brain injury, maintaining physiologic stability, and initiating early rehabilitation interventions. The physiatrist should be consulted within 24-48 hours of admission for patients with moderate-to-severe TBI.

#### 14.5.1 DVT Prophylaxis

TBI patients have one of the highest DVT rates of any hospitalized population, with an incidence of approximately **54% without prophylaxis**. This is a frequently tested board statistic.

- **Sequential compression devices (SCDs):** Initiated immediately upon admission
- **Pharmacologic prophylaxis:** Low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) or unfractionated heparin (UFH) initiated when deemed safe by neurosurgery — typically 24-72 hours after stable neuroimaging
- **Pulmonary embolism** is a significant cause of preventable death in TBI patients
- **IVC filter placement** may be considered in patients with contraindications to pharmacologic prophylaxis, though evidence of benefit is limited

#### 14.5.2 Nutrition

TBI patients are hypermetabolic, with caloric expenditure reaching approximately 140% of resting metabolic rate.

- **Protein requirements:** 1.5-2 g/kg/day (significantly higher than the standard 0.8 g/kg/day)
- **Caloric goal:** 140% of resting metabolic expenditure (indirect calorimetry preferred for accurate measurement)
- **Replace at least 15% of calories as protein by day 7**
- **BTF 4th Edition recommendation:** Feeding should be initiated by at least day 5 and at most day 7 post-injury to decrease mortality (Level IIA)
- **Transgastric jejunal feeding** is recommended to reduce ventilator-associated pneumonia (Level IIB)
- Early nutrition is associated with improved outcomes, reduced infection rates, and shorter ICU stays

#### 14.5.3 Seizure Prophylaxis

- **Phenytoin or levetiracetam** for 7 days prevents early post-traumatic seizures (within the first 7 days) but does NOT prevent late post-traumatic seizures or epilepsy
- Levetiracetam is increasingly preferred due to a more favorable side effect profile (no need for drug level monitoring, fewer drug interactions)
- Routine prophylaxis beyond 7 days is not recommended (BTF 4th Edition)
- Risk factors for early PTS: GCS ≤10, cortical contusion, depressed skull fracture, subdural hematoma, epidural hematoma, penetrating injury, seizure within 24 hours of injury

#### 14.5.4 ICP Management

The ICP management ladder represents an escalating hierarchy of interventions:

1. **General measures:** Head of bed elevation to 30 degrees, midline head position (facilitate venous drainage), avoid tight cervical collars, prevent hyperthermia (target normothermia), adequate sedation and analgesia
2. **CSF drainage:** Via external ventricular drain (EVD) — both diagnostic and therapeutic
3. **Osmotic therapy:** Mannitol (0.25-1 g/kg; monitor osmolality <320 mOsm/L) or hypertonic saline (3%, 7.5%, 23.4%; monitor Na <160 mEq/L)
4. **Moderate hyperventilation:** Target PaCO2 30-35 mmHg (temporary measure only; causes cerebral vasoconstriction)
5. **Second-tier therapies:** High-dose barbiturates (pentobarbital coma), therapeutic hypothermia (32-34°C for refractory ICP), decompressive craniectomy

**Clinical Pearl:** The BTF 4th Edition ICP treatment threshold is **22 mmHg** (changed from 20 mmHg in prior editions). This is a commonly tested update. Mannitol is contraindicated in hypotensive patients because it causes diuresis and volume depletion; use hypertonic saline in hypotensive patients with elevated ICP.

#### 14.5.5 Early Rehabilitation Interventions

Even during the acute care phase, early rehabilitation measures should be initiated:

- Range of motion exercises to prevent contractures
- Positioning and splinting programs
- Early mobilization (sitting, standing) when hemodynamically stable
- Bowel and bladder program initiation
- Sensory stimulation for patients in disorders of consciousness
- Communication assessment and augmentative communication as needed
- Family education and emotional support

### 14.6 Post-Acute Rehabilitation Options [COMPREHENSIVE]

Following discharge from an IRF, patients may transition to several levels of post-acute care depending on their functional status, supervision needs, and rehabilitation goals.

#### 14.6.1 Day Treatment Programs

- Structured outpatient rehabilitation program, 4-6 hours per day, 3-5 days per week
- The patient lives at home or in supported housing and commutes to the program
- Provides intensive therapy (physical, occupational, speech, neuropsychology, therapeutic recreation) without 24-hour medical supervision
- Typically includes group therapy components for social skills, community reintegration, and cognitive rehabilitation
- Appropriate for patients who are medically stable, do not require 24-hour supervision, but need more intensity than standard outpatient therapy

#### 14.6.2 Residential Rehabilitation

- 24-hour supervised living with rehabilitation services integrated into daily routines
- For patients who need structure, behavioral management, and supervision but do not require skilled nursing care
- May include vocational training, community reintegration activities, and life skills training
- Length of stay may range from months to years depending on progress and goals
- Examples include dedicated neurobehavioral programs for patients with severe behavioral dysregulation

#### 14.6.3 Supported Living

- Supervised apartments or group homes with staff support for activities of daily living, medication management, and community access
- For patients who cannot live independently but do not require institutional care
- Graduated independence: support is titrated based on the patient's evolving abilities
- May include job coaching, transportation assistance, and social programming

### 14.7 Cognitive Rehabilitation Approaches [BOARD]

Cognitive rehabilitation is a cornerstone of TBI recovery and encompasses structured interventions designed to restore or compensate for cognitive deficits. Three broad approaches are recognized, and understanding the distinctions among them is essential for board preparation.

#### 14.7.1 Restorative Approach

The restorative approach aims to directly retrain the impaired cognitive function through repetitive practice and exercises designed to strengthen neural pathways.

- **Mechanism:** Based on the principle of activity-dependent neuroplasticity — repeated activation of neural circuits promotes synaptic strengthening and circuit reorganization
- **Examples:** Attention training drills, computer-based cognitive training programs, working memory exercises, processing speed training
- **Best for:** Milder deficits with recovery potential, particularly in the first 6-12 months post-injury when neuroplasticity is greatest
- **Limitations:** Limited generalization from trained tasks to untrained tasks; evidence is stronger for attention training than for memory or executive function training

#### 14.7.2 Compensatory Approach

The compensatory approach teaches patients to work around cognitive deficits using external aids and internal strategies, rather than attempting to restore the impaired function itself.

- **External aids:** Memory notebooks, smartphone applications (alarms, calendars, task lists), checklists, structured daily schedules, environmental labels and signage
- **Internal strategies:** Visual imagery, verbal elaboration, spaced retrieval, errorless learning
- **Best for:** More severe or persistent deficits where functional restoration is unlikely
- **Evidence:** External compensatory strategies for memory have stronger evidence than restorative memory drills (Cicerone et al., Practice Standard recommendation)

#### 14.7.3 Metacognitive Approach

The metacognitive approach focuses on improving self-awareness and self-monitoring of cognitive processes, enabling patients to recognize their deficits and deploy appropriate strategies.

- **Self-prediction:** Before performing a task, the patient predicts their performance; after the task, they compare the prediction with actual performance
- **Self-evaluation:** Systematic review of one's own work for errors
- **Strategy selection:** Teaching patients to identify the demands of a situation and choose the appropriate compensatory strategy
- **Best for:** Patients with impaired awareness (anosognosia), which is common after frontal lobe injury and represents a major barrier to rehabilitation participation and community safety

#### 14.7.4 Evidence-Based Practice

Cicerone et al. have published four successive systematic reviews of cognitive rehabilitation (2000, 2005, 2011, 2019), evaluating a cumulative total of 491 studies and producing 29 practice recommendations:

- **9 Practice Standards** (strongest evidence)
- **9 Practice Guidelines** (moderate evidence)
- **11 Practice Options** (emerging evidence)

Key Practice Standard recommendations include:

1. **Attention deficits after TBI:** Direct attention training combined with metacognitive strategy training
2. **Compensatory strategies for mild memory deficits:** External memory aids (notebooks, electronic devices) are recommended as a Practice Standard
3. **Social communication deficits after TBI:** Group-based interventions targeting pragmatic communication
4. **Executive function deficits:** Metacognitive strategy training, including goal management training
5. **Comprehensive-holistic neuropsychological rehabilitation:** Reduces cognitive and functional disability after TBI

The INCOG guidelines (2023 update) provide additional evidence-based recommendations that largely align with and extend the Cicerone reviews.

**Clinical Pearl:** For board purposes, the key distinction is: restorative approaches aim to restore the impaired function, compensatory approaches work around the deficit, and metacognitive approaches improve awareness and self-monitoring. Evidence supports compensatory strategies (especially external aids) as having stronger evidence for memory deficits than restorative drills. A combined approach, tailored to the individual patient's deficit profile and recovery potential, is recommended.

**References:**
1. Cicerone KD, Goldin Y, Ganci K, et al. Evidence-based cognitive rehabilitation: systematic review of the literature from 2009 through 2014. *Arch Phys Med Rehabil*. 2019;100(8):1515-1533.
2. Cicerone KD, Langenbahn DM, Braden C, et al. Evidence-based cognitive rehabilitation: updated review of the literature from 2003 through 2008. *Arch Phys Med Rehabil*. 2011;92(4):519-530.
3. Velikonja D, Tate R, Ponsford J, et al. INCOG 2.0 guidelines for cognitive rehabilitation following traumatic brain injury. *J Head Trauma Rehabil*. 2023;38(1):52-76.

### 14.8 Cognitive Domains Assessment [BOARD]

Assessment of specific cognitive domains is essential for treatment planning, progress monitoring, and prognostication. The following table summarizes the most commonly affected domains, assessment tools, and evidence-based interventions.

| Cognitive Domain | Typical Deficits in TBI | Key Assessment Tools | Evidence-Based Interventions |
|-----------------|------------------------|---------------------|----------------------------|
| **Attention** | Sustained, selective, divided, and alternating attention impairments; processing speed deficits | Trail Making Test A, PASAT, Symbol Digit Modalities Test (SDMT), Conners CPT | Attention Process Training (APT); direct attention training + metacognitive strategies; methylphenidate (pharmacologic) |
| **Memory** | Encoding and retrieval deficits; declarative memory impairment; prospective memory failures | CVLT-II, RAVLT, WMS-IV, RBMT, GOAT/O-Log (for PTA) | External compensatory aids (memory notebook, smartphone); spaced retrieval; errorless learning; donepezil (pharmacologic) |
| **Executive Function** | Impaired planning, organization, initiation, cognitive flexibility, impulse control, abstract reasoning | Trail Making Test B, WCST, Stroop Test, Tower of London, BADS | Goal Management Training (GMT); problem-solving therapy; metacognitive strategy training; bromocriptine (pharmacologic) |
| **Processing Speed** | Slowed information processing (one of the most common and persistent deficits) | SDMT, PASAT, Trail Making Test A, WAIS-IV Processing Speed Index | Computer-based speed training; compensatory strategies (extra time); methylphenidate |
| **Self-Awareness** | Anosognosia; impaired awareness of deficits; unrealistic self-appraisal | Self-Other Awareness Questionnaire (SOAQ), Self-Awareness of Deficits Interview (SADI) | Self-prediction and feedback; video self-confrontation; structured feedback from therapists |
| **Language** | Word-finding difficulty; pragmatic communication deficits; disorganized discourse; impaired social communication | Token Test (90% sensitivity for aphasia), Boston Naming Test, discourse analysis, La Trobe Communication Questionnaire | SLP therapy; social communication groups; pragmatic language intervention |
| **Visuospatial** | Visual neglect; visuoconstructional deficits; impaired spatial orientation | Line bisection, clock drawing, Rey Complex Figure, cancellation tests | Visual scanning training; prism adaptation; compensatory strategies |

**Clinical Pearl — Key Neuropsychological Tests for Boards:**
1. **Halstead-Reitan Neuropsychological Battery:** Comprehensive diagnostic battery for brain damage
2. **WAIS (Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale):** Most commonly used IQ measure
3. **Token Test:** 90% sensitivity for aphasia
4. **GOAT/O-Log:** PTA assessment tools (GOAT ≥75 on 2 consecutive days = out of PTA; O-Log ≥25 on 2 consecutive days)
5. **Trail Making Test A:** Processing speed and sustained attention
6. **Trail Making Test B:** Executive function and cognitive flexibility (time >90 seconds associated with impaired driving)
7. **Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST):** Measures perseveration and cognitive flexibility
8. **Symbol Digit Modalities Test (SDMT):** Processing speed; highly sensitive to TBI

### 14.9 Neurogenic Bladder [BOARD]

TBI typically produces an upper motor neuron (UMN) bladder pattern characterized by disruption of cortical and pontine inhibitory pathways to the micturition reflex, resulting in detrusor overactivity.

#### 14.9.1 Pathophysiology

Normal micturition requires coordinated function of the pontine micturition center, sacral micturition center (S2-S4), and cortical inhibitory pathways. TBI disrupts the cortical and suprapontine inhibitory pathways, resulting in:

- **Uninhibited (hyperreflexic) detrusor contractions:** The detrusor muscle contracts reflexively at lower-than-normal volumes
- **Urinary urgency, frequency, and incontinence**
- **Reduced functional bladder capacity**
- **Detrusor-sphincter synergy is typically preserved** (unlike spinal cord injury, where detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia occurs)

#### 14.9.2 Prevalence

Urinary incontinence affects 40-60% of TBI patients at admission to inpatient rehabilitation. Most cases resolve over weeks to months as cognitive function improves, behavioral strategies are implemented, and neurologic recovery progresses.

#### 14.9.3 Assessment

- **Post-void residual (PVR):** Measured by bladder scan or in-and-out catheterization; elevated PVR (>100-150 mL) suggests urinary retention
- **Voiding diary:** Documents voiding frequency, volumes, incontinence episodes, and fluid intake
- **Urodynamic studies:** Indicated for persistent bladder dysfunction; demonstrates detrusor overactivity, measures bladder compliance and capacity, evaluates for detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia

#### 14.9.4 Management

1. **Remove indwelling catheter as early as possible** — prolonged catheterization increases UTI risk and delays recovery of normal voiding patterns
2. **Intermittent catheterization:** Preferred over indwelling catheter when retention is present; clean technique; schedule every 4-6 hours to keep volumes <400-500 mL
3. **Timed voiding program:** Schedule voiding every 2-3 hours to prevent incontinence episodes; gradually extend intervals as bladder capacity increases and urgency decreases
4. **Behavioral strategies:** Fluid management (adequate intake but not excessive; avoid caffeine and alcohol); prompted voiding; habit training
5. **Medications for overactive bladder:**
   - Oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin — anticholinergic agents that reduce detrusor overactivity
   - **Use with extreme caution in TBI:** Anticholinergic side effects (cognitive impairment, sedation, confusion) compound existing TBI deficits
   - **Mirabegron (Myrbetriq):** Beta-3 adrenergic agonist; relaxes the detrusor without anticholinergic effects; preferred in TBI patients who need pharmacotherapy for overactive bladder

**Clinical Pearl:** The typical bladder pattern after TBI is uninhibited (UMN pattern) with detrusor overactivity, urgency, and incontinence. This is fundamentally different from the areflexic (LMN) bladder seen in cauda equina or conus medullaris injuries. Avoid anticholinergic bladder medications when possible in TBI patients due to cognitive impairment; prefer mirabegron if pharmacotherapy is needed.

### 14.10 Neurogenic Bowel [BOARD]

TBI produces an upper motor neuron bowel pattern characterized by loss of voluntary control over the defecation reflex while the reflex arc itself remains intact.

#### 14.10.1 Pathophysiology

- **UMN bowel pattern:** Spastic external anal sphincter, intact bulbocavernosus reflex, hyperreflexic bowel
- **Constipation** is the most common bowel complaint in TBI patients, resulting from:
  - Immobility and reduced physical activity
  - Medications (opioids, anticholinergics, iron supplements, calcium channel blockers)
  - Altered diet and reduced fluid intake
  - Impaired cognitive awareness of bowel signals

#### 14.10.2 Bowel Program Components

A structured bowel program aims to achieve predictable, complete bowel evacuation on a regular schedule (daily or every other day):

1. **Consistent timing:** Perform the bowel program at the same time daily, ideally 15-30 minutes after a meal to capitalize on the gastrocolic reflex
2. **Adequate fluid intake:** 1.5-2 liters per day
3. **High-fiber diet:** 25-30 g/day (gradually increase to minimize gas and bloating)
4. **Activity and mobility:** Regular physical activity promotes bowel motility
5. **Stool softeners:** Docusate sodium (Colace) 100-250 mg BID — softens stool but does not stimulate peristalsis
6. **Stimulant laxatives:** Bisacodyl (Dulcolax) suppository or tablet; senna — stimulates peristalsis
7. **Digital rectal stimulation or suppository:** To trigger reflex evacuation in the UMN bowel pattern
8. **Upright positioning:** Gravity assists evacuation

**Clinical Pearl:** Constipation is a treatable and often overlooked cause of increased agitation and spasticity in TBI patients. In the agitated TBI patient, always evaluate and address bowel function before escalating psychotropic medications. Fecal impaction can also exacerbate autonomic instability in patients with concurrent spinal cord injury.

### 14.11 Dysphagia Management [BOARD]

Dysphagia is common after TBI, with prevalence estimates ranging from 25-61% depending on injury severity and timing of assessment. Dysphagia management is critical for preventing aspiration pneumonia, ensuring adequate nutrition, and reducing morbidity and mortality.

#### 14.11.1 Etiology in TBI

Dysphagia after TBI is multifactorial:

- Reduced level of consciousness and cognitive impairment (impulsive feeding, poor judgment, inattention)
- Cranial nerve injury (CN V — mastication; CN VII — facial sensation and lip closure; CN IX — pharyngeal sensation; CN X — vocal fold function and pharyngeal motor; CN XII — tongue movement)
- Brainstem injury affecting the swallowing center
- Reduced laryngeal sensation and impaired protective reflexes
- Tracheostomy interference with swallowing mechanics (anchoring of the larynx, reduced subglottic pressure, disuse atrophy)

#### 14.11.2 Assessment

| Assessment Method | Description | Strengths | Limitations |
|------------------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Bedside swallow evaluation (SLP) | Clinical assessment of oral motor function, voice quality, cough reflex, trial swallows | Non-invasive, immediate availability | Cannot detect silent aspiration |
| Videofluoroscopic swallow study (VFSS / Modified Barium Swallow) | Fluoroscopic imaging of swallow physiology with barium-impregnated food/liquids | Gold standard; visualizes all phases of swallow; identifies aspiration and penetration | Radiation exposure; requires patient cooperation; snapshot in time |
| Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES) | Flexible endoscope passed transnasally to visualize pharyngeal and laryngeal structures during swallowing | Bedside procedure; directly visualizes anatomy; can detect silent aspiration; no radiation | "White-out" during swallow (pharyngeal contraction obscures view); cannot assess oral phase |

#### 14.11.3 Silent Aspiration

Silent aspiration — aspiration of food, liquid, or secretions into the airway without triggering a cough reflex — is a critically important concept in TBI dysphagia management. It occurs in up to **30% of dysphagic TBI patients** and cannot be detected by clinical bedside assessment alone. Instrumental assessment (VFSS or FEES) is required to identify silent aspiration.

#### 14.11.4 Diet Modification

The International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI) provides a standardized framework for modified diets:

| IDDSI Level | Food Texture | Fluid Thickness |
|------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Level 0 | — | Thin (water, juice, coffee) |
| Level 1 | — | Slightly thick |
| Level 2 | — | Mildly thick |
| Level 3 | Liquidised/pureed | Moderately thick |
| Level 4 | Pureed (no lumps) | Extremely thick |
| Level 5 | Minced and moist | — |
| Level 6 | Soft and bite-sized | — |
| Level 7 | Regular/easy to chew | — |

#### 14.11.5 Compensatory Swallowing Strategies

- **Chin tuck:** Narrows the airway entrance and widens the vallecular space; reduces aspiration risk with thin liquids
- **Head turn (to weak side):** Redirects bolus to the stronger pharyngeal side
- **Supraglottic swallow:** Voluntary breath hold before and during the swallow, followed by cough after the swallow — closes the vocal folds before the swallow
- **Super-supraglottic swallow:** Bear down while swallowing — provides additional airway protection
- **Small bolus sizes and slowed rate of intake**
- **Effortful swallow:** Increased tongue-base contact with the posterior pharyngeal wall

**Clinical Pearl:** Silent aspiration (aspiration without cough) cannot be detected clinically and requires instrumental assessment (VFSS or FEES). Up to 30% of dysphagic TBI patients aspirate silently. This is a commonly tested board concept. Any patient with suspected dysphagia who has risk factors for silent aspiration (reduced consciousness, tracheostomy, absent gag reflex) should undergo instrumental evaluation.

### 14.12 Nutrition: Enteral vs. Parenteral [BOARD]

#### 14.12.1 Enteral Nutrition (Preferred)

Enteral nutrition is the preferred route for nutritional support in TBI patients with a functioning GI tract.

**Advantages over parenteral nutrition:**
- Maintains gut mucosal integrity and barrier function
- Reduces bacterial translocation risk
- Lower infection rates
- Lower cost
- Easier to administer

**Routes of enteral access:**

| Route | Duration | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Nasogastric (NG) tube | Short-term (<4 weeks) | Simple insertion; widely available | Aspiration risk; sinusitis; nasal erosion; easily dislodged |
| Nasojejunal (NJ) tube | Short-term | Post-pyloric; reduces aspiration and VAP risk (BTF recommendation) | Requires radiographic or endoscopic placement confirmation |
| PEG (percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy) | Long-term (>4 weeks anticipated) | Durable; less conspicuous; easier care | Requires endoscopic procedure; site infection risk; buried bumper |
| PEG-J (PEG with jejunal extension) | Long-term | Combines gastric decompression with jejunal feeding | More complex; tube migration |

#### 14.12.2 Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

Total parenteral nutrition is reserved for patients in whom enteral feeding is not possible or insufficient:
- Severe ileus, GI obstruction, short bowel syndrome, GI ischemia
- Requires central venous access (PICC or central line)
- Higher infection risk (catheter-related bloodstream infections)
- Does not maintain gut mucosal integrity
- Monitor closely: glucose, electrolytes, triglycerides, liver function

**Clinical Pearl:** "If the gut works, use it." Enteral nutrition is always preferred over parenteral when the GI tract is functional. Transgastric jejunal feeding reduces aspiration and VAP risk per BTF Level IIB recommendation. Head of bed elevation to 30-45 degrees during and after feeding further reduces aspiration risk.

### 14.13 Vocational Rehabilitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

Return to work is one of the most important functional outcomes after TBI and a key determinant of life satisfaction, self-esteem, and community integration. However, employment outcomes after moderate-to-severe TBI remain suboptimal, with only 30-40% of patients returning to competitive employment within 2 years.

#### 14.13.1 Employment Rates

TBIMS National Database data demonstrate the following employment trajectory after moderate-to-severe TBI:

| Timepoint | Return to Competitive Employment | Productive Activity (employment + school + homemaking) |
|-----------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| 1 year | ~25-30% | ~50% |
| 2 years | ~30-40% | ~55% |
| 5 years | ~40-50% | ~60% |
| 10 years | ~40-45% | ~55% |

#### 14.13.2 Predictors of Return to Work

Positive predictors include: higher pre-injury education level, pre-injury employment stability, younger age (but not pediatric), shorter PTA duration, better executive function and self-awareness, intact behavioral regulation, and strong social support.

Negative predictors include: longer PTA duration (strongest injury severity predictor), substance abuse, behavioral dysregulation (disinhibition, aggression), impaired self-awareness (anosognosia), severe memory deficits, pre-injury unemployment, and older age.

#### 14.13.3 The IPS Model of Supported Employment

The Individual Placement and Support (IPS) model is the most rigorously studied supported employment model and has the strongest evidence base for vocational rehabilitation after TBI.

**Eight evidence-based principles of IPS:**

1. **Competitive employment is the goal** — not sheltered workshops or volunteer positions
2. **Zero exclusion criteria** — anyone who wants to work is eligible
3. **Attention to client preferences** — job search based on individual interests and strengths
4. **Rapid job search** — begin job development within 30 days (do not delay with extensive pre-employment assessment and training)
5. **Integration with treatment team** — employment specialist is embedded in the rehabilitation team
6. **Systematic job development** — employment specialists build relationships with local employers
7. **Benefits counseling** — address Social Security and health insurance implications
8. **Time-unlimited support** — ongoing job coaching and support as long as needed

**Outcomes:** IPS achieves competitive employment rates of approximately **58% versus 21%** with traditional vocational rehabilitation — nearly a 3-fold improvement. These rates are drawn primarily from IPS randomized controlled trials in severe mental illness populations; TBI-specific IPS evidence is growing but more limited. The "place-then-train" philosophy (IPS) is fundamentally different from the traditional "train-then-place" approach and consistently outperforms it across all studied populations.

**Clinical Pearl:** For board purposes, the key distinction is: IPS = place-then-train (place in a job first, then provide on-the-job support); traditional vocational rehabilitation = train-then-place (assess and train in a rehabilitation setting first, then seek employment). IPS achieves 2-3 times higher employment rates.

**References:**
1. Twamley EW, Thomas KR, Gregory AM, et al. CogSMART compensatory cognitive training for traumatic brain injury: effects over 1 year. *J Head Trauma Rehabil*. 2015;30(6):391-401.
2. Wehman P, Targett P, West M, Kregel J. Productive work and employment for persons with traumatic brain injury: what have we learned after 20 years? *J Head Trauma Rehabil*. 2005;20(2):115-127.

### 14.14 Driving Rehabilitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

Driving is among the most important and most dangerous instrumental activities of daily living (IADLs) to assess after TBI. The decision to return to driving has profound implications for independence, community participation, and public safety.

#### 14.14.1 Assessment Components

**Pre-driving clinical assessment:**
- **Vision:** Acuity (minimum 20/40 in most states), visual fields (minimum 120-degree horizontal field), contrast sensitivity, depth perception, diplopia assessment
- **Cognition:** Attention (sustained, divided, selective), processing speed, executive function (judgment, planning, problem-solving), visuospatial processing, memory
- **Motor:** Reaction time, upper and lower extremity ROM and strength, coordination, cervical ROM
- **Perception:** Visual neglect screening (line bisection, cancellation tasks)
- **Behavioral:** Impulsivity assessment, frustration tolerance, insight into deficits

**Predictive screening tools:**
- **Useful Field of View (UFOV):** Computer-based visual attention test; impaired scores associated with 2-3 times increased crash risk
- **Trail Making Test B:** Time >90 seconds associated with impaired driving performance

**Gold standard assessment:**
- **On-road evaluation** with a Certified Driver Rehabilitation Specialist (CDRS) — evaluates actual driving performance in progressively complex traffic situations over 45-90 minutes

#### 14.14.2 Adaptive Equipment

| Equipment | Indication |
|----------|-----------|
| Hand controls | Lower extremity weakness, amputation, or paralysis |
| Steering knob/spinner | Upper extremity weakness; one-handed steering |
| Left-foot accelerator | Right lower extremity impairment |
| Pedal extensions | Short stature or lower extremity limitation |
| Electronic aids | Various sensory or motor limitations |
| Van modifications | Wheelchair accessibility; lift/ramp |
| Wide-angle mirrors | Visual field deficits; neck ROM limitations |

#### 14.14.3 State Reporting Requirements

Physician reporting requirements for conditions that impair driving vary significantly by state:

- **Mandatory reporting states** (e.g., California, Delaware, Oregon, Pennsylvania): Physicians must report conditions that impair driving to the Department of Motor Vehicles
- **Permissive reporting states** (majority of states): Physicians may report but are not required to
- Most states provide physician immunity from liability for good-faith reporting
- Seizures typically require a seizure-free period of 3-12 months (state-specific) before driving is permitted

**Clinical Pearl:** On-road evaluation with a CDRS is the gold standard for driving fitness determination. No single cognitive or vision test is sufficient alone. Trail Making Test B and UFOV are the best cognitive predictors of driving fitness. Physician reporting requirements vary by state, and clinicians should know their state's requirements.

### 14.15 Sexuality After TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

Sexual dysfunction is one of the most common yet most underaddressed rehabilitation issues after TBI, with **40-60%** of survivors reporting some form of sexual dysfunction.

#### 14.15.1 Types and Prevalence

| Type of Dysfunction | Estimated Prevalence |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Decreased libido/desire | 24-86% |
| Decreased arousal | 24-86% |
| Erectile dysfunction (males) | 24-57% |
| Orgasmic difficulties | 29-40% |
| Decreased frequency of sexual activity | 47-62% |
| Inappropriate sexual behavior (disinhibition) | 5-9% |

#### 14.15.2 Multifactorial Etiology

1. **Neuroendocrine:** Hypogonadism from pituitary injury (hypopituitarism affects 25-50% of moderate-severe TBI patients). Low testosterone (males) or estradiol (females) directly reduces libido and sexual function. Hormonal evaluation is mandatory.
2. **Neurological:** Frontal lobe damage affects desire and initiation; temporal lobe injury may alter sexual behavior regulation; concurrent spinal cord injury affects genital sensation and erectile function.
3. **Pharmacologic:** SSRIs cause sexual side effects in 30-70% of users; antiepileptics, beta-blockers, antipsychotics, and opioids also impair sexual function.
4. **Psychological:** Depression, anxiety, PTSD, body image changes, relationship strain, and role changes within the partnership.
5. **Fatigue and pain:** Both reduce desire and participation.

#### 14.15.3 Evaluation

- **Males:** Morning total and free testosterone, LH, FSH, prolactin
- **Females:** Estradiol, LH, FSH, prolactin, menstrual history
- **Both sexes:** Thyroid function, medication review, depression screening, relationship assessment

#### 14.15.4 Management

1. **Hormonal replacement:** Testosterone replacement for documented hypogonadism in males; estrogen/progesterone for premenopausal females with documented deficiency
2. **Medication adjustment:** Switch SSRIs to bupropion (fewer sexual side effects) or add buspirone; consider PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) for erectile dysfunction
3. **Psychological interventions:** CBT for sexual anxiety; couples/relationship counseling to address communication, role changes, and intimacy beyond intercourse
4. **Education:** Normalize the discussion of sexuality in rehabilitation; educate patients and partners about TBI effects on sexual function
5. **Management of disinhibited sexual behavior:** Environmental modification, redirection, social skills training; SSRIs or antiandrogens in severe cases

**Clinical Pearl:** Sexual dysfunction after TBI is multifactorial. The workup should always include hormonal evaluation (neuroendocrine cause is treatable), medication review (SSRIs are common culprits), and assessment of psychological factors. Sexuality should be routinely addressed in rehabilitation as part of comprehensive care.

### 14.16 Caregiver Burden [COMPREHENSIVE]

Caregiver burden is highly prevalent after TBI, with 40-70% of family caregivers reporting significant burden, depression, and reduced quality of life. Caregiver well-being directly affects patient outcomes and must be addressed as an integral component of TBI rehabilitation.

#### 14.16.1 The Zarit Burden Interview (ZBI)

The ZBI is the most widely used standardized tool for assessing caregiver burden:

- **22 items**, each scored 0-4 (0 = never, 1 = rarely, 2 = sometimes, 3 = quite frequently, 4 = nearly always)
- **Total score range:** 0-88
- **Score interpretation:**
  - 0-20: Little or no burden
  - 21-40: Mild to moderate burden
  - 41-60: Moderate to severe burden
  - 61-88: Severe burden

Sample items include: feeling that the care recipient is dependent, feeling stressed between caregiving and other responsibilities, feeling angry around the care recipient, and feeling that one's health has suffered as a result of caregiving.

#### 14.16.2 Risk Factors for High Caregiver Burden

- **Patient behavioral problems** — the strongest predictor of caregiver burden (stronger than physical disability)
- Cognitive impairment, particularly executive dysfunction and impaired self-awareness
- Lack of social support and isolation
- Pre-injury relationship quality
- Financial strain
- Caregiver's own health problems
- Duration of caregiving (burden tends to increase over time as initial recovery enthusiasm fades)

#### 14.16.3 Interventions

1. **Psychoeducation:** About TBI, expected recovery trajectory, and behavioral management strategies
2. **Skill-building:** Behavioral management techniques, communication strategies, stress management
3. **Caregiver support groups:** Peer support from others in similar situations
4. **Respite care:** Planned temporary relief from caregiving duties — in-home respite, adult day programs, short-term residential respite
5. **Individual counseling or family therapy:** To address emotional distress, grief, role changes, and relationship dynamics
6. **Community resources:** Brain Injury Association chapters, state vocational rehabilitation services, Medicaid waiver programs, Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)

**Clinical Pearl:** Patient behavioral problems — not physical disability — are the strongest predictor of caregiver burden. This is an important distinction for board preparation. Families who cope well with physical care needs may become overwhelmed by aggression, disinhibition, apathy, or personality change. Interventions that target behavioral management skills for caregivers are therefore among the most effective burden-reduction strategies.

### 14.17 Community Integration [COMPREHENSIVE]

Community integration is the ultimate goal of TBI rehabilitation — returning the patient to the highest level of independent functioning within their home, social environment, and community.

#### 14.17.1 Community Integration Questionnaire (CIQ)

The CIQ is a 15-item measure of community integration across three domains:

| Domain | Items | Examples |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Home integration (4 items) | Grocery shopping, meal preparation, housework, childcare/finances | Participation in household tasks and management |
| Social integration (6 items) | Social activities with friends, leisure activities, loneliness, shopping with others | Quality and frequency of social participation |
| Productive activity (5 items) | Employment, school, volunteer work, homemaking | Engagement in structured productive activities |

- **Total score range:** 0-29 (higher = better integration)
- Widely used in TBI outcomes research, including the TBIMS National Database

#### 14.17.2 Community Programs and Services

- **Supported living programs:** Supervised apartments or group homes with graduated independence
- **Peer mentoring:** TBI survivors mentor others with more recent injuries; evidence shows benefits for both mentors and mentees (improved self-efficacy, social participation)
- **Day treatment programs:** Structured outpatient rehabilitation 4-6 hours/day
- **Clubhouse model:** Community-based psychosocial rehabilitation; members participate in work-ordered day activities
- **Brain Injury Associations:** State-level organizations providing information, referrals, support groups, and advocacy (Brain Injury Association of America and state chapters)

### 14.18 TBIMS National Database [COMPREHENSIVE]

The Traumatic Brain Injury Model Systems (TBIMS) National Database is the largest longitudinal TBI outcomes database in the world, providing invaluable data on recovery, long-term outcomes, and system-of-care effectiveness.

#### 14.18.1 Overview

- **Established:** 1987
- **Funding:** National Institute on Disability, Independent Living, and Rehabilitation Research (NIDILRR)
- **Current size:** Data on more than 20,000 individuals from 16 currently funded TBIMS centers (plus 4 longitudinal follow-up centers) nationwide (as of 2024)
- **Eligibility:** Moderate-to-severe TBI, age ≥16 at injury, admitted to a TBIMS acute rehabilitation center within 72 hours of acute care admission
- **Follow-up:** At 1, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20+ years post-injury, with 83-87% retention rates

#### 14.18.2 Key Outcomes Data

| Outcome | Finding |
|---------|---------|
| Return to competitive employment — 2 years | ~30-40% (moderate-severe TBI) |
| Return to competitive employment — 5 years | ~40-50% |
| Productive activity at 5 years | ~60% (employment + school + homemaking) |
| Independent living at 5 years | ~75% living independently or with family |
| Life satisfaction | Significant improvement from year 1 to year 5; remains below population norms |
| Mortality | 1.5-2.9x higher than general population across all follow-up periods |
| Leading causes of excess mortality | Seizures, pneumonia, sepsis |
| Rehospitalization within 1 year | 25-30% |

#### 14.18.3 Clinical Significance

The TBIMS database provides the evidence base for long-term counseling of TBI patients and families about expected outcomes. It demonstrates that while significant recovery continues years after injury, long-term deficits and elevated mortality risk persist indefinitely. The database has produced hundreds of published studies examining predictors of outcome, intervention effectiveness, and long-term trajectories across demographic subgroups.

**Clinical Pearl:** The TBIMS is an outcomes database — it tracks what happens long-term after TBI (employment, independence, mortality, quality of life). Do not confuse it with the IMPACT calculator, which is a prognostic tool that predicts 6-month outcome based on acute admission variables. TBIMS answers "what happens over years?" while IMPACT answers "what will happen in 6 months?"

**References:**
1. Dijkers MP, Harrison-Felix C, Marwitz JH. The Traumatic Brain Injury Model Systems: history and contributions to clinical service and research. *J Head Trauma Rehabil*. 2010;25(2):81-91.
2. Corrigan JD, Cuthbert JP, Harrison-Felix C, et al. US population estimates of health and social outcomes 5 years after rehabilitation for traumatic brain injury. *J Head Trauma Rehabil*. 2014;29(6):E1-E9.

### 14.19 IMPACT Prognostic Calculator [COMPREHENSIVE]

The International Mission for Prognosis and Analysis of Clinical Trials in TBI (IMPACT) is an evidence-based prognostic calculator for predicting 6-month outcomes after moderate-to-severe TBI (GCS ≤12 within the first 24 hours). It was developed from a pooled database of over 9,000 patients from eight randomized controlled trials and three observational studies.

#### 14.19.1 Three Models

| Model | Variables | Incremental Value |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| **Core Model** | (1) Age, (2) GCS Motor Score, (3) Pupillary Reactivity | Strongest three predictors; foundation for all models |
| **Extended Model** | Core + (4) Hypoxia (PaO2 <60 or SpO2 <90), (5) Hypotension (SBP <90), (6) CT Classification (Marshall), (7) Traumatic SAH presence, (8) Epidural hematoma presence | Adds secondary insult and imaging variables |
| **Lab Model** | Extended + (9) Glucose, (10) Hemoglobin | Adds laboratory markers of physiologic status |

#### 14.19.2 Key Points

- **GCS Motor Score** (not total GCS) is used because it has the best predictive value and can be assessed even in intubated and sedated patients who cannot have verbal scores evaluated
- **Age** is a continuous predictor; each decade worsens prognosis
- **Pupillary reactivity:** Both pupils unreactive = worst prognosis
- **Outcome prediction:** 6-month mortality and 6-month unfavorable outcome (GOS 1-3: death, vegetative state, or severe disability)
- **Available online** at tbi-impact.org for clinical use
- **Used in research** for clinical trial design, risk stratification, and covariate-adjusted outcome analysis

**Clinical Pearl:** The three strongest IMPACT predictors are age, GCS motor score, and pupillary reactivity. These three variables form the "Core Model" and are the most important prognostic factors to know for boards. Note that GCS motor score is preferred over total GCS for prognostication.

**References:**
1. Steyerberg EW, Mushkudiani N, Perel P, et al. Predicting outcome after traumatic brain injury: development and international validation of prognostic scores based on admission characteristics. *PLoS Med*. 2008;5(8):e165.
2. Marmarou A, Lu J, Butcher I, et al. IMPACT database of traumatic brain injury: design and description. *J Neurotrauma*. 2007;24(2):239-250.

### 14.20 Life Care Planning for TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

A life care plan is a comprehensive, individually tailored document that identifies all anticipated future medical, rehabilitation, psychosocial, and supportive needs of a TBI patient across the lifespan, along with the associated costs. Life care plans are most commonly developed for litigation purposes but are also valuable for long-term care planning, insurance negotiations, and family education.

#### 14.20.1 Components of a TBI Life Care Plan

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Medical care | Physician visits (PM&R, neurology, psychiatry, endocrinology), emergency department visits, hospitalizations, laboratory tests, imaging studies |
| Medications | Anticonvulsants, antidepressants, cognitive enhancers, pain management, hormone replacement |
| Rehabilitation therapies | Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, neuropsychology, vocational rehabilitation |
| Nursing care | Skilled nursing, home health aides, attendant care (24-hour, overnight, daytime) |
| Durable medical equipment | Wheelchair, hospital bed, adaptive equipment, communication devices, orthotics |
| Home modification | Wheelchair accessibility, bathroom modifications, grab bars, widened doorways, environmental controls |
| Transportation | Wheelchair-accessible vehicle, adaptive driving equipment, transportation services |
| Psychological services | Individual therapy, family therapy, substance abuse counseling |
| Vocational services | Job coaching, supported employment, benefits counseling |
| Residential care | Assisted living, group home, supervised apartment (if independent living is not achievable) |
| Case management | Coordination of services, advocacy, problem-solving |
| Recreational therapy | Adapted sports, community outings, social programs |

#### 14.20.2 Development Process

Life care plans are typically developed by certified life care planners (CLCPs) in collaboration with the treating physicians and rehabilitation team. The process includes:

1. Comprehensive medical record review
2. Patient examination and functional assessment
3. Consultation with treating physicians regarding anticipated future needs
4. Research of costs for identified services and equipment
5. Projection of needs across the patient's expected lifespan
6. Documentation in a standardized format with supporting rationale

#### 14.20.3 Cost Considerations

The total lifetime cost of care for a patient with severe TBI can range from $600,000 to several million dollars, depending on injury severity, age at injury, level of attendant care needed, and geographic cost variations. The CDC estimates that the total annual healthcare cost of nonfatal TBIs in the United States exceeds $40 billion.

**Clinical Pearl:** The physiatrist plays a critical role in life care planning by providing medical opinions about future care needs, prognosis, and anticipated complications. Board questions on life care planning focus on understanding the components of a comprehensive plan rather than specific cost figures.

### 14.21 Ethical Considerations in TBI Rehabilitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

TBI raises profound ethical challenges that the physiatrist must navigate with clinical skill, moral sensitivity, and legal awareness. The intersection of cognitive impairment, evolving capacity, complex family dynamics, and life-altering decisions creates a landscape of ethical complexity that is unique to brain injury rehabilitation.

#### 14.21.1 Capacity Assessment

Medical decision-making capacity (MDC) is a clinical determination, distinct from the legal concept of competency (which is determined by courts). Capacity assessment in TBI is particularly complex because:

- Capacity is **domain-specific:** A patient may have capacity to make some decisions (e.g., dietary preferences) but not others (e.g., surgical consent)
- Capacity is **dynamic:** It may fluctuate over the course of recovery, requiring reassessment at multiple time points
- Capacity is **decision-specific:** The complexity and consequences of the decision determine the threshold for capacity

The four widely adopted standards for capacity assessment (derived from Appelbaum and Grisso's work) are:

1. **Expressing a choice:** The patient can communicate a consistent treatment preference
2. **Understanding:** The patient can grasp the relevant information about their condition, the proposed treatment, alternatives, and risks/benefits
3. **Appreciation:** The patient can apply the information to their own situation and recognize the consequences
4. **Reasoning:** The patient can engage in a rational process of weighing options based on their own values and preferences

#### 14.21.2 Challenges to Capacity in TBI

Several TBI-specific factors complicate capacity assessment:

- **Anosognosia (impaired self-awareness):** Patients may deny or minimize their deficits, leading to poor appreciation of their situation and unrealistic decision-making
- **Memory impairment:** Patients may not retain the information needed to make informed decisions
- **Executive dysfunction:** Impaired reasoning and judgment may compromise the ability to weigh options
- **Impulsivity:** Decisions may be made without adequate consideration of consequences
- **Fluctuating consciousness:** Patients emerging from disorders of consciousness may have intermittent capacity
- **Communication impairment:** Aphasia or cognitive-communication deficits may obscure intact capacity

#### 14.21.3 Surrogate Decision-Making

When a patient lacks capacity, decisions must be made by a surrogate, following a hierarchy of decision-making standards:

1. **Advance directive:** The patient's previously expressed wishes in a living will or durable power of attorney for healthcare take precedence
2. **Substituted judgment:** If the patient's specific wishes are not documented, the surrogate attempts to make the decision the patient would have made based on known values, beliefs, and prior statements
3. **Best interests:** If the patient's values and preferences are unknown, the surrogate makes the decision that best serves the patient's overall well-being

The legal hierarchy of surrogates (if no healthcare proxy has been designated) typically follows: spouse, adult children, parents, siblings — though this varies by state law.

#### 14.21.4 Autonomy vs. Safety

A common ethical tension in TBI rehabilitation involves respecting patient autonomy when the patient's decisions may be unsafe or unrealistic. Examples include:

- A patient with severe executive dysfunction and anosognosia who insists on driving
- A patient who refuses to participate in rehabilitation despite potential for improvement
- A patient who wants to return to independent living but lacks the safety awareness to do so
- A patient who refuses medications that are critical for seizure prevention

The ethical framework for resolving these tensions involves:

1. Assessing capacity for the specific decision in question
2. Maximizing patient participation in decision-making to the extent of their capacity
3. Using the least restrictive interventions to ensure safety
4. Involving ethics consultation when conflicts cannot be resolved at the team level
5. Seeking guardianship only when less restrictive alternatives are insufficient

#### 14.21.5 Other Ethical Issues

- **Withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment:** Decisions about ventilator withdrawal, cessation of artificial nutrition, and transition to comfort care in patients with severe disorders of consciousness require careful prognostic assessment, family communication, and ethical deliberation
- **Research consent:** Patients with TBI may lack capacity to consent to research participation, requiring surrogate consent and additional ethical safeguards
- **Restraint use:** Physical and chemical restraints should be used only as a last resort for imminent safety threats and discontinued as soon as possible
- **Resource allocation:** Decisions about rehabilitation intensity, duration, and level of care involve implicit allocation judgments that should be based on medical evidence and patient values, not bias

**Clinical Pearl:** Capacity is not an all-or-nothing determination. A TBI patient may have capacity for simple decisions while lacking capacity for complex ones. Capacity must be reassessed serially as the patient recovers, and every effort should be made to support and enhance the patient's own decision-making rather than defaulting to surrogate decisions.

**References:**
1. Appelbaum PS, Grisso T. Assessing patients' capacities to consent to treatment. *N Engl J Med*. 1988;319(25):1635-1638.
2. Marson DC, Dreer LE, Krzywanski S, Huthwaite JS, Devivo MJ, Novack TA. Impairment and partial recovery of medical decision-making capacity in traumatic brain injury: a 6-month longitudinal study. *Arch Phys Med Rehabil*. 2005;86(5):889-895.
3. Beauchamp TL, Childress JF. *Principles of Biomedical Ethics*. 8th ed. Oxford University Press; 2019.

### 14.23 Key Comparisons for Board Review

#### IRF vs. SNF

| Feature | IRF | SNF |

| Feature | Restorative | Compensatory | Metacognitive |
|---------|------------|-------------|---------------|
| Goal | Restore impaired function | Work around deficit | Improve self-awareness |
| Method | Repetitive practice; exercises | External aids; strategies | Self-prediction; feedback |

| Best for | Milder deficits, early recovery | Severe/persistent deficits | Impaired awareness (anosognosia) |
| Evidence | Strongest for attention training | Strongest for memory (Practice Standard) | Strong for executive function |

#### Enteral vs. Parenteral Nutrition

| Feature | Enteral (Preferred) | Parenteral (TPN) |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| Route | GI tract (NG, NJ, PEG, PEG-J) | Central venous access |

| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Indication | Functioning GI tract | GI tract not functional |
| Aspiration risk | Reduced with post-pyloric feeding | None (IV route) |
| BTF recommendation | Transgastric jejunal feeding (Level IIB) | Not recommended if GI works |

#### TBIMS vs. IMPACT
