# Chapter 8: Pharmacology of Traumatic Brain Injury

---

## Chapter Overview

The pharmacologic management of traumatic brain injury occupies a unique position within rehabilitation medicine. Unlike most other conditions where medications are prescribed to treat a disease process or alleviate symptoms, pharmacotherapy after TBI must simultaneously accomplish three distinct and sometimes competing objectives: (1) augment the injured brain's diminished neurotransmitter function to accelerate recovery, (2) manage the secondary complications of brain injury without compounding existing deficits, and (3) avoid iatrogenic harm to the fragile process of neural repair and plasticity. This chapter provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the neuropharmacological principles that govern medication selection after TBI, organized around the central tension between medications that promote recovery and those that impede it.

The catecholaminergic hypothesis of TBI recovery provides the theoretical foundation for modern TBI pharmacology. This hypothesis, supported by both animal and human data, posits that dopaminergic, noradrenergic, and cholinergic neurotransmitter systems are critical for the neural repair processes that underlie functional recovery. Medications that augment these systems may accelerate recovery; medications that antagonize them may delay or prevent it. This dichotomy -- the "PROMOTE" list versus the "AVOID" list -- represents one of the highest-yield pharmacology topics on the PM&R board examination.

## 8.1 Medications to AVOID After TBI [BOARD]

### 8.1.1 Rationale: The Catecholaminergic Hypothesis

The conceptual framework for understanding why certain medications are harmful after TBI rests on the catecholaminergic hypothesis of recovery, which identifies three neurotransmitter systems as critical mediators of neural repair and functional recovery:

1. **Dopaminergic system**: Mediates attention, executive function, motor initiation, motivation, and reward-based learning through four major pathways (mesolimbic, mesocortical, nigrostriatal, and tuberoinfundibular)
2. **Noradrenergic system**: Modulates arousal, attention, vigilance, and stress responses through projections from the locus coeruleus
3. **Cholinergic system**: Supports memory formation, attention, and learning through basal forebrain projections to hippocampus and cortex

After TBI, all three systems are disrupted to varying degrees by diffuse axonal injury, neuronal death, receptor downregulation, and neurotransmitter depletion. Medications that further reduce the activity of these already-impaired systems are expected to compound existing deficits and slow recovery. Conversely, medications that enhance activity in these systems may compensate for injury-induced deficits and accelerate functional restoration.

### 8.1.2 Haloperidol and Typical Antipsychotics

**Drug class**: Typical antipsychotic (butyrophenone)
**Mechanism of harm**: Potent D2 dopamine receptor antagonist; blocks dopaminergic neurotransmission across ALL four dopamine pathways

Haloperidol is the prototypical medication to avoid after TBI and serves as the exemplar of the principle that dopamine antagonism impairs recovery. Animal studies consistently demonstrate that haloperidol hinders spatial learning, delays motor recovery, and prolongs the period of disability after experimental brain injury. The mechanism is straightforward: by blocking D2 receptors in the frontal-subcortical circuits that mediate executive function, motivation, motor initiation, and cognitive flexibility, haloperidol directly antagonizes the neurochemical substrates of recovery.

**Additional adverse effects in TBI:**
- Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): acute dystonia, akathisia, Parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia
- Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS): a life-threatening complication with fever, rigidity, autonomic instability, and altered mental status that can mimic or worsen paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity
- QTc prolongation: risk of Torsades de Pointes
- Prolongation of posttraumatic amnesia (PTA)
- Lowered seizure threshold

> **Clinical Pearl:** Haloperidol is the single most commonly tested "avoid" medication in TBI board questions. If a clinical scenario presents a TBI patient requiring an antipsychotic (e.g., for severe refractory psychotic agitation), the correct answer is always an atypical antipsychotic at the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible duration. Quetiapine (which has weaker D2 binding and stronger 5-HT2A antagonism) is generally preferred over other atypicals when antipsychotic therapy is unavoidable. But the ideal answer is to avoid antipsychotics entirely and use the agitation pharmacotherapy hierarchy instead (beta-blockers first).

### 8.1.3 Phenytoin (Chronic Use)

**Drug class**: Anticonvulsant (hydantoin)
**Mechanism of harm**: Sodium channel blockade slowing neuronal firing; potent CYP450 enzyme inducer (CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP3A4) causing numerous drug interactions; cognitive impairment with chronic use

The distinction between appropriate acute use and harmful chronic use of phenytoin is critical. Phenytoin is indicated for 7-day seizure prophylaxis after severe TBI (Temkin trial evidence; BTF Level IIA). However, chronic phenytoin use beyond 7 days provides no benefit for preventing late posttraumatic seizures and imposes substantial harm through cognitive impairment (reduced attention, processing speed, and memory), impaired motor recovery, drug interaction burden, and the need for therapeutic drug monitoring.

**Phenytoin drug interaction profile (CYP450 inducer):**
- Reduces plasma levels of: warfarin, oral contraceptives, corticosteroids, methadone, many antibiotics, cyclosporine
- Protein-bound (albumin displacement interactions): free drug levels fluctuate with albumin changes, common in critically ill TBI patients
- Narrow therapeutic index: requires frequent drug level monitoring
- Chronic effects: gingival hyperplasia, cerebellar toxicity, osteoporosis, peripheral neuropathy, folate deficiency, hepatotoxicity

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phenytoin is effective for EARLY PTS prophylaxis (7 days only) but must NOT be continued chronically for the purpose of preventing late seizures. After 7 days, phenytoin should be discontinued unless the patient has developed documented posttraumatic epilepsy requiring ongoing anticonvulsant therapy, in which case switching to levetiracetam (with its minimal CYP450 interactions and lesser cognitive burden) should be strongly considered.

### 8.1.4 Benzodiazepines

**Drug class**: GABA-A receptor positive allosteric modulator (increases chloride channel opening FREQUENCY)
**Mechanism of harm**: Enhances GABAergic inhibition broadly across the CNS, producing sedation, cognitive impairment, impaired learning, and disrupted neuroplasticity

Benzodiazepines are among the most commonly tested "avoid" medications in TBI and for good reason. Their GABAergic mechanism produces widespread CNS depression that is particularly harmful to the injured brain. Specific concerns include:

- **Cognitive impairment**: Impaired attention, memory encoding, processing speed, and new learning
- **Paradoxical agitation**: Brain-injured patients are particularly susceptible to paradoxical excitation with benzodiazepines, which can dramatically worsen agitation rather than treat it
- **Impaired neuroplasticity**: GABAergic enhancement suppresses the synaptic remodeling processes that underlie recovery
- **Dependence and withdrawal**: Physiologic dependence develops with regular use; abrupt discontinuation can precipitate seizures (paradoxically dangerous in a seizure-prone population)
- **Falls risk**: Sedation, ataxia, and impaired coordination increase fall risk

**The ONE exception**: Acute seizure management. Intravenous lorazepam remains the first-line treatment for status epilepticus, including in TBI patients. This emergency use is distinct from routine prescribing for agitation, anxiety, or insomnia.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key pharmacologic distinction tested on boards: Benzodiazepines increase chloride channel opening FREQUENCY, while barbiturates increase chloride channel opening DURATION. At high doses, barbiturates can directly open the chloride channel without GABA binding (causing potentially lethal respiratory depression), whereas benzodiazepines require GABA to be present to enhance channel opening (providing a ceiling effect that makes them safer in overdose but still dangerous in TBI).

### 8.1.5 Anticholinergics

**Drug class**: Muscarinic acetylcholine receptor antagonists
**Mechanism of harm**: Block muscarinic receptors critical for memory formation, attention, and learning in hippocampal and cortical circuits that are already impaired after TBI

The cholinergic system is one of the most vulnerable neurotransmitter systems in TBI because the basal forebrain cholinergic nuclei (nucleus basalis of Meynert) and their long cortical projections are particularly susceptible to diffuse axonal injury. Anticholinergic medications further suppress this already-compromised system, producing dramatic worsening of cognition, delirium, confusion, and paradoxical agitation.

**Common anticholinergic offenders to recognize:**
- Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) -- commonly used for sleep and allergies; one of the most harmful OTC medications after TBI
- Oxybutynin (Ditropan) -- commonly prescribed for overactive bladder; crosses BBB readily
- Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine)
- Promethazine (Phenergan) -- antiemetic with strong anticholinergic properties
- Scopolamine -- used for motion sickness
- First-generation antihistamines as a class

### 8.1.6 Dopamine Antagonists (Beyond Typical Antipsychotics)

Several commonly used medications beyond the antipsychotic class are dopamine antagonists and should be avoided or used with caution after TBI:

- **Metoclopramide (Reglan)**: D2 antagonist used as an antiemetic and prokinetic; impairs recovery through the same dopamine-blocking mechanism as antipsychotics. Alternative: ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist), which does not block dopamine receptors
- **Prochlorperazine (Compazine)**: Phenothiazine antiemetic with potent D2 antagonism
- **Droperidol**: Butyrophenone similar to haloperidol; same concerns plus significant QTc prolongation risk

### 8.1.7 Phenobarbital

**Drug class**: Barbiturate anticonvulsant
**Mechanism of harm**: Enhances GABA-A receptor function by increasing chloride channel opening DURATION; at high doses, directly activates GABA-A receptors without requiring GABA binding

Phenobarbital produces profound sedation, cognitive impairment, and respiratory depression. It should never be used as a first-line anticonvulsant in TBI. High-dose pentobarbital-induced coma is reserved as a last-tier therapy for refractory intracranial hypertension.

### 8.1.8 Summary: The AVOID List

| Drug/Class | Primary Mechanism of Harm | Key Point |
|---|---|---|
| Haloperidol / Typical antipsychotics | D2 blockade impairs recovery | Blocks dopamine = blocks recovery |
| Phenytoin (chronic > 7 days) | Cognitive impairment, impedes recovery | Use for 7 days ONLY for prophylaxis |
| Benzodiazepines | GABAergic sedation, paradoxical agitation | Paradoxical agitation common in TBI |
| Anticholinergics | Block ACh needed for memory/learning | Cholinergic system already damaged in TBI |
| Metoclopramide | D2 antagonist | Use ondansetron instead |
| Phenobarbital | GABAergic sedation (duration) | Never first-line AED in TBI |

## 8.2 Medications to PROMOTE Recovery After TBI [BOARD]

### 8.2.1 Overview

The medications that promote recovery after TBI share a common pharmacological theme: they enhance catecholaminergic (dopaminergic, noradrenergic) or cholinergic neurotransmission. Each agent has a distinct mechanism and targets specific neurocognitive deficits, making appropriate selection dependent on accurate characterization of the patient's primary deficit.

### 8.2.2 Methylphenidate

**Drug class**: Psychostimulant (phenethylamine derivative)
**Mechanism**: Blocks dopamine transporter (DAT) and norepinephrine transporter (NET) reuptake, increasing catecholamine availability in the synaptic cleft. Importantly, methylphenidate blocks REUPTAKE of dopamine and norepinephrine -- it does NOT directly release these neurotransmitters (distinguishing it pharmacologically from amphetamines)
**Primary indication**: Attention deficits and processing speed impairment after TBI
**Evidence level**: Strongest evidence among neurostimulants for attention improvement post-TBI

**Dosing:**
- Start: 5 mg BID (morning and noon -- avoid afternoon/evening dosing due to insomnia)
- Titrate: Increase by 5 mg every 3-7 days based on response and tolerability
- Maximum: 60 mg/day in divided doses
- Extended-release formulations available for simplified dosing

**Adverse effects**: Insomnia (most common), decreased appetite, tachycardia, anxiety, headache; theoretically may lower seizure threshold (low clinical risk in practice)

**DEA schedule**: Schedule II (highest abuse potential among the neurostimulants used in TBI)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Methylphenidate is the first-line pharmacotherapy for attention deficits after TBI based on the strongest evidence base among neurostimulants. The key mechanism to remember is DAT/NET REUPTAKE inhibition (not release). When a board question presents a TBI patient with attention deficit as the primary complaint, methylphenidate is the correct answer.

### 8.2.3 Amantadine

**Drug class**: Dopaminergic agent / NMDA receptor antagonist
**Mechanism**: Dual mechanism -- (1) enhances presynaptic dopamine RELEASE (increases exogenous dopamine), and (2) weak NMDA receptor antagonist (may reduce excitotoxicity). Also has mild anticholinergic properties and may enhance norepinephrine release.
**Primary indications**: (1) Disorders of consciousness (VS/MCS) -- the ONLY agent with Level 1/Class I evidence; (2) agitation at 100 mg BID; (3) fatigue; (4) cognitive slowing and initiation deficits

**Evidence level**: AAN Level B recommendation based on Giacino NEJM 2012 (the only Class I study for any pharmacologic agent in disorders of consciousness)

**Dosing:**
- Starting dose: 100 mg BID
- Titration: May increase to 200 mg BID
- Critical dosing rule: Second dose by NOON to avoid insomnia
- Renal adjustment REQUIRED (amantadine is renally cleared)

**Adverse effects**: Insomnia, agitation at higher doses, livedo reticularis (purplish mottled skin discoloration), peripheral edema, may lower seizure threshold

### 8.2.4 The Giacino NEJM 2012 Trial: Deep Dive [BOARD]

The Giacino et al. trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2012, is the landmark pharmacotherapy study in TBI rehabilitation and is among the most frequently referenced TBI studies on board examinations.

**Study Design:**
- **Type**: Multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial
- **Sample size**: n = 184 patients
- **Population**: Patients in vegetative state (VS) or minimally conscious state (MCS), 4-16 weeks post-traumatic brain injury, receiving inpatient rehabilitation
- **Intervention**: Amantadine escalated from 100 mg BID (weeks 1-2) to 150 mg BID (week 3) to 200 mg BID (week 4) (total 4-week treatment period), followed by a 2-week washout period
- **Primary outcome**: Disability Rating Scale (DRS) slope of improvement during the 4-week treatment period

**Key Results:**

1. **During treatment (weeks 1-4)**: Amantadine group demonstrated significantly faster DRS improvement than placebo -- 0.24 points per week faster (p = 0.007). Patients receiving amantadine had higher rates of recovery across all six behavioral domains measured by the Coma Recovery Scale-Revised (CRS-R).

2. **After treatment discontinuation (weeks 5-6, washout)**: The rate of improvement SLOWED in the amantadine group (0.30 points/week slower than placebo, p = 0.02). The groups converged by week 6, with no significant difference in overall improvement from baseline to week 6.

3. **Interpretation**: Amantadine produces a PHARMACOLOGICAL effect (accelerates recovery during active treatment) but is NOT disease-modifying (groups equalized after washout). The drug speeds the trajectory of recovery but does not change the ultimate endpoint.

| Trial Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Design | Multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT |
| N | 184 |
| Population | VS or MCS, 4-16 weeks post-TBI, inpatient rehabilitation |
| Dose escalation | 100 mg BID (weeks 1-2), 150 mg BID (week 3), up to 200 mg BID (week 4) |
| Primary outcome | DRS improvement slope: 0.24 points/week faster (p = 0.007) |
| Washout finding | Improvement slowed after discontinuation (0.30 points/week slower, p = 0.02) |
| Groups at week 6 | Converged (no overall difference from baseline to week 6) |
| AAN recommendation | Level B: amantadine 100-200 mg BID for VS/MCS after TBI |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Three facts about the Giacino trial that are tested repeatedly: (1) Amantadine is the ONLY medication with Class I evidence for disorders of consciousness after TBI, (2) the effect is pharmacological, not disease-modifying (groups converged after washout), and (3) the AAN Level B recommendation supports amantadine 100-200 mg BID for functional recovery from VS/MCS. Beyond DOC, amantadine is also used for agitation (100 mg BID), fatigue, and initiation deficits in higher-functioning TBI patients.

### 8.2.5 Donepezil

**Drug class**: Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor
**Mechanism**: Inhibits the enzyme acetylcholinesterase, preventing the breakdown of acetylcholine in the synaptic cleft and thereby increasing cholinergic neurotransmission -- specifically targeting the cholinergic deficit that occurs after TBI
**Primary indication**: Memory deficits after TBI, particularly declarative (episodic) memory impairment
**Dosing**: Start 5 mg daily; may increase to 10 mg daily after 4-6 weeks
**Adverse effects**: Nausea, diarrhea, insomnia, vivid dreams, bradycardia, muscle cramps

> **Clinical Pearl:** Donepezil complements the dopaminergic agents by targeting a different neurotransmitter system. While methylphenidate, amantadine, and bromocriptine address dopaminergic deficits (attention, executive function, arousal, initiation), donepezil addresses cholinergic deficits (memory formation). The two systems are complementary, and combination therapy is common in clinical practice -- for example, methylphenidate for attention plus donepezil for memory.

### 8.2.6 The "Start Low, Go Slow" Principle [BOARD]

This cardinal principle of TBI pharmacology deserves its own section because it governs ALL medication prescribing in this population. The injured brain differs from the uninjured brain in several pharmacologically relevant ways:

1. **Altered blood-brain barrier permeability**: Disruption of tight junctions allows drugs to reach higher CNS concentrations than expected from peripheral dosing
2. **Disrupted neurotransmitter systems**: Remaining receptors are more sensitive to pharmacologic modulation because of reduced baseline neurotransmitter tone
3. **Impaired hepatic and renal drug metabolism**: Critical illness, immobility, and polypharmacy alter drug clearance
4. **Increased susceptibility to sedation**: Lower threshold for drug-induced somnolence due to diminished arousal systems
5. **Altered protein binding**: Hypoalbuminemia (common in malnourished TBI patients) increases free drug fraction for protein-bound medications

The practical implication: Start every medication at the lowest available dose, titrate slowly (every 5-7 days minimum), use the fewest medications possible, and reassess regularly for continued need. Polypharmacy is the enemy of recovery.

## 8.3 Modafinil [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.3.1 Mechanism and Pharmacology

**Drug class**: Wakefulness-promoting agent
**Mechanism**: The exact mechanism is not fully understood but involves weak inhibition of the dopamine transporter (DAT) plus enhancement of norepinephrine, histamine, and orexin/hypocretin neurotransmission. Pharmacologically distinct from traditional psychostimulants.
**DEA schedule**: Schedule IV (lower abuse potential than methylphenidate, which is Schedule II)

### 8.3.2 Clinical Evidence in TBI

The evidence base for modafinil after TBI is growing but requires nuanced interpretation:

- **Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS)**: Class I evidence supports modafinil 100-200 mg daily for post-TBI EDS, with improved scores on the Maintenance of Wakefulness Test
- **Fatigue**: Evidence is INCONSISTENT. A Class I randomized trial found NO significant improvement in subjective fatigue versus placebo, despite the common clinical practice of prescribing modafinil for this indication
- **Critical distinction**: EDS (difficulty staying awake, objective sleepiness) is a different clinical entity from fatigue (subjective exhaustion, lack of energy). Modafinil is more effective for EDS than for fatigue

**Dosing**: Start 100 mg daily in the morning; may increase to 200 mg; maximum 400 mg/day
**Adverse effects**: Headache, nausea, anxiety, insomnia; RARE but serious: Stevens-Johnson syndrome (especially in pediatric populations -- discontinue immediately if rash develops)

### 8.3.3 Modafinil vs. Methylphenidate: When to Choose Which

| Feature | Modafinil | Methylphenidate |
|---|---|---|
| Primary target | Excessive daytime sleepiness | Attention deficits, processing speed |
| Mechanism | Weak DAT inhibitor + NE/histamine/orexin | DA/NE reuptake inhibitor |
| Schedule | IV (lower abuse potential) | II (higher abuse potential) |
| Cardiovascular effects | Minimal | Tachycardia, hypertension possible |
| Appetite suppression | Mild | Moderate |
| Evidence for attention | Limited | Strongest among neurostimulants |
| Evidence for EDS | Class I | Limited for EDS specifically |
| Best use case | Sleepiness is the primary complaint | Attention deficit is the primary complaint |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Always clarify whether the patient's primary complaint is sleepiness (cannot stay awake), attention deficit (awake but cannot focus), or fatigue (subjective exhaustion despite adequate sleep). The treatment differs substantially: modafinil for sleepiness, methylphenidate for attention deficit, and neither has strong evidence for isolated fatigue. Mismatching the drug to the symptom leads to treatment failure.

## 8.4 Bromocriptine [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.4.1 Mechanism and Clinical Role

**Drug class**: Ergot-derived dopamine agonist
**Mechanism**: Direct D2 dopamine receptor agonist -- stimulates postsynaptic D2 receptors, thereby increasing ENDOGENOUS dopaminergic activity. This is mechanistically distinct from amantadine, which increases EXOGENOUS dopamine release from presynaptic terminals.
**Primary indications**: Executive dysfunction, initiation deficits, akinetic mutism, apathy/abulia -- all frontal lobe-mediated deficits

### 8.4.2 Key Evidence: Whyte 2008

The Whyte et al. 2008 study is a pilot placebo-controlled trial of low-dose bromocriptine for attention deficits after TBI. Key findings:

- Improved executive function and dual-task performance
- Did NOT improve simple working memory maintenance
- Interpretation: Bromocriptine targets EXECUTIVE/FRONTAL deficits (planning, initiation, cognitive flexibility, dual-task management) rather than basic memory storage or simple attention

The earlier McDowell 1998 study similarly demonstrated improvement in executive function measures in TBI patients with frontal lobe injury treated with bromocriptine.

**Dosing**: Start 1.25-2.5 mg daily; titrate slowly to 5-15 mg/day in divided doses
**Adverse effects**: HYPOTENSION (especially first-dose phenomenon -- give initial dose at bedside with blood pressure monitoring), nausea, dizziness, confusion at high doses

> **Clinical Pearl:** Bromocriptine is the agent of choice for frontal lobe-mediated executive dysfunction and akinetic mutism. The pharmacologic distinction is important: bromocriptine is a direct D2 agonist (stimulates endogenous dopamine pathways), while amantadine enhances presynaptic dopamine release (exogenous dopamine effect). When a board question presents a TBI patient with akinetic mutism, severe apathy, or executive dysfunction as the primary complaint (not attention or arousal), bromocriptine is the preferred agent.

## 8.5 Dopaminergic Pharmacology: Synthesis and Decision Framework [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.5.0 Integrating the Dopaminergic Agents

The four dopaminergic agents used in TBI (methylphenidate, amantadine, bromocriptine, and modafinil) act through fundamentally different mechanisms on the dopaminergic system, and this mechanistic diversity is what allows targeted treatment of distinct neurocognitive deficits.

**Presynaptic vs. Postsynaptic Targeting:**

Understanding whether an agent acts presynaptically (increasing dopamine availability in the synapse) or postsynaptically (directly stimulating dopamine receptors) has practical implications for drug selection:

- **Methylphenidate**: Acts presynaptically by blocking the dopamine transporter (DAT), preventing reuptake. Requires intact presynaptic terminals capable of releasing dopamine. In severe injuries with extensive neuronal death, fewer presynaptic terminals remain functional, and blocking reuptake at remaining terminals may have diminishing returns.

- **Amantadine**: Acts presynaptically by enhancing dopamine RELEASE from intact terminals. Also requires some preserved presynaptic architecture. The additional NMDA antagonism provides a complementary neuroprotective mechanism. This dual mechanism explains its unique efficacy in disorders of consciousness, where both dopaminergic augmentation and glutamate modulation are beneficial.

- **Bromocriptine**: Acts postsynaptically as a direct D2 receptor agonist. Does NOT require intact presynaptic terminals. Theoretically effective even when presynaptic neurons are destroyed, because it directly stimulates the postsynaptic receptor. This makes it the logical choice when the frontal dopaminergic system has sustained severe structural damage (as in akinetic mutism with bilateral frontal injury).

- **Modafinil**: Acts weakly on DAT but its primary effects involve multiple neurotransmitter systems (norepinephrine, histamine, orexin). Its broad mechanism makes it less specific than the other agents but more versatile for diffuse deficits like sleepiness.

**Clinical Decision Tree for Dopaminergic Agent Selection:**

1. Is the patient in a disorder of consciousness (VS/MCS)? -> **Amantadine** (only Class I evidence)
2. Is the primary deficit attention or processing speed? -> **Methylphenidate** (strongest evidence for attention)
3. Is the primary deficit executive function, initiation, or akinetic mutism? -> **Bromocriptine** (direct D2 agonist for frontal deficits)
4. Is the primary deficit excessive daytime sleepiness? -> **Modafinil** (wakefulness-promoting without stimulant side effects)
5. Is the primary deficit memory? -> **Donepezil** (cholinergic, not dopaminergic)
6. Is the patient both agitated AND cognitively impaired? -> **Amantadine** (dual benefit for agitation and cognitive recovery)

> **Clinical Pearl:** When choosing among dopaminergic agents, think about the anatomic level of the lesion. Brainstem/thalamic injury producing arousal deficits responds best to amantadine (enhances global dopamine release and blocks NMDA excitotoxicity). Prefrontal cortex injury producing executive dysfunction responds best to bromocriptine (direct stimulation of prefrontal D2 receptors). Diffuse injury producing attention deficits responds best to methylphenidate (blocks reuptake across widespread catecholaminergic projections). Hypothalamic injury producing sleepiness responds best to modafinil (engages wake-promoting orexin and histamine systems).

## 8.6 SSRIs in TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.6.1 First-line for Depression and Anxiety

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors remain the first-line pharmacotherapy for depression (affecting 25-50% of TBI survivors) and anxiety disorders (affecting 11-24%) after TBI. Their favorable side effect profile, low seizure risk, and minimal cognitive impairment make them preferable to tricyclic antidepressants and MAOIs in this population.

**Preferred SSRIs:**
- **Sertraline**: First choice due to fewest CYP450 drug interactions (minimal CYP2D6 and CYP3A4 inhibition) and generally good tolerability; 50-200 mg daily
- **Citalopram/Escitalopram**: Evidence for agitation reduction and irritability/emotional lability in TBI; citalopram 20 mg studied for these indications; note QTc prolongation risk at higher doses

### 8.6.2 The FLAME Trial and Its Aftermath

The FLAME trial (Chollet et al., Lancet Neurology 2011) generated significant excitement by demonstrating that fluoxetine 20 mg daily improved motor recovery after acute ischemic stroke (higher Fugl-Meyer Motor Scale scores at 90 days). The motor benefit appeared independent of antidepressant effects, suggesting a direct neuroplasticity mechanism possibly mediated by BDNF upregulation.

**However**, three subsequent large, well-designed trials FAILED to replicate the FLAME findings:

| Trial | N | Year | Primary Outcome | SSRI Benefit? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FLAME | 118 | 2011 | Fugl-Meyer Motor Scale | Yes (motor recovery) |
| FOCUS | 3,127 | 2018 | Modified Rankin Scale | **No** |
| AFFINITY | 1,280 | 2020 | Modified Rankin Scale | **No** |
| EFFECTS | 1,500 | 2020 | Modified Rankin Scale | **No** |

All three negative trials found NO effect on functional outcome but DID find increased rates of bone fractures and hyponatremia in the fluoxetine groups.

> **Clinical Pearl:** For board purposes, the key takeaways regarding SSRIs in TBI/brain injury are: (1) SSRIs remain FIRST-LINE for depression and anxiety after TBI, (2) the motor recovery hypothesis from the FLAME trial is NOT supported by subsequent evidence, (3) sertraline is preferred due to fewest drug interactions, (4) always distinguish depression from apathy before prescribing -- SSRIs may worsen apathy.

### 8.6.3 SSRIs and Seizure Risk

SSRIs are generally considered safe with respect to seizure threshold, making them preferable in patients with posttraumatic epilepsy or elevated seizure risk. This distinguishes them from bupropion, which significantly lowers the seizure threshold and is relatively contraindicated in patients with active seizure disorders.

### 8.6.4 Serotonin Syndrome Awareness

Serotonin syndrome is a potentially life-threatening condition that may occur when SSRIs are combined with other serotonergic agents commonly encountered in TBI rehabilitation:
- Tramadol (commonly prescribed for pain)
- Triptans (for posttraumatic headache)
- Linezolid (antibiotic with MAO-inhibiting properties)
- Fentanyl (less serotonergic than other opioids but still a potential contributor)

The clinical triad of serotonin syndrome includes altered mental status, neuromuscular hyperactivity (clonus, hyperreflexia, myoclonus), and autonomic instability. It can be confused with paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity or neuroleptic malignant syndrome.

## 8.7 Sleep Medications in TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.7.1 Pharmacologic Choices

Sleep disturbance affects 30-70% of TBI patients and significantly impairs recovery. Medication selection must account for the vulnerabilities of the TBI-impaired brain: heightened sensitivity to sedation, impaired cognition, and compromised neuroplasticity.

| Medication | Mechanism | Status in TBI | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trazodone | 5-HT2A antagonist; mild H1 antihistaminism | **FIRST CHOICE** | Non-habit forming; 50-100 mg QHS; does not significantly impair cognition; mild antidepressant benefit |
| Melatonin | MT1/MT2 melatonin receptor agonist | **Safe, Recommended** | Addresses circadian disruption; 3-5 mg QHS at consistent time; helps entrain sleep-wake cycle |
| Ramelteon | Selective MT1/MT2 agonist | **Safe alternative** | 8 mg QHS; fewer drug interactions than melatonin supplements; prescription-strength melatonin receptor targeting |
| Low-dose quetiapine | D2/5-HT2A/H1 antagonist | **Use cautiously** | 25-50 mg QHS; sedation from H1 antagonism; may impair recovery at higher doses (dopamine blockade) |
| Benzodiazepines | GABA-A positive allosteric modulator | **AVOID** | Impair cognition, paradoxical agitation, dependence, impair neuroplasticity |
| Diphenhydramine | H1 antihistamine / anticholinergic | **AVOID** | Significant anticholinergic effects worsen TBI cognition; not merely sedating -- actively harmful |
| Zolpidem | GABA-A modulator (imidazopyridine) | **AVOID (for sleep)** | Falls risk, paradoxical excitation possible; NOTE: the paradoxical arousal effect observed in some DOC patients is a separate clinical phenomenon |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Trazodone is the first-line sleep medication in TBI based on its serotonergic mechanism (no anticholinergic or GABAergic activity), absence of habit-forming potential, lack of significant cognitive impairment, and potential mild antidepressant benefit. This is consistently the correct board answer when asked about sleep medication selection after TBI.

## 8.8 Depression vs. Apathy: A Critical Distinction [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.8.1 Clinical Differentiation

One of the most clinically important distinctions in TBI neuropsychiatry is differentiating true depression from apathy/abulia, because they have different underlying mechanisms and respond to different treatments despite sharing superficial similarities.

| Feature | Depression | Apathy/Abulia |
|---|---|---|
| Underlying deficit | Serotonergic/noradrenergic; mood regulation | Dopaminergic; frontal-subcortical circuit dysfunction |
| Mood quality | Sad, hopeless, guilty | Indifferent, emotionally flat, unconcerned |
| Subjective distress | Present (patient feels bad about feeling bad) | Absent (patient is not troubled by their lack of motivation) |
| Suicidality | May be present | Absent |
| Sleep disturbance | Insomnia or hypersomnia with distress | May have altered sleep without subjective complaint |
| Appetite | Decreased (or increased in atypical depression) | Decreased (from disinterest, not despair) |
| Response to SSRIs | Typically improves | May WORSEN (SSRIs can further dampen emotional reactivity) |
| Preferred treatment | SSRIs (sertraline), SNRIs, psychotherapy | Dopaminergic agents: methylphenidate, amantadine, bromocriptine |
| Anatomic correlate | Limbic and prefrontal circuits | Frontal-subcortical circuits (medial prefrontal, anterior cingulate) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** This distinction has direct treatment implications. Prescribing an SSRI to a patient with apathy (misdiagnosed as depression) may worsen the apathy by further dampening emotional reactivity and motivation. Conversely, prescribing a stimulant to a truly depressed patient may temporarily improve energy without addressing the underlying mood disorder. Always ask: "Is the patient sad and distressed, or indifferent and unconcerned?" Sadness points to depression (SSRI); indifference points to apathy (dopaminergic agent).

## 8.9 Neurostimulant Comparison Table [COMPREHENSIVE]

The following comprehensive comparison table is essential for selecting the appropriate neurostimulant based on the patient's primary deficit.

| Feature | Methylphenidate | Amantadine | Modafinil | Bromocriptine |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | DA/NE reuptake inhibitor | DA release + NMDA antagonist | Weak DAT inhibitor + NE/histamine/orexin | Direct D2 agonist |
| **Primary target** | Attention, processing speed | Arousal, DOC recovery, agitation | Excessive daytime sleepiness | Executive function, initiation |
| **Evidence level** | Strongest for attention | ONLY Class I for DOC (Giacino 2012) | Class I for EDS; inconsistent for fatigue | Moderate (Whyte 2008 pilot) |
| **Typical dosing** | 5-20 mg BID (max 60 mg/day) | 100-200 mg BID (2nd dose by noon) | 100-200 mg daily (AM) | 1.25-15 mg/day (divided) |
| **Key side effects** | Insomnia, anorexia, tachycardia | Insomnia, livedo reticularis, edema | Headache, nausea, rare SJS | HYPOTENSION (first-dose), nausea |
| **DEA schedule** | Schedule II (highest abuse risk) | Not scheduled | Schedule IV (low abuse risk) | Not scheduled |
| **Renal adjustment** | No | YES (renally cleared) | No (hepatic metabolism) | No (hepatic metabolism) |
| **Seizure threshold** | May lower (low risk) | May lower | No significant effect | No significant effect |
| **Unique advantage** | Best evidence for attention | Only proven agent for DOC; also treats agitation | Lowest abuse potential; wakefulness-specific | Best for frontal lobe deficits |
| **Best use scenario** | "Can't focus" | "Can't wake up" (DOC) or "Can't stop being agitated" | "Can't stay awake" (but can focus when awake) | "Can't initiate or plan" |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Match the neurostimulant to the deficit: Attention = methylphenidate. DOC/arousal = amantadine. Sleepiness = modafinil. Executive dysfunction/akinetic mutism = bromocriptine. Memory = donepezil (cholinergic, not dopaminergic). These agents are NOT interchangeable -- choosing the wrong drug for the wrong deficit will not produce benefit.

## 8.10 Anticonvulsant Drug Interactions (CYP450) [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.10.1 Clinical Importance

For TBI patients who develop posttraumatic epilepsy requiring chronic anticonvulsant therapy, understanding CYP450 drug interactions is critical because these patients are invariably on multiple medications for cognitive rehabilitation, mood management, spasticity, pain, and other complications.

| Anticonvulsant | CYP450 Effect | Key Interactions | Impact on TBI Recovery |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phenytoin | Strong INDUCER (2C9, 2C19, 3A4) | Reduces levels of: warfarin, OCs, corticosteroids, methadone, many antibiotics; albumin displacement interactions | Impairs cognition; chronic use impedes recovery; narrow TI requires monitoring |
| Carbamazepine | Strong INDUCER (3A4) | Auto-induction (induces own metabolism); reduces OC and warfarin efficacy; SIADH/hyponatremia risk | Moderate cognitive effects; hyponatremia risk critical in TBI |
| Valproic acid | INHIBITOR (2C9) | Increases lamotrigine levels (SJS risk); displaces phenytoin from albumin; hepatotoxic | Moderate cognitive effects; useful mood stabilizer; weight gain, tremor, thrombocytopenia |
| Levetiracetam | NO significant CYP450 interactions | Minimal drug interactions (renally excreted) | LEAST cognitive impairment; preferred in TBI; behavioral irritability risk |
| Lamotrigine | Mild inducer (glucuronidation) | Level DOUBLED by valproic acid (requires dose reduction); SJS risk with rapid titration | Minimal cognitive effects; mood stabilizing; must titrate very slowly |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Levetiracetam is increasingly preferred for ongoing anticonvulsant therapy in TBI for three reasons: (1) no significant CYP450 drug interactions (critical in polypharmacy patients), (2) no therapeutic drug monitoring needed (wide therapeutic index), and (3) least cognitive impairment among commonly used AEDs. The main caveat is behavioral irritability/aggression, which is ironic given the TBI population's susceptibility to agitation. Despite practical advantages, the BTF notes "insufficient evidence to recommend levetiracetam over phenytoin" based on the limited head-to-head RCT data.

## 8.11 Cannabis and CBD in TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.11.1 Current Evidence Landscape

Cannabis and cannabidiol (CBD) are increasingly used by TBI survivors, making it essential for physiatrists to understand the current evidence, regulatory landscape, and risk profile.

**Preclinical evidence**: CBD demonstrates anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticonvulsant properties in animal models, with theoretical neuroprotective potential against the secondary injury cascade (reduced excitotoxicity, neuroinflammation, and oxidative stress).

**Clinical evidence**: There are NO randomized controlled trials of cannabis or CBD specifically for TBI recovery outcomes. All clinical evidence is preclinical, observational, or extrapolated from other conditions.

### 8.11.2 Risks in the TBI Population

1. **Cognitive dysfunction**: A 2024 study found veterans with TBI and cannabis use disorder (CUD) had significantly higher risk of developing cognitive disorders including dementia
2. **Psychosis risk**: Cannabis use increases risk of psychotic symptoms; particularly concerning in TBI patients with frontal lobe vulnerability
3. **Seizure threshold**: THC may LOWER seizure threshold (dangerous in PTE-prone population); CBD may have anticonvulsant effects (Epidiolex is FDA-approved for Lennox-Gastaut syndrome, Dravet syndrome, and tuberous sclerosis complex, but NOT for TBI)
4. **Executive function**: Cannabis use may worsen frontal lobe deficits including impaired motivation, planning, and decision-making
5. **Memory**: THC impairs hippocampal function, compounding existing hippocampal injury
6. **Drug interactions**: Cannabis inhibits CYP2C19 and CYP3A4, potentially affecting levels of anticonvulsants, antidepressants, and other medications

### 8.11.3 Regulatory and Policy Considerations

- **VA/DOD position**: Strongly against medical marijuana use; VA cannot prescribe or recommend cannabis (remains Schedule I federally)
- **VA clinician guidance**: May discuss cannabis use with patients but cannot recommend it; veterans using cannabis are not penalized in VA care
- **Clinical bottom line**: Insufficient evidence to recommend for TBI recovery; significant risks in the TBI population; discuss openly with patients but do not prescribe; document the conversation

## 8.12 Pharmacologic Prescribing Principles [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.12.1 The Adequate Therapeutic Trial

Before concluding that a medication is ineffective, ensure that an adequate therapeutic trial has been completed:

1. **Sufficient dose**: The medication must have been titrated to an adequate therapeutic dose (not just the starting dose)
2. **Sufficient duration**: Most neurostimulants require 2-4 weeks at therapeutic dose for full effect assessment; some (donepezil, SSRIs) require 4-8 weeks
3. **Adequate assessment**: Use objective measures (neuropsychological testing, standardized scales) rather than subjective impression alone
4. **Rule out confounders**: Assess for polypharmacy interactions, non-adherence, progression of underlying injury, or new complications that may mask drug benefit

### 8.12.2 Continuous Reassessment

The TBI brain is a dynamic system. Neurotransmitter function, receptor density, and neural network connectivity evolve continuously throughout the recovery trajectory. Consequently:

- Medications that are necessary during early recovery (e.g., amantadine for DOC) may become unnecessary as endogenous neurotransmitter function recovers
- Medications that are harmful early (e.g., anticonvulsants for prophylaxis) should be discontinued when the prophylaxis window closes
- Regular medication reviews (at least quarterly) with deliberate attempts to taper and discontinue drugs that are no longer needed
- Each medication should have a documented target symptom and a reassessment timeline

### 8.12.3 Drug Augmentation Strategy

When monotherapy is insufficient, rational drug augmentation follows the principle of targeting complementary neurotransmitter systems rather than stacking drugs with identical mechanisms:

- **Dopaminergic + Cholinergic**: Methylphenidate (attention) + donepezil (memory) -- targets different systems for comprehensive cognitive support
- **Dopaminergic + Serotonergic**: Amantadine (arousal/initiation) + sertraline (depression) -- addresses both cognitive and mood domains
- **Avoid**: Multiple drugs targeting the same receptor system (e.g., methylphenidate + amantadine + bromocriptine simultaneously) without clear rationale -- increased side effects without proportional benefit

### 8.12.4 General Pharmacologic Guidelines Summary

1. **Start low, go slow**: Begin at the lowest available dose; titrate slowly (every 5-7 days minimum)
2. **One change at a time**: Add or adjust only one medication at a time to accurately attribute benefit or harm
3. **Minimize polypharmacy**: Every additional medication increases the risk of drug interactions and cognitive side effects
4. **Match drug to deficit**: Use the neurostimulant comparison framework to select the agent that targets the primary deficit
5. **Document target symptoms**: For every medication, identify the specific symptom being treated and the expected timeline for reassessment
6. **Plan for discontinuation**: Most TBI medications should be trialed with a planned reassessment and possible taper rather than continued indefinitely
7. **Screen for interactions**: CYP450 interactions are particularly important in patients on anticonvulsants, and anticholinergic burden should be assessed using a tool such as the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden Scale

## 8.13 Special Pharmacologic Considerations [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 8.13.1 Pharmacotherapy Across the Recovery Continuum

The pharmacologic needs of TBI patients evolve dramatically as they progress through the recovery continuum. Understanding these phase-specific considerations is essential for longitudinal care.

**ICU Phase (Days 1-14):**
- Primary pharmacologic goals: ICP management, seizure prophylaxis, sedation/analgesia for mechanical ventilation, hemodynamic support
- Seizure prophylaxis: Levetiracetam 500-1000 mg BID (or phenytoin) for 7 days
- Sedation: Propofol (preferred for neurological monitoring due to short half-life), dexmedetomidine (preserves exam); AVOID benzodiazepine infusions when possible
- Osmolar therapy: Mannitol (0.25-1 g/kg, keep serum osmolality < 320) or hypertonic saline (3-23.4%, keep serum Na < 160)
- Analgesics: Fentanyl or hydromorphone; avoid morphine in renal impairment; minimize opioid use when possible
- Stress ulcer prophylaxis: Proton pump inhibitors or H2 blockers
- DVT prophylaxis: Mechanical immediately; pharmacologic (LMWH) when hemorrhage stable (24-72 hours)

**Acute Rehabilitation Phase (Weeks 2-12):**
- Transition from ICU-oriented medications to recovery-promoting agents
- Discontinue seizure prophylaxis at day 7 unless documented PTE
- Initiate amantadine if DOC persists (100 mg BID, titrate to 200 mg BID)
- Address agitation: Environmental modifications first, then propranolol per hierarchy
- Begin neurostimulant therapy matched to primary deficit (methylphenidate for attention, modafinil for EDS)
- Manage spasticity: Begin oral agents (baclofen, tizanidine) and consider focal interventions (botulinum toxin)
- Address sleep: Trazodone, melatonin; discontinue ICU sedatives

**Subacute and Community Reintegration Phase (Months 3-12+):**
- Reassess all medications; attempt to taper agents that may no longer be needed
- Optimize cognitive pharmacotherapy based on comprehensive neuropsychological testing
- Address mood disorders: SSRIs for depression; distinguish from apathy
- Long-term anticonvulsant management if PTE has developed
- Neuroendocrine screening and replacement therapy as indicated (Ghigo algorithm)
- Pain management: Non-opioid strategies preferred (gabapentin for neuropathic, acetaminophen/NSAIDs for nociceptive)

### 8.13.2 Pharmacotherapy in Specific TBI Populations

**Elderly TBI Patients:**
- Heightened sensitivity to all centrally-acting medications due to age-related changes in pharmacokinetics (reduced renal and hepatic clearance, increased body fat ratio altering volume of distribution, reduced protein binding) and pharmacodynamics (reduced receptor density, impaired homeostatic mechanisms)
- Start at 25-50% of standard adult doses for most agents
- Anticholinergic burden assessment is critical (Beers Criteria medications are prevalent in this population)
- Falls risk is compounded by sedating medications, orthostatic hypotension (from alpha-blockers, bromocriptine), and impaired balance
- Polypharmacy is the norm; drug-drug interactions are a major concern

**Pediatric TBI Patients:**
- Limited pharmacologic evidence base; most studies are extrapolated from adult data
- Amantadine has been studied in pediatric DOC with promising results
- Methylphenidate dosing follows ADHD protocols with lower starting doses
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk with modafinil is higher in pediatric populations
- Developing brain may have different neurotransmitter response profiles

**Military/Blast TBI:**
- High prevalence of comorbid PTSD, chronic pain, and substance use disorders
- Medication selection must account for all comorbid conditions simultaneously
- SSRIs serve dual purpose for both depression and PTSD symptoms
- Prazosin (alpha-1 antagonist) for PTSD-associated nightmares; 1-15 mg QHS; monitor for orthostatic hypotension
- Cannabis use is prevalent; counsel regarding risks in TBI population
- VA formulary restrictions may limit medication options

### 8.13.3 The Anticholinergic Burden Concept

The Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) Scale provides a framework for quantifying the cumulative anticholinergic load from all medications a patient is taking. Each medication is scored from 0 (no anticholinergic activity) to 3 (definite anticholinergic activity), and the scores are summed across all medications.

In TBI patients, cumulative ACB scores of 3 or higher are associated with measurable cognitive impairment, and the effect is additive. A patient on oxybutynin (ACB 3) + diphenhydramine (ACB 3) + amitriptyline (ACB 3) has a cumulative ACB of 9 -- a devastating anticholinergic burden on an already-injured cholinergic system.

**Practical approach**: At every medication review, calculate the total ACB score. Identify the highest-scoring medications and evaluate whether they can be substituted with lower-ACB alternatives (e.g., trospium for oxybutynin, trazodone for diphenhydramine, sertraline for amitriptyline).

### 8.13.4 Drug-Induced Movement Disorders in TBI

TBI patients are at increased risk for drug-induced movement disorders due to preexisting dopaminergic system disruption. The three most important categories to recognize are:

1. **Acute dystonia**: Sustained muscle contractions causing twisting or repetitive movements; occurs within hours to days of starting a dopamine antagonist; treat with benztropine 1-2 mg IM/IV (recognizing the anticholinergic trade-off) or diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV

2. **Akathisia**: Intense subjective restlessness with an inability to sit still; may be misdiagnosed as agitation in TBI patients, leading to escalating doses of the causative medication; treat by discontinuing the offending agent; propranolol 10-20 mg BID-TID may help

3. **Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS)**: A medical emergency characterized by fever, severe rigidity (lead-pipe), autonomic instability, and altered mental status; CK markedly elevated; can occur with any dopamine antagonist; treatment: discontinue the offending agent, dantrolene for rigidity, bromocriptine for dopaminergic support, aggressive cooling and hydration

> **Clinical Pearl:** NMS can mimic paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity in TBI patients (both present with fever, autonomic instability, and rigidity). The key distinguishing feature is timing: NMS occurs after initiation or dose escalation of a dopamine antagonist, while PSH occurs in the context of severe brain injury without a clear medication precipitant. Always review the medication administration record when evaluating acute-onset fever and rigidity in a TBI patient.

### 8.13.5 Emerging Pharmacologic Approaches

Several pharmacologic strategies are under active investigation for TBI, though none have sufficient evidence for routine clinical use:

- **Progesterone**: Despite promising preclinical and early clinical data, the PROTECT III and SyNAPSe trials demonstrated no benefit over placebo for moderate-to-severe TBI
- **Erythropoietin (EPO)**: Neuroprotective properties in animal models; the EPO-TBI trial showed no improvement in neurological outcomes but did reduce mortality in some subgroups
- **Cerebrolysin**: A peptide preparation with neurotrophic activity used in some international settings; evidence insufficient for recommendation
- **Citicoline (CDP-choline)**: Cholinergic precursor; the COBRIT trial showed no benefit over placebo
- **NMDA receptor modulators (beyond amantadine)**: Memantine is under study for chronic cognitive deficits
- **Tranexamic acid**: The CRASH-3 trial (2019) demonstrated that early tranexamic acid (within 3 hours of injury) reduces head injury-related death in patients with mild-to-moderate TBI (GCS 9-15) but not in severe TBI (GCS 3-8) or in those with pupil abnormalities

### 8.13.6 Pain Management Pharmacology in TBI

Pain management after TBI presents a pharmacologic challenge because many conventional analgesics carry risks that are amplified in the brain-injured population.

**Preferred Analgesics:**
- **Acetaminophen**: First-line for mild-moderate pain; no CNS depression; no seizure threshold effect; hepatotoxicity risk at high doses (limit to < 3g/day in TBI patients, who may have impaired hepatic function)
- **NSAIDs**: Effective for musculoskeletal and inflammatory pain; ibuprofen and naproxen preferred; short-term use only (GI bleeding risk, renal effects); may be beneficial for HO prophylaxis (indomethacin)
- **Gabapentin/Pregabalin**: First-line for neuropathic pain; additional benefit for PSH management; does not impair recovery; sedation is the main concern

**Analgesics Requiring Caution:**
- **Tramadol**: Mixed opioid/serotonergic mechanism; lowers seizure threshold (relatively contraindicated in TBI with seizure risk); serotonin syndrome risk when combined with SSRIs
- **Opioids**: Should be minimized; cause cognitive impairment, constipation (triggers agitation), respiratory depression; contribute to medication overuse headache; highest priority for tapering during rehabilitation
- **Muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol)**: Anticholinergic properties (cyclobenzaprine) or CNS depression; use cautiously and briefly if at all

**Analgesics to AVOID:**
- **Meperidine (Demerol)**: Active metabolite (normeperidine) lowers seizure threshold significantly; anticholinergic properties; should never be used in TBI
- **Butalbital combinations (Fioricet/Fiorinal)**: Barbiturate component impairs cognitive recovery; high risk for medication overuse headache

> **Clinical Pearl:** Pain management in TBI follows the same "start low, go slow" principle as all TBI pharmacology, with the additional mandate to minimize opioid exposure. A non-opioid, multimodal approach using acetaminophen scheduled around the clock, short-course NSAIDs for acute musculoskeletal pain, and gabapentin for neuropathic components is the standard of care. Remember that untreated pain is a common CAUSE of agitation -- the goal is to treat pain effectively without cognitive impairment, not to avoid all analgesics.

### 8.13.7 Pharmacologic Management of Spasticity-Related Considerations

While spasticity management is addressed comprehensively in its own chapter, the pharmacologic principles as they relate to the broader TBI medication regimen deserve mention here for completeness:

- **Baclofen**: GABA-B agonist; 5-20 mg TID-QID (max 80 mg/day); MUST NOT be discontinued abruptly (withdrawal seizures, severe rebound spasticity); important drug interaction with other sedating agents
- **Tizanidine**: Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist; 2-8 mg TID (max 36 mg/day); monitor LFTs (hepatotoxicity); sedation can be leveraged for nighttime dosing to aid sleep; note overlap of mechanism with clonidine (used for PSH)
- **Dantrolene**: Direct muscle relaxant acting at the sarcoplasmic reticulum (peripheral mechanism); unique among spasticity agents in that it does NOT act centrally; therefore does not add to CNS sedation burden; monitor LFTs closely; also used in NMS treatment
- **Botulinum toxin**: Focal injection targeting specific muscles; does not contribute to systemic cognitive burden; increasingly used as first-line for focal spasticity in TBI
