## 1. Glasgow Coma Scale [BOARD]

### 1.1 Historical Development and Clinical Significance

The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) was introduced by Graham Teasdale and Bryan Jennett at the University of Glasgow in 1974 as a standardized method for assessing the depth and duration of impaired consciousness after brain injury. In the ensuing five decades, the GCS has become the most widely used neurological assessment tool in the world, embedded in trauma triage protocols, clinical practice guidelines, and severity classification systems across virtually every healthcare system globally.

The GCS assesses three independent domains of neurological function --- eye opening, verbal response, and motor response --- yielding a composite score ranging from 3 (deepest coma) to 15 (fully alert and oriented). It is critical to appreciate that the minimum possible GCS score is 3, not 0, because each subscale has a minimum value of 1 (representing no response).

### 1.2 Complete GCS Scoring

#### Eye Opening Response (E: 1--4)

The eye opening component evaluates the arousal system, primarily mediated by the ascending reticular activating system (ARAS) in the brainstem. Spontaneous eye opening indicates intact arousal mechanisms, while the absence of eye opening to any stimulus suggests severe brainstem dysfunction.

| Score | Response | Assessment Method |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| 4 | Spontaneous | Eyes open without stimulation; intact arousal system |
| 3 | To voice/command | Eyes open in response to verbal stimulation; no physical stimulus required |
| 2 | To pressure/pain | Eyes open only in response to peripheral pressure (e.g., trapezius squeeze, nail bed pressure) |
| 1 | None | No eye opening to any stimulus |

#### Verbal Response (V: 1--5)

The verbal component assesses language function, orientation, and higher cortical processing. It requires intact speech production pathways, language comprehension, and the cognitive capacity for orientation to person, place, and time.

| Score | Response | Clinical Characteristics |
|-------|----------|------------------------|
| 5 | Oriented | Correctly identifies who, where, and when; sustained conversational ability |
| 4 | Confused | Speaks in grammatically correct sentences but disoriented to person, place, or time |
| 3 | Inappropriate words | Random or exclamatory single words; no sustained conversational exchange; may include profanity |
| 2 | Incomprehensible sounds | Moaning, groaning, or other vocalizations without recognizable words |
| 1 | None | No verbal output |

#### Motor Response (M: 1--6)

The motor component is the most clinically significant element of the GCS. It has the widest scoring range (6 points), the greatest prognostic value, and the highest reliability among the three subscales. Motor responses reflect the level of functional integrity along the neuraxis from cortex to brainstem.

| Score | Response | Anatomical Correlate | Clinical Significance |
|-------|----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 6 | Obeys commands | Intact cortical motor pathways | Best response; demonstrates comprehension and voluntary motor control |
| 5 | Localizes pain | Purposeful cortical involvement | Patient reaches toward stimulus and attempts to remove it; must cross midline |
| 4 | Normal flexion (withdrawal) | Spinal reflex arc | Stereotypical flexor withdrawal away from painful stimulus; not purposeful |
| 3 | Abnormal flexion (decorticate) | Lesion ABOVE red nucleus (cortical/subcortical) | Stereotypical upper extremity flexion with wrist flexion, adduction; lower extremity extension |
| 2 | Extension (decerebrate) | Lesion BELOW red nucleus (brainstem) | Upper extremity extension with internal rotation and wrist pronation; lower extremity extension |
| 1 | None | Severe brainstem or spinal cord dysfunction | No motor response to any stimulus |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between decorticate (M3) and decerebrate (M2) posturing is one of the most commonly tested TBI concepts. Decorticate posturing indicates a lesion above the red nucleus and carries a BETTER prognosis. Decerebrate posturing indicates a brainstem-level lesion and carries a WORSE prognosis. Mnemonic: deCORticate = COR = core/heart = arms flex toward the heart (elbows bent). DeceREBRate = arms extend rigidly.

### 1.3 GCS Severity Classification

The GCS total score is used to classify TBI into three severity categories:

| Severity | GCS Score | Clinical Correlate |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Mild | 13--15 | May have brief LOC, confusion, or amnesia |
| Moderate | 9--12 | Prolonged confusion, significant neurological deficits |
| Severe | 3--8 | Coma; defines the threshold for intubation in many protocols |

A GCS of 8 or less defines coma and is the traditional threshold for endotracheal intubation for airway protection. The GCS should ideally be assessed after resuscitation (adequate blood pressure, oxygenation, and glucose correction) and before administration of sedatives or paralytics.

### 1.4 Prognostic Value of the Motor Component

Among the three GCS subscales, the motor component has consistently demonstrated the strongest prognostic value in multiple large databases, including the IMPACT (International Mission for Prognosis and Analysis of Clinical Trials in TBI) and CRASH (Corticosteroid Randomization After Significant Head Injury) datasets.

The best motor response at two weeks post-injury is the strongest single prognostic indicator on the GCS. This finding has been replicated across multiple studies and populations and is a high-yield board examination concept. The motor score alone performs nearly as well as the total GCS in predicting six-month outcomes, leading some investigators to advocate for use of the motor score as a standalone severity measure.

### 1.5 GCS Limitations

Despite its widespread adoption, the GCS has several well-documented limitations that must be understood for both clinical practice and board examinations:

1. **Intubation:** The verbal component cannot be assessed in intubated patients. By convention, the verbal score is reported with a "T" suffix (e.g., "3T + 5" for E3VT M5) or the motor and eye components are reported alone. This effectively reduces the GCS to a 10-point scale (E + M) in ICU patients.

2. **Sedation and paralysis:** Pharmacological agents routinely used in ICU settings profoundly affect GCS scoring. Sedation depresses the GCS by an average of 1.7 points. The GCS is unreliable in patients receiving neuromuscular blockade.

3. **Alcohol and drug intoxication:** Ethanol, opioids, benzodiazepines, and stimulants each confound GCS components differently, and toxicology screening is essential for accurate interpretation.

4. **Inter-rater variability:** Agreement between raters is moderate at best, with the motor component paradoxically showing both the highest prognostic value and the greatest inter-rater discordance, particularly in distinguishing withdrawal (M4) from localization (M5).

5. **Floor effect:** The minimum GCS of 3 provides no discrimination among patients at the most severe end of the spectrum, precluding identification of brain death or differentiation among the most severely injured.

6. **Absence of brainstem assessment:** The GCS does not evaluate brainstem reflexes (pupillary response, corneal reflex, cough reflex), which carry critical prognostic information.

These limitations motivated the development of alternative scoring systems, most notably the FOUR Score and the GCS-Pupils score.

## 2. GCS-Pupils Score (GCS-P) [BOARD]

### 2.1 Development and Rationale

The GCS-Pupils score (GCS-P) was introduced by Brennan, Murray, and Teasdale in 2018 as a strategy to combine the two most important clinical indicators of TBI severity --- the GCS score and pupillary reactivity --- into a single, simple index. Analysis of the IMPACT database, comprising more than 15,000 patients with TBI, demonstrated that the prognostic information provided by pupillary reactivity was complementary to that of the GCS and that combining them enhanced prognostic accuracy.

### 2.2 Calculation

The GCS-P is calculated by subtracting the Pupil Reactivity Score (PRS) from the total GCS score:

**GCS-P = GCS - PRS**

The Pupil Reactivity Score is assigned as follows:

| Pupillary Status | PRS |
|-----------------|-----|
| Both pupils reactive to light | 0 |
| One pupil unreactive to light | 1 |
| Both pupils unreactive to light | 2 |

This yields a GCS-P range of 1 to 15 (GCS minimum of 3 minus maximum PRS of 2 = 1; GCS maximum of 15 minus minimum PRS of 0 = 15).

### 2.3 Prognostic Performance

The addition of pupillary reactivity to the GCS substantially improves prognostic discrimination. In the IMPACT database:

- Loss of pupillary reactivity was associated with a progressive increase in mortality: 16.3% when both pupils reacted, 38.3% when one pupil was unreactive, and 58.7% when neither pupil was reactive
- The GCS-P predicted 74% of deaths compared to 51% with GCS alone
- The performance of the simple GCS-P was comparable to more complex multivariate prognostic models
- The GCS-P provided particularly strong discrimination at the severe end of the spectrum, where the GCS alone has a floor effect

> **Clinical Pearl:** The GCS-P score ranges from 1 to 15 and is calculated as GCS minus PRS (where PRS is 0 for both reactive, 1 for one unreactive, 2 for both unreactive). On boards, remember that GCS-P predicted 74% of deaths versus 51% for GCS alone. The GCS-P is especially useful for improving prognostic discrimination among patients with low GCS scores.

### 2.4 Clinical Applications

The GCS-P has been validated in multiple independent datasets across diverse geographic and healthcare settings. It has been incorporated into updated prognostic models and is increasingly referenced in clinical practice guidelines. Its simplicity --- requiring only the GCS score and a simple pupillary assessment already performed in standard trauma evaluation --- facilitates rapid bedside application without additional equipment or training.

## 3. FOUR Score (Full Outline of UnResponsiveness) [BOARD]

### 3.1 Development

The FOUR Score was developed by Eelco F.M. Wijdicks and colleagues at the Mayo Clinic in 2005, specifically designed to address the limitations of the GCS in critically ill patients, particularly those who are intubated. The acronym "FOUR" represents both the four components of the scale and the fact that each component is scored from 0 to 4, yielding a total score range of 0 to 16.

### 3.2 Scoring Components

#### Eye Response (E: 0--4)

| Score | Response |
|-------|----------|
| 4 | Eyelids open, tracking OR blinking to command |
| 3 | Eyelids open but NOT tracking |
| 2 | Eyelids closed, open to LOUD VOICE |
| 1 | Eyelids closed, open to PAIN |
| 0 | Eyelids remain closed with pain |

#### Motor Response (M: 0--4)

| Score | Response |
|-------|----------|
| 4 | Thumbs up, fist, or peace sign (to command) |
| 3 | Localizing to pain |
| 2 | Flexion response to pain |
| 1 | Extension response to pain |
| 0 | No response OR generalized myoclonus status |

#### Brainstem Reflexes (B: 0--4)

| Score | Response |
|-------|----------|
| 4 | Pupil AND corneal reflexes present |
| 3 | ONE pupil wide and fixed |
| 2 | Pupil OR corneal reflexes absent |
| 1 | Pupil AND corneal reflexes absent |
| 0 | Absent pupil, corneal, AND cough reflex |

#### Respiration (R: 0--4)

| Score | Response |
|-------|----------|
| 4 | Not intubated, regular breathing pattern |
| 3 | Not intubated, Cheyne-Stokes breathing |
| 2 | Not intubated, irregular breathing |
| 1 | Intubated, breathes ABOVE ventilator rate |
| 0 | Intubated, breathes AT ventilator rate or apnea |

### 3.3 Advantages Over GCS

The FOUR Score offers several distinct advantages that make it particularly suited for the ICU environment:

| Feature | GCS | FOUR Score |
|---------|-----|------------|
| Assesses intubated patients | No (verbal component lost) | Yes (no verbal component) |
| Brainstem reflex assessment | No | Yes (pupil, corneal, cough) |
| Detects locked-in syndrome | No | Yes (blink to command = E4) |
| Identifies brain death | No (minimum = 3) | Yes (score of 0 suggests brain death) |
| Minimum score | 3 | 0 |
| Score range per component | Variable (1--4, 1--5, 1--6) | Uniform (0--4 each) |
| Inter-rater reliability | Moderate | Good to excellent |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The FOUR Score is superior to GCS for ICU patients because: (1) It can assess intubated patients; (2) It includes brainstem reflexes; (3) It distinguishes locked-in syndrome from vegetative state (locked-in patients can blink to command = Eye 4); (4) A score of 0 suggests brain death; (5) Each component has the same 0--4 range, making it easier to learn. Mnemonic: FOUR = 4 components, each scored 0--4, total 0--16. Components: Eye, Motor, Brainstem, Respiration ("E-M-B-R").

### 3.4 Prognostic Value

Multiple validation studies have demonstrated that the FOUR Score has prognostic accuracy comparable to or exceeding the GCS in predicting in-hospital mortality, ICU mortality, and functional outcomes in critically ill patients. The brainstem reflexes component provides unique prognostic information not captured by the GCS, and the respiration component helps identify respiratory drive, which is relevant for ventilator weaning decisions and brain death determination.

A FOUR Score of 0 indicates absent eye opening, absent motor response, absent brainstem reflexes, and absent spontaneous respiratory effort --- a constellation of findings consistent with brain death. This capacity for brain death screening is a unique advantage not available with the GCS.

### 3.5 When to Use the FOUR Score

The FOUR Score is recommended in the following clinical contexts:

- Intubated patients in whom the GCS verbal component cannot be assessed
- ICU patients requiring comprehensive neurological monitoring
- Evaluation of patients with suspected brainstem pathology
- Screening for brain death
- Differentiating locked-in syndrome from vegetative state
- Serial neurological assessment in neurocritical care settings

## 4. Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS) and GOS-Extended [BOARD]

### 4.1 Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS)

The Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS) was developed by Jennett and Bond in 1975 as a companion to the GCS, providing a standardized measure of global outcome after brain injury. The GOS is a five-point ordinal scale ranging from 1 (death) to 5 (good recovery).

| GOS Score | Category | Description |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Dead | -- |
| 2 | Vegetative State | No awareness of environment; sleep-wake cycles present |
| 3 | Severe Disability | Conscious but dependent; requires daily support for ADLs |
| 4 | Moderate Disability | Independent but disabled; can work in sheltered or supported setting |
| 5 | Good Recovery | Resumption of normal life; may have minor residual deficits |

The GOS is assessed through a structured interview, typically administered at 6 or 12 months post-injury. A higher score indicates a better outcome.

### 4.2 Glasgow Outcome Scale-Extended (GOS-E)

Recognizing the limited sensitivity of the five-point GOS to clinically meaningful changes, Wilson and colleagues (1998) developed the GOS-Extended (GOS-E), which subdivides each GOS category (except Death and Vegetative State) into upper and lower tiers, yielding an eight-point scale.

| GOS-E Score | Category |
|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Dead |
| 2 | Vegetative State |
| 3 | Lower Severe Disability |
| 4 | Upper Severe Disability |
| 5 | Lower Moderate Disability |
| 6 | Upper Moderate Disability |
| 7 | Lower Good Recovery |
| 8 | Upper Good Recovery |

### 4.3 Clinical Significance

The GOS-E is considered the gold standard outcome measure for TBI clinical trials and has been used as the primary endpoint in major randomized controlled trials, including the DECRA trial (Cooper et al., 2011), the RESCUEicp trial (Hutchinson et al., 2016), and the CRASH-3 trial (2019). Its widespread adoption facilitates comparison across studies and populations.

> **Clinical Pearl:** GOS-E is the gold standard for TBI clinical trials. Higher score = better outcome. The key advantage of GOS-E over GOS is greater sensitivity to clinically meaningful change by splitting each functional category (except death and VS) into upper and lower tiers. Know both scales and their scoring for boards.

### 4.4 Limitations

The GOS and GOS-E are global outcome measures that do not capture specific domains of impairment. A patient classified as GOS 5 (Good Recovery) may nonetheless have significant cognitive, emotional, or behavioral impairments that affect quality of life. The scales are best suited for population-level outcome assessment in clinical trials rather than for individual patient rehabilitation planning.

## 5. Disability Rating Scale (DRS) [BOARD]

### 5.1 Overview and Structure

The Disability Rating Scale (DRS) was developed by Rappaport and colleagues in 1982 specifically for the TBI population. Unlike the GOS, which provides only a global snapshot, the DRS captures the full spectrum of recovery from coma through community reintegration, making it particularly useful for tracking rehabilitation progress.

The DRS comprises eight items organized across four domains:

| Domain | Items | Based On |
|--------|-------|----------|
| Arousability/Awareness | Eye opening, communication ability, motor response | Modified GCS components |
| Cognitive Ability for Self-Care | Feeding, toileting, grooming | Awareness of how and when to perform these activities |
| Dependence on Others | Level of functioning | Degree of independence vs. dependence |
| Psychosocial Adaptability | Employability | Community reintegration and vocational capacity |

### 5.2 Scoring and Interpretation

The DRS total score ranges from 0 to 30, with LOWER scores indicating BETTER outcomes (opposite direction from GOS).

| Score Range | Disability Level |
|-------------|-----------------|
| 0 | No disability |
| 1 | Mild disability |
| 2--3 | Partial disability |
| 4--6 | Moderate disability |
| 7--11 | Moderately severe disability |
| 12--16 | Severe disability |
| 17--21 | Extremely severe disability |
| 22--24 | Vegetative state |
| 25--29 | Extreme vegetative state |
| 30 | Death |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The DRS score direction is OPPOSITE to GOS: DRS 0 = no disability (best), DRS 30 = death (worst), whereas GOS 1 = death (worst), GOS 5 = good recovery (best). The DRS is more sensitive to change during rehabilitation than the GOS because it captures the full spectrum from coma to community reintegration. This is a commonly tested distinction.

### 5.3 Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages:**
- Developed specifically for TBI populations
- Captures the full continuum from coma through community reintegration
- More sensitive to rehabilitation-phase changes than GOS
- Validated against functional outcomes and vocational status
- Can be administered in approximately 5--10 minutes

**Limitations:**
- Ceiling effect for mildly impaired patients (many score 0--1)
- Limited sensitivity to cognitive and behavioral impairments that do not affect physical independence
- Employability assessment may not reflect the complexity of modern vocational rehabilitation
- Less commonly used in TBI clinical trials than GOS-E

### 5.4 Comparison with Other Scales

| Feature | DRS | GOS | FIM |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|
| Score range | 0--30 | 1--5 | 18--126 |
| Direction | Lower = better | Higher = better | Higher = better |
| TBI-specific | Yes | No (but widely used) | No |
| Spectrum covered | Coma to community | Global outcome only | ADL independence only |
| Sensitivity to change during rehab | Good | Poor | Moderate |
| Ceiling effect | Mild impairment | Moderate disability | Moderate-severe TBI |
| Primary use | Rehabilitation monitoring | Clinical trials | Rehabilitation outcomes |

## 6. Rancho Los Amigos Levels of Cognitive Functioning (I--X) [BOARD]

### 6.1 Overview

The Rancho Los Amigos Levels of Cognitive Functioning Scale (LCFS), originally developed at Rancho Los Amigos National Rehabilitation Center in Downey, California, is one of the most commonly tested TBI assessment tools on PM&R board examinations. The scale describes a hierarchical progression of cognitive and behavioral recovery after TBI through ten levels, from complete unresponsiveness (Level I) to modified independence (Level X).

The original scale (1972) comprised eight levels. Levels IX and X were added in 1998 to better characterize the community reintegration phase of recovery.

### 6.2 Complete Rancho Scale: All Ten Levels

#### Level I: No Response
- **Description:** No response to any stimulation --- visual, auditory, tactile, or painful
- **Clinical correlate:** Deep coma; absent arousal
- **Management:** Sensory stimulation program; positioning and range of motion to prevent contractures; skin integrity monitoring; family education about prognosis and trajectory

#### Level II: Generalized Response
- **Description:** Inconsistent, nonpurposeful reactions to stimuli; responds to pain with gross body movement or physiological changes (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate)
- **Clinical correlate:** Emerging arousal; sleep-wake cycles may begin to appear
- **Management:** Continue sensory stimulation; structured multimodal stimulation program; positioning; monitor for signs of progression

#### Level III: Localized Response
- **Description:** Inconsistent but purposeful responses; may follow simple one-step commands; turns head toward sound; pulls at tubes and restraints; may visually track intermittently
- **Clinical correlate:** Emerging awareness; transition from vegetative state toward minimally conscious state
- **Management:** Simple commands; orientation cues; structured stimulation; safety measures for tube-pulling; begin evaluating for disorders of consciousness diagnosis using CRS-R

#### Level IV: Confused-Agitated
- **Description:** Heightened activity level with bizarre, nonpurposeful behavior; no short-term memory; incoherent or inappropriate verbalization; may be combative; severe attentional deficits; maximal confusion and disorientation
- **Clinical correlate:** Posttraumatic agitation (occurs in approximately 33% of TBI patients in acute rehabilitation)
- **Management:** Minimize environmental stimulation; quiet, structured environment; 1:1 supervision; padded environment; consistent routine with familiar caregivers; pharmacotherapy when environmental modifications are insufficient

> **Clinical Pearl:** Rancho Level IV (Confused-Agitated) is the most commonly tested level and the most challenging for rehabilitation teams. Key management principles: (1) Environmental modifications FIRST --- before medications; (2) Beta-blockers (propranolol) have the best evidence for pharmacological management per Cochrane review; (3) AVOID haloperidol (irreversible D2 blockade, slows motor recovery) and benzodiazepines (paradoxical agitation, impairs recovery, amnestic effects); (4) The Agitated Behavior Scale (ABS) has 14 items scored 1--4 (range 14--56), with scores >21 indicating clinically significant agitation.

#### Level V: Confused-Inappropriate, Non-Agitated
- **Description:** Alert and responds to simple commands; poor memory with confabulation; unable to learn new information; inappropriate verbalizations; agitation has resolved but confusion persists
- **Clinical correlate:** Posttraumatic amnesia (PTA) phase; patient appears alert but cannot encode new memories
- **Management:** Structure and consistency; memory aids; repetitive orientation; simple ADL training with maximum cueing; redirection rather than confrontation

#### Level VI: Confused-Appropriate
- **Description:** Goal-directed behavior with cues; follows simple directions consistently; improved remote memory; emerging awareness of deficits; recognizes self, family, and familiar staff
- **Clinical correlate:** Late PTA phase; beginning of purposeful engagement with rehabilitation
- **Management:** Increase functional task complexity; supervised ADL training; safety awareness education; begin supervised community outings; capitalize on emerging self-awareness to motivate participation

#### Level VII: Automatic-Appropriate
- **Description:** Oriented to setting; goes through daily routine automatically; minimal confusion; shallow recall; poor insight and judgment; requires supervision for safety; appears functional but operates on autopilot
- **Clinical correlate:** Post-PTA phase; routine is preserved but novel problem-solving is impaired
- **Management:** Community re-entry training; supervised community outings; vocational assessment; compensatory strategy training; driving evaluation consideration; safety judgment assessment

#### Level VIII: Purposeful-Appropriate
- **Description:** Alert and oriented; recalls and integrates past and recent events; independent in familiar environments; impaired abstract reasoning and stress tolerance; may have cognitive impairments but functional in structured settings
- **Clinical correlate:** Transitioning to community independence; residual cognitive deficits become apparent in demanding situations
- **Management:** Community reintegration; vocational rehabilitation; driving evaluation; independent living skills training; judgment and problem-solving training; coping strategy development

#### Level IX: Purposeful-Appropriate (Standby Assistance)
- **Description:** Uses assistive memory devices; aware of and self-monitors impairments; anticipates consequences of actions; depression may emerge as insight improves; self-directed with standby assistance for novel situations
- **Clinical correlate:** Adjustment phase; psychological challenges may increase as insight develops
- **Management:** Advance vocational goals; counseling for adjustment and depression; increasing independence with periodic monitoring; social skills refinement; family counseling

#### Level X: Purposeful-Appropriate (Modified Independent)
- **Description:** Handles multiple tasks simultaneously; independently uses compensatory strategies; may need more time or periodic breaks; functionally independent in the community; residual impairments managed through self-directed compensatory approaches
- **Clinical correlate:** Maximum functional recovery; may continue to make gains but functions independently
- **Management:** Discharge from structured rehabilitation; periodic community follow-up as needed; support for novel or stressful situations; long-term monitoring for late complications

### 6.3 Phase Groupings

For clinical and board purposes, the Rancho levels can be organized into functional phases:

| Phase | Rancho Levels | Clinical Stage |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Disorders of consciousness | I--III | Coma, vegetative state, minimally conscious state |
| Posttraumatic agitation | IV | Agitation phase; peak pharmacological intervention |
| Posttraumatic amnesia | V--VI | Confusion and memory impairment phase |
| Higher-level cognitive recovery | VII--VIII | Gaining independence; emerging community function |
| Community reintegration | IX--X | Added in expanded scale; fine-tuning community skills |

## 7. Functional Independence Measure (FIM) in TBI [BOARD]

### 7.1 Structure

The Functional Independence Measure (FIM) is a widely used rehabilitation outcome measure comprising 18 items organized into two domains:

**Motor Domain (13 items):**
- Self-care (6 items): eating, grooming, bathing, upper body dressing, lower body dressing, toileting
- Sphincter control (2 items): bladder management, bowel management
- Transfers (3 items): bed/chair/wheelchair, toilet, tub/shower
- Locomotion (2 items): walk/wheelchair, stairs

**Cognitive Domain (5 items):**
- Communication (2 items): comprehension, expression
- Social cognition (3 items): social interaction, problem solving, memory

Each item is scored on a 7-point ordinal scale:

| Score | Level of Independence |
|-------|-----------------------|
| 7 | Complete independence |
| 6 | Modified independence (device, extra time) |
| 5 | Supervision |
| 4 | Minimal assistance (patient performs >=75%) |
| 3 | Moderate assistance (patient performs >=50%) |
| 2 | Maximal assistance (patient performs >=25%) |
| 1 | Total assistance (patient performs <25%) |

Total FIM score range: 18 (total dependence) to 126 (complete independence).

### 7.2 FIM Efficiency

FIM efficiency (FIM gain divided by length of stay) is a commonly reported rehabilitation outcome metric:

**FIM Efficiency = (Discharge FIM - Admission FIM) / Length of Stay (days)**

Higher FIM efficiency indicates more rapid functional improvement per day of rehabilitation. This metric is used for program evaluation, reimbursement justification, and comparison across rehabilitation facilities.

### 7.3 Limitations of FIM in TBI

The FIM was originally developed for general rehabilitation populations and has significant limitations when applied to TBI:

1. **Motor weighting:** 13 of 18 items (72%) are motor-based, meaning the FIM is heavily biased toward physical function. Many TBI patients achieve near-perfect motor scores while retaining substantial cognitive and behavioral impairments.

2. **Cognitive undercounting:** Only 5 items address cognitive function, and these are limited to basic communication and social cognition. Executive dysfunction, memory deficits, impulsivity, disinhibition, and behavioral dysregulation --- the hallmark impairments of TBI --- are not adequately captured.

3. **Ceiling effect:** A substantial proportion of TBI patients, particularly those with mild-to-moderate injuries, achieve maximum or near-maximum FIM scores at discharge from inpatient rehabilitation despite ongoing functional limitations in community settings. This ceiling effect makes the FIM a poor tool for detecting disability in higher-functioning TBI patients.

4. **Insensitivity to behavioral impairment:** Agitation, apathy, emotional lability, poor social judgment, and impaired self-awareness --- common after TBI --- do not reliably depress FIM scores, leading to underestimation of disability.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The FIM has a ceiling effect in TBI because it is heavily weighted toward motor function (13/18 items) and has only 5 cognitive items that fail to capture executive dysfunction, memory deficits, and behavioral impairments. A TBI patient with a perfect FIM score may still have profound cognitive and behavioral deficits that prevent independent community living or return to work. This is a commonly tested limitation.

## 8. Coma Recovery Scale-Revised (CRS-R) [BOARD]

### 8.1 Overview

The Coma Recovery Scale-Revised (CRS-R), developed by Joseph Giacino and colleagues, is the gold standard standardized assessment tool for differentiating vegetative state (VS)/unresponsive wakefulness syndrome (UWS) from minimally conscious state (MCS). The AAN/ACRM practice guidelines designate the CRS-R as the only Level B recommended assessment instrument for disorders of consciousness.

The CRS-R comprises six hierarchically organized subscales with a total of 23 items and a score range of 0--23.

### 8.2 Subscale Structure

| Subscale | Score Range | Key Diagnostic Items |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Auditory Function | 0--4 | Score 4 = consistent movement to command (MCS+) |
| Visual Function | 0--5 | Score 5 = object recognition (MCS+); Score 3 = visual pursuit (MCS-); Score 2 = visual fixation (MCS-) |
| Motor Function | 0--6 | Score 6 = functional object use (EMERGENCE from MCS); Score 5 = automatic motor response (MCS) |
| Oromotor/Verbal Function | 0--3 | Score 3 = intelligible verbalization (EMERGENCE from MCS) |
| Communication | 0--2 | Score 2 = functional accurate communication (EMERGENCE from MCS) |
| Arousal | 0--3 | Attention and eye opening sustainability |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Mnemonic for CRS-R subscales: "AVMOCA" --- Auditory (0--4), Visual (0--5), Motor (0--6), Oromotor/verbal (0--3), Communication (0--2), Arousal (0--3). Total range 0--23. The Visual and Motor subscales have the highest score ranges and contain the key diagnostic items for MCS and emergence.

### 8.3 Diagnostic Criteria Using the CRS-R

The CRS-R enables differentiation among diagnostic categories in disorders of consciousness:

**Vegetative State (VS) / Unresponsive Wakefulness Syndrome (UWS):**
- Sleep-wake cycles present (eyes open spontaneously)
- NO evidence of awareness of self or environment
- Reflexive responses only (startle, withdrawal) but no purposeful behavior
- No visual fixation, no tracking, no command following

**Minimally Conscious State (MCS):**
- Definite but INCONSISTENT evidence of awareness
- Key behaviors include: visual pursuit, visual fixation, localization to noxious stimuli, reproducible movement to command, automatic motor responses, intelligible verbalization

The MCS has been further subcategorized into two diagnostically and prognostically important subgroups:

| Subcategory | Behaviors | Defining Feature | Prognostic Significance |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **MCS-minus (MCS-)** | Visual pursuit, visual fixation, localization to noxious stimuli, automatic motor responses | Non-language-mediated purposeful behaviors | Emergence to full consciousness: ~50% |
| **MCS-plus (MCS+)** | Command following, intelligible verbalization, object recognition | Language-mediated behaviors | Emergence to full consciousness: ~100% |

**Emergence from MCS:**
Emergence from MCS is defined by the presence of at least one of:
- Functional object use (Motor subscale score 6)
- Functional accurate communication, including reliable yes/no responses (Communication subscale score 2)
- Intelligible verbalization (Oromotor/Verbal subscale score 3)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between MCS+ and MCS- has critical prognostic and treatment implications. MCS+ patients (language behaviors present) have nearly 100% probability of emerging to full consciousness, while MCS- patients have approximately 50% probability. MCS+ indicates preserved cortical language networks and predicts better long-term functional outcomes. This subcategorization should guide goals-of-care discussions and rehabilitation intensity decisions.

### 8.4 Misdiagnosis Rate and Clinical Significance

One of the most clinically important findings in disorders of consciousness research is the high rate of misdiagnosis of VS/UWS. Up to 40% of patients clinically diagnosed as being in a vegetative state are found to actually be in MCS when formally assessed with the CRS-R. This misdiagnosis rate has profound implications:

- MCS patients have significantly better prognosis than VS patients
- Misdiagnosis as VS may lead to premature withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment
- Misdiagnosis may result in undertreatment (e.g., failure to provide amantadine, which has evidence for accelerating recovery in MCS)
- The AAN/ACRM guidelines now mandate serial CRS-R assessments with arousal facilitation protocols to minimize misdiagnosis

### 8.5 Assessment Protocol

The AAN/ACRM guidelines specify several important procedural requirements for CRS-R administration:

1. **Serial assessments:** Single assessments are insufficient due to fluctuating arousal and awareness in DoC patients; repeated assessments over time are required
2. **Arousal facilitation protocol:** Deep pressure stimulation, environmental stimulation, and upright positioning should precede each assessment to optimize arousal state
3. **VS/UWS terminology:** Vegetative state has been renamed to Unresponsive Wakefulness Syndrome (UWS) to reduce stigma and improve clinical accuracy

### 8.6 Timing Definitions for VS/UWS

| Term | Duration | Injury Type |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Persistent VS | >= 1 month | After traumatic OR non-traumatic brain injury |
| Permanent VS (traditional) | > 12 months | After TBI |
| Permanent VS (traditional) | > 3 months | After non-traumatic (anoxic) brain injury |

> **Clinical Pearl:** VS after TBI has a BETTER prognosis than VS after anoxic brain injury. Late recoveries beyond 12 months after TBI have been documented, leading the ACRM to prefer the term "chronic" over "permanent" to avoid prognostic nihilism. The practice of early withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment is identified as a major contributor to poor outcomes in the guidelines.

## 9. Posttraumatic Amnesia Assessment [BOARD]

### 9.1 Definition and Clinical Significance

Posttraumatic amnesia (PTA) is the period following TBI during which the patient is unable to form new continuous day-to-day memories. It encompasses the period of coma (if present), the period of confusion, and the period before continuous memory encoding resumes. PTA duration is the single most reliable predictor of long-term functional outcome after TBI and is superior to GCS for prognostication in many studies.

During PTA, patients characteristically demonstrate:
- Disorientation to person, place, time, and situation
- Inability to encode new memories (anterograde amnesia)
- Confusion and inattention
- Confabulation (production of false memories to fill gaps)
- Behavioral dysregulation
- Agitation (overlapping with Rancho Level IV)

### 9.2 Assessment Tools: GOAT

The Galveston Orientation and Amnesia Test (GOAT), developed by Levin, O'Donnell, and Grossman in 1979, was the first standardized instrument for assessing PTA. It comprises 10 questions addressing orientation (person, place, time) and memory for events before and after the injury.

**GOAT Scoring:**
- Score range: 0--100 (deductions are made for errors)
- Score >= 75 on 2 consecutive days = out of PTA
- Score 66--75 = borderline (equivocal PTA status)
- Score < 66 = in PTA (impaired)

### 9.3 Assessment Tools: Orientation Log (O-Log)

The Orientation Log (O-Log), developed as a simpler alternative to the GOAT, consists of 10 orientation items, each scored 0--3.

**O-Log Scoring:**
- Score range: 0--30
- Score >= 25 on 2 consecutive days = out of PTA
- Score < 25 = in PTA

### 9.4 Assessment Tools: Children's Orientation and Amnesia Test (COAT)

The COAT is a modified PTA assessment tool designed for children aged 3--15 years. It adapts the content and administration of orientation and memory questions to developmentally appropriate levels.

### 9.5 GOAT vs. O-Log Comparison

| Feature | GOAT | O-Log |
|---------|------|-------|
| Full name | Galveston Orientation and Amnesia Test | Orientation Log |
| Number of items | 10 questions | 10 orientation items |
| Score range | 0--100 | 0--30 |
| Resolution threshold | >= 75 on 2 consecutive days | >= 25 on 2 consecutive days |
| Relies on retrograde memory | Yes | No |
| Key advantage | Traditional; well-validated; long track record | Simpler; does not rely on retrograde memory; better for serial assessment |
| Key limitation | Retrograde memory component may be independently impaired | Less established than GOAT |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The O-Log has a practical advantage over the GOAT in that it does not rely on retrograde memory assessment, which can be independently impaired after TBI. This makes the O-Log more appropriate for serial assessment during the PTA resolution process. For boards, remember: GOAT >= 75 x 2 consecutive days; O-Log >= 25 x 2 consecutive days.

### 9.6 PTA Duration as Prognostic Indicator

PTA duration correlates with functional outcome more strongly than GCS or any other single clinical variable:

| PTA Duration | Severity | Expected Outcome |
|-------------|----------|------------------|
| < 5 minutes | Very mild | Good recovery expected |
| 5--60 minutes | Mild | Good recovery expected |
| 1--24 hours | Moderate | Good recovery likely |
| 1--7 days | Severe | May have persistent deficits |
| 1--4 weeks | Very severe | Significant disability likely |
| > 4 weeks | Extremely severe | Poor functional outcome likely |

**Critical prognostic thresholds:**
- PTA < 2 months: severe disability is unlikely
- PTA > 3 months: good recovery is unlikely

## 10. Mayo Classification System for TBI Severity [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 10.1 Development and Rationale

The Mayo Classification System was developed by Malec, Brown, and Leibson in 2007 to address a common clinical problem: the frequent absence of reliable acute severity data (GCS, LOC duration, PTA duration) in medical records, particularly for retrospective research and for patients who present with delayed evaluation. The system integrates multiple available indicators --- including neuroimaging findings, GCS, LOC, PTA, and post-concussive symptoms --- into a single classification framework.

### 10.2 Three-Tier Classification

| Tier | Classification | Criteria |
|------|---------------|----------|
| **Definite (Moderate-Severe)** | At least ONE of: | Death due to TBI; LOC >= 30 min; PTA >= 24 hours; GCS < 13 in first 24 hours; positive neuroimaging (hematoma, contusion, hemorrhagic DAI, brainstem injury, depressed skull fracture) |
| **Probable (Mild)** | At least ONE of: | LOC < 30 min; PTA < 24 hours; depressed, dazed, or confused mental state |
| **Possible (Symptomatic)** | At least ONE of: | Blurred vision, confusion, dizziness, headache, or nausea, but NO LOC, PTA, or documented alteration of mental status |

### 10.3 Performance Characteristics

In validation studies, the Mayo Classification achieved:
- **Sensitivity:** 89% for identifying Definite (Moderate-Severe) TBI
- **Specificity:** 98% for identifying Definite (Moderate-Severe) TBI

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Mayo Classification is most useful when acute severity data are incomplete or missing. Its incorporation of neuroimaging findings means that a patient with a normal GCS but a subdural hematoma on CT would be classified as Definite (Moderate-Severe), appropriately reflecting the clinical significance of intracranial pathology.

## 11. CT Scoring Systems [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 11.1 Overview

Several computed tomography (CT) based scoring systems have been developed to prognosticate outcomes after TBI. These systems quantify specific imaging features known to correlate with mortality and functional outcome.

### 11.2 Marshall CT Classification (1991)

The Marshall CT Classification, introduced by Marshall and colleagues in 1991 as part of the Traumatic Coma Data Bank, is the oldest and most widely recognized CT classification system for TBI. It categorizes injuries into six categories based on the presence of mass lesions, midline shift, and basal cistern compression.

| Category | Description | Key Features |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Diffuse Injury I | No visible intracranial pathology | Normal CT |
| Diffuse Injury II | Cisterns present, midline shift 0--5 mm | No high- or mixed-density lesion > 25 cm^3 |
| Diffuse Injury III (swelling) | Cisterns compressed or absent | Midline shift 0--5 mm; no high-density lesion > 25 cm^3 |
| Diffuse Injury IV (shift) | Midline shift > 5 mm | No high-density lesion > 25 cm^3 |
| Evacuated Mass Lesion (V) | Any lesion surgically evacuated | -- |
| Non-Evacuated Mass Lesion (VI) | High- or mixed-density lesion > 25 cm^3 | Not surgically evacuated |

**Limitations:** Categorical nature limits prognostic precision; does not account for traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage, intraventricular hemorrhage, or DAI; many patients do not fit neatly into one category.

### 11.3 Rotterdam CT Score (2005)

The Rotterdam CT Score was developed by Maas and colleagues using the IMPACT database. It is an ordinal sum score (range 1--6) that improved upon the Marshall Classification by incorporating traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage and epidural hematoma status.

| Component | Feature | Score |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Basal cisterns | Normal | 0 |
| | Compressed | 1 |
| | Absent | 2 |
| Midline shift | <= 5 mm | 0 |
| | > 5 mm | 1 |
| Epidural mass lesion | Present | 0 |
| | Absent | 1 |
| IVH or tSAH | Absent | 0 |
| | Present | 1 |
| **Sum score** | | **+1** (base) |

**Total range: 1--6** (higher score = worse prognosis)

Note: The presence of an epidural mass lesion paradoxically receives a score of 0 because EDH, when evacuated, carries a relatively favorable prognosis compared to other intracranial lesions.

### 11.4 Helsinki CT Score (2014)

The Helsinki CT Score was developed by Raj and colleagues as a continuous scoring system incorporating mass lesion type, suprasellar cistern status, intraventricular hemorrhage, and other features. It was designed specifically for predicting long-term outcomes including mortality and unfavorable neurological outcome at six months.

### 11.5 Stockholm CT Score (2010)

The Stockholm CT Score, developed by Nelson and colleagues, is distinguished by its use of midline shift as a continuous variable and its unique inclusion of diffuse axonal injury visible on CT. It demonstrated the best overall prognostic performance in multicenter validation studies.

### 11.6 Comparative Performance

In head-to-head comparisons across multicenter databases involving over 1,100 patients:

| Rank | Scoring System | Prognostic Accuracy (Pseudo-R^2) |
|------|---------------|--------------------------------|
| 1 (Best) | Stockholm CT Score | 0.24--0.28 |
| 2 | Helsinki CT Score | 0.18--0.22 |
| 3 | Rotterdam CT Score | 0.13--0.15 |
| 4 (Weakest) | Marshall CT Classification | 0.03--0.05 |

> **Clinical Pearl:** For prognostic accuracy: Stockholm > Helsinki > Rotterdam > Marshall. However, the Marshall classification remains the most commonly referenced in clinical practice due to its simplicity and historical precedent, while the Rotterdam score is most commonly used in TBI research trials. The Stockholm score uniquely accounts for DAI on CT and uses midline shift as a continuous variable.

## 12. Blood Biomarkers [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 12.1 FDA-Cleared Biomarkers: GFAP and UCH-L1

In February 2018, the FDA cleared the first blood test for TBI evaluation --- the Banyan Brain Trauma Indicator (BTI) --- under the Breakthrough Devices Program. This test measures two protein biomarkers released into the bloodstream following head trauma:

**GFAP (Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein):**
- Cell of origin: Astrocytes
- Marker type: Astrocyte activation/injury
- Cutoff value: < 30 pg/mL
- Temporal profile: Elevated within 1 hour of injury; peaks at 20--24 hours
- Clinical significance: Highly sensitive for intracranial lesions on CT

**UCH-L1 (Ubiquitin C-terminal Hydrolase L1):**
- Cell of origin: Neurons
- Marker type: Neuronal injury
- Cutoff value: < 360 pg/mL
- Temporal profile: Elevated within 1 hour; peaks at approximately 8 hours; shorter half-life than GFAP
- Clinical significance: Complements GFAP for neuronal-specific injury detection

### 12.2 Combined GFAP + UCH-L1 Performance

When combined, these biomarkers demonstrated exceptional performance in the ALERT-TBI trial:

| Performance Metric | Value |
|-------------------|-------|
| AUC for intracranial lesion detection | 0.94 |
| Negative predictive value (NPV) | 99.3% |
| Sensitivity | 97.6% |
| Estimated CT scan reduction | 30--40% in GCS 13--15 patients |

The test is intended for patients aged 18 years and older with suspected mild TBI (GCS 13--15), with blood samples collected within 12 hours of injury. A negative result (both biomarkers below cutoff) has a 99.3% negative predictive value for intracranial lesion on CT, potentially allowing clinicians to safely forego CT scanning in a substantial proportion of mTBI patients.

In January 2021, Abbott received FDA 510(k) clearance for a handheld point-of-care TBI plasma test (i-STAT TBI Plasma) based on the Banyan technology, enabling near-patient testing in emergency departments, sports venues, and military settings.

### 12.3 Emerging Biomarkers

**Neurofilament Light Chain (NfL):**
- Cell of origin: Large-caliber myelinated axons
- Marker type: Axonal injury (especially DAI)
- Temporal profile: Delayed peak (days to weeks), reflecting ongoing axonal degeneration
- Clinical significance: Emerging as a chronic-phase biomarker; elevated NfL at 6 months post-injury correlates with long-term disability; potential for monitoring recovery trajectory

**S100B:**
- Cell of origin: Astrocytes and Schwann cells
- Marker type: Glial injury
- Temporal profile: Rapid rise and decline (half-life 1--2 hours)
- Clinical significance: Widely used in European emergency departments to guide CT decisions (Scandinavian Neurotrauma Committee guidelines); limited specificity due to extracranial sources

**Tau and Phosphorylated Tau (p-tau):**
- Cell of origin: Neurons (axonal)
- Marker type: Axonal injury and tau pathology
- Temporal profile: Elevated acutely and may persist chronically
- Clinical significance: Plasma p-tau181 and p-tau231 are emerging as biomarkers for both acute TBI severity and chronic neurodegenerative risk; p-tau231 in CSF may distinguish CTE from Alzheimer disease

**MAP-2 (Microtubule-Associated Protein 2):**
- Cell of origin: Dendrites
- Marker type: Dendritic injury
- Clinical significance: Emerging marker that may improve specificity when combined with GFAP and UCH-L1; reflects dendritic pathology not captured by existing markers

### 12.4 Clinical Integration

The integration of blood biomarkers into TBI clinical pathways represents a paradigm shift from purely clinical assessment toward biologically informed decision-making. Potential applications include:

1. **Acute triage:** Reducing unnecessary CT scans in mTBI (GFAP/UCH-L1)
2. **Injury characterization:** Identifying the predominant type of cellular injury (astrocytic, neuronal, axonal, dendritic)
3. **Prognostication:** Correlating biomarker levels with expected recovery trajectory
4. **Chronic monitoring:** Tracking ongoing neurodegeneration via NfL and p-tau
5. **Clinical trial enrichment:** Using biomarker profiles for patient selection and stratification in therapeutic trials

## 13. Cognitive Motor Dissociation (CMD) [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 13.1 Definition and Discovery

Cognitive motor dissociation (CMD), also termed covert consciousness or covert cognition, refers to a condition in which patients who appear behaviorally unresponsive at the bedside demonstrate reproducible brain activation patterns consistent with consciousness when assessed with advanced neuroimaging or electrophysiology. These patients have preserved cognitive function that is disconnected from motor output pathways, rendering their awareness undetectable by standard behavioral examination.

The concept was first demonstrated by Owen and colleagues (2006) using task-based functional MRI (fMRI), in which a patient clinically diagnosed as being in a vegetative state showed brain activation patterns during mental imagery tasks (imagining playing tennis, imagining walking through rooms of her house) that were indistinguishable from those of healthy controls.

### 13.2 The Claassen NEJM 2024 Study

A landmark multicenter study by Bodien, Giacino, Claassen, and colleagues, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2024, provided the most comprehensive characterization of CMD prevalence and implications to date. Key findings include:

- **Prevalence:** Cognitive motor dissociation was detected in 25% (60/241) of behaviorally unresponsive patients (those without observable command-following on CRS-R)
- **Detection methods:** Task-based fMRI (motor imagery paradigms) and task-based EEG (motor imagery with machine learning classification)
- **Diagnostic categories affected:** CMD was identified in patients clinically diagnosed as coma, vegetative state/UWS, and MCS-minus
- **Prognostic significance:** CMD patients demonstrated better long-term functional outcomes than patients without covert awareness, even when behavioral presentations were identical at baseline

### 13.3 Detection Methods

| Method | Paradigm | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|----------|------------|-------------|
| Task-based fMRI | Mental imagery (e.g., imagine playing tennis, imagine navigating rooms) | High spatial resolution; well-validated | Requires transport to scanner; motion artifact; limited availability |
| Task-based EEG | Motor imagery with machine learning classification | Portable; bedside-capable; lower cost | Lower spatial resolution; requires specialized analysis |
| High-density EEG connectivity | Resting-state and perturbational approaches | No task required (useful in severely impaired) | Technical complexity; standardization challenges |

### 13.4 Clinical Implications

The detection of CMD has transformative implications for clinical practice:

1. **Goals of care:** CMD diagnosis may alter life-sustaining treatment decisions, as these patients have demonstrable awareness despite appearing unresponsive
2. **Rehabilitation intensity:** Patients with CMD may benefit from more aggressive rehabilitation interventions, including amantadine therapy and structured sensory-motor programs
3. **Communication augmentation:** Emerging brain-computer interface (BCI) technologies may enable communication in CMD patients through direct neural decoding
4. **Medicolegal considerations:** CMD raises important questions about consent, decision-making capacity, and quality-of-life assessment in patients previously assumed to lack awareness
5. **Prognostication:** CMD status provides independent prognostic information beyond what is available from behavioral assessment alone

### 13.5 AAN/ACRM Practice Guidelines

The AAN/ACRM practice guidelines for disorders of consciousness (Giacino et al., 2018; updated recommendations 2023) address CMD with the following key points:

1. The CRS-R is the ONLY Level B recommended standardized assessment tool for disorders of consciousness
2. VS has been renamed to Unresponsive Wakefulness Syndrome (UWS) to reduce stigma
3. Serial assessments with arousal facilitation protocol are required because up to 40% of patients diagnosed as VS/UWS on single assessment are reclassified as MCS on repeated structured assessment
4. Advanced neuroimaging (fMRI, PET) and electrophysiology (EEG) should be pursued when CMD is suspected based on clinical features or inconsistency between behavioral assessment and perceived responsiveness

> **Clinical Pearl:** Three critical points for boards regarding DoC assessment: (1) CRS-R is the only Level B recommended assessment tool; (2) VS is now termed UWS (Unresponsive Wakefulness Syndrome); (3) Approximately 25% of behaviorally unresponsive patients demonstrate covert consciousness on fMRI/EEG (CMD). Early withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment is identified as a major modifiable contributor to poor outcomes --- prognostic nihilism should be avoided.
