# Chapter 7: Medical and Neurological Complications After Traumatic Brain Injury

---

## Chapter Overview

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is a systemic disease process that extends far beyond the confines of the cranial vault. The secondary medical and neurological complications that arise in the days, weeks, months, and years following the initial insult represent some of the most challenging management dilemmas in rehabilitation medicine and are among the most heavily tested topics on the PM&R board examination. This chapter provides a comprehensive analysis of the major medical complications encountered after TBI, organized by system and clinical significance, with particular attention to the evidence base that informs current practice.

The complications discussed herein -- posttraumatic seizures, hydrocephalus, cranial nerve injuries, heterotopic ossification, venous thromboembolism, headache, sleep disorders, endocrine dysfunction, bowel and bladder dysfunction, and pressure injuries -- collectively account for a substantial portion of the morbidity burden borne by TBI survivors. Mastery of these topics requires not merely factual recall but an integrated understanding of the pathophysiological mechanisms linking primary brain injury to each downstream complication, the diagnostic algorithms used to identify them, and the treatment strategies that optimize recovery while avoiding iatrogenic harm.

## 7.1 Posttraumatic Seizures and Posttraumatic Epilepsy [BOARD]

### 7.1.1 Definitions and Temporal Classification

Posttraumatic seizures (PTS) are seizures that occur as a direct consequence of traumatic brain injury and represent one of the most commonly tested TBI complications on the PM&R board examination. The temporal classification system, which divides PTS into three categories based on their relationship to the time of injury, is fundamental to understanding both pathophysiology and management.

| Category | Timing | Pathophysiology | Clinical Significance | Prophylaxis Effective? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Immediate PTS | < 24 hours | Direct mechanical depolarization of neurons; impact seizures; acute metabolic derangement (ionic flux, glutamate excitotoxicity) | Generally benign; represent acute provoked seizures; rarely portend long-term epilepsy risk | Yes |
| Early PTS | 24 hours to 7 days | Acute pathophysiological cascades including cerebral edema, intracranial hemorrhage expansion, blood-brain barrier disruption, and acute inflammatory mediator release | Increased risk of status epilepticus; associated with secondary brain injury from metabolic demand; may worsen intracranial pressure | Yes |
| Late PTS | > 7 days | Chronic processes including cortical reorganization, gliotic scarring, aberrant neuronal network formation (epileptogenesis), iron deposition from hemoglobin degradation, chronic neuroinflammation | When recurrent, defines posttraumatic epilepsy (PTE); represents a permanent change in seizure susceptibility | **No -- prophylaxis NOT effective** |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between early and late PTS at the 7-day boundary is not arbitrary. It reflects a fundamental shift in underlying pathophysiology from acute provoked seizures (amenable to suppression with antiepileptic drugs) to chronic epileptogenesis (a structural reorganization process that current medications cannot prevent). This is the single most important concept in PTS management and underpins the "7-day rule" for prophylaxis.

Posttraumatic epilepsy (PTE) is defined as two or more unprovoked late posttraumatic seizures and represents one of the most common forms of acquired epilepsy. The distinction between a single late PTS and PTE has implications for long-term anticonvulsant management, driving restrictions, and employment considerations.

### 7.1.2 Incidence by TBI Severity

The incidence of posttraumatic seizures varies substantially with injury severity, and these numerical relationships are frequently tested on board examinations.

| TBI Severity | Overall PTS Incidence | Late PTS / PTE Risk | Key Determinant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild TBI (GCS 13-15) | 1-1.5% | < 1% | Low risk unless concurrent risk factors |
| Moderate TBI (GCS 9-12) | 2.9% | ~2% | Intermediate risk |
| Severe TBI (GCS 3-8) | 12-17% | 10-17% | Presence of cortical contusion, ICH |
| Penetrating TBI | 33-50% | 33-50% | **Highest risk category** |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Penetrating head injury carries the highest risk of late posttraumatic seizures and posttraumatic epilepsy, with incidence rates of 33-50%. This risk persists for decades after injury. In military populations with combat-related penetrating TBI, late PTS has been documented in approximately 25.8% at a median follow-up of 7.4 years, with some studies documenting even higher rates extending 15-35 years post-injury.

### 7.1.3 Risk Factors for Late Posttraumatic Seizures

Understanding the risk factors for late PTS and PTE is essential for clinical decision-making regarding long-term monitoring and patient counseling. The following risk factors have been identified through epidemiological studies spanning several decades.

1. **Penetrating head injury**: 33-50% incidence of late PTS; represents the single highest risk factor
2. **Intracranial hematoma** (epidural, subdural, or intraparenchymal hemorrhage): 25-30% risk; hemorrhage products (iron, hemoglobin degradation) are directly epileptogenic
3. **Early PTS** (seizure within the first week): Approximately 25% risk of subsequently developing late PTS; early seizure activity may prime epileptogenic networks
4. **Depressed skull fracture**: 3-70% depending on depression depth, dural penetration, and presence of underlying cortical injury; dural penetration substantially elevates risk
5. **Prolonged coma** (> 24 hours) or prolonged posttraumatic amnesia (> 24 hours): Up to 35% risk; reflects severity of diffuse brain injury
6. **Cortical contusion**, particularly temporal and frontal locations: Cortical tissue disruption provides the nidus for epileptogenic scar formation
7. **GCS < 10** at presentation: Significantly increased risk independent of other factors
8. **Age > 65 years**: Elevated risk, possibly related to comorbid cerebrovascular disease, reduced neural reserve, and polypharmacy
9. **Craniotomy or craniectomy**: Surgical intervention reflects injury severity and creates additional cortical disruption
10. **Multiple intracranial hemorrhage types**: The presence of more than one type of hemorrhage (e.g., combined SDH and SAH) compounds risk

### 7.1.4 Seizure Prophylaxis: The Temkin Trial and the 7-Day Rule

The evidence base for posttraumatic seizure prophylaxis rests primarily on the landmark randomized controlled trial published by Temkin and colleagues in the New England Journal of Medicine in 1990.

**Temkin et al. (NEJM 1990) -- Study Design and Results:**

- **Design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial
- **Population**: 404 patients with serious head trauma
- **Intervention**: Intravenous phenytoin loading within 24 hours of injury, followed by oral maintenance for one year; serum levels maintained in the high therapeutic range (3-6 micromol/L of free phenytoin)
- **Early PTS results (days 0-7)**: Phenytoin group 3.6% vs. placebo 14.2% (p < 0.001; risk ratio 0.27, 95% CI 0.12-0.62)
- **Late PTS results (day 8 through year 1)**: Phenytoin 21.5% vs. placebo 15.7% (p > 0.2)
- **Two-year cumulative late PTS**: Phenytoin 27.5% vs. placebo 21.1% (risk ratio 1.20, 95% CI 0.71-2.02; not significant)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Temkin trial conclusively demonstrated that phenytoin reduces early PTS (within 7 days) by approximately 73% but has absolutely no effect on late PTS. In fact, the numerical trend at two years actually favored placebo, though not statistically significant. This finding established the "7-day rule": antiepileptic drug (AED) prophylaxis should be used for 7 days only. Continuing prophylaxis beyond one week exposes patients to the cognitive side effects of AEDs without any benefit. The Brain Trauma Foundation 4th Edition codified this as a Level IIA recommendation.

### 7.1.5 Phenytoin vs. Levetiracetam: The Inaba 2013 Study

Since the Temkin trial established phenytoin as the reference standard, subsequent studies have compared levetiracetam (Keppra) to phenytoin as an alternative for early PTS prophylaxis. The most influential of these is the multicenter study by Inaba and colleagues published in 2013.

**Inaba et al. (J Trauma Acute Care Surg 2013) -- Key Findings:**

- **Design**: Prospective, observational, multicenter study across 9 Level I trauma centers
- **Population**: 813 patients with severe TBI requiring seizure prophylaxis
- **Groups**: Levetiracetam 1000 mg every 12 hours (n=406) vs. phenytoin per protocol (n=407)
- **Primary outcome**: Early PTS incidence -- levetiracetam 1.5% vs. phenytoin 1.5% (p = 0.997)
- **Adverse drug reactions**: Levetiracetam had fewer adverse effects
- **Mortality**: No significant difference

| Parameter | Phenytoin | Levetiracetam |
|---|---|---|
| Efficacy for early PTS | Effective (Temkin 1990 RCT) | Effective (non-inferior) |
| Efficacy for late PTS | NOT effective | NOT effective |
| Drug level monitoring | Required (narrow therapeutic index) | NOT required (wide therapeutic index) |
| Drug interactions | Many (CYP450 inducer: CYP2C9, 2C19, 3A4) | Few (primarily renal excretion) |
| Cognitive side effects | Significant (impairs attention, processing speed, motor recovery) | Minimal |
| Effect on neurological recovery | May impede recovery with chronic use | Minimal impact on recovery |
| BTF 4th Edition recommendation | Level IIA (7-day prophylaxis) | Insufficient evidence to recommend over phenytoin |
| Current US trauma center practice | Declining use | Used by ~98% of US Level I trauma centers |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Although the BTF 4th Edition technically still lists phenytoin as the Level IIA recommendation (because the original Temkin RCT used phenytoin), clinical practice has shifted overwhelmingly to levetiracetam based on equivalent efficacy with a superior safety profile. Levetiracetam requires no therapeutic drug monitoring, has minimal CYP450 drug interactions (critical in polypharmacy-prone TBI patients), and does not impair neurological recovery. However, clinicians should be aware that levetiracetam can cause behavioral irritability -- a potentially problematic side effect in TBI patients already prone to agitation.

### 7.1.6 AED Withdrawal Timing

For patients who develop documented posttraumatic epilepsy and are placed on chronic anticonvulsant therapy, the question of when to attempt medication withdrawal is clinically important.

- **No seizures for 2 years**: Generally accepted as the minimum duration of seizure freedom before considering AED taper
- **EEG normalization**: Supports but does not guarantee successful withdrawal
- **Withdrawal should be gradual**: Taper over 2-6 months to minimize withdrawal seizure risk
- **Risk factors for recurrence**: Penetrating injury, persistent EEG abnormalities, multiple seizure types, structural lesion on imaging

The Neurocritical Care Society published updated guidelines in 2024 reaffirming the 7-day prophylaxis window for moderate-to-severe TBI and recommending levetiracetam as an acceptable alternative to phenytoin, with the notation that prophylaxis in mild and moderate TBI yields a very small absolute risk reduction of approximately 0.6%.

## 7.2 Posttraumatic Hydrocephalus [BOARD]

### 7.2.1 Epidemiology and Pathophysiology

Ventriculomegaly is an extraordinarily common imaging finding after severe TBI, present on CT scanning in 40-72% of patients. However, distinguishing clinically significant posttraumatic hydrocephalus (PTH) requiring intervention from passive ventricular dilation secondary to brain atrophy (hydrocephalus ex vacuo) is one of the most important diagnostic challenges in TBI rehabilitation.

True posttraumatic hydrocephalus requiring shunt placement occurs in approximately 3.9-8% of moderate-to-severe TBI cases. The pathophysiology differs depending on whether the hydrocephalus is communicating or non-communicating.

### 7.2.2 Classification

**Communicating Hydrocephalus (Most Common in TBI)**

Communicating hydrocephalus arises from impaired CSF absorption at the arachnoid granulations rather than from physical obstruction within the ventricular system. After TBI, blood products (from subarachnoid hemorrhage, intraventricular hemorrhage, or surgical contamination) and inflammatory proteins obstruct the arachnoid villi, preventing normal CSF reabsorption into the venous sinuses. CSF continues to circulate freely through the ventricular system but accumulates because it cannot be adequately absorbed. This mechanism produces the clinical syndrome of normal pressure hydrocephalus (NPH).

**Non-communicating (Obstructive) Hydrocephalus**

Non-communicating hydrocephalus results from physical blockage within the ventricular system itself. In the context of TBI, this most commonly occurs from intraventricular hemorrhage obstructing the foramen of Monro, the cerebral aqueduct (of Sylvius), or the fourth ventricular outlets (foramina of Luschka and Magendie). This form presents acutely with signs of elevated intracranial pressure and typically requires urgent neurosurgical intervention (external ventricular drain placement).

### 7.2.3 Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus: The Classic Triad

The Hakim-Adams triad (described in 1965) remains the clinical hallmark of communicating hydrocephalus presenting as NPH. The classic mnemonic is "Wet, Wacky, and Wobbly."

1. **Gait disturbance ("Wobbly")**: The FIRST symptom to appear and the MOST responsive to treatment. Characterized by a magnetic, wide-based, shuffling gait described as "feet stuck to the floor" with short stride length, reduced foot clearance, and impaired postural reflexes. Distinguished from Parkinsonian gait by the wider base of support and absence of true rigidity.

2. **Urinary incontinence ("Wet")**: The SECOND most responsive to shunting. Often preceded by urinary urgency and increased frequency before progressing to frank incontinence. The mechanism involves disruption of periventricular white matter pathways that mediate voluntary bladder control.

3. **Dementia ("Wacky")**: The LEAST responsive to shunting. Presents as a subcortical pattern of cognitive impairment with psychomotor slowing, apathy, inattention, and impaired executive function rather than the cortical pattern (aphasia, agnosia, apraxia) seen in Alzheimer disease. Memory retrieval is impaired but encoding may be relatively preserved (responds to cueing).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Gait disturbance is the cardinal feature of NPH and the symptom most likely to improve after VP shunt placement. If a TBI patient demonstrates clinical plateau or decline with new gait difficulty, urinary incontinence, and cognitive worsening in the setting of ventriculomegaly, PTH should be strongly suspected. Dementia, unfortunately, is the least likely component of the triad to respond to shunting, particularly if long-standing.

### 7.2.4 CT Findings: Distinguishing PTH from Ex Vacuo Ventriculomegaly

The radiographic distinction between true PTH and ex vacuo ventriculomegaly (passive ventricular dilation from cerebral atrophy) is critical and frequently tested.

| CT Finding | True PTH | Ex Vacuo (Atrophy) |
|---|---|---|
| Ventricular dilation | Uniform, all ventricles dilated out of proportion | Proportional to degree of sulcal widening |
| Periventricular lucency | **PRESENT** (transependymal CSF flow/migration) | Absent |
| Sulcal pattern | Effaced (compressed sulci) | Widened sulci (reflecting generalized brain volume loss) |
| Temporal horn dilation | Early and prominent (disproportionate) | Proportional to other ventricular changes |
| Evans index (frontal horn width / biparietal diameter) | > 0.30 (supports hydrocephalus) | May be elevated but accompanied by proportional atrophy |
| MRI CSF flow void at aqueduct | Accentuated (hyperdynamic flow) | Normal |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Periventricular lucency (also termed transependymal edema or transependymal CSF migration) on CT is the single most important imaging finding that distinguishes true PTH from ex vacuo ventriculomegaly. It represents CSF being forced through the ependymal lining of the ventricles into the periventricular white matter due to elevated intraventricular pressure. Its presence in a patient with clinical decline and ventriculomegaly strongly supports the diagnosis of treatable PTH.

### 7.2.5 Diagnostic Algorithm and Treatment

The standard diagnostic approach for suspected PTH involves a high-volume lumbar puncture (30-50 mL CSF removal) with pre- and post-procedure assessment of gait and cognition. Clinical improvement after large-volume CSF removal (even transiently) predicts favorable response to permanent CSF diversion. Patients who demonstrate improvement should be referred for ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt placement.

Additional diagnostic adjuncts include CSF infusion studies (measuring outflow resistance), extended lumbar drainage trials (72-hour external drainage), and radionuclide cisternography (demonstrating delayed or absent convexity flow), though the high-volume LP remains the most practical and widely used screening test.

## 7.3 Cranial Nerve Injuries [BOARD]

### 7.3.1 Overview and Frequency Ranking

Cranial nerve injuries are common after TBI, with the overall incidence varying by injury mechanism and severity. Registry data suggest that approximately 1% of moderate-to-severe TBI patients sustain clinically documented cranial nerve injuries, though subclinical injuries (particularly to CN I) are substantially more prevalent.

The frequency ranking of cranial nerve injuries after TBI is a high-yield board topic:

| Rank | Cranial Nerve | Name | Incidence Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| #1 | CN I | Olfactory | Most common overall; ONLY CN injury in mild TBI |
| #2 | CN VII | Facial | Associated with temporal bone fractures |
| #3 | CN VIII | Vestibulocochlear | Associated with temporal bone fractures |
| #4 | CN IV | Trochlear | Most common OCULOMOTOR nerve injury |
| #5 | CN III | Oculomotor | Associated with uncal herniation |
| #6 | CN VI | Abducens | False localizing sign of elevated ICP |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ranking "1-7-8" for the top three cranial nerve injuries in TBI should be committed to memory. CN I (olfactory) is the most commonly injured nerve overall and is the only cranial neuropathy seen in mild TBI. CN IV (trochlear) is the most commonly injured oculomotor nerve -- not CN III (oculomotor), which is a common misconception.

### 7.3.2 Individual Cranial Nerve Injury Profiles

**CN I (Olfactory Nerve)**

The olfactory nerve is uniquely vulnerable to traumatic injury due to the delicate nature of its unmyelinated fibers as they traverse the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone. During acceleration-deceleration injury, shearing forces disrupt these thin filaments at their passage through the bony foramina. CN I injury is also associated with anterior cranial fossa fractures and frontal lobe contusions.

- **Clinical presentation**: Anosmia (complete loss of smell) or hyposmia (partial loss); associated reduced flavor perception (ageusia) because flavor is predominantly smell-mediated
- **Testing**: University of Pennsylvania Smell Identification Test (UPSIT) -- a 40-item scratch-and-sniff standardized assessment
- **Recovery**: Approximately one-third of patients demonstrate some improvement within 3 months; hyposmia carries a better prognosis for recovery than complete anosmia
- **Safety implications**: Patients cannot detect gas leaks, smoke, or spoiled food; formal counseling regarding home safety (smoke detectors, gas alarms) is mandatory

**CN VII (Facial Nerve)**

The facial nerve has the longest intratemporal course of any cranial nerve, making it susceptible to injury from temporal bone fractures. CN VII palsy after TBI may be immediate (suggesting nerve transection with poorer prognosis) or delayed in onset (suggesting edema or neuropraxia with better recovery potential).

- **Longitudinal temporal bone fractures** (70-80% of temporal bone fractures): CN VII injury in 10-25%; associated with conductive hearing loss from ossicular chain disruption
- **Transverse temporal bone fractures** (10-20% of temporal bone fractures): CN VII injury in 30-50%; associated with sensorineural hearing loss from labyrinthine destruction

| Feature | Longitudinal Fracture (70-80%) | Transverse Fracture (10-20%) |
|---|---|---|
| Direction relative to petrous bone | Parallel to long axis | Perpendicular to long axis |
| CN VII injury rate | 10-25% | 30-50% |
| Hearing loss type | **Conductive** (ossicular chain disruption) | **Sensorineural** (labyrinthine damage) |
| CSF otorrhea | More common | Less common |
| Vertigo severity | Less severe | More severe |
| CN VIII injury | Less common | More common |

**CN VIII (Vestibulocochlear Nerve)**

Injury to the vestibulocochlear nerve produces sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and vestibular dysfunction (vertigo, nystagmus, disequilibrium). Transverse temporal bone fractures carry a higher risk of CN VIII injury than longitudinal fractures because the fracture line crosses the labyrinth directly. Labyrinthine concussion (vestibular injury without fracture) and perilymphatic fistula are additional mechanisms.

**CN IV (Trochlear Nerve) -- Most Common Oculomotor Injury**

The trochlear nerve is uniquely vulnerable to traumatic injury for two anatomical reasons: (1) it has the longest intracranial course of any cranial nerve, and (2) it is the only cranial nerve that exits dorsally from the brainstem. Both features expose it to stretching forces during trauma. CN IV palsy may also occur as a contrecoup injury (contralateral to the side of impact).

- **Clinical findings**: Superior oblique palsy producing hypertropia (affected eye elevated), excyclotorsion
- **Parks-Bielschowsky three-step test**: (1) Identify which eye is hypertropic, (2) determine if hypertropia worsens in contralateral gaze, (3) determine if it worsens with ipsilateral head tilt -- if all three steps localize to the same muscle, the diagnosis is CN IV palsy
- **Compensatory behavior**: Head tilt AWAY from the affected side with chin tuck to minimize diplopia
- **Functional impact**: Difficulty reading and descending stairs (activities requiring downgaze)
- **Treatment**: Prism glasses for small deviations; surgical correction (inferior oblique weakening procedure) for persistent large deviations

**CN III (Oculomotor Nerve)**

CN III palsy in TBI most ominously occurs in the context of transtentorial (uncal) herniation, where the medial temporal lobe herniates over the tentorial edge and compresses the nerve against the posterior communicating artery. Orbital fractures and direct midbrain injury are additional mechanisms.

- **Clinical findings**: Ptosis, "down and out" eye position (unopposed action of lateral rectus and superior oblique), and mydriasis (dilated fixed pupil from loss of parasympathetic innervation to the pupillary sphincter)
- **Pupil involvement**: Suggests compression (surgical CN III palsy) because parasympathetic fibers travel on the periphery of the nerve and are affected first by external compression

**CN VI (Abducens Nerve)**

The abducens nerve has the longest intracranial subarachnoid course, making it susceptible to traction injury from elevated intracranial pressure. CN VI palsy may therefore be a "false localizing sign" -- indicating generalized elevated ICP rather than a focal brainstem lesion.

- **Clinical findings**: Lateral rectus palsy producing esotropia (inward deviation) and inability to abduct the affected eye
- **May be bilateral**: With diffusely elevated ICP
- **Also injured by**: Petrous bone (Dorello canal) fractures (Gradenigo syndrome when associated with otitis media)

### 7.3.3 Cranial Nerves II, V, IX-XII

While less commonly tested, injuries to the remaining cranial nerves should be recognized:

- **CN II (Optic)**: Traumatic optic neuropathy from fractures involving the optic canal; monocular vision loss; afferent pupillary defect (Marcus Gunn pupil) on swinging flashlight test
- **CN V (Trigeminal)**: Middle fossa or orbital fractures; facial sensory loss; corneal reflex impairment (risk of exposure keratopathy)
- **CN IX, X (Glossopharyngeal, Vagus)**: Skull base or posterior fossa injury; dysphagia, dysphonia, impaired gag reflex; aspiration risk
- **CN XI (Spinal Accessory)**: Rarely injured in isolated TBI; more common with cervical spine injuries; trapezius and sternocleidomastoid weakness
- **CN XII (Hypoglossal)**: Occipital condyle fractures; tongue deviation toward the affected side; dysarthria

## 7.4 Heterotopic Ossification [BOARD]

### 7.4.1 Definition, Epidemiology, and Pathophysiology

Heterotopic ossification (HO) is the pathological formation of mature lamellar bone within soft tissues surrounding peripheral joints -- tissues that do not normally undergo ossification. It is a significant rehabilitation complication following TBI and represents an important intersection of neurological injury and musculoskeletal pathology.

- **Clinically significant HO**: 10-20% of moderate-to-severe TBI patients
- **Radiographic HO (subclinical)**: Up to 76% on screening imaging
- **Rehabilitation impact**: Can produce joint ankylosis, pain, and loss of functional range of motion, significantly impeding rehabilitation progress

The pathophysiology of neurogenic HO remains incompletely understood but appears to involve aberrant signaling between the injured central nervous system and peripheral mesenchymal progenitor cells. Proposed mechanisms include:

1. **Neuroinflammatory mediators**: Substance P, calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), and other neuropeptides released after CNS injury may stimulate osteoblastic differentiation in periarticular tissues
2. **Sympathetic dysregulation**: Altered autonomic output to peripheral tissues may create a permissive environment for ectopic bone formation
3. **Bone morphogenetic proteins (BMPs)**: Upregulation of BMP signaling in response to tissue injury and inflammation
4. **Prostaglandin-mediated pathways**: Explain the mechanism by which indomethacin (a prostaglandin synthesis inhibitor) provides prophylaxis

### 7.4.2 Common Sites, Timing, and Risk Factors

**Sites of HO (in order of frequency):**
1. **Hips** -- most common location (approximately two-thirds of cases); the most commonly tested answer
2. **Elbows** -- second most common
3. **Shoulders**
4. **Knees**

**Timing**: Onset typically 1-4 months post-injury, with peak incidence at approximately 2 months. Presentation ranges from 3 weeks to 6 months after the inciting event.

**Risk factors:**
- Spasticity (strongest clinical predictor)
- Prolonged immobilization
- Coma duration > 2 weeks
- Long bone fractures (particularly when ipsilateral to the paretic limb)
- Blast injury (military TBI populations)
- Younger age
- Note: HO risk correlates more strongly with spasticity and immobilization than with initial GCS severity

### 7.4.3 Clinical Presentation

The earliest clinical signs of HO include:
- Decreased range of motion (often the first sign)
- Periarticular warmth, swelling, and erythema
- Pain with passive range of motion
- Palpable firm mass in periarticular soft tissues

These findings can mimic deep vein thrombosis, septic arthritis, cellulitis, or fracture, making the differential diagnosis important.

### 7.4.4 Diagnostic Modalities

| Diagnostic Test | Sensitivity | Timing of Positivity | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Triple-phase bone scan | **Most sensitive** (gold standard for early detection) | Positive 2-4 weeks **BEFORE** plain radiographs | Phase 1 (flow), Phase 2 (blood pool), Phase 3 (delayed bone) -- all three phases positive in active HO |
| Serum alkaline phosphatase (SAP) | Elevated but **nonspecific** | Early (may precede radiographic findings) | Useful for monitoring maturation; normalizes as bone matures; NOT diagnostic alone (many causes of elevation) |
| Plain radiograph | Low sensitivity early | Positive at 4-6 weeks post-onset | Shows mature calcification; negative in early HO; useful for assessing extent once established |
| CT scan | Moderate (superior to X-ray) | 2-4 weeks post-onset | Better anatomic detail; most useful for pre-surgical planning |
| Ultrasound | Emerging modality | May detect before X-ray | Can identify "zone phenomenon" (organized layers of ossification); operator-dependent |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The triple-phase bone scan is the most sensitive test for early detection of heterotopic ossification and becomes positive 2-4 weeks before plain radiographs can demonstrate the abnormality. In active HO, all three phases are positive, reflecting increased blood flow (phase 1), blood pool activity (phase 2), and active bone formation (phase 3). As HO matures, phases 1 and 2 normalize while phase 3 remains positive. This "cooling" pattern on sequential bone scans is a critical indicator of bone maturation and guides the timing of surgical excision.

### 7.4.5 Management

**Prevention:**
- Gentle range of motion exercises performed daily (avoiding aggressive or painful stretching, which may paradoxically worsen HO)
- Proper positioning and mobilization
- Early weight-bearing when feasible

**Pharmacologic Prophylaxis:**
- **Indomethacin** (NSAID): Inhibits prostaglandin-mediated osteoblast activity; the most commonly used prophylactic agent; evidence strongest in the arthroplasty literature; efficacy in TBI-specific HO is less well established
- **Etidronate** (bisphosphonate): Inhibits ectite calcium phosphate crystal formation; less commonly used due to mineralization defects with prolonged use

**Radiation Prophylaxis:**
- Single-dose radiation (700-800 cGy) effective in the hip arthroplasty population; limited evidence specifically for TBI-associated HO

**Surgical Excision -- Garland Timing:**

> **Clinical Pearl:** Garland established that surgical excision of HO should be delayed until the ectopic bone has fully matured, typically 12-18 months post-onset. The criteria for maturity include: (1) serum alkaline phosphatase has normalized, AND (2) triple-phase bone scan demonstrates "cooling" (normalization of phases 1 and 2 with persistent phase 3 uptake only). Early excision carries a high recurrence rate because immature, metabolically active bone will reform aggressively. Post-operative indomethacin and/or single-dose radiation may be administered to reduce recurrence risk.

## 7.5 Venous Thromboembolism [BOARD]

### 7.5.1 Epidemiology and Risk

Venous thromboembolism (VTE) is a leading cause of preventable death after traumatic brain injury, and pulmonary embolism remains one of the most feared complications in rehabilitation medicine.

- **DVT incidence WITHOUT prophylaxis**: Approximately 54% (Kaufman et al.) -- this high-yield number reflects the extraordinary thrombotic risk in immobilized TBI patients
- **DVT incidence WITH prophylaxis**: 18-28% -- still significant despite treatment, underscoring the need for vigilance
- **Pulmonary embolism**: Leading cause of preventable death in rehabilitation patients

**TBI-Specific Risk Factors:**
- Prolonged immobility (the dominant risk factor)
- Lower extremity fractures (polytrauma)
- Paralysis or paresis
- Central venous catheter placement
- Advanced age
- Obesity
- Prior VTE history
- The critical TBI-specific challenge: intracranial hemorrhage limits the timing and safety of pharmacologic anticoagulation

### 7.5.2 Prophylaxis Strategy

| Method | Timing | Implementation | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mechanical prophylaxis (SCDs) | **Immediately** upon admission | Sequential compression devices and/or graduated compression stockings | First-line; no hemorrhage risk; START immediately |
| LMWH (enoxaparin) | 24-72 hours post-injury if hemorrhage stable | 30-40 mg SC BID (or 40 mg daily) | Timing depends on stability of intracranial hemorrhage on repeat CT imaging |
| UFH (unfractionated heparin) | 24-72 hours post-injury | 5000 units SC Q8-12H | May have slightly lower efficacy than LMWH; simpler to reverse |
| IVC filter | When anticoagulation is contraindicated | Retrievable filter placement | For patients with active or expanding hemorrhage who cannot receive anticoagulation |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The central dilemma in TBI VTE prophylaxis is balancing the extraordinarily high thrombotic risk (54% DVT without prophylaxis) against the hemorrhagic risk posed by pharmacologic anticoagulation in the setting of intracranial bleeding. Current evidence (2024 guidelines) indicates that chemical DVT prophylaxis initiated within 48-72 hours following TBI with intracranial hemorrhage does NOT significantly increase the risk of intracranial bleed progression, supporting earlier initiation than was historically practiced.

### 7.5.3 IVC Filter Indications and Evolving Recommendations

The role of prophylactic inferior vena cava (IVC) filters has evolved substantially. Historically, IVC filters were placed liberally in TBI patients who could not receive anticoagulation. However, current evidence (2024 guidelines from the American College of Surgeons and the Society of Interventional Radiology) recommends against routine prophylactic IVC filter placement. Data suggest that prophylactic IVC filtration is paradoxically associated with increased DVT and PE rates without mortality benefit, likely due to filter-related thrombosis and subsequent embolization.

**Current IVC Filter Indications:**
- Documented acute proximal DVT or PE when anticoagulation is absolutely contraindicated
- Recurrent PE despite adequate anticoagulation
- Filters should be retrievable and removed as soon as anticoagulation can be safely initiated

### 7.5.4 Diagnostic Testing

- **Duplex ultrasonography**: First-line for DVT screening; sensitivity >95% for proximal DVT; less sensitive for calf DVT
- **CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)**: Gold standard for PE diagnosis
- **D-dimer**: High sensitivity but low specificity; useful for exclusion in low-probability patients; frequently elevated in trauma patients (limiting utility)
- **Venography**: Historical gold standard for DVT; rarely used now due to invasiveness

## 7.6 Posttraumatic Headache [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.6.1 Epidemiology and ICHD-3 Classification

Posttraumatic headache (PTH) is the most common symptom following TBI, occurring acutely in up to 90% of patients and persisting beyond 3 months in 25-50%. The International Classification of Headache Disorders, 3rd Edition (ICHD-3) provides the formal classification framework, categorizing headaches attributed to trauma by both timing and injury severity.

| ICHD-3 Category | Code | Timing Criteria | Injury Criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acute PTH attributed to moderate/severe TBI | 5.1.1 | Onset within 7 days of injury; resolves within 3 months | LOC > 30 min, GCS < 13, PTA > 24h, or imaging abnormality |
| Persistent PTH attributed to moderate/severe TBI | 5.1.2 | Onset within 7 days; persists > 3 months | Same injury criteria; headache has NOT resolved by 3 months |
| Acute PTH attributed to mild TBI | 5.2.1 | Onset within 7 days; resolves within 3 months | Mild TBI (GCS 13-15) with or without CT findings |
| Persistent PTH attributed to mild TBI | 5.2.2 | Onset within 7 days; persists > 3 months | Mild TBI criteria; most common clinical challenge |

A notable limitation of the ICHD-3 criteria is that they do not provide phenotypic descriptors for PTH -- they classify by etiology and timeline but not by headache character. Clinical management therefore requires phenotypic characterization using primary headache disorder criteria.

### 7.6.2 Phenotypic Classification and Treatment

PTH is heterogeneous, and the headache phenotype determines the treatment approach. Research suggests that the two most common phenotypes are migraine-like and tension-type, though many patients have overlapping or mixed features.

**Migraine-like PTH (approximately 50% of cases):**
- Unilateral, pulsating quality; moderate to severe intensity
- Associated with nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia
- Acute treatment: Triptans (sumatriptan, rizatriptan), NSAIDs, antiemetics
- Preventive treatment: Topiramate, amitriptyline (low dose), propranolol, CGRP monoclonal antibodies (emerging data in PTH)

**Tension-type PTH (approximately 30%):**
- Bilateral, pressing or tightening quality; mild to moderate intensity
- Lacks the migrainous features of nausea and photosensitivity
- Acute treatment: Simple analgesics (acetaminophen, NSAIDs)
- Preventive treatment: Amitriptyline, physical therapy, stress management, biofeedback

**Cervicogenic headache:**
- Referred pain from cervical spine or occipital structures
- Common after whiplash-associated TBI
- Treatment: Cervical physical therapy, occipital nerve blocks, trigger point injections, manual therapy

**Occipital neuralgia:**
- Sharp, stabbing, or electric pain in greater or lesser occipital nerve distribution
- May follow direct occipital trauma or cervical whiplash
- Diagnostic and therapeutic: Occipital nerve block (both confirms the diagnosis and provides treatment)

**Medication Overuse Headache (MOH):**
- Develops with > 15 days/month analgesic use for > 3 months (or > 10 days for triptans/opioids)
- Paradoxical worsening of headache despite treatment
- Management: Withdrawal of offending medication, bridge therapy (nerve blocks, brief corticosteroid taper), and initiation of preventive therapy

> **Clinical Pearl:** Medication overuse headache is a critical iatrogenic complication in persistent PTH management. Opioids have the lowest threshold for MOH development and should be avoided in PTH management entirely. Triptans and combination analgesics are intermediate risk, while simple analgesics (acetaminophen, NSAIDs) carry the highest threshold but still produce MOH with excessive use. When a patient with initially improving PTH develops progressively worsening daily headache despite escalating analgesic use, MOH must be suspected.

## 7.7 Sleep Disorders After TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.7.1 Overview and Prevalence

Sleep disturbances represent some of the most prevalent yet underrecognized complications of TBI. Meta-analytic data indicate that approximately 50% of TBI survivors experience clinically significant sleep disturbance, with 25-29% meeting criteria for a diagnosable sleep disorder. These rates substantially exceed those observed in the general population and persist chronically in many patients.

Sleep disruption after TBI is not merely an inconvenience. Impaired sleep undermines every domain of rehabilitation: cognitive recovery (sleep is critical for memory consolidation and neuroplasticity), mood regulation (sleep deprivation exacerbates depression and anxiety), pain perception (poor sleep lowers pain thresholds), immune function (impaired healing), and rehabilitation participation (daytime fatigue limits therapy engagement).

### 7.7.2 Specific Sleep Disorders

| Disorder | Prevalence | Pathophysiology | Key Features | First-line Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Insomnia | 30-70% | Disruption of sleep-promoting nuclei (ventrolateral preoptic area, basal forebrain); hyperarousal; pain and mood comorbidity | Most common sleep complaint; difficulty initiating and/or maintaining sleep; worsened by ICU environment and medications | CBT-I (first-line); melatonin 3-5 mg; trazodone 25-100 mg |
| Hypersomnia / EDS | 25-55% | Damage to hypothalamic wake-promoting neurons (orexin/hypocretin system); diffuse axonal injury affecting ARAS | Excessive sleep need (>10 hrs/day); irresistible daytime sleepiness; low CSF hypocretin levels documented | Modafinil 100-200 mg AM; methylphenidate; rule out OSA |
| Circadian rhythm disruption | ~36% | Damage to suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) or retinohypothalamic tract; documented reduced melatonin production | Delayed/advanced sleep phase; irregular sleep-wake pattern; reduced evening melatonin on assay | Melatonin 0.5-5 mg at fixed evening time; morning bright light therapy |
| Obstructive sleep apnea | 23-36% | Not directly caused by TBI but UNMASKED or WORSENED by weight gain, reduced muscle tone, opioid use, altered pharyngeal reflexes | Snoring, witnessed apneas, fragmented sleep; significantly impairs cognitive recovery | Polysomnography for diagnosis; CPAP therapy |
| Posttraumatic narcolepsy | 3-5% | Destruction of hypothalamic orexin/hypocretin neurons; low CSF hypocretin levels | Irresistible sleep attacks; cataplexy (rare in post-traumatic form); sleep paralysis; onset weeks to months post-injury | Modafinil/armodafinil; sodium oxybate for cataplexy |

### 7.7.3 Management Principles

**Pharmacologic Choices for Sleep in TBI:**
- **Trazodone** (FIRST-LINE): 5-HT2A antagonist; 50-100 mg QHS; non-habit forming; does not impair cognition; may have mild antidepressant benefit
- **Melatonin**: 0.5-5 mg at fixed evening time; safe; helps circadian entrainment; evidence for overall sleep quality improvement is mixed
- **Ramelteon**: Melatonin receptor agonist (MT1/MT2); 8 mg QHS; fewer drug interactions than melatonin supplements
- **AVOID benzodiazepines**: Impair neuroplasticity, worsen cognition, cause paradoxical agitation, dependence
- **AVOID diphenhydramine**: Significant anticholinergic effects; not merely sedating -- actively harmful to TBI recovery
- **AVOID zolpidem for sleep**: Falls risk, paradoxical excitation; note the separate paradoxical arousal phenomenon in some DOC patients

> **Clinical Pearl:** TBI patients often demonstrate reduced evening melatonin production due to damage to the suprachiasmatic nucleus or retinohypothalamic tract. Exogenous melatonin supplementation at a consistent evening time is safe and physiologically rational for circadian entrainment, though clinical trial evidence for substantial sleep quality improvement remains mixed. The 2024 DoD/VA Clinical Recommendation emphasizes cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) as the first-line intervention, with pharmacotherapy reserved for cases where behavioral approaches are insufficient or impractical.

## 7.8 Cardiovascular Complications: Hypertension Post-TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.8.1 Mechanisms and Prevalence

Hypertension after TBI occurs through multiple mechanisms and may be transient (acute phase) or persistent (chronic phase).

**Acute Phase Hypertension:**
- Sympathetic hyperactivation (Cushing response to elevated ICP, paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity)
- Pain and agitation
- Medication effects (vasopressors for CPP maintenance)
- Renal sympathetic activation

**Chronic Hypertension:**
- Persistent autonomic dysregulation following hypothalamic or brainstem injury
- Deconditioning and weight gain during rehabilitation
- Medication effects (corticosteroids, NSAIDs)
- Pre-existing hypertension that was undiagnosed or uncontrolled

### 7.8.2 Management Considerations

Blood pressure management in TBI requires balancing the need for adequate cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP - ICP; target 60-70 mmHg per BTF guidelines) against the risks of sustained hypertension. In the acute phase, blood pressure management is guided by ICP monitoring and CPP targets. In the rehabilitation and chronic phases, standard antihypertensive guidelines apply, with preference for agents that do not impair cognitive recovery (avoiding centrally-acting alpha agonists such as clonidine in high doses when possible; considering the dual benefit of beta-blockers for hypertension and agitation management).

## 7.9 Neurogenic Bladder Dysfunction [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.9.1 Pathophysiology

Urinary dysfunction after TBI differs fundamentally from that seen in spinal cord injury. In TBI, the primary deficit involves loss of cortical control over micturition rather than disruption of the spinal reflex arc. The pontine micturition center and sacral reflex arc are typically intact; the deficiency lies in the inability of the injured frontal lobes to appropriately inhibit the voiding reflex and in the cognitive impairment that prevents the patient from recognizing and responding to bladder fullness cues.

**Most Common Pattern: Uninhibited Neurogenic Bladder**

Failure of cortical inhibition of the pontine micturition center produces an uninhibited pattern characterized by urgency, frequency, and urge incontinence with small-volume voids. The detrusor contracts reflexively at relatively low filling volumes because the normal cortical suppression signal is absent or impaired.

### 7.9.2 Management

1. **Timed voiding program**: Scheduled voiding every 2-4 hours regardless of urge sensation; prompted voiding for cognitively impaired patients; gradually increasing intervals as awareness improves
2. **Fluid management**: Structured intake schedule; limit evening fluids; avoid bladder irritants (caffeine, alcohol)
3. **Pharmacologic management -- Anticholinergic Considerations**:
   - **Oxybutynin**: AVOID in TBI -- crosses the blood-brain barrier readily and causes significant cognitive impairment (worsens memory, attention, and processing speed); listed on the Beers Criteria as high-risk in elderly patients
   - **Trospium**: PREFERRED anticholinergic in TBI because it does NOT cross the BBB
   - **Mirabegron**: Beta-3 adrenergic agonist with ZERO anticholinergic activity; excellent alternative
   - **Solifenacin, darifenacin**: Less CNS penetration than oxybutynin but not completely BBB-impermeable

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between oxybutynin (BBB-permeable, cognitive impairment) and trospium (BBB-impermeable, preferred in TBI) is a testable pharmacologic concept. When a TBI patient requires anticholinergic therapy for overactive bladder, trospium chloride is the preferred agent because it does not cross the blood-brain barrier and therefore does not compound the existing cognitive deficits of brain injury. Mirabegron (a beta-3 agonist) provides a non-anticholinergic alternative.

4. **Intermittent catheterization**: If post-void residual volume exceeds 200 mL
5. **Avoid indwelling catheters**: Significantly elevated UTI risk; remove as soon as feasible

## 7.10 Neurogenic Bowel Dysfunction [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.10.1 Pathophysiology

Constipation is the predominant bowel complication after TBI, resulting from a combination of immobility, opioid use, anticholinergic medication effects, inadequate fluid and fiber intake, and reduced awareness of bowel cues. Fecal incontinence, when present, typically results from cognitive unawareness and impaired voluntary sphincter control rather than lower motor neuron dysfunction.

### 7.10.2 Bowel Management Program

A structured neurogenic bowel program is essential for TBI rehabilitation:

1. **Timing**: Establish consistent bowel care at the same time daily, ideally 30 minutes after a meal to capitalize on the gastrocolic reflex
2. **Oral regimen**: Senna + docusate (Senokot-S) as first-line stimulant/softener combination; polyethylene glycol (MiraLAX) for osmotic effect
3. **Rectal interventions**: Digital stimulation or suppository (bisacodyl or glycerin) as needed to initiate reflex evacuation
4. **Dietary modifications**: Adequate fiber intake (25-30 g/day), adequate hydration (minimum 1.5-2 L/day unless fluid-restricted)
5. **Mobility**: Upright positioning and ambulation promote colonic motility
6. **Opioid minimization**: Opioids are the most common cause of refractory constipation in TBI rehabilitation; opioid-sparing strategies should be prioritized

> **Clinical Pearl:** Constipation and fecal impaction are frequently overlooked causes of agitation in TBI patients. Before attributing behavioral escalation to the brain injury itself, always evaluate for bowel distention, rectal impaction, and adequacy of the bowel program. Similarly, monitor for Clostridium difficile infection in patients who have received prolonged antibiotic courses during the acute care phase.

## 7.11 Pressure Injuries [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.11.1 Risk Factors in TBI

TBI patients are at particularly elevated risk for pressure injuries due to the convergence of multiple predisposing factors:

- Impaired mobility and prolonged bedrest (dominant risk factor)
- Reduced sensation and awareness (unable to perceive discomfort from sustained pressure)
- Cognitive impairment (inability to communicate discomfort, follow repositioning instructions, or self-reposition)
- Agitation, disinhibition, and impulsivity (may interfere with regular repositioning or use of support surfaces)
- Nutritional deficiency (catabolic state, inadequate protein intake)
- Moisture exposure (urinary and fecal incontinence, diaphoresis)
- Spasticity (sustained abnormal posturing increases pressure over bony prominences)
- Vasopressor use in the ICU phase (reduces capillary perfusion pressure)

Studies report a pressure injury prevalence of approximately 26.1% in TBI rehabilitation patients and even higher rates (28.8%) during the ICU phase.

### 7.11.2 Prevention

Prevention is the cornerstone of pressure injury management and is a shared responsibility of the entire interdisciplinary rehabilitation team.

1. **Risk assessment**: Braden Scale (6 subscales: sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, friction/shear; score ranges from 6-23; lower scores indicate higher risk; scores below 18 indicate "at risk")
2. **Repositioning**: Every 2 hours while in bed; weight shifts every 15-30 minutes while seated; documented turning schedules
3. **Support surfaces**: Pressure-redistributing mattresses (foam, alternating pressure, low air-loss); appropriate wheelchair cushion selection
4. **Skin inspection**: At least daily, with particular attention to sacrum, heels, ischial tuberosities, and greater trochanters
5. **Moisture management**: Incontinence programs, moisture barrier creams, prompt cleaning
6. **Nutritional optimization**: Adequate protein (1.25-1.5 g/kg/day minimum), caloric intake, and micronutrient supplementation (zinc, vitamin C)

### 7.11.3 Staging and Treatment

| Stage | Description | Typical Healing Time | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stage 1 | Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin | Days to weeks | Pressure offloading; moisture barrier; monitoring |
| Stage 2 | Partial-thickness skin loss (shallow ulcer, blister) | Weeks | Moist wound environment; hydrocolloid or foam dressings |
| Stage 3 | Full-thickness skin loss (subcutaneous fat visible; bone/tendon NOT exposed) | Weeks to months | Wound care protocols; debridement if needed; nutritional support |
| Stage 4 | Full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle | 6-12 months conservative; may require surgical closure | Surgical consultation; flap coverage may be required; aggressive nutritional support |
| Unstageable | Full-thickness loss with base obscured by slough or eschar | Variable | Debridement required for accurate staging |
| Deep tissue injury | Intact or non-intact skin with localized persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discoloration | Variable; may evolve | Close monitoring; may rapidly deteriorate to Stage 3 or 4 |

## 7.12 Neuroendocrine Dysfunction and Screening [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.12.1 Epidemiology and Anatomy

Posttraumatic hypopituitarism occurs in 25-50% of moderate-to-severe TBI patients and represents one of the most underdiagnosed complications of brain injury. The pituitary gland's vulnerability stems from its precarious vascular anatomy: the hypothalamic-pituitary portal venous system, which supplies the anterior pituitary, is susceptible to shearing forces, edema, and compression against the diaphragma sellae during acceleration-deceleration injury. The pituitary stalk, which connects the hypothalamus to the pituitary gland, is also vulnerable to torsional forces.

### 7.12.2 Axis-Specific Deficiencies

| Pituitary Axis | Prevalence After TBI | Clinical Manifestations | Screening Test | Confirmatory Test |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Growth Hormone (GH) | 15-20% (MOST COMMON) | Fatigue, decreased lean mass, increased body fat, impaired cognition, reduced QoL, poor rehabilitation progress | IGF-1 (screening; NOTE: normal IGF-1 does NOT exclude GHD due to low sensitivity) | GH stimulation test: insulin tolerance test (gold standard) or glucagon stimulation test |
| Gonadotropins (LH/FSH) | 10-15% (second most common) | Males: low testosterone, fatigue, decreased libido, muscle wasting. Females: amenorrhea, infertility, osteoporosis | AM total testosterone (males); estradiol + LH/FSH (females) | Repeat testing; gonadotropin levels for central vs. peripheral distinction |
| ACTH (Cortisol) | 8-12% | Fatigue, hypotension, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia; adrenal crisis (life-threatening) | AM cortisol (< 3 mcg/dL diagnostic; > 18 mcg/dL excludes deficiency) | Cosyntropin (ACTH) stimulation test |
| TSH (Thyroid) | 5-10% | Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, cognitive slowing, constipation | TSH + free T4 | Note: central hypothyroidism shows LOW free T4 with LOW/normal TSH (not elevated TSH as in primary) |
| ADH (Diabetes Insipidus) | 3-5% permanent; up to 26% transient | Polyuria (>3 L/day), polydipsia, hypernatremia, low urine specific gravity (<1.005) | Serum sodium, urine osmolality, urine specific gravity | Water deprivation test |

### 7.12.3 The Ghigo Screening Algorithm

The Ghigo algorithm, based on the work of Ghigo, Aimaretti, and colleagues in Turin, provides a systematic framework for neuroendocrine screening after TBI. The algorithm is timed to account for the dynamic nature of pituitary dysfunction after injury -- some deficiencies are transient and resolve spontaneously, while others emerge late.

**Acute Phase (Days 1-14):**
- Screen ONLY for life-threatening deficiencies: cortisol (adrenal crisis risk) and thyroid (TSH/free T4)
- Treat immediately if AM cortisol < 15 mcg/dL in a physiologically stressed patient (stress dose hydrocortisone)
- DI screening with serial sodium, urine output monitoring

**3 Months Post-Injury:**
- First comprehensive neuroendocrine screening
- Full panel: AM cortisol, TSH/free T4, testosterone (males)/estradiol + LH/FSH (females), IGF-1 (GH axis screen), prolactin, electrolytes
- Identifies early-onset persistent deficiencies

**12 Months Post-Injury:**
- Repeat comprehensive screening
- Critical because: (1) some early deficiencies RESOLVE (transient), and (2) others EMERGE late (particularly GH deficiency, which may not manifest until 12+ months post-injury)
- GH stimulation testing if IGF-1 is low or clinical suspicion is high

**Annual Screening for 3-5 Years:**
- Recommended in severe TBI
- Late-onset deficiencies continue to emerge

> **Clinical Pearl:** Growth hormone deficiency is the most common pituitary axis affected after TBI but may not manifest for 12 or more months after injury. Importantly, a normal IGF-1 level does NOT exclude GH deficiency because IGF-1 has low sensitivity as a screening test. If clinical suspicion is high (fatigue, body composition changes, poor rehabilitation progress, impaired cognition), formal GH stimulation testing should be performed regardless of IGF-1 level.

### 7.12.4 The Triphasic Response

After severe TBI with pituitary stalk injury, a characteristic triphasic pattern of water balance dysregulation may occur:

1. **Phase 1 (Days 1-5)**: Diabetes insipidus -- disrupted ADH transport from hypothalamus to posterior pituitary produces polyuria, hypernatremia, and dilute urine
2. **Phase 2 (Days 5-10)**: SIADH -- unregulated ADH release from dying posterior pituitary neurons produces hyponatremia, concentrated urine, and fluid retention
3. **Phase 3 (Day 10+)**: Permanent DI -- occurs if > 80% of hypothalamic ADH-producing magnocellular neurons are destroyed; requires lifelong desmopressin replacement

> **Clinical Pearl:** Not all patients complete all three phases of the triphasic response. Some patients present with isolated transient DI that resolves, others with isolated SIADH, and a minority progress through the full triphasic sequence to permanent DI. This pattern is a classic board question and requires understanding of the anatomical distinction between ADH synthesis (hypothalamic supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei) and ADH storage/release (posterior pituitary).
