1. Definition of Traumatic Brain Injury [BOARD]

1.1 Overview and Historical Context

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) represents one of the most consequential neurological disorders encountered in clinical medicine. Despite decades of research, achieving consensus on a single definition has proven challenging, as various organizations have proposed criteria that emphasize different aspects of the injury. For the Brain Injury Medicine board examination, familiarity with the three principal definitions — those of the American Congress of Rehabilitation Medicine (ACRM), the World Health Organization (WHO), and the Department of Defense/Veterans Affairs (DoD/VA) — is essential. Each definition reflects different clinical priorities, whether emphasizing diagnostic sensitivity, epidemiological standardization, or military-specific considerations.

1.2 ACRM Definition of Mild TBI (1993 and 2023 Revision)

The ACRM published the original operational definition of mild TBI in 1993, which became the most widely cited definition in rehabilitation medicine and remains foundational for board examinations. The 1993 definition characterized mild TBI as a traumatically induced physiological disruption of brain function, manifested by at least one of the following:

  1. Any period of loss of consciousness (LOC)
  2. Any loss of memory for events immediately before or after the injury (posttraumatic amnesia, PTA)
  3. Any alteration in mental status at the time of injury (feeling dazed, disoriented, or confused)
  4. Focal neurological deficit(s) that may or may not be transient

To qualify as mild, the severity must not exceed LOC of 30 minutes, initial Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score of 13–15 (after 30 minutes), or PTA duration of 24 hours.

Clinical Pearl: The 1993 ACRM definition requires only ONE of the four clinical criteria to be present. It does not require loss of consciousness — an alteration in mental status alone (e.g., feeling dazed) is sufficient for diagnosis. This remains one of the most commonly tested concepts on the ABPM&R Brain Injury Medicine examination.

In 2023, the ACRM published a major revision of its mild TBI diagnostic criteria, developed through systematic evidence review, expert survey, and Delphi consensus process (Silverberg et al., 2023). The updated 2023 ACRM criteria introduced several important changes:

Feature 1993 ACRM Criteria 2023 ACRM Criteria
Mechanism requirement Implied Explicit: plausible traumatic mechanism required
Clinical signs LOC, PTA, AMS, focal deficits Expanded: clinical signs attributed to mechanism sufficient
Acute symptoms Not separately categorized Two or more symptoms + abnormal clinical/lab findings sufficient
Neuroimaging Not addressed Trauma-related intracranial abnormality sufficient for diagnosis
Diagnostic certainty Binary (present/absent) Tiered: confirmed, suspected, possible
Clinical examination Not specified Cognitive impairment, oculomotor abnormality as contributing findings

The 2023 revision introduced the concept of diagnostic certainty levels, allowing for a “suspected” mTBI designation when information is incomplete. This permits clinicians to initiate treatment even without definitive diagnosis, reducing the risk of false-negative diagnoses.

Clinical Pearl: The 2023 ACRM criteria add clinical examination findings (cognitive impairment, oculomotor abnormalities) and blood biomarkers as supporting diagnostic elements. Neuroimaging showing trauma-related intracranial abnormalities is now independently sufficient for diagnosis. The severity thresholds (LOC < 30 min, GCS 13–15, PTA < 24 h) remain unchanged.

1.3 WHO Collaborating Centre Task Force Definition

The WHO Collaborating Centre Task Force on Mild Traumatic Brain Injury (Carroll et al., 2004) proposed a definition closely aligned with the ACRM criteria but with additional operational specifications intended for epidemiological research. The WHO definition characterizes mTBI as an acute brain injury resulting from mechanical energy to the head from external physical forces, operationally defined as:

  • One or more of: confusion or disorientation, LOC of 30 minutes or less, PTA of less than 24 hours, and/or other transient neurological abnormalities (focal signs, seizure, intracranial lesion not requiring surgery)
  • GCS score of 13–15 after 30 minutes post-injury or later upon presentation for healthcare

The WHO task force notably excluded injuries caused by drugs, alcohol, medications, or other non-traumatic causes, as well as injuries due to penetrating trauma. This exclusionary approach was designed to improve the specificity of epidemiological estimates.

1.4 DoD/VA Common Definition

The Department of Defense and Department of Veterans Affairs jointly published a common definition in 2007, subsequently updated in the VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management and Rehabilitation of Post-Acute Mild Traumatic Brain Injury (Version 3.0, 2021). The DoD/VA defines TBI as a traumatically induced structural injury and/or physiological disruption of brain function resulting from an external force, indicated by new onset or worsening of at least one of the following:

  1. Any period of loss of consciousness
  2. Any loss of memory for events immediately before or after the injury
  3. Any alteration in mental state at the time of injury (dazed, confused, disoriented)
  4. Neurological deficit(s) that may or may not be transient
  5. Intracranial lesion

The DoD/VA definition is notable for its inclusion of intracranial lesion as an independent diagnostic criterion and its application of the same severity classification matrix used across the military health system.

Severity LOC PTA GCS Imaging
Mild 0–30 minutes 0–24 hours 13–15 Normal
Moderate 30 min–24 hours 1–7 days 9–12 Normal or abnormal
Severe > 24 hours > 7 days 3–8 Normal or abnormal

Clinical Pearl: The DoD/VA definition uniquely includes intracranial lesion as a fifth diagnostic criterion, meaning that imaging-positive TBI can be diagnosed even without LOC, PTA, or AMS. For board purposes, note that the DoD/VA does not routinely use GCS for classification in clinical practice and instead relies more heavily on LOC and PTA duration.

1.5 Comparison of Definitions and Board Relevance

For the Brain Injury Medicine examination, the critical distinction among these definitions lies not in their overlapping criteria but in their unique features. The following table summarizes key differentiators:

Definition Unique Feature Primary Application
ACRM 1993 Foundational; AMS alone is sufficient Clinical diagnosis, rehabilitation
ACRM 2023 Tiered certainty; biomarkers and imaging integrated Updated clinical framework
WHO 2004 Exclusion criteria (drugs, penetrating injury) Epidemiological research
DoD/VA 2007/2021 Intracranial lesion as independent criterion Military populations

1.6 The CBI-M Framework: Toward Precision Classification

In 2025, the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke (NINDS) proposed the CBI-M framework as a multidimensional approach to TBI classification that transcends traditional single-axis severity grading:

  • C (Clinical): Traditional severity measures including GCS, LOC, PTA, symptom profiles, and functional status
  • B (Biomarker): Blood-based biomarkers (GFAP, UCH-L1, NfL, tau), cerebrospinal fluid markers, and genetic risk factors (e.g., APOE4 status)
  • I (Imaging): Structural findings on CT and MRI as well as advanced imaging modalities (diffusion tensor imaging, functional MRI, PET) categorized by pathoanatomic type
  • M (Modifier): Patient-level factors influencing outcome, including age, sex, comorbidities, medications, social determinants of health, and injury mechanism

This framework represents a paradigm shift from categorical severity labels toward a precision medicine approach, in which diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment selection are informed by the integration of multiple data streams.

2. Epidemiology [BOARD]

2.1 Incidence and Prevalence in the United States

Traumatic brain injury has been described as a “silent epidemic” due to its enormous public health burden and the historical underrecognition of its long-term consequences. Current epidemiological data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) paint a picture of a pervasive health problem affecting millions annually.

Incidence: Approximately 2.8 million TBI-related emergency department (ED) visits, hospitalizations, and deaths occur in the United States each year. This figure, derived from the CDC’s TBI surveillance reports (Taylor et al., 2017), likely underestimates the true incidence because many individuals with mild TBI never seek medical attention, particularly in sports, military, and intimate partner violence contexts.

Prevalence of Disability: An estimated 5.3 million Americans live with TBI-related disability, representing approximately 1.7% of the U.S. population. Data from the TBI Model Systems National Database demonstrate that disability trajectories after TBI are dynamic rather than static, with individuals exhibiting improvement, plateau, or decline over decades of follow-up.

Mortality: More than 69,000 TBI-related deaths occur annually in the United States, equating to approximately 190 deaths per day. During 2016–2018, the age-adjusted TBI-related mortality rate was 17.3 per 100,000 population per year. This represents a significant increase from earlier estimates of approximately 52,000 deaths annually, attributable in part to improved surveillance methodology and the rising toll of firearm-related TBI deaths.

Clinical Pearl: When asked about TBI epidemiology on boards, remember the “big numbers”: 2.8 million incidents annually, 5.3 million living with disability, and 69,000+ deaths per year. TBI accounts for approximately 30% of all injury-related deaths in the United States.

2.2 Economic Burden

The economic cost of TBI is staggering and extends far beyond acute medical expenditures. The CDC estimated the total economic burden of TBI in the United States at $76.5 billion annually (in 2010 dollars), encompassing direct medical costs, work-loss costs, and the estimated value of quality-of-life years lost. Earlier estimates by Finkelstein and colleagues (2006) placed the figure at $60 billion (in 2000 dollars), and costs have continued to rise with increasing survival rates and recognition of long-term care needs.

Cost Category Estimated Annual Cost
Direct medical costs $9.2 billion
Work-loss costs $6.5 billion
Quality-of-life value lost $60.8 billion
Total $76.5 billion

The per-person lifetime cost is highest for fatal TBI (estimated at $400,000 per case) and lowest for emergency department visits that do not result in hospitalization (approximately $1,000–$3,000 per case). However, the aggregate economic impact is dominated by mild TBI due to its far greater incidence.

2.3 Demographic Patterns

Sex Distribution: Males are affected by TBI at higher rates than females across virtually all age groups, with an overall male-to-female ratio of approximately 2.5:1 for TBI-related hospitalizations and 3:1 for TBI-related deaths. Men are three times as likely to die from TBI as women, at a rate of 28.3 per 100,000 compared to 9.3 per 100,000.

Age Distribution: The epidemiology of TBI exhibits a bimodal age distribution. The highest rates of ED visits occur in children aged 0–4 years and older adults aged 75 and above. Young adults aged 15–24 demonstrate the highest overall incidence. Adults aged 75 and older account for nearly one-third of TBI-related hospitalizations and more than one-quarter of all TBI-related deaths, making advanced age a critical risk factor for both TBI occurrence and poor outcome.

Temporal Trends: TBI-related mortality has decreased substantially since the late 1980s, attributable to improvements in seatbelt and airbag use, trauma system development, and advances in acute neurosurgical and neurocritical care. However, this trend has been partially offset by rising firearm-related TBI deaths.

2.4 Causes of TBI-Related Death

A significant shift in the leading causes of TBI-related death has occurred over recent decades. Current CDC data rank the leading causes of TBI-related death as follows:

  1. Firearms (now the leading cause of TBI-related death, primarily driven by suicide)
  2. Falls
  3. Motor vehicle crashes
  4. Assaults

Clinical Pearl: The distinction between causes of TBI-related ED visits and causes of TBI-related death is a common board question. Falls remain the number one cause of TBI-related ED visits and hospitalizations across all age groups. However, firearms have surpassed all other mechanisms as the leading cause of TBI-related death, driven predominantly by firearm suicide. This shift has significant implications for prevention strategies.

3. Etiology by Age Group [BOARD]

3.1 Age-Specific Injury Mechanisms

The mechanism of TBI varies dramatically across the lifespan, reflecting differences in developmental stage, activity patterns, risk-taking behavior, and physiological vulnerability. Understanding these age-specific patterns is essential for both clinical management and prevention efforts.

Age Group Leading Cause Second Cause Key Clinical Considerations
0–4 years Falls Struck by/against objects Highest ED visit rate; must evaluate for non-accidental trauma (NAT)
5–14 years Falls, sports Struck by/against objects Sports concussion increasingly recognized; bicycle-related injuries
15–24 years Motor vehicle crashes Sports Highest overall incidence; risk-taking behavior; alcohol involvement
25–64 years Motor vehicle crashes Falls Alcohol involved in 25–50% of injuries; occupational injuries
>=75 years Falls Motor vehicle crashes Highest mortality; increased SDH risk from cortical atrophy; anticoagulant use

3.2 Falls

Falls constitute the leading cause of TBI across the population and are the dominant mechanism in the extremes of age. In children aged 0–4 years, falls from furniture, stairs, and playground equipment account for the majority of TBI-related ED visits. In adults aged 75 and older, falls represent the leading cause of TBI-related hospitalization and death.

The pathophysiology of fall-related TBI in the elderly population deserves special emphasis. Age-related cortical atrophy increases the distance between the brain surface and the inner table of the skull, stretching the bridging veins that traverse the subdural space. This anatomical change dramatically increases susceptibility to subdural hematoma formation, even from relatively minor falls. Furthermore, the widespread use of anticoagulant and antiplatelet medications in the geriatric population increases the risk of hemorrhage expansion and complicates acute management.

3.3 Motor Vehicle Crashes

Motor vehicle crashes (MVCs) are the second leading cause of TBI overall and the leading cause in adolescents and young adults aged 15–24. They are responsible for the largest proportion of severe TBI and TBI-related deaths in the working-age adult population. Mechanisms include vehicle occupant injuries, pedestrian strikes, motorcycle crashes, and bicycle collisions.

Alcohol involvement is documented in 25–50% of MVC-related TBIs in adults, a factor that complicates both acute assessment (confounding GCS scoring) and rehabilitation (increasing risk of cognitive impairment and poor engagement with therapy).

3.4 Assault

Assault is the fourth leading cause of TBI overall but shows marked demographic variation. It is most prevalent in young adult males and in urban settings. Assault-related TBI carries distinct clinical characteristics, including a higher incidence of facial and orbital fractures, contralateral contusions from directed blows, and frequently concurrent cervical spine injuries. In children, assault-related TBI raises concern for non-accidental trauma, which requires specific evaluation and reporting protocols.

3.5 Sport-Related Concussion

Sport-related concussion has emerged as a major public health concern, particularly among youth athletes. An estimated 1.6–3.8 million sport-related concussions occur annually in the United States. Contact and collision sports — including American football, ice hockey, rugby, soccer, and lacrosse — carry the highest risk. The recognition that subconcussive impacts (repetitive head impacts below the threshold for clinical concussion) may contribute to long-term neurological sequelae has further amplified concern.

3.6 Blast Injury in Military Populations

Blast injury is the dominant mechanism of TBI in military populations and is discussed in detail in Section 5.

4. TBI as a Chronic Disease Process [COMPREHENSIVE]

4.1 Conceptual Shift: From Acute Event to Lifelong Condition

For decades, TBI was conceptualized as an acute event with a finite recovery period. This model implied that neurological deficits stabilized within one to two years, after which no further meaningful recovery or deterioration would occur. Contemporary evidence has decisively overturned this paradigm. It is now recognized that TBI initiates a cascade of pathological processes that may continue for years to decades after the initial injury, affecting multiple organ systems and increasing susceptibility to a range of chronic health conditions.

4.2 The Masel and DeWitt Framework

In a landmark 2010 publication, Masel and DeWitt argued that TBI should be reclassified as a chronic disease process rather than a discrete event. Their review compiled extensive evidence that TBI increases long-term mortality and reduces life expectancy, independent of injury severity. They demonstrated that TBI meets the World Health Organization’s definition of a chronic disease in that it: (a) is permanent, (b) is caused by non-reversible pathological alterations, (c) requires special training of the patient for rehabilitation, and (d) may require a long period of observation, supervision, or care.

The chronic sequelae of TBI span virtually every organ system:

  • Neurological: Increased risk of posttraumatic epilepsy, movement disorders (parkinsonism, tremor), chronic headache, and sleep-wake disturbances
  • Neurodegenerative: Elevated risk of all-cause dementia (63–96% increase), Alzheimer disease (58–82% increase), and chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE)
  • Neuroendocrine: Hypopituitarism affecting growth hormone, gonadal, thyroid, and adrenal axes (prevalence 25–50% in moderate-to-severe TBI)
  • Psychiatric: Depression, anxiety, PTSD, substance use disorders, and suicidality
  • Systemic: Cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and immune dysregulation

4.3 CMS Recognition (2024)

In June 2024, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) formally added TBI to its official list of chronic conditions. This regulatory action, published in the Federal Register with an effective date of January 1, 2025, has several significant implications:

  1. Insurance coverage: TBI now qualifies for chronic special needs plans (C-SNPs) through the Medicare Advantage program, enabling long-term coordinated care
  2. Healthcare delivery: Providers can design chronic disease management models analogous to those used for diabetes or heart failure
  3. Research priorities: Federal funding agencies are increasingly directing resources toward understanding the chronic phase of TBI
  4. Clinical follow-up: The recognition supports lifelong surveillance for late-emerging complications, including neuroendocrine dysfunction, neurodegeneration, and psychosocial deterioration

4.4 TBI Model Systems Longitudinal Data

The TBI Model Systems (TBIMS) National Database, established in 1987, provides the most comprehensive longitudinal data on TBI outcomes in the United States. With follow-up extending to 30+ years post-injury, TBIMS data have demonstrated that outcomes after TBI are dynamic rather than static. Key findings include:

  • Functional gains during the first two years are common, but significant proportions of individuals experience functional decline after five years
  • Late-onset seizures can develop years to decades after injury
  • Cognitive deterioration in the chronic phase may reflect accelerated aging processes
  • Employment rates at five years post-injury remain substantially below pre-injury levels
  • Life expectancy is reduced by 6–9 years on average after moderate-to-severe TBI

4.5 TBI and Neurodegenerative Risk

Meta-analytic evidence demonstrates a dose-dependent relationship between TBI exposure and subsequent neurodegenerative disease. A history of TBI is associated with a 63–96% increased risk of all-cause dementia and a 58–82% increased risk of Alzheimer disease specifically. The risk is modulated by injury severity, with more severe injuries and repeated exposures conferring greater risk. Males are disproportionately affected, likely reflecting both higher TBI incidence and potentially greater biological susceptibility.

Proposed mechanisms linking TBI to neurodegeneration include chronic neuroinflammation (persistent microglial activation documented 17+ years post-injury), tau accumulation and propagation, disrupted blood-brain barrier integrity, impaired glymphatic clearance of neurotoxic proteins, and accelerated amyloid pathology.

4.6 Traumatic Encephalopathy Syndrome (TES) and CTE

Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease associated with repetitive head impacts. CTE can currently be definitively diagnosed only on post-mortem neuropathological examination, with the pathognomonic finding being perivascular accumulation of hyperphosphorylated tau at the depths of cortical sulci — a pattern distinct from Alzheimer disease, in which tau deposits preferentially at the crests of gyri.

The NINDS consensus criteria for the clinical diagnosis of Traumatic Encephalopathy Syndrome (TES) require all four of the following:

  1. Substantial history of repetitive head trauma
  2. Cognitive impairment in memory and/or executive function AND/OR neurobehavioral dysregulation (impulsivity, aggression, emotional lability)
  3. Progressive course of decline
  4. No better explanation by another neurological or psychiatric disorder

Clinical Pearl: CTE is associated with REPETITIVE head impacts, not a single TBI. The total burden of subconcussive exposure may be more important than the number of diagnosed concussions. The key neuropathological distinction from Alzheimer disease is the location of tau deposits: sulcal depths (CTE) versus gyral crests (Alzheimer disease). This distinction is commonly tested.

5. Military and Blast-Related TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

5.1 Epidemiology of Military TBI

Traumatic brain injury has been designated the “signature injury” of the conflicts in Iraq and Afghanistan (Operations Iraqi Freedom, Enduring Freedom, and New Dawn). Since 2000, more than 414,000 service members have been diagnosed with TBI according to the Department of Defense TBI Worldwide Numbers. Approximately 82% of these injuries are classified as mild. In post-deployment screening studies, up to 56% of combat veterans reported deployment-acquired mTBI.

The military TBI population presents unique challenges due to high rates of polytrauma. The “polytrauma triad” — consisting of TBI, posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and chronic pain — is present in a significant proportion of combat-injured veterans, with TBI-PTSD comorbidity rates of 33–44%. The overlapping symptom profiles of these conditions complicate both diagnosis and treatment.

5.2 Blast Injury Mechanisms

Blast exposure produces brain injury through four distinct mechanisms, each with unique pathophysiology:

Blast Category Mechanism Pathophysiology
Primary Direct blast overpressure wave Diffuse cerebral edema, vasospasm, petechial hemorrhages; can cause concussion WITHOUT direct head impact
Secondary Penetrating injury from shrapnel and debris Focal parenchymal damage; highest seizure risk
Tertiary Acceleration/deceleration from being thrown by blast DAI, contusions, SDH — similar to blunt trauma mechanisms
Quaternary Burns, inhalation injury, crush Hypoxic-ischemic injury; systemic inflammatory response

Clinical Pearl: Primary blast injury is unique to blast exposure and can cause concussion WITHOUT direct head impact. The blast overpressure wave alone is sufficient to disrupt brain function. This mechanism has no civilian correlate and is the most commonly tested blast-related concept on examinations.

5.3 Clinical Features of Blast TBI

Blast-related TBI differs from civilian blunt-force TBI in several important respects:

  • Vestibular symptoms: More pronounced vestibular dysfunction, including vertigo, disequilibrium, and motion sensitivity
  • Headache chronicity: Greater prevalence of persistent post-traumatic headache
  • PTSD comorbidity: Higher rates of comorbid PTSD, with symptom overlap creating diagnostic challenges
  • Neuropsychiatric complexity: More complex behavioral presentations, including irritability, insomnia, and cognitive complaints that may reflect combined TBI and PTSD pathology

5.4 Assessment Tools: MACE-2

The Military Acute Concussion Evaluation, 2nd Edition (MACE-2), is the standardized sideline assessment tool for military populations, analogous to the SCAT6 used in civilian sport concussion. Components include:

  • History and mechanism of injury assessment
  • Red flag screening (seizure, repeated vomiting, worsening headache)
  • Cognitive assessment (orientation, immediate memory, concentration, delayed recall)
  • Neurological screening (pupils, balance, coordination)
  • Balance assessment (modified Balance Error Scoring System)
  • Symptom checklist

5.5 The PACT Act (2022)

The Sergeant First Class Heath Robinson Honoring our Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics (PACT) Act, signed into law on August 10, 2022, represents the most significant expansion of VA healthcare benefits in decades. While primarily focused on toxic exposure (burn pits, Agent Orange, radiation), the PACT Act has important implications for TBI care:

  • Expanded VA healthcare eligibility for veterans with toxic exposures and TBI
  • Presumptive service connection for certain conditions, reducing the burden of proof for disability claims
  • Enhanced funding for TBI research and clinical programs within the VA system
  • Authorization of $797 billion in spending for veteran healthcare expansion

5.6 Return-to-Duty Protocol

The military graduated return-to-duty protocol mirrors the structure of sport return-to-play protocols:

  1. Symptom-limited activity: Daily activities that do not provoke symptoms
  2. Light aerobic exercise: Walking, stationary cycling
  3. Duty-specific exercise: Running drills, occupational activities
  4. Full duty activities without combat: Non-combat operational duties
  5. Full duty with medical clearance: Return to all duties including combat operations

Each step requires a minimum of 24 hours symptom-free before progression. Symptom recurrence mandates return to the previous asymptomatic step. Medical clearance is required before return to full duty.

6. Intimate Partner Violence and TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

6.1 Epidemiology of IPV-Related TBI

Intimate partner violence (IPV) represents a severely underrecognized cause of traumatic brain injury, particularly among women. Epidemiological studies estimate that 31–39% of IPV episodes involve mechanisms capable of producing TBI, with some screening studies reporting positive TBI screens in up to 75–78% of shelter-seeking women with IPV-related head injuries.

Key demographic and clinical characteristics include:

  • 73% of IPV-TBI patients are female
  • Higher ICU admission rates compared to non-IPV TBI (odds ratio 4.29)
  • Higher mortality rates compared to non-IPV TBI (odds ratio 3.20)
  • Frequently involves repetitive injury, creating cumulative brain damage analogous to sport-related repetitive head impacts

6.2 Mechanisms of Injury

The mechanisms of IPV-related TBI are diverse and often involve multiple injury modalities:

  • Direct blows: Strikes to the head, face, and jaw
  • Strangulation: Non-fatal strangulation produces anoxic brain injury and may cause TBI through loss of consciousness without direct head impact
  • Impact against objects: Being pushed, thrown, or slammed against walls, floors, or furniture
  • Shaking: Particularly relevant in the context of combined assault mechanisms

6.3 Barriers to Diagnosis

IPV-related TBI is frequently underdiagnosed due to multiple overlapping barriers:

  • Delayed presentation: Victims often do not seek medical care immediately after injury
  • Minimization: Both patients and providers may minimize injury severity
  • Symptom attribution: TBI symptoms (cognitive impairment, emotional dysregulation, sleep disturbance) may be attributed to psychological trauma rather than neurological injury
  • Lack of screening: Despite surgical professional societies recommending universal IPV screening in trauma patients, implementation remains inconsistent
  • Absence of standardized tools: No universally accepted screening instrument for IPV-related brain injury exists, though several tools (BISA, BISQ-IPV, HELPS, OSU TBI-ID) have been used in research settings

6.4 Clinical Implications

Recognition of IPV-TBI has significant implications for clinical practice:

  1. All patients presenting with head, face, or neck injuries should be screened for IPV
  2. Cognitive and behavioral symptoms in IPV survivors should prompt consideration of brain injury as a contributing factor
  3. Repetitive IPV-related head trauma may produce cumulative neurocognitive effects similar to those observed in sport-related repetitive head impacts
  4. Safety planning must be integrated with TBI management, as cognitive impairment from brain injury may compromise a survivor’s ability to execute safety plans

7. TBI Severity Classification [BOARD]

7.1 The Three Pillars of Severity Classification

TBI severity is classified using three clinical parameters assessed during the acute injury phase: the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score, the duration of loss of consciousness (LOC), and the duration of posttraumatic amnesia (PTA). Classification is determined by the most severe indicator among these three measures.

Parameter Mild Moderate Severe
GCS 13–15 9–12 3–8
LOC < 30 minutes 30 min – 24 hours > 24 hours
PTA < 24 hours 1–7 days > 7 days
Imaging Normal May be abnormal Often abnormal

Clinical Pearl: When severity parameters conflict (e.g., GCS 14 but PTA of 5 days), the patient is classified by the MOST SEVERE indicator. In this example, the patient would be classified as having a moderate TBI based on PTA duration, even though the GCS suggests mild injury. This “worst indicator rules” principle is commonly tested.

7.2 Glasgow Coma Scale

The GCS, developed by Teasdale and Jennett in 1974, remains the most widely used acute severity measure worldwide. It assesses three domains:

Eye Opening (E: 1–4) | Score | Response | |——-|———-| | 4 | Spontaneous | | 3 | To voice/command | | 2 | To pain/pressure | | 1 | None |

Verbal Response (V: 1–5) | Score | Response | |——-|———-| | 5 | Oriented | | 4 | Confused (sentences but disoriented) | | 3 | Inappropriate words (random words, no sustained conversation) | | 2 | Incomprehensible sounds (moaning, no words) | | 1 | None |

Motor Response (M: 1–6) | Score | Response | Clinical Significance | |——-|———-|———————-| | 6 | Obeys commands | Intact cortical function | | 5 | Localizes pain (crosses midline) | Purposeful; cortical involvement | | 4 | Withdrawal/Normal flexion | Spinal reflex level | | 3 | Abnormal flexion (decorticate) | Lesion above red nucleus; BETTER prognosis | | 2 | Extension (decerebrate) | Lesion below red nucleus; WORSE prognosis | | 1 | None | No motor response |

Clinical Pearl: The motor component is the single best prognostic predictor within the GCS. The best motor response at 2 weeks post-injury is the strongest single prognostic indicator. Decorticate posturing (M3) carries a better prognosis than decerebrate posturing (M2) because it indicates a higher-level lesion above the red nucleus. Mnemonic: deCORticate = COR = core/heart = arms flex toward the heart.

7.3 GCS Limitations

Despite its ubiquity, the GCS has well-documented limitations:

  • Cannot assess verbal response in intubated patients (recorded as “VT” or E+M only)
  • Confounded by sedation (average 1.7-point depression), alcohol intoxication, and polysubstance use
  • Does not assess brainstem reflexes
  • Significant inter-rater variability, particularly for motor scoring
  • Minimum possible score is 3 (not 0), which limits floor discrimination
  • Should be assessed after resuscitation, not during acute hemodynamic instability

7.4 Loss of Consciousness

LOC duration provides important prognostic information. The absence of LOC does not exclude a TBI diagnosis, as the ACRM definition requires only an alteration in mental status. When present, LOC duration helps stratify severity but may be difficult to determine accurately, particularly when the injury is unwitnessed.

7.5 Posttraumatic Amnesia

PTA refers to the period of confusion and inability to form new continuous day-to-day memories after TBI. It encompasses both the period of coma (if present) and the subsequent period of confusion before continuous memory formation resumes. PTA duration is the single best predictor of long-term functional outcome after TBI and is more reliable than GCS for prognostication.

Key prognostic thresholds include:

PTA Duration Severity Expected Outcome
< 5 minutes Very mild Good recovery expected
5–60 minutes Mild Good recovery expected
1–24 hours Moderate Good recovery likely
1–7 days Severe May have persistent deficits
1–4 weeks Very severe Significant disability likely
> 4 weeks Extremely severe Poor functional outcome likely

Critical prognostic rules for boards: - PTA < 2 months: severe disability is unlikely - PTA > 3 months: good recovery is unlikely

7.6 Complicated vs. Uncomplicated Mild TBI

An important subclassification within mild TBI distinguishes complicated from uncomplicated injuries:

  • Uncomplicated mTBI: GCS 13–15 with normal neuroimaging
  • Complicated mTBI: GCS 13–15 with trauma-related abnormalities on neuroimaging (contusion, hemorrhage, skull fracture)

Patients with complicated mTBI have outcomes more closely resembling moderate TBI than uncomplicated mTBI, with higher rates of persistent symptoms, longer recovery times, and greater functional disability. This distinction is clinically relevant for disposition decisions, follow-up planning, and prognostic counseling.

8. Prevention Strategies [COMPREHENSIVE]

8.1 Primary Prevention

Primary prevention aims to prevent the occurrence of TBI altogether. Strategies are organized by mechanism of injury:

Motor Vehicle Crash Prevention: - Seatbelt legislation and enforcement (associated with 45% reduction in TBI mortality) - Airbag technology, including side-curtain and advanced frontal airbags - Graduated driver licensing programs for adolescents - Distracted driving legislation (texting bans) - Impaired driving enforcement (legal BAC limits, sobriety checkpoints) - Advanced vehicle safety technology (automatic emergency braking, lane departure warning)

Fall Prevention: - Multifactorial fall prevention programs for older adults (exercise, medication review, home safety assessment, vision correction) - CDC STEADI (Stopping Elderly Accidents, Deaths, and Injuries) initiative - Environmental modifications (grab bars, improved lighting, non-slip surfaces) - Medication management (reducing polypharmacy, minimizing sedative use) - Strength and balance training programs

Sport-Related Concussion Prevention: - Rule changes limiting high-impact contact (e.g., targeting penalties in football, body-checking restrictions in youth hockey) - Equipment standards and helmet certification programs - Sideline assessment protocols and return-to-play guidelines - Education programs for athletes, coaches, parents, and officials

8.2 Youth Concussion Legislation: The Lystedt Law

In 2009, the State of Washington enacted the Zackery Lystedt Law, named after a 13-year-old football player who sustained catastrophic brain injury from second impact syndrome after returning to play following an initial concussion. This landmark legislation became the template for concussion laws adopted by all 50 states by 2014.

The three mandatory components of Lystedt-type laws are:

  1. Preseason education: Athletes, parents, and coaches must receive education about concussion recognition and risks
  2. Mandatory removal from play: Any athlete suspected of sustaining a concussion must be immediately removed from play — “when in doubt, sit them out”
  3. Written medical clearance: A licensed healthcare provider must provide written authorization before the athlete may return to play

Variation exists among state laws regarding which sports are covered, which healthcare professionals can provide clearance, age limits of applicability, and enforcement mechanisms. Most laws apply to youth and high school athletes, with some states extending coverage to collegiate level.

8.3 Helmet Legislation

Helmet use is one of the most effective interventions for reducing TBI severity in specific populations:

  • Motorcycle helmets: Universal motorcycle helmet laws (covering all riders) are associated with 42% reductions in motorcycle-related TBI mortality compared to no-helmet laws, and 11% reductions compared to partial-coverage laws (covering only riders under age 18–21)
  • Bicycle helmets: Bicycle helmet use reduces the risk of head injury by approximately 48% and the risk of severe brain injury by 60%. However, only 22 states and the District of Columbia have any bicycle helmet legislation, and most apply only to children
  • Sports helmets: Helmet standards for football, hockey, skiing, and cycling are regulated by organizations such as NOCSAE (National Operating Committee on Standards for Athletic Equipment) and CPSC (Consumer Product Safety Commission)

8.4 Secondary and Tertiary Prevention

Secondary prevention focuses on reducing the severity and complications of TBI after it has occurred: - Rapid access to trauma systems and neurosurgical care - Pre-hospital guidelines emphasizing avoidance of hypotension and hypoxia - CT decision rules (Canadian CT Head Rule, New Orleans Criteria) to identify patients requiring neurosurgical intervention - Early rehabilitation referral

Tertiary prevention targets the reduction of long-term disability: - Evidence-based rehabilitation across the continuum of care - Management of chronic complications (seizures, neuroendocrine dysfunction, psychiatric comorbidities) - Community reintegration and vocational rehabilitation programs - Long-term follow-up for late-emerging complications

9. Health Disparities in TBI [COMPREHENSIVE]

9.1 Racial and Ethnic Disparities

Substantial racial and ethnic disparities exist across the TBI continuum, from incidence and mechanism through acute care access, rehabilitation utilization, and long-term outcomes.

Incidence and Mortality: - American Indian/Alaska Native populations have the highest rates of TBI-related hospitalizations and deaths among all racial/ethnic groups - African American patients demonstrate an adjusted odds ratio of mortality of 1.57 compared to White patients - Hispanic and non-Hispanic Black populations experience higher proportionate exposure to assault-related TBI

Access to Care and Rehabilitation: - Racial and ethnic minority groups are less likely to receive follow-up care and rehabilitation services following TBI compared to non-Hispanic White patients - African American and Hispanic patients are less likely to be discharged to inpatient rehabilitation facilities and more likely to be discharged to nursing facilities or home without services - Language barriers, cultural differences in healthcare seeking, and implicit provider bias contribute to disparities in care delivery

Outcomes: - After adjusting for injury severity, minority patients demonstrate worse functional outcomes at discharge and at follow-up - Disparities in outcome are partially mediated by differences in insurance status, rehabilitation access, and community resources

9.2 Socioeconomic Disparities

Socioeconomic status exerts a powerful influence on TBI risk, access to care, and outcomes:

  • Patients in the lowest income quartile have 2.2 times the odds of mortality compared to higher-income patients
  • Lower educational attainment and income levels are associated with higher odds of TBI occurrence
  • Insurance status significantly impacts outcomes: Medicaid and self-pay patients have higher odds of mortality (1.19 and 2.36, respectively) compared to privately insured patients, as well as longer hospital lengths of stay
  • Socioeconomic disadvantage is associated with reduced access to inpatient rehabilitation, outpatient therapies, and community reintegration services

9.3 Rural-Urban Disparities

Geographic location creates additional layers of disparity:

  • People living in rural areas have significantly greater risk of dying from TBI compared to urban residents, with rural counties demonstrating adjusted mortality odds ratios of 1.27
  • Contributing factors include longer transport times to definitive care, reduced access to Level I trauma centers, fewer neurosurgeons and neurocritical care specialists, and limited availability of specialized TBI rehabilitation programs
  • During 2016–2018, states in the South had the highest TBI-related death rates (19.2 per 100,000) while Northeastern states had the lowest (12.8 per 100,000)
  • Telehealth and telemedicine consultations represent emerging strategies to bridge rural-urban gaps in TBI expertise, but infrastructure and reimbursement challenges remain

9.4 Addressing Disparities

Strategies to reduce TBI-related health disparities include:

  1. System-level interventions: Expanding trauma system coverage to rural areas, increasing inpatient rehabilitation capacity in underserved regions, and developing culturally and linguistically appropriate care delivery models
  2. Policy interventions: Expanding Medicaid coverage, reducing barriers to rehabilitation access, and implementing universal screening protocols for TBI in vulnerable populations (IPV survivors, incarcerated individuals, persons experiencing homelessness)
  3. Research priorities: Ensuring diverse representation in TBI clinical trials, developing culturally validated assessment tools, and studying the intersection of social determinants with TBI outcomes
  4. Workforce development: Training a diverse rehabilitation workforce, supporting pipeline programs for underrepresented minorities in brain injury medicine, and expanding community health worker roles in TBI follow-up
