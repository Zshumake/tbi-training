# Chapter 9: Posttraumatic Agitation and Behavioral Management

---

## Chapter Overview

Posttraumatic agitation is among the most clinically challenging and commonly tested complications in traumatic brain injury rehabilitation. It represents a transient but often dramatic behavioral syndrome that arises during the period of emerging consciousness, corresponding to Rancho Los Amigos Level IV (Confused-Agitated), and affects approximately one-third of TBI patients admitted to acute inpatient rehabilitation. The effective management of agitation requires a systematic, protocol-driven approach: first rule out medical causes, then implement environmental modifications as the cornerstone of care, and finally deploy pharmacotherapy as an adjunct when environmental interventions are insufficient.

This chapter also addresses paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (PSH), a related but distinct autonomic phenomenon that occurs in severe TBI and requires a different therapeutic strategy, as well as the broader spectrum of neurobehavioral sequelae that may persist into the chronic phase of recovery.

---

## 9.1 Posttraumatic Agitation: Definition and Epidemiology [BOARD]

### 9.1.1 Defining Agitation in TBI

Posttraumatic agitation is defined as a state of excess physical and/or verbal activity that is non-purposeful, potentially harmful, and interferes with medical care and rehabilitation. This definition emphasizes several key elements that distinguish pathologic agitation from goal-directed behavior:

1. **Excess**: The activity level exceeds what is expected for the clinical situation
2. **Non-purposeful**: The behaviors do not serve an identifiable adaptive goal (though they may reflect the patient's confused attempts to make sense of an incomprehensible environment)
3. **Potentially harmful**: The behaviors create risk for the patient (falls, line removal, self-injury) or for staff and family (physical aggression)

### 9.1.2 Epidemiology

| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | Approximately 33% of TBI patients in acute inpatient rehabilitation |
| Rancho Los Amigos level | Level IV: Confused and Agitated |
| Timing | Typically occurs during emergence from posttraumatic amnesia (PTA), usually weeks 2-8 post-injury |
| Duration | Usually self-limited; most episodes resolve within 1-2 months |
| Severity spectrum | Ranges from mild restlessness to severe physical aggression |

### 9.1.3 The Neurocognitive Context of Agitation

Understanding the cognitive landscape of the agitated TBI patient is essential for appropriate management. Patients at Rancho Level IV exhibit:

- **No short-term memory formation**: Cannot encode new information; therefore cannot learn from experience, remember explanations, or recall instructions
- **No orientation**: Disoriented to person, place, time, and situation; may confabulate elaborate explanations for their hospitalization
- **No capacity for learning**: Behavioral interventions that rely on operant conditioning are ineffective because the patient cannot link consequences to actions
- **Intact emotional responsiveness**: May experience genuine fear, frustration, and confusion despite cognitive deficits
- **Heightened stimulus sensitivity**: Overwhelmed by environmental input that the intact brain would filter effortlessly

This cognitive profile explains why certain interventions work (reducing stimulation, providing consistency) and why others fail (arguing, correcting, attempting to reason) or are counterproductive (restraints, which increase confusion and fear without the possibility of the patient understanding why they are restrained).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Agitation at Rancho Level IV is a NORMAL stage of TBI recovery, not a behavioral disorder requiring suppression. It represents the brain's confused attempt to interact with an environment it cannot comprehend. This perspective should guide management: the goal is to keep the patient safe during this transient phase, not to eliminate all behavioral output through sedation. Over-treatment with sedating medications may actually prolong the duration of PTA and delay recovery.

## 9.2 Agitated Behavior Scale (ABS) [BOARD]

### 9.2.1 Overview and Development

The Agitated Behavior Scale (ABS), developed by Corrigan in 1989, is the standard validated instrument for quantifying agitation after TBI. It was developed specifically for use in TBI rehabilitation settings and remains the most widely used and psychometrically validated agitation measure in this population.

### 9.2.2 Scale Structure and Administration

- **Items**: 14 behavioral items
- **Scoring**: Each item scored 1 (absent) to 4 (present to an extreme degree)
- **Score range**: 14 (minimum, no agitation) to 56 (maximum, extreme agitation on all items)
- **Administration**: Scored by clinical observation over a defined time period (typically a nursing shift); NOT by patient self-report

**The 14 ABS Items:**

1. Short attention span, easy distractibility, inability to concentrate
2. Impulsive, impatient, low tolerance for pain or frustration
3. Uncooperative, resistant to care, demanding
4. Violent and/or threatening violence toward people or property
5. Explosive and/or unpredictable anger
6. Rocking, rubbing, moaning, or other self-stimulating behavior
7. Pulling at tubes, restraints, etc.
8. Wandering from treatment areas
9. Restlessness, pacing, excessive movement
10. Repetitive behaviors, motor and/or verbal
11. Rapid, loud, or excessive talking
12. Sudden changes of mood
13. Self-abusiveness, physical and/or verbal
14. Attempts to get out of bed/chair when unsafe without supervision

### 9.2.3 Score Interpretation

| ABS Score Range | Severity | Clinical Interpretation | Recommended Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| 14-21 | Normal / Absent | No clinically significant agitation; below treatment threshold | Routine monitoring; continue rehabilitation program |
| 22-28 | Mild | Agitation present but manageable | Environmental modifications usually sufficient |
| 29-35 | Moderate | Significant agitation interfering with care | Environmental modifications plus consider pharmacotherapy |
| > 35 | Severe | High risk of self-harm or harm to staff | Environmental modifications plus pharmacotherapy usually indicated |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The critical ABS threshold is > 21 for clinically significant agitation. Serial ABS scoring (performed 2-3 times daily by nursing staff) provides objective data for tracking response to interventions and guiding medication titration. A common board stem will present ABS scores and ask for the appropriate management level. Remember: 14 items, scored 1-4, range 14-56, clinically significant > 21, severe > 35.

## 9.3 Differential Diagnosis of Agitation [BOARD]

### 9.3.1 The Cardinal Rule: Medical Causes First

The single most important principle in managing posttraumatic agitation is to rule out medical causes before attributing behavioral changes to the brain injury itself. Medical causes of agitation are often treatable, and failure to identify them results in inappropriate sedation of a patient whose agitation has a correctable underlying cause.

### 9.3.2 Systematic Medical Evaluation

The following medical causes must be systematically evaluated in every agitated TBI patient:

**1. Pain (Most Common TREATABLE Cause)**
- Occult fractures (especially extremity fractures in polytrauma patients who cannot localize or report pain)
- Heterotopic ossification (periarticular pain and swelling)
- Visceral injury (missed abdominal injury, retroperitoneal hemorrhage)
- Headache (posttraumatic headache is nearly universal)
- Musculoskeletal injury (soft tissue injuries, sprains, joint inflammation)
- Positioning-related discomfort (pressure, contracture, spasticity-related pain)

**2. Infection**
- Urinary tract infection (extremely common due to indwelling catheterization)
- Pneumonia (aspiration pneumonia, ventilator-associated pneumonia)
- Meningitis or ventriculitis (post-surgical, post-EVD)
- Wound infection (surgical site, traumatic wounds)
- Line sepsis (central venous catheter-related bloodstream infection)

**3. Hydrocephalus**
- Acute obstructive hydrocephalus (from intraventricular hemorrhage clot)
- Communicating hydrocephalus (developing PTH)
- Shunt malfunction (in previously shunted patients)
- May present as acute behavioral change without classic NPH triad

**4. Seizures**
- Nonconvulsive status epilepticus (NCSE): May present ONLY as behavioral change without visible motor seizure activity; the diagnosis requires EEG
- Subclinical seizure activity
- Post-ictal confusion mimicking agitation

**5. Medication Effects**
- Paradoxical reactions to benzodiazepines (common in brain-injured patients)
- Anticholinergic toxicity (confusion, agitation, tachycardia, dry mucous membranes)
- Corticosteroid-induced psychosis or agitation
- Opioid-induced delirium (especially at high doses or with metabolic impairment)
- Medication withdrawal (alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids, nicotine)

**6. Gastrointestinal Causes**
- Constipation and fecal impaction (extremely common and frequently overlooked)
- Abdominal distention
- Gastric ulcer or GI bleeding

**7. Urinary Causes**
- Urinary retention (bladder distention causes autonomic discomfort)
- Obstructed catheter

**8. Respiratory Causes**
- Hypoxia (pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, atelectasis, mucus plugging)
- Hypercapnia

**9. Metabolic Derangement**
- Hyponatremia (SIADH, cerebral salt wasting, medication-related)
- Hypoglycemia
- Hepatic or renal encephalopathy
- Thyroid dysfunction (hyper- or hypothyroidism)
- Hypercalcemia

**10. Environmental Factors**
- Sleep deprivation (ICU/hospital environment disrupts sleep-wake cycle)
- Overstimulation (excessive noise, visitors, television, lighting)

> **Clinical Pearl:** ALWAYS rule out medical causes before attributing agitation to the TBI itself. Pain and infection are the two most common reversible medical causes of agitation. Nonconvulsive status epilepticus can present ONLY as behavioral change -- if agitation is new, worsening, or atypical, obtain an EEG. Constipation and urinary retention are the most frequently overlooked triggers of agitation in TBI rehabilitation.

## 9.4 Environmental Management: First-Line Interventions [BOARD]

### 9.4.1 Rationale

Environmental modification is the cornerstone of agitation management in TBI and should ALWAYS be implemented before pharmacotherapy. The rationale is grounded in the neurocognitive profile of the agitated patient: a brain that cannot process, filter, or integrate environmental stimuli requires a simplified, predictable, low-stimulus environment to reduce confusion and its behavioral manifestation.

### 9.4.2 The Table 2-11 Approach

The following environmental interventions, drawn from the Cuccurullo reference framework (Table 2-11) and expanded with current evidence, constitute the standard of care:

**1. Reduce Stimulation**
- Dim lights (especially overhead fluorescent lighting)
- Minimize ambient noise (turn off television, radio; close doors)
- Limit visitors to 1-2 familiar family members at a time
- Avoid overstimulating activities; do not force therapeutic activities when the patient is agitated
- Use calm, low-volume voice tones in all interactions

**2. Maintain Consistent Routine**
- Same nursing staff assignment whenever possible
- Same daily schedule: wake time, meals, therapy, rest, bedtime
- Predictable structure reduces confusion and anxiety
- Brief, structured therapy sessions (15-20 minutes maximum)
- Allow rest periods between activities

**3. Provide Reorientation Cues**
- Calendar, clock, family photographs, familiar personal objects at bedside
- Orient the patient frequently to identity, location, and situation (without arguing about confabulations)
- Whiteboards with daily schedule, care team names, and key messages

**4. Remove Physical Restraints**
- Physical restraints WORSEN agitation in most TBI patients by increasing fear, confusion, and autonomic activation
- Use padded mitts ONLY when absolutely necessary to prevent life-threatening line removal
- Replace restraints with less restrictive alternatives: 1:1 sitter, low bed, padded environment

**5. Provide 1:1 Supervision**
- Dedicated sitter for continuous observation and calm redirection
- More effective than restraints in reducing falls, injury, and agitation escalation
- Provides consistent, non-threatening human presence

**6. Implement Safety Modifications**
- Low bed position or mattress on floor
- Padded side rails (not to restrict movement but to prevent injury)
- Helmet if fall risk is elevated and patient has craniectomy defect or history of head impact
- Remove dangerous objects from the environment (sharp items, glass, heavy objects)

**7. Structure Therapy Sessions Appropriately**
- Short sessions (15-20 minutes) matched to the patient's cognitive and attentional capacity
- Frequent breaks; terminate sessions if agitation escalates
- Avoid frustrating tasks; match demands to current cognitive level
- Functional activities in familiar contexts (eating, grooming) may be tolerated better than abstract exercises

**8. Promote Sleep Hygiene**
- Maintain day-night cycle: lights on during day, dark and quiet at night
- Minimize nighttime disruptions (bundle nursing assessments)
- Consider melatonin for circadian entrainment
- Avoid stimulants and activating medications after noon

**9. Allow Mobility**
- Walking or wheelchair mobility with supervision is therapeutic
- Movement may reduce restlessness more effectively than confinement
- Structured mobility programs (walking schedules) channel physical energy constructively

**10. Educate Family**
- Teach family NOT to correct, argue with, or overwhelm the patient
- Model calm, simple redirection techniques
- Explain that agitation is a normal, temporary phase of recovery
- Provide emotional support for family distress (witnessing a loved one in this state is deeply upsetting)

> **Clinical Pearl:** On the board exam, the correct first step in agitation management is ALWAYS environmental modification, NOT pharmacotherapy. Physical restraints are generally CONTRAINDICATED because they paradoxically increase agitation, injury risk, and autonomic instability. The 1:1 sitter, reduced stimulation, and consistent routine are the core interventions. Pharmacotherapy is an adjunct to environmental management, not a substitute for it.

## 9.5 Pharmacotherapy Hierarchy for Agitation [BOARD]

### 9.5.1 When to Initiate Pharmacotherapy

Pharmacotherapy should be considered when:
- Environmental modifications have been fully implemented but are insufficient to maintain safety
- ABS score remains > 21 (particularly > 35) despite environmental interventions
- The patient poses a risk of self-harm, harm to staff, or interference with essential medical care (e.g., repeatedly removing critical lines, tubes, or monitoring devices)
- Agitation is preventing participation in essential rehabilitation activities

### 9.5.2 The Pharmacotherapy Hierarchy

| Priority | Medication | Dose Range | Mechanism | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1st line** | Propranolol | 20-60 mg TID-QID | Non-selective beta-blocker | **BEST Cochrane evidence** for TBI agitation; controls sympathetic hyperactivity; lipophilic (crosses BBB for central calming effect); does NOT impair cognitive recovery |
| **2nd line** | Amantadine | 100 mg BID | Dopaminergic / NMDA antagonist | Dual benefit: reduces agitation AND promotes cognitive recovery; second dose by noon; adjust for renal function |
| **3rd line** | Valproic acid | 250-500 mg BID-TID | Anticonvulsant / mood stabilizer | Dual benefit if seizure risk is elevated; monitor LFTs, CBC, drug levels; weight gain, tremor, thrombocytopenia |
| **4th line** | Trazodone | 50-100 mg QHS (or BID-TID for daytime agitation) | Serotonergic (5-HT2A antagonist) | Useful when sleep disturbance contributes to agitation; sedating; first-line for sleep in TBI |
| **5th line** | Low-dose quetiapine | 25-50 mg BID | Atypical antipsychotic (D2/5-HT2A) | LEAST preferred; may impair recovery via dopamine blockade; use lowest dose, shortest duration; better than typical antipsychotics |

### 9.5.3 Beta-Blockers: The Evidence Leader

The Cochrane systematic review of pharmacologic interventions for agitation after TBI found that beta-blockers (propranolol, pindolol) have the BEST level of evidence for efficacy. The mechanism of beta-blocker effectiveness in TBI agitation is multifactorial:

1. **Peripheral sympatholytic effect**: Reduces tachycardia, diaphoresis, and other manifestations of sympathetic hyperactivation that accompany and perpetuate agitation
2. **Central calming effect**: Propranolol is lipophilic and crosses the blood-brain barrier, where beta-adrenergic blockade in central noradrenergic circuits may reduce arousal and aggression
3. **Recovery-neutral**: Beta-blockers do NOT impair cognitive recovery, making them preferable to sedating alternatives
4. **Dual benefit**: Also useful for concurrent autonomic symptoms such as tremor, tachycardia, and hypertension

**Propranolol dosing**: Start 20 mg TID; titrate to 40-60 mg TID-QID based on response; monitor heart rate (hold if HR < 60) and blood pressure (hold if SBP < 90)

**Pindolol**: An alternative beta-blocker with intrinsic sympathomimetic activity (ISA), which may produce slightly less bradycardia than propranolol

> **Clinical Pearl:** Propranolol has the BEST evidence for TBI agitation per the Cochrane review. It is recovery-neutral (does not impair cognition), addresses the sympathetic hyperactivation component of agitation, and has a well-established safety profile. When a board question asks for the first-line pharmacotherapy for posttraumatic agitation (after environmental modifications have been implemented), propranolol is the correct answer.

### 9.5.4 Medications to AVOID for Agitation [BOARD]

| Medication | Mechanism of Harm | Why It Worsens TBI |
|---|---|---|
| Haloperidol | Typical antipsychotic; potent D2 blockade | Hinders motor and cognitive recovery; prolongs PTA; EPS risk; NMS risk |
| Benzodiazepines | GABAergic sedation | Impairs cognition; PARADOXICAL agitation common in brain-injured patients; impairs neuroplasticity; dependence |
| Droperidol | Typical antipsychotic (butyrophenone) | Same concerns as haloperidol plus significant QTc prolongation |
| Phenobarbital | GABAergic sedation (duration) | Profound cognitive impairment; excessive sedation |
| Physical restraints | Not pharmacologic but included | Generally CONTRAINDICATED: increase agitation, injury risk, and autonomic instability |

### 9.5.5 Clinical Decision Algorithm

1. **Step 1**: Rule out medical causes (pain, infection, hydrocephalus, seizures, medications, constipation, urinary retention, hypoxia, metabolic derangement)
2. **Step 2**: Implement environmental modifications (reduce stimulation, 1:1 sitter, remove restraints, consistent routine, reorientation, safety measures)
3. **Step 3**: Assess with ABS (score > 21 = clinically significant; > 35 = severe; track serially)
4. **Step 4**: Start propranolol if pharmacotherapy needed (20 mg TID, titrate to 60 mg TID-QID; monitor HR and BP)
5. **Step 5**: Add amantadine for dual benefit (100 mg BID, second dose by noon; promotes cognitive recovery while reducing agitation)
6. **Step 6**: Consider valproic acid if seizure risk is elevated (dual benefit; monitor levels, LFTs, CBC)
7. **Step 7**: Reserve atypical antipsychotics for refractory cases only (low-dose quetiapine 25-50 mg; shortest duration possible)

## 9.6 Paroxysmal Sympathetic Hyperactivity: Diagnostic Criteria [BOARD]

### 9.6.1 Definition, Nomenclature, and Epidemiology

Paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (PSH) -- previously known by numerous terms including sympathetic storming, dysautonomia, diencephalic storming, paroxysmal autonomic instability with dystonia (PAID), and autonomic dysfunction syndrome -- is a syndrome of episodic, paroxysmal sympathetic nervous system discharge in patients with severe acquired brain injury. The international expert consensus panel (Baguley et al., 2014) adopted "paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity" as the standardized term.

- **Incidence**: 15-33% of severe TBI patients
- **Onset timing**: Usually within the first 5-7 days post-injury, but can occur up to 2 weeks
- **Duration**: Episodes may persist for weeks to months
- **Associations**: Younger age, diffuse axonal injury, brainstem injury, bilateral hemispheric damage
- **Prognostic significance**: Associated with longer ICU stays, worse functional outcomes, and higher mortality

### 9.6.2 The Six Diagnostic Features

PSH is diagnosed when a patient with severe acquired brain injury demonstrates **4 of 6** of the following clinical features during paroxysmal episodes, in the absence of an alternative explanation:

1. **Fever**: Temperature > 38.5 degrees C (101.3 degrees F) without an infectious source
2. **Tachycardia**: Heart rate > 120-130 bpm, often occurring in paroxysmal surges
3. **Hypertension**: Systolic BP > 160 mmHg or > 20% above baseline
4. **Tachypnea**: Respiratory rate > 30 breaths/min
5. **Diaphoresis**: Profuse sweating, often described as drenching
6. **Extensor posturing / Rigidity**: Decerebrate or mixed posturing; dystonic postures of the extremities

> **Clinical Pearl:** PSH requires 4 of 6 features and is a diagnosis of EXCLUSION. The episodes are PAROXYSMAL (coming in waves lasting minutes to hours) and are often triggered by noxious stimuli such as suctioning, turning, pain, bladder distention, or constipation. The absence of an infectious source for fever is critical for establishing the diagnosis. Always rule out sepsis, pulmonary embolism, seizures, medication withdrawal, and neuroleptic malignant syndrome before diagnosing PSH.

### 9.6.3 Pathophysiology

The prevailing pathophysiological model for PSH is the Excitatory-Inhibitory Ratio (EIR) model:

- **Normal state**: Descending inhibitory pathways from the cortex, diencephalon, and hypothalamus tonically suppress sympathetic outflow from the brainstem and spinal cord
- **After severe TBI**: Loss of cortical and diencephalic inhibitory input (from diffuse axonal injury, brainstem injury, or bilateral hemispheric damage) removes the tonic brake on sympathetic circuits
- **Result**: Intact brainstem and spinal sympathetic circuits discharge without inhibition, producing paroxysmal catecholamine surges and uninhibited sympathoexcitatory responses to stimuli that would normally be below the threshold for sympathetic activation (allodynia model)
- **Triggers**: Noxious stimuli (suctioning, turning, pain) and even normally innocuous stimuli (touch, sounds) can trigger episodes because the threshold for sympathetic activation is pathologically lowered

## 9.7 PSH Treatment: Morphine and Propranolol [BOARD]

### 9.7.1 First-Line Treatment: Morphine

Morphine (or other opioids) serves as the first-line treatment for acute PSH episodes. The mechanism involves central sympatholytic activity through mu-receptor agonism in the brainstem and spinal cord, which directly reduces sympathetic outflow. Morphine also addresses the afferent pain/nociceptive component that frequently triggers episodes.

- **Dosing**: IV morphine 2-4 mg Q2-4H PRN for acute episodes, or scheduled dosing for frequent episodes
- **Alternative opioids**: Fentanyl (IV infusion for ICU patients), hydromorphone
- **Goal**: Terminate the acute sympathetic surge and reduce episode severity

### 9.7.2 Prevention: Propranolol

Propranolol serves as the maintenance agent for preventing recurrent PSH episodes. Its non-selective beta-blockade addresses the end-organ effects of sympathetic hyperactivation (tachycardia, hypertension) while its lipophilic nature allows central nervous system penetration for direct modulation of central sympathetic circuits.

- **Dosing**: 20-60 mg TID-QID
- **Monitoring**: Heart rate (hold if < 60 bpm) and blood pressure (hold if SBP < 90 mmHg)

### 9.7.3 Complete PSH Treatment Algorithm

| Agent | Mechanism | Role | Dosing | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Morphine / Opioids | Central sympatholytic (mu-receptor) | **Acute episode termination (1st line)** | IV morphine 2-4 mg Q2-4H PRN or scheduled | Terminates sympathetic surge; addresses nociceptive trigger |
| Propranolol | Non-selective beta-blocker | **Episode prevention** | 20-60 mg TID-QID | Lipophilic; crosses BBB; controls HR, BP; combine with morphine |
| Bromocriptine | D2 dopamine agonist | Dopaminergic dysfunction | 2.5-5 mg BID-TID | Addresses dopaminergic component; watch for hypotension |
| Clonidine | Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist | Central sympatholytic | 0.1-0.3 mg BID-TID or transdermal patch | Reduces central sympathetic outflow; avoid rebound HTN if stopped abruptly |
| Gabapentin | Calcium channel modulator (alpha-2-delta subunit) | Adjunctive | 300-1200 mg TID | Reduces paroxysmal episodes; safe; minimal drug interactions |
| Dantrolene | Direct muscle relaxant (blocks Ca2+ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum) | Rigidity / posturing component | 25-100 mg BID-QID | Acts at MUSCLE level (peripheral); monitor LFTs for hepatotoxicity |
| Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) | GABA-B agonist (intrathecal) | **REFRACTORY PSH** | Trial: 50-100 mcg IT; maintenance: ~400-600 mcg/day | Definitive therapy for refractory cases; 100x less drug than oral |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The PSH treatment approach is: morphine + propranolol for most cases, add bromocriptine/clonidine/gabapentin as adjuncts, and reserve intrathecal baclofen for refractory cases. Dantrolene specifically targets the posturing/rigidity component. Remember that treatment is typically combination therapy -- no single agent controls all features of PSH.

## 9.8 PSH Assessment Measure (PSH-AM) [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.8.1 Development and Structure

The PSH Assessment Measure (PSH-AM) was developed by Baguley et al. (2014) through international expert consensus to replace subjective clinical impression with a standardized, reproducible diagnostic framework. It combines two components:

**Component 1: Clinical Feature Scale (CFS)**

The CFS measures the severity of each of the six cardinal PSH features during a paroxysmal episode. Each feature is graded 0-3 based on quantitative severity thresholds.

| Feature | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 | Score 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Heart rate | < 100 bpm | 100-119 | 120-139 | >= 140 |
| Respiratory rate | < 18/min | 18-23 | 24-29 | >= 30 |
| Systolic BP | < 140 mmHg | 140-159 | 160-179 | >= 180 |
| Temperature | < 37 degrees C | 37-37.9 | 38-38.9 | >= 39 |
| Sweating | None | Mild | Moderate | Drenching |
| Posturing | None | Mild | Moderate | Severe |

**Total CFS range**: 0-18 (score during the peak of the episode for maximum accuracy)

**Component 2: Diagnosis Likelihood Tool (DLT)**

The DLT assesses 11 clinical context features that support PSH diagnosis. Each criterion is scored 0 (absent) or 1 (present).

The 11 DLT criteria:
1. Clinical features occur simultaneously
2. Episodes are paroxysmal in nature
3. Sympathetic overreactivity to normally non-painful stimuli (allodynia)
4. Features persist for >= 3 consecutive days
5. Features persist >= 2 weeks post-brain injury
6. Features persist despite treatment of alternative differential diagnoses
7. Medication administered to decrease sympathetic features (>= 2 agents)
8. >= 2 episodes daily
9. Absence of parasympathetic features during episodes
10. Absence of other presumed cause of features
11. Antecedent acquired brain injury (GCS < 8 at time of injury)

**Total DLT range**: 0-11

### 9.8.2 Combined Score Interpretation

| Combined Score (CFS + DLT) | Diagnosis Likelihood | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| < 8 | **UNLIKELY** | PSH unlikely; pursue alternative diagnoses (sepsis, PE, seizures, medication effects, withdrawal) |
| 8-16 | **POSSIBLE** | PSH possible; monitor closely, repeat scoring, consider empiric treatment while continuing workup |
| >= 17 | **PROBABLE** | PSH probable; initiate targeted PSH treatment protocol |

> **Clinical Pearl:** PSH-AM = CFS (0-18) + DLT (0-11) = total range 0-29. The three probability thresholds (< 8, 8-16, >= 17) should be memorized. The CFS captures episode SEVERITY (how bad is this particular episode), while the DLT assesses DIAGNOSTIC LIKELIHOOD (how confident are we that this IS PSH based on clinical context). Both components are needed for accurate diagnosis.

## 9.9 Intrathecal Baclofen for Refractory PSH [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.9.1 Rationale and Evidence

Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) is the definitive treatment for PSH refractory to maximum doses of oral medications. By delivering baclofen directly to the intrathecal space, ITB achieves CSF drug concentrations approximately 100 times higher than achievable with oral dosing, with dramatically reduced systemic exposure and side effects.

**Evidence base**: Multiple case series demonstrate immediate and dramatic improvement in autonomic storming parameters following ITB initiation in severe TBI patients who failed oral medication regimens.

### 9.9.2 Clinical Protocol

1. **Trial dose**: 50-100 mcg intrathecal bolus administered via lumbar puncture
2. **Positive response**: Significant reduction in autonomic features (tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, posturing) within 4-8 hours
3. **Pump placement**: If trial is positive, a programmable baclofen pump is surgically implanted in the abdominal wall with a catheter tunneled subcutaneously to the intrathecal space (typically at thoracic level)
4. **Maintenance dosing**: Mean doses for PSH are approximately 400-600 mcg/day, which are higher than typical spasticity doses due to the additional autonomic component
5. **Oral medication taper**: After pump placement, oral baclofen can typically be discontinued within 5 days; oral propranolol discontinued in approximately 75% of patients within 24 days

### 9.9.3 Dual Benefit

ITB for PSH provides a dual benefit by simultaneously addressing both the autonomic dysfunction (sympathetic storming) and the motor dysfunction (spasticity, extensor posturing) that frequently coexist in severe TBI.

### 9.9.4 ITB Withdrawal Emergency

> **Clinical Pearl:** Abrupt ITB withdrawal (from catheter kink, migration, pump malfunction, or missed refill) is a medical emergency that can be FATAL. The withdrawal syndrome includes: rebound spasticity (severe), hyperthermia (> 40 degrees C), rhabdomyolysis, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), seizures, and multi-organ failure. Treatment includes oral baclofen (high doses via NG tube if needed), benzodiazepines, cyproheptadine, and emergent evaluation/correction of the pump system. This complication must be in the differential for any ITB pump patient presenting with sudden fever, rigidity, and autonomic instability.

## 9.10 Dexmedetomidine for Acute ICU Agitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.10.1 Mechanism and Pharmacology

Dexmedetomidine (Precedex) is a selective alpha-2 adrenergic receptor agonist and imidazoline receptor agonist that acts primarily in the locus coeruleus to produce sedation, anxiolysis, and sympatholysis. It modulates autonomic function and may reduce neuroinflammation.

### 9.10.2 Advantages in TBI

Dexmedetomidine provides "cooperative sedation" -- a unique pharmacologic property whereby patients remain arousable and able to follow commands even while sedated. This characteristic makes it particularly useful in TBI because:

1. **No respiratory depression**: Can be used in non-intubated patients (with caution and monitoring)
2. **Neurological examination preserved**: Does not obscure the neurological examination to the extent that benzodiazepines or propofol do
3. **Reduced delirium**: Associated with lower delirium rates compared to benzodiazepine-based sedation
4. **No paradoxical agitation**: Unlike benzodiazepines, does not produce paradoxical excitation
5. **Opioid-sparing effect**: Reduces opioid requirements
6. **TRACK-TBI data**: Early dexmedetomidine use is not associated with neurological decline in severe TBI

### 9.10.3 Dosing and Monitoring

- **Loading dose**: 0.5-1 mcg/kg over 10 minutes (often OMITTED in TBI to avoid hypotension)
- **Maintenance infusion**: 0.2-0.7 mcg/kg/hr; titrate to effect
- **Setting**: ICU only; requires continuous cardiac monitoring

**Adverse effects**: Bradycardia (most common, dose-related), hypotension, transient hypertension with loading dose. Recent data have raised concerns about increased 90-day mortality at higher doses in younger patients (age < 65), though this remains under investigation.

### 9.10.4 Limitations

- **IV only**: No oral formulation limits outpatient use
- **ICU setting only**: Requires continuous hemodynamic monitoring
- **Cost**: Significantly more expensive than beta-blocker or amantadine-based regimens
- **Not a rehabilitation strategy**: Bridge to more sustainable medication regimens rather than a long-term solution

## 9.11 Gabapentin for Agitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.11.1 Evidence and Role

Gabapentin occupies a nuanced role in TBI agitation management. Despite its name, gabapentin does NOT bind to GABA receptors; rather, it binds to the alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels, reducing excitatory neurotransmitter release. Its primary role is as an adjunctive agent for PSH/sympathetic storming rather than as a primary treatment for posttraumatic agitation.

**PSH evidence**: Case series demonstrate that gabapentin reduces the frequency and severity of sympathetic storming episodes and reduces the need for sedation boluses.

**Agitation evidence**: LIMITED and MIXED. Some case reports suggest benefit, but others have documented paradoxical psychomotor agitation in brain-injured patients.

**Dosing for PSH**: 300-1200 mg TID; start low (100-300 mg TID) and titrate based on response
**Dosing in acute TBI agitation**: Mean initial dose approximately 165 mg, titrated to a mean of approximately 775 mg/day in ICU studies

**Advantages**: Minimal drug interactions, no hepatotoxicity, no CYP450 effects, renally excreted, safe to combine with other agents
**Caution**: May cause sedation, dizziness, ataxia; can paradoxically INCREASE agitation in some cognitively impaired TBI patients

> **Clinical Pearl:** Gabapentin is NOT first-line for posttraumatic agitation. Its primary role is as an ADJUNCT for PSH/sympathetic storming where it reduces paroxysmal episodes. Paradoxically, gabapentin can increase agitation in some brain-injured patients -- monitor closely when initiating. Best used when neuropathic pain coexists with agitation or when PSH features are present.

## 9.12 Restraint-Free Environment [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.12.1 Joint Commission Standards

The Joint Commission mandates that physical restraints may ONLY be used for management of violent or self-destructive behavior that jeopardizes the immediate physical safety of the patient, staff, or others. Restraints must be a LAST RESORT after less restrictive interventions have failed.

**Regulatory requirements when restraints are used:**
- Written physician order required, time-limited:
  - Adults: 4-hour maximum per order
  - Ages 9-17: 2-hour maximum
  - Under age 9: 1-hour maximum
- Orders must be renewed with face-to-face assessment at each interval
- Continuous monitoring: circulation checks, skin integrity, neurovascular status, and patient needs at regular intervals
- Documented evidence that less restrictive alternatives were attempted and failed

### 9.12.2 Alternatives to Physical Restraints

| Alternative | Description | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1:1 sitter | Dedicated staff providing continuous supervision and calm redirection | Reduces falls, agitation, and restraint-related injuries |
| Bed/chair alarms | Motion sensors alerting staff to patient movement | Less restrictive; maintains safety awareness |
| Low bed with floor mat | Reduces fall injury risk without restricting movement | Eliminates bed-height fall risk |
| Padded side rails | Protects from impact injury without restricting movement | Safety without confinement |
| Helmets | Protects craniectomy defects and prevents head injury from falls | Essential for patients with skull defects |
| Enclosed beds | Allow safe movement within protected space (Posey beds, Craig beds) | Used in some specialized TBI rehab units for severe agitation |
| Door alarms | Alert staff when patient leaves room or unit | Maintains mobility while ensuring supervision |
| Diversionary activities | Familiar music, family photos, comfort objects, television | Redirects attention and reduces restlessness |
| Environmental design | Locked units, wandering paths/gardens for safe ambulation | Institutional-level design supporting safety |

## 9.13 Verbal De-Escalation Techniques [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.13.1 Principles Adapted for TBI

Verbal de-escalation in TBI requires modification of standard psychiatric techniques to account for the severe cognitive impairments present during posttraumatic amnesia. Standard approaches that rely on reasoning, insight, or memory formation are ineffective.

**1. Respect Personal Space**
- Maintain at least arm's length distance
- Never corner the patient or block the exit
- Position yourself near an exit for your own safety

**2. Speak Slowly and Simply**
- Use short sentences (5-7 words maximum)
- Simple, concrete language; avoid abstractions
- Repeat key messages without variation in wording

**3. Allow Processing Time**
- Pause 10-15 seconds after each statement
- Do NOT repeat or rephrase too quickly; the injured brain processes slowly
- Silence is therapeutic, not awkward

**4. Use Calm, Low Tone**
- Match emotional intensity DOWN, not up
- Avoid raising voice even if the patient is yelling
- Steady, predictable vocal rhythm provides unconscious calming

**5. Avoid Arguing or Correcting**
- Patients in PTA cannot form new memories or reason logically
- Correcting confabulations or disorientation ESCALATES agitation
- Validation is more effective than correction

**6. Validate Emotions Without Reinforcing Behavior**
- "I can see you are frustrated" is effective
- "Stop yelling" or "Calm down" is counterproductive
- Acknowledge the emotional experience without engaging the content

**7. Offer Limited Choices**
- "Would you like to sit here or walk with me?" gives a sense of control
- Open-ended questions are overwhelming; binary choices are manageable
- The illusion of control reduces resistance

**8. Remove Triggers**
- Identify and eliminate the specific stimulus causing escalation
- Often a particular noise, person, or activity is the trigger
- Simply changing the environment may de-escalate without verbal intervention

**9. Redirect, Do Not Restrict**
- Guide toward a safe alternative activity rather than preventing all movement
- Physical activity (walking) often channels aggressive energy constructively

**10. Know When to Disengage**
- If verbal de-escalation is not working after 2-3 attempts, step back
- Allow the patient space and a cooling period
- Re-approach after the agitation wave has passed

## 9.14 Trauma-Informed Care in TBI Rehabilitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.14.1 Core Principles

Trauma-informed care recognizes that TBI patients have experienced a potentially life-altering traumatic event that disrupts their sense of safety, autonomy, and identity. The rehabilitation environment can inadvertently replicate aspects of trauma (loss of control, physical vulnerability, dependence on others, pain). Applying trauma-informed principles mitigates re-traumatization and supports behavioral stability.

**1. Safety**: Create physical and emotional safety at all times; the injured brain perceives threats more readily than the intact brain; minimize startle stimuli

**2. Trustworthiness**: Be consistent; follow through on promises; maintain predictable routines; TBI patients cannot adapt to changing expectations; staff inconsistency breeds confusion and behavioral escalation

**3. Choice**: Offer choices whenever possible to restore the sense of control lost through injury and hospitalization; even small choices (which shirt to wear, which activity to do first) are psychologically meaningful

**4. Collaboration**: Include the patient in care planning to the extent cognitively possible; a plan imposed without patient input generates resistance; shared decision-making (even at a basic level) improves engagement

**5. Empowerment**: Focus on what the patient CAN do, not what they cannot; build on preserved strengths; celebrate small gains; a strengths-based framework reduces frustration

**6. Cultural Sensitivity**: Consider cultural background in behavioral interpretation; some behaviors that appear "agitated" in one cultural context may be normative in another; involve cultural liaisons when available

### 9.14.2 Staff Education and Debriefing

All team members -- including nursing, therapy (PT, OT, SLP), dietary, transport, housekeeping, and administrative staff -- should receive training in de-escalation techniques and trauma-informed principles. Most agitation encounters involve non-physician staff who may have had no formal training in managing behavioral dysregulation.

Team debriefing after aggressive incidents serves two purposes: (1) processing the emotional impact on staff (reducing burnout, compassion fatigue, and moral injury), and (2) analyzing the incident to identify triggers, evaluate the response, and refine future management strategies.

## 9.15 Pathologic Behaviors: Targeted Treatment [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.15.1 Aggression vs. Disinhibition vs. Emotional Lability

Chronic neurobehavioral sequelae of TBI extend beyond the acute agitation phase and may persist for months to years after injury. These pathologic behaviors are categorized into distinct phenotypes, each with different neuroanatomic correlates and treatment approaches.

**Aggression**

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Definition | Hostile or violent behavior directed at people or property; may be reactive (provoked) or unprovoked |
| Neuroanatomic correlate | Ventral prefrontal cortex, orbitofrontal cortex, amygdala, temporal lobe damage |
| Distinguishing features | Goal-directed or reactive; may be associated with irritability, low frustration tolerance, or paranoia |
| First-line treatment | Beta-blockers (propranolol); behavioral therapy (anger management when cognitively ready) |
| Second-line treatment | SSRIs (citalopram for irritability); anticonvulsants (valproic acid, carbamazepine) |
| AVOID | Typical antipsychotics, benzodiazepines |

**Disinhibition**

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Definition | Inability to suppress inappropriate behaviors, comments, or impulses; socially inappropriate conduct |
| Neuroanatomic correlate | Orbitofrontal cortex (classic "orbitofrontal syndrome"); medial prefrontal cortex |
| Distinguishing features | Inappropriate sexual comments/behavior, crude language, poor social judgment, impulsive spending, overfamiliarity with strangers; the patient is often UNAWARE of the inappropriateness |
| Treatment | SSRIs (may reduce impulsivity); behavioral strategies (structured environment, social skills training); family/caregiver education |
| Hormonal intervention | Anti-androgens (medroxyprogesterone) or GnRH agonists (leuprolide) for severe sexual disinhibition refractory to behavioral management |

**Emotional Lability (Pseudobulbar Affect)**

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Definition | Involuntary, exaggerated, or contextually inappropriate emotional expression (crying, laughing) disproportionate to or disconnected from the patient's actual emotional state |
| Neuroanatomic correlate | Disruption of corticobulbar pathways connecting prefrontal cortex to brainstem emotional expression centers (pons); bilateral hemisphere or brainstem injury |
| Distinguishing features | The patient may laugh during sad situations or cry without feeling sad; episodes are brief, stereotyped, and stimulus-driven; the patient is often distressed by the involuntary nature of the episodes |
| First-line treatment | Dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta): FDA-approved for pseudobulbar affect |
| Alternative treatment | SSRIs (particularly citalopram/escitalopram); TCAs (if seizure risk is low) |
| Distinguishing from depression | Emotional lability is an expressive dyscontrol disorder, not a mood disorder; the patient's subjective emotional state does not match the emotional display |

> **Clinical Pearl:** When a board question presents a TBI patient who cries uncontrollably during therapy but denies feeling sad, this is pseudobulbar affect (emotional lability), NOT depression. The key distinction is the disconnect between emotional expression and subjective emotional experience. Dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta) is FDA-approved for this condition. SSRIs may also be effective. Do NOT assume the patient is depressed and prescribe antidepressants for the wrong indication.

### 9.15.2 PSH vs. Posttraumatic Agitation: The Key Differences

Distinguishing PSH from posttraumatic agitation is essential because their treatments differ substantially.

| Feature | PSH | Posttraumatic Agitation |
|---|---|---|
| Consciousness level | Usually in coma, VS, or MCS (low awareness) | Usually in PTA / Rancho IV (emerging awareness) |
| Vital sign changes | MARKED: fever, tachycardia, HTN, tachypnea | Minimal or absent vital sign changes |
| Diaphoresis | PROFUSE, drenching | Mild or absent |
| Posturing | Extensor posturing / dystonia PROMINENT | Absent (purposeful, non-purposeful movements) |
| Episode pattern | PAROXYSMAL (waves lasting minutes to hours) | More CONTINUOUS (sustained agitated state) |
| Triggers | Noxious stimuli (suctioning, turning, pain) | Overstimulation, confusion, frustration |
| Treatment | Morphine + propranolol; ITB for refractory | Environmental modification first; propranolol, amantadine |
| Timing post-injury | Usually within first 1-2 weeks | Usually weeks 2-8 (during PTA emergence) |

## 9.17 Neuropharmacologic Mechanisms of Agitation [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.17.1 The Neurochemistry of Posttraumatic Agitation

Posttraumatic agitation is not merely a behavioral phenomenon but reflects fundamental disruptions in multiple neurotransmitter systems that normally regulate arousal, inhibition, and emotional processing. Understanding these neurochemical underpinnings explains both why agitation occurs and why certain medications are effective while others are harmful.

**Noradrenergic Dysregulation:**
The locus coeruleus, the primary source of noradrenergic innervation to the cortex, is frequently affected by diffuse axonal injury and brainstem contusion. In the acute post-injury period, there is often an initial surge of norepinephrine release (contributing to sympathetic hyperactivation, tachycardia, and hypertension) followed by a period of relative noradrenergic depletion. This biphasic pattern may explain the temporal evolution from PSH-like presentations (early, sympathetically-driven) to agitation during PTA emergence (later, cognitively-driven). Beta-blockers address the excess noradrenergic tone directly, which is why they are effective.

**Dopaminergic Disruption:**
Disruption of the mesocortical dopaminergic pathway, projecting from the ventral tegmental area to the prefrontal cortex, impairs inhibitory control, impulse regulation, and executive function. The frontal-subcortical circuits that normally suppress inappropriate behavioral responses are dopamine-dependent, and their disruption produces a state of behavioral disinhibition that manifests as agitation. Amantadine, by enhancing dopamine release, partially restores function in these inhibitory circuits, explaining its dual benefit for both agitation and cognitive recovery.

**Serotonergic Imbalance:**
Serotonergic projections from the dorsal raphe nuclei are involved in mood regulation, impulse control, and aggression. Posttraumatic serotonergic dysfunction contributes to irritability, impulsive aggression, and emotional dysregulation. SSRIs like citalopram address this component, which is why they are useful as second-line agents for chronic irritability and aggression after the acute agitation phase has resolved.

**GABAergic Disruption:**
The inhibitory GABAergic system is widely distributed and modulates virtually all other neurotransmitter systems. While GABAergic enhancement (via benzodiazepines or barbiturates) produces sedation that may superficially suppress agitated behavior, it does so at the cost of impairing all cognitive recovery processes and may produce paradoxical excitation in the brain-injured population. This is why GABAergic sedation is the wrong approach to agitation management: it treats the symptom while worsening the disease.

### 9.17.2 The Excitatory-Inhibitory Imbalance Model

The overarching framework for understanding agitation (and, more broadly, PSH) is the excitatory-inhibitory imbalance model. The intact brain maintains a carefully calibrated balance between excitatory (glutamatergic, catecholaminergic) and inhibitory (GABAergic, serotonergic) neurotransmission. TBI disrupts this balance in favor of excitation through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Loss of descending inhibitory control**: Cortical and diencephalic injury removes the tonic inhibitory influence over subcortical and brainstem excitatory circuits
2. **Glutamate excitotoxicity**: Excessive glutamate release in the acute phase creates a hyperexcitable neural environment
3. **Inflammatory mediator effects**: Neuroinflammatory cytokines (IL-1beta, TNF-alpha, IL-6) increase neuronal excitability and lower seizure/agitation thresholds
4. **Sympathetic hyperactivation**: Loss of parasympathetic tone and excessive sympathetic output create a peripheral state of hyperarousal that feeds back to exacerbate central agitation

This model explains why the pharmacotherapy hierarchy progresses from agents that dampen excitation without cognitive cost (beta-blockers), to agents that restore inhibitory balance through dopaminergic modulation (amantadine), to mood stabilizers (valproic acid), before resorting to direct neuronal inhibition (antipsychotics) as a last resort.

## 9.18 Behavioral Management Across the Recovery Continuum [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 9.18.1 Rancho Level-Specific Behavioral Expectations

Understanding the expected behavioral profile at each Rancho Los Amigos level helps clinicians recognize when agitation is a normal developmental phase of recovery versus an atypical behavioral presentation requiring additional investigation.

| Rancho Level | Behavioral Profile | Expected Agitation | Management Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| I (No Response) | No behavioral output | None | Medical stability; prevention of complications |
| II (Generalized Response) | Non-specific, inconsistent responses to stimuli | None | Sensory stimulation program; minimize noxious stimuli |
| III (Localized Response) | Inconsistent but recognizable responses; pulls away from pain, turns toward sound | Minimal | Structured sensory stimulation; begin consistent schedule |
| **IV (Confused-Agitated)** | **Peak agitation phase; bizarre, non-purposeful behavior; no short-term memory; severely impaired attention** | **MAXIMUM** | **Environmental modification; pharmacotherapy hierarchy; safety focus; family education** |
| V (Confused-Inappropriate) | Non-agitated but confused; responds to simple commands inconsistently; highly distractible; memory severely impaired | Decreasing | Begin structured cognitive rehabilitation; consistent routine; cueing and repetition |
| VI (Confused-Appropriate) | Goal-directed behavior with cueing; emerging awareness of disability; may become depressed or frustrated | Occasional (frustration-based) | Cognitive rehabilitation; begin emotional processing; community reentry planning |
| VII (Automatic-Appropriate) | Performs daily routine automatically; impaired judgment and planning; lacks insight | Rare (situational) | Executive function training; community reentry; vocational assessment |
| VIII (Purposeful-Appropriate) | New learning capability; may have persistent cognitive deficits; good self-awareness | Rare | Community reintegration; vocational rehabilitation; long-term cognitive strategies |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Agitation at Rancho Level IV is a NORMAL and expected phase of TBI recovery. It does NOT indicate a poor prognosis. In fact, progression through Level IV (as opposed to remaining at Levels I-III) suggests that the brain is recovering sufficiently to generate behavioral output, even if that output is disorganized. Families should be educated that agitation, while distressing to witness, often represents neurological progress.

### 9.18.2 Long-Term Behavioral Sequelae and Management

Beyond the acute agitation phase, TBI survivors may exhibit chronic neurobehavioral changes that require ongoing management:

**Irritability and Anger Dyscontrol:**
Chronic irritability affects 30-70% of TBI survivors and is one of the most burdensome symptoms for families and caregivers. The threshold for anger activation is pathologically lowered by frontal lobe injury, and the normal inhibitory mechanisms that allow emotional self-regulation are impaired. Management includes:
- Pharmacologic: SSRIs (sertraline, citalopram), beta-blockers (propranolol), anticonvulsants (valproic acid, carbamazepine)
- Behavioral: Anger management training (when cognitively ready, typically Rancho VI+), cognitive behavioral therapy adapted for TBI, stress inoculation training
- Environmental: Identification and avoidance of triggers; structured routines; reduced cognitive demands during high-stress periods

**Impulsivity:**
Impulsivity after TBI reflects disruption of the prefrontal cortex's role in response inhibition, delay of gratification, and consequence evaluation. Clinical manifestations include impulsive spending, impulsive eating, socially inappropriate comments, and risk-taking behavior. Management combines structured environmental controls (limited access to finances, supervised decision-making) with pharmacotherapy (methylphenidate for attention-related impulsivity; SSRIs for serotonergic impulsivity) and cognitive rehabilitation targeting executive function.

**Apathy and Abulia:**
As discussed in Chapter 8, apathy and abulia reflect dopaminergic dysfunction in frontal-subcortical circuits and should NOT be treated with SSRIs, which may worsen the condition. Dopaminergic agents (methylphenidate, amantadine, bromocriptine) are the pharmacologic mainstay, combined with behavioral activation strategies, structured scheduling, and progressive goal-setting.

### 9.18.3 The Role of Neuropsychological Assessment

Neuropsychological testing provides the objective data foundation for behavioral management planning. Key assessments relevant to behavioral management include:

- **Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory-2 (MMPI-2)**: Personality and psychopathology screening; identifies depression, anxiety, and personality traits that may contribute to behavioral problems
- **Frontal Systems Behavior Scale (FrSBe)**: Specifically designed to measure apathy, disinhibition, and executive dysfunction -- the three behavioral domains most commonly affected by frontal lobe injury
- **Neuropsychiatric Inventory (NPI)**: Assesses 12 neuropsychiatric domains including delusions, hallucinations, agitation, dysphoria, anxiety, apathy, irritability, euphoria, disinhibition, aberrant motor behavior, nighttime behavior, and appetite changes
- **Overt Aggression Scale (OAS)**: Quantifies aggressive behavior across four domains: verbal aggression, physical aggression against objects, physical aggression against self, and physical aggression against others

These assessments inform treatment planning by identifying the specific behavioral phenotype (aggression vs. disinhibition vs. apathy vs. emotional lability), which determines the targeted pharmacologic and behavioral intervention.
